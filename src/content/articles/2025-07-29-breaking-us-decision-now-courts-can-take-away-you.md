## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit, sitting in Richmond, Virginia, has issued a decision in **United States v. Gould** rejecting a facial constitutional challenge to 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4), the federal statute that prohibits the possession of firearms by any person who has been adjudicated as a mental defective or who has been committed to a mental institution. Mr. Gould brought a facial challenge — arguing that the statute was unconstitutional in all its applications — after being prosecuted for possession of a shotgun following a prior involuntary civil commitment. The Fourth Circuit, applying the framework established by the Supreme Court in **United States v. Rahimi**, 144 S. Ct. 1889 (2024), rejected the facial challenge on the ground that there are at least some constitutional applications of § 922(g)(4): specifically, those cases in which an individual has been adjudicated by a court as a violent danger to himself or others by reason of mental illness. The result in **Gould** was predictable and, under existing doctrine, correct — but it leaves open the considerably more consequential question of whether § 922(g)(4) is constitutional as applied to individuals whose civil commitments were not accompanied by judicial findings of violent dangerousness.

## Background

18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4) provides that it is unlawful for any person who has been adjudicated as a mental defective, or who has been committed to a mental institution, to ship, transport, possess, or receive any firearm or ammunition in interstate commerce. This provision has been on the books since the Gun Control Act of 1968 and has historically been understood as one of the categorical disqualifications from firearm possession that Congress codified alongside § 922(g)(1) through § 922(g)(9).

Mr. Gould was convicted of firearm possession in violation of § 922(g)(4) following a prior involuntary civil commitment. He challenged the statute as facially unconstitutional under the Second Amendment, arguing that no application of § 922(g)(4) could survive scrutiny under the *Bruen* framework. He did not, apparently, raise an as-applied challenge asserting that the specific circumstances of his civil commitment did not give rise to a constitutionally permissible basis for disarmament.

## The Facial Challenge Standard and Rahimi's Analytical Framework

A facial challenge to a statute is among the most demanding arguments a litigant can make. To succeed on a facial Second Amendment challenge, a party must establish that there is no set of circumstances in which the challenged law could constitutionally be applied. If the government can identify even a single constitutional application of the challenged statute, the facial challenge fails.

In **United States v. Rahimi**, the Supreme Court addressed a Second Amendment challenge to § 922(g)(8), which prohibits firearm possession by persons subject to civil protective orders following judicial findings of a credible threat of physical force. The Court upheld the statute against facial challenge, reasoning that the founding era recognized a category of individuals — those who had been adjudicated as dangerous to others — who could lawfully be disarmed. The Court identified two categories of founding-era law as relevant analogs: surety laws, which required individuals found likely to commit violent offenses to post security for their good behavior; and "going armed" laws, which prohibited individuals from carrying weapons in public in ways calculated to terrorize others. Together, these historical sources established that the founding era recognized a limited but genuine power to disarm individuals judicially found to pose a credible danger to others.

The Fourth Circuit applied this same framework to § 922(g)(4). The court reasoned that there are civil commitment cases — specifically those in which a court has found that the individual poses a violent danger to himself or to others by reason of mental illness — that are indistinguishable in principle from the circumstances the Supreme Court recognized in *Rahimi*. Where an objective judicial proceeding has produced a finding that an individual is a violent danger, the historical tradition identified in *Rahimi* supports the constitutionality of disarming that individual, at least for the duration of the danger. The facial challenge therefore necessarily fails: § 922(g)(4) has at least some constitutional applications, and Mr. Gould's facial attack cannot eliminate the statute in its entirety.

## Why the Facial Challenge Was Always Going to Fail

The facial challenge posture in **Gould** was, in my view, always unlikely to succeed, and the Fourth Circuit's rejection of it is not surprising. **Rahimi** clearly contemplates that the founding era's tradition of disarming judicially adjudicated dangerous persons extends to contexts beyond domestic violence protective orders. Civil commitment proceedings in which a court finds that an individual poses a violent danger to himself or others are a reasonable analog to the surety law proceedings that **Rahimi** relied upon. In both contexts, an objective judicial finding of individual dangerousness precedes the disarmament consequence.

The more legally interesting — and ultimately more consequential — inquiry involves not the facial validity of § 922(g)(4) but its application to individuals whose civil commitments were not premised on judicial findings of violent dangerousness. The statute sweeps broadly: it disarms all persons who have been "committed to a mental institution," regardless of the basis for commitment. A person committed for suicidal ideation without any history or threat of violence against others, a person committed for severe depression, or a person committed pursuant to an emergency hold that expired without any formal adjudication of dangerousness — all of these individuals are categorically barred from firearm possession for life under the statute's plain terms.

Whether § 922(g)(4) is constitutional as applied to these individuals is a different and harder question than the facial challenge Mr. Gould presented. The founding-era analogs identified in *Rahimi* require a judicial finding of dangerousness as the predicate for disarmament. A commitment that lacks any such finding — or that was based on a finding of risk of self-harm only, without any threat to others — may not satisfy the historical tradition requirement. These as-applied challenges remain live and, under *Bruen*'s methodology, have genuine constitutional merit.

## Implications for Future Litigation

The **Gould** decision illustrates the strategic importance of challenging § 922(g)(4) on as-applied grounds rather than facially. A litigant who presents an as-applied challenge can focus the court's attention on the specific facts of his civil commitment — the nature of the proceeding, the findings made, the duration of the commitment, and the basis for the danger determination — and argue that those facts do not satisfy the historical tradition requirement. That argument is substantially more viable than the categorical facial attack that Mr. Gould advanced.

The Second Amendment community should expect this statute to face sustained as-applied challenges as litigants develop the factual records necessary to distinguish their civil commitment histories from the narrow category of cases that **Rahimi** and **Gould** recognize as constitutionally permissible. Courts applying *Bruen* faithfully will be required to examine the factual predicate for each commitment, not simply to defer to the legislative judgment that all persons who have ever been committed are categorically disqualified. The breadth of § 922(g)(4) — which makes no distinction between a violent psychotic adjudicated dangerous to others and a person who voluntarily sought treatment for depression — will face increasing constitutional pressure as the as-applied challenge methodology is developed by skilled litigants.
