The shooting attempt at the White House Correspondents' Dinner on Saturday night at the Washington Hilton is not just a security story. It is a Second Amendment story, and it ties directly to the lawfare campaign now obstructing efforts to make the White House itself a more secure venue for events exactly like this one.

## The Three-Part Test for a Constitutional Gun-Free Zone

For years I have argued that there is only one coherent, history-grounded theory under which the government may declare a place a so-called "sensitive place" and disarm law-abiding Americans inside it. That theory rests on three concrete elements working together as comprehensive security.

First, limited points of entry and exit. The government has to funnel everyone through a tightly controlled choke point, not let crowds drift in from a dozen directions.

Second, magnetometers and screening at those choke points. Concealed handguns are a fact of modern life. If the government wants to keep my firearm out of a venue, it must keep the bad guy's out too. Otherwise the "gun-free zone" sign is just a target painted on the law-abiding.

Third, armed government officers actually present and ready to defend the people inside. Sometimes overt, like a uniformed officer at the door; sometimes covert, like a U.S. Marshal or a Federal Air Marshal in plain clothes. The state must accept the duty of armed defense it is forbidding me to perform for myself. TSA checkpoints are the cleanest illustration: limited entry, screening, and armed officers, all three.

The Hilton on Saturday is the case study. The venue had those three elements in place, and the attacker was apparently stopped at the perimeter rather than reaching the room. Where the government does not provide all three, it has no constitutional warrant to strip ordinary citizens of the means to defend themselves.

## Justice Thomas, Marbled Halls, and the Two-Tier Republic

The deeper problem with the sensitive-places debate is that the people writing the rules already enjoy comprehensive security every day of their lives. Justice Clarence Thomas captured the point a decade ago in his dissent from the denial of certiorari in *Peruta v. California*, 137 S. Ct. 1995 (2017), the case that ultimately set the stage for the Court's recognition five years later in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), that the Second Amendment protects the right to carry in public for self-defense.

Justice Thomas wrote: "For those of us who work in marbled halls, guarded constantly by a vigilant and dedicated police force, the guarantees of the Second Amendment might seem antiquated and superfluous."

That is the whole quarrel in one sentence. Justices, members of Congress, and presidents move through buildings with limited entry, magnetometers, and armed officers as routine architecture. The single mom walking to her car in a parking garage at midnight does not. When a judge tells her she may not carry a firearm in some broad swath of public life, he is projecting his own armed bubble outward and assuming everyone else lives inside it. They do not. The Second Amendment was written for the people outside the marbled halls, not the few inside them.

## Lawfare Against a Safer White House

That brings me to the third thread, and it is the one most viewers may not have connected to the dinner at all. President Trump has been trying to build a new ballroom and security complex on the East Wing of the White House grounds, funded with donor dollars rather than tax money, precisely so that events like the Correspondents' Dinner would not have to be held off-site at private hotels with thinner perimeters.

Federal District Judge Richard J. Leon of the U.S. District Court for the District of Columbia has now issued an amended preliminary injunction halting that project, on a lawsuit brought by the National Trust for Historic Preservation. The asserted standing? An "aesthetic injury" to a member of the trust who walks past the grounds and dislikes the proposed look. Anyone in our community who has watched a Second Amendment plaintiff thrown out for lack of standing under Article III's case-or-controversy requirement should find that head-spinning.

The Department of Justice's brief is blunt: the injunction "indefinitely and unlawfully delays and threatens to completely derail the project, which is absolutely necessary to protect the personal safety and the security of President Trump, who has already been the subject of and survived multiple assassination attempts," along with future presidents, their families, staff, and visitors. The filing describes threat-resistant materials, ballistic glass, hardened columns, and bomb shelters integrated into a single national-security complex.

The executive branch is asking to harden its own house, and a single district judge, on the strength of one woman's stated displeasure with the architecture, has stopped him. Saturday night is the answer to whether that injunction is doing any harm in the real world.

The dots connect cleanly. Comprehensive security is the only constitutional foundation for any "sensitive place." Where the government provides it, ordinary Americans are protected even when disarmed; where it does not, our right to bear arms is not optional. Saturday's attack at the Hilton is now Exhibit A for why blocking a fortified White House venue is not historic preservation. It is lawfare with a body count waiting to happen.
