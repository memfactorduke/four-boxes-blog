## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit recently heard oral argument in **Sanchez v. Bonta**, a case challenging California's near-century-old outright ban on suppressors and silencers as unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. Two of the three judges on the panel appeared sympathetic to the Second Amendment challenger's position, though one cannot predict outcomes with certainty from oral argument. This article analyzes the legal framework governing the case, California's arguments for the ban's constitutionality, why those arguments are inconsistent with **Heller** and **Bruen**, and the significant procedural hurdle created by adverse Ninth Circuit precedent.

## Background

California has prohibited the civilian possession of suppressors and silencers for nearly one hundred years. The state's ban is total — with limited exceptions for certain law enforcement and film industry uses — and no California resident may lawfully possess a suppressor without running afoul of state criminal law.

**Sanchez v. Bonta** originated as a pro se lawsuit filed by Gary Sanchez challenging the constitutionality of that prohibition under the Second Amendment. The district court dismissed Mr. Sanchez's complaint without leave to amend, holding that suppressors are not constitutionally protected "arms" under the Second Amendment. The case is now before the Ninth Circuit on appeal.

The oral argument panel consisted of three judges, two of whom appeared to be meaningfully receptive to the Second Amendment arguments. This is a noteworthy development in a circuit that has historically been one of the least hospitable to Second Amendment claims in the nation.

## The Central Legal Question: Are Suppressors "Arms"?

The threshold issue in **Sanchez v. Bonta** is whether suppressors qualify as "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment. This matters because **Heller** established that the Second Amendment's protection extends to items that are "in common use" for lawful purposes and that are not "dangerous and unusual weapons." An item that is not an "arm" at all — or that falls within the "dangerous and unusual" exception — is not constitutionally protected regardless of how commonly it is owned.

**The Affirmative Case.** The Supreme Court in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), defined "arms" as anything that can be "used offensively or defensively." A suppressor, by reducing the acoustic signature of a firearm discharge, materially affects the defensive utility of a firearm. For a person defending her home against a violent intruder at 3:00 AM, the ability to fire without suffering potentially permanent hearing damage is not a luxury — it is a practical necessity. The suppressor therefore directly influences the arms-bearing conduct that the Second Amendment protects.

Moreover, in **United States v. Rahimi**, 602 U.S. ___ (2024), the Supreme Court reaffirmed that the Second Amendment's plain text covers not just the physical objects denominated "arms," but "arms-bearing conduct" — meaning any regulation that substantially affects the exercise of the right is subject to constitutional scrutiny. Even if one were to argue that a suppressor is an accessory rather than an arm, the regulation of that accessory falls within the Second Amendment's protected scope under **Rahimi**'s framework.

**The Common Use Standard.** There are approximately 3.5 million suppressors legally registered under the NFA in the United States. Suppressors are standard equipment for hunters, competitive shooters, and home defenders across the country. They are commonly sold at firearms retailers, manufactured by major American companies, and used daily for lawful purposes. By any measure, suppressors meet the **Heller** standard of being "in common use" for lawful purposes.

The "dangerous and unusual" exception identified in **Heller** applies to weapons that are beyond the ordinary civilian purpose — the Court cited machine guns as an example. Suppressors do not reduce the lethality of a firearm; they reduce its noise. They are safety devices that protect the hearing of the shooter and those nearby. Characterizing a hearing protection device as "dangerous and unusual" inverts the relevant inquiry.

## California's Arguments and Their Weaknesses

California offered the following justifications for its ban, which deserve examination.

**Argument 1: Suppressors make it harder to detect and flee from gunfire.** California's brief argued that suppressors are dangerous because they render it more difficult for potential victims to detect and flee from a shooting, and for law enforcement to locate and neutralize a shooter.

This argument is constitutionally irrelevant under **Heller**'s framework for a fundamental reason: the Supreme Court has been explicit that the Second Amendment's scope is determined by reference to the lawful uses of an arm, not its potential for misuse. Virtually any object — a car, a kitchen knife, a baseball bat — can be used as an instrument of violence. The Second Amendment's analysis focuses on the ordinary, lawful population of users, not the marginal criminal misuse of the item.

Furthermore, the empirical premise of California's argument is questionable. Movie suppressors fire essentially in silence. Real suppressors reduce gunshot noise to approximately 130 decibels — still significantly louder than ordinary conversation and well within the audible range that would alert bystanders to a shooting. The Hollywood fiction of the "silent killer" suppressor is not the operational reality.

**Argument 2: Suppressors have no counterbalancing self-defense utility.** California's brief asserted that suppressors "lack a counterbalancing self-defense use or utility." This claim is demonstrably false. The primary legal consumer use of suppressors is hearing protection — both for the shooter and for family members present during defensive gun use in the home. The suppressor also reduces the startle effect from a loud noise in an enclosed space, which can improve accuracy under stress. These are real, documented, defensive utilities that California's brief simply ignores.

## The **Duncan v. Bonta** Complication

One of the most significant procedural issues at oral argument was the relationship between **Sanchez** and **Duncan v. Bonta**, the Ninth Circuit's prior precedent addressing large-capacity magazines. The **Duncan** line of cases established a Ninth Circuit-specific analytical framework for Second Amendment challenges that, in my view, is inconsistent with **Bruen**'s text-and-history standard.

The lawyers arguing in favor of Mr. Sanchez faced the difficult task of arguing in the alternative: first, that **Duncan** should be interpreted to compel a pro-Second Amendment result even under its own framework; and second, that **Duncan** is wrong and should be overruled in light of **Bruen**. The problem is that a three-judge Ninth Circuit panel lacks authority to overrule prior Ninth Circuit precedent without convening an en banc panel.

This means that even if the panel rules in favor of Mr. Sanchez, its ruling will need to navigate around or distinguish **Duncan** rather than squarely overrule it. If the panel attempts to issue a broad ruling that effectively repudiates **Duncan**, it will likely be taken up en banc — which, in the Ninth Circuit, is generally bad news for Second Amendment claims.

The ultimate resolution of this tension likely requires the United States Supreme Court to grant certiorari in a Ninth Circuit Second Amendment case and issue a ruling that either expressly overrules the Ninth Circuit's aberrant framework or makes clear, as a matter of the **Bruen** test, that categorical bans on commonly owned arms are categorically unconstitutional.

## Implications

**Sanchez v. Bonta** is an important case for several reasons. First, suppressors are the next frontier of Second Amendment litigation after the **Bruen** revolution. If the Ninth Circuit can ban suppressors outright, so can other anti-gun circuits — and the result would be an effective nationwide prohibition on a lawful, commonly owned, safety-enhancing firearm accessory in the most populous areas of the country.

Second, the oral argument dynamic — two sympathetic judges out of three — suggests the possibility of a favorable panel ruling, which would establish Ninth Circuit precedent that California's ban is unconstitutional. That precedent, in turn, would affect similar bans in other states that fall within the Ninth Circuit's jurisdiction, including Hawaii, Oregon, and Washington.

Third, and most broadly, the case presents the question of whether the **Bruen** revolution is real and durable in the lower courts or whether it can be effectively circumvented by courts that are ideologically hostile to Second Amendment rights. The answer to that question will be determined not by any single case but by the accumulation of lower court decisions over the next several years — and by the Supreme Court's willingness to intervene when those decisions go wrong.
