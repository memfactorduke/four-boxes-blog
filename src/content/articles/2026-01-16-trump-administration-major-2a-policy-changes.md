## Summary

The Trump administration's Department of Justice, through the Office of Legal Counsel, has issued a memorandum of opinion that constitutes one of the most significant executive branch pronouncements on Second Amendment rights in the modern era. The memorandum declares the 1927 federal statute prohibiting the shipment of handguns through the United States Postal Service unconstitutional under the Second Amendment, recognizes a constitutional right to group firearms training and instruction, articulates an ancillary rights doctrine that brings acquisition, maintenance, and repair of firearms within the Second Amendment's plain text protection, and establishes the conceptual foundation for what I assess to be a developing administrative position in favor of nationwide concealed carry portability. Taken together, the memorandum represents a comprehensive recalibration of the executive branch's understanding of the Second Amendment's scope — and its downstream consequences for pending and future litigation may prove as significant as any single court decision.

## Background: The Nature and Authority of OLC Opinions

Before turning to the substance of the memorandum, it is necessary to explain what an Office of Legal Counsel opinion is and why it matters for purposes well beyond the specific legal question it formally addresses.

The Office of Legal Counsel functions, within the executive branch, as a form of supreme court for the executive departments and agencies. OLC opinions are binding on all federal executive agencies and departments; they represent the authoritative legal position of the United States government on the questions they address. When the OLC declares a federal statute unconstitutional, the executive branch is directed to treat that statute as unenforceable — regardless of whether any court has yet passed on the question.

As I have argued consistently in the context of court decisions, there are two distinct dimensions to any authoritative legal pronouncement, and both must be attended to carefully.

The first dimension is the formal holding — the specific legal question asked and answered. In the case of this OLC memorandum, the formal holding is that the 1927 federal postal handgun ban is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment.

The second dimension — and in many respects the more consequential one for the long-term trajectory of Second Amendment law — consists of the reasoning, methodology, citations, and parenthetical characterizations employed in reaching that holding. These elements of an opinion signal the issuing authority's broader understanding of the law and provide the doctrinal raw material from which future arguments will be constructed. An opinion that holds X while articulating the reasoning in a manner that necessarily implies Y and Z has, as a practical matter, substantially advanced the legal landscape regarding Y and Z — even before any formal ruling on those questions is issued. This OLC memorandum is, I submit, precisely such an opinion.

## The 1927 Federal Postal Handgun Ban Declared Unconstitutional

The specific statute addressed in the memorandum is the federal law enacted in 1927 that prohibits ordinary citizens from shipping handguns through the United States Postal Service. The ban is not absolute — it contains carve-outs permitting licensed firearms dealers, manufacturers, and importers (federal firearms licensees, or FFLs) to use the postal system for handgun shipments in connection with their commercial activity. What it prohibits is the use of postal mail by ordinary private citizens for the shipment of their own handguns.

The OLC concluded that this statute cannot survive scrutiny under the Second Amendment as interpreted by the Supreme Court in **District of Columbia v. Heller** and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**. The reasoning proceeds along two related lines.

First, the government cannot impose special disabilities on ordinary citizens with respect to protected arms that it does not impose on commercially licensed actors exercising the same activity. When the postal system permits FFLs to ship handguns but denies the same access to ordinary law-abiding citizens, the government has created a two-tiered system in which some Americans enjoy a practical exercise of the constitutional right that others are denied. The OLC memorandum states specifically that the government "cannot carve out ordinary citizens and protected arms for special disabilities" when it comes to the distribution channels of America. The asymmetry between FFL-privileged access and citizen-denied access is, on this analysis, constitutionally indefensible.

Second, handguns are protected arms under *Heller* — the Supreme Court held explicitly that the handgun is the quintessential self-defense firearm and that its protection under the Second Amendment is beyond serious question. When the government interferes with the ability of citizens to ship and receive handguns through the primary distribution channel of the country, it imposes a significant burden on the practical exercise of the constitutional right to keep and bear arms. The postal system is not a peripheral convenience; for many Americans, particularly those in rural or geographically isolated communities, it is the most effective — and sometimes the only practical — means of transporting a firearm to a desired destination. Denying citizens access to that channel for the shipment of constitutionally protected arms is, the OLC correctly concludes, a violation of the Second Amendment.

## The Ancillary Rights Doctrine: Acquisition, Maintenance, and Repair

Beyond the specific holding on the postal handgun ban, the OLC memorandum advances a broader doctrinal framework that I regard as one of the most important aspects of the opinion. The memorandum articulates what might be termed an ancillary or concomitant rights doctrine with respect to the Second Amendment.

The core proposition is this: the right to keep and bear arms necessarily entails, as inherent predicates, certain ancillary rights without which the constitutional right cannot be meaningfully exercised. The memorandum identifies several such ancillary rights.

The right to acquire a firearm is characterized, on page six of the memorandum, as "almost always a necessary predicate for the right to keep and to bear arms." The logic is straightforward. A constitutional right to keep and bear arms that carries no corresponding right to obtain arms in the first place is a hollow guarantee. If the government may freely obstruct, burden, or prohibit the acquisition of firearms by law-abiding citizens without implicating the Second Amendment, then *Heller*'s recognition of the individual right becomes practically meaningless. The OLC's explicit identification of acquisition as a constitutional predicate — and its quotation of language from prior authority recognizing "the right to use arms for all ordinary purposes and in all ordinary modes usual in the country," including target shooting, firearms training, and hunting — lays the groundwork for a significant body of future litigation.

The memorandum further recognizes a right to maintain firearms in working condition, which necessarily includes a right to repair. This is not, as it may appear on first reading, a trivial or narrowly technical point. State and local governments hostile to the Second Amendment have shown increasing creativity in devising regulatory frameworks that technically permit firearm ownership while making the practical exercise of that ownership prohibitively burdensome. Restrictions on firearm maintenance, access to replacement parts, or the availability of repair services are precisely the kind of targeted burden that the Bruen framework — properly applied — is designed to address. By identifying the right to maintain and repair firearms as a component of the Second Amendment right, the OLC memorandum forecloses a significant category of potential anti-gun regulatory maneuvering and shifts the *Bruen* burden to any government seeking to restrict these activities.

## Group Firearms Training as a Constitutional Right

Among the most practically significant holdings of the OLC memorandum is its recognition that group firearms training and instruction is constitutionally protected activity under the Second Amendment — and, I submit, under the First Amendment as well.

The doctrinal framework rests on what I have articulated as a First Amendment assembly analogy. The principle is this: the First Amendment protects not only individual speech, petition, and religious exercise, but the right to assemble in groups to engage in those activities collectively. An individual may write a letter to his representative; a group may gather to petition collectively. An individual may pray privately; a congregation may worship collectively. The exercise of a constitutional right by a group of individuals acting together is as protected as the exercise of that right by a single individual acting alone.

Applied to the Second Amendment: whatever conduct the Second Amendment protects when engaged in by a single individual, it protects equally when engaged in by a group of individuals acting together. An individual may train with his firearm; a group may assemble for collective firearms training and instruction. There is no principled basis on which to hold that individual target practice is constitutionally protected while organized group training is not. The OLC memorandum reaches precisely this conclusion, with support from Thomas Cooley's foundational treatise, which recognized "the right to meet for voluntary discipline in arms" as among the natural accompaniments of the constitutional right to keep and bear arms.

The practical significance of this holding is substantial. It directly implicates the legality of state and local laws that have sought to restrict, license, or prohibit organized firearms training activities and what some jurisdictions have characterized as "paramilitary" or "militia" conduct. Under the OLC's analysis, such laws burden constitutionally protected activity and must survive the demanding historical tradition scrutiny that *Bruen* demands — scrutiny they are unlikely to survive.

## The Foundation for Nationwide Carry Portability

The final dimension of the OLC memorandum that I submit warrants careful attention is its implicit but discernible development of the conceptual foundation for nationwide concealed carry portability — that is, the principle that a citizen licensed to carry a firearm in his or her state of residence retains, by constitutional right rather than merely by statutory grace, the practical ability to exercise that right when traveling interstate.

The memorandum makes this argument through the lens of the postal ban analysis. It observes that traveling with a firearm can be "difficult, if not impossible" — and that the postal system may therefore represent "the most effective way to transport an individual's firearm to a destination." The illustrative examples cited involve scenarios in which a law-abiding citizen is traveling from one permissive state to another, but the route requires passage through or a layover in a restrictive jurisdiction where possession of the firearm would be criminal.

The constitutional dimension of this observation is significant. A citizen who may lawfully exercise First Amendment rights — worship, speak, petition, assemble — in every state of the Union regardless of where he resides cannot, by any principled constitutional theory, be denied the equivalent portability of Second Amendment rights. If I may travel to any state and attend a church, express a political opinion, or protest a government official, there is no sound basis in constitutional law for the proposition that I may not travel to any state and maintain my ability to exercise my right of self-defense. The OLC's recognition of this symmetry is, in my assessment, the doctrinal seedbed from which a broader administrative and judicial push toward nationwide carry portability will grow.

The specific provision of federal law that currently requires handgun purchases to be completed through an FFL in the buyer's state of residence — meaning that a citizen who passes a background check in a state where she is visiting may not take possession of the firearm there, but must have it shipped to her home state — is another manifestation of the same constitutional problem the memorandum is beginning to address. The receipt of a firearm is, as the OLC states, "almost always a necessary predicate for the right to keep and to bear arms." Restrictions that impede that receipt by constructing artificial state-residency barriers impose a burden on the plain text of the Second Amendment that must be justified by historical tradition — and that, I submit, cannot be so justified.

## The Broader Significance: Building the Second Amendment Infrastructure

I want to situate this OLC memorandum within the larger strategic project of restoring the Second Amendment as a first-class constitutional right, because its significance cannot be fully appreciated in isolation.

The anti-gun coalition, confronted with the Supreme Court's decisions in *Heller*, *McDonald*, and *Bruen*, has adopted a strategy of attrition: accepting that direct prohibition of firearm ownership may be constitutionally foreclosed, while pursuing an increasingly targeted regulatory apparatus designed to burden the practical exercise of the right at every stage — acquisition, maintenance, repair, carry, training, and commercial distribution. The goal is not to ban guns outright; it is to make the ownership and use of guns so practically burdensome, so administratively complex, and so legally precarious that the right becomes effectively nominal.

The OLC memorandum counters this strategy by identifying the specific ancillary activities — acquisition, maintenance, repair, training, and portability — as themselves protected by the Second Amendment's text. This is precisely the correct analytical move: if these activities fall within the Second Amendment's plain text, then any regulation of them must satisfy *Bruen*'s demanding historical tradition test, which the vast majority of modern anti-gun regulatory burdens cannot satisfy. The OLC is not merely declaring one statute unconstitutional; it is establishing the constitutional vocabulary with which future challenges to anti-gun regulatory burdens will be framed and litigated.

The Operation Chokepoint program under the Obama administration — under which federal banking regulators pressured financial institutions to deny services to federally licensed firearms dealers and manufacturers — represents the kind of targeted anti-gun discrimination that the OLC memorandum implicitly addresses. Anti-gun discrimination, whether through postal access, financial services, insurance, or any other commercially essential activity, constitutes an unconstitutional burden on a protected right when it is directed specifically at the exercise of Second Amendment-protected conduct.

## Implications

The Trump administration's OLC memorandum is, in my assessment, among the most practically significant executive branch developments for Second Amendment rights since the publication of *Heller* itself. It does not merely declare one obscure statute unconstitutional; it establishes, through the reasoning and doctrinal architecture it deploys, a comprehensive framework for understanding the Second Amendment's scope that is considerably more protective of constitutional rights than the framework that has governed executive branch action in prior administrations.

The memorandum's recognition of ancillary rights — acquisition, maintenance, repair — its protection of group firearms training, its implicit development of nationwide portability principles, and its condemnation of government discrimination against ordinary citizens and protected arms in the distribution of commercially essential services together constitute a doctrinal platform from which the Second Amendment community can build a substantial body of administrative and judicial challenge.

This is, as I have argued consistently, a step-by-step project. No single opinion — whether from the Supreme Court, a circuit court, or the Office of Legal Counsel — resolves the entire contest at once. What the OLC memorandum represents is a meaningful and well-reasoned step forward: it identifies the relevant constitutional territory, establishes the analytical framework, and signals clearly that the executive branch of the United States government intends to treat the Second Amendment as what it is — a genuine, fully enforceable constitutional right, no less entitled to the government's protection and respect than any other guarantee in the Bill of Rights.
