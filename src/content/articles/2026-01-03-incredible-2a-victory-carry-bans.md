## Summary

A three-judge panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit, in an opinion authored by Judge Lawrence VanDyke, has declared California's de jure ban on open carrying of handguns unconstitutional under the Second and Fourteenth Amendments. The panel held that there is no historical record of any law restricting open carry at the founding, that antebellum courts explicitly recognized open carry's unique constitutional protection, and that California's population-based licensing scheme — which effectively ensured that no open carry license was ever issued to anyone — was inconsistent with the right to bear arms as incorporated against the states through the Fourteenth Amendment. A concurring opinion by Judge Kenneth Lee, joined by Judge VanDyke, compared California's conduct to that of "an unscrupulous telemarketer" — a rebuke that warrants careful attention. In my assessment, **Baird v. Bonta** represents a significant pro-Second Amendment victory in the Ninth Circuit and a textbook application of the **Bruen** methodology.

## Background: California's Open Carry Regime

The case arises out of plaintiff Mark Baird's civil rights lawsuit against the California Attorney General. Baird is a law-abiding citizen who wished to openly carry a firearm in California. California law, however, banned open carry in all counties with a population exceeding 200,000 people, while nominally permitting open carry in smaller counties subject to a licensing requirement.

In practice, this framework operated as a total prohibition. California admitted in the record that it had no documentation of a single open carry license ever being issued to any applicant anywhere in the state. This extraordinary admission — that a nominally available legal process had never once resulted in the issuance of the license it purported to offer — formed the evidentiary foundation for the panel's and concurrence's conclusions about California's conduct.

## The Panel Opinion: Judge VanDyke's Analysis

Judge Lawrence VanDyke, writing for the panel, applied the two-step **Bruen** framework: first, whether the challenged conduct falls within the plain text of the Second Amendment; second, whether the challenged regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States.

On the first question, the panel held without difficulty that open carrying of a handgun falls within the plain text of the right to "bear arms." The word "bear" has always encompassed carrying a weapon on one's person outside the home, as **Heller** confirmed and **Bruen** reaffirmed. California's population-based prohibition therefore implicated the constitutional right and required historical justification.

On the second question — historical tradition — the panel's conclusion was unambiguous: "There is no record of any law restricting open carry at the founding, let alone a distinctly similar historical regulation." The panel further noted that "[i]n the antebellum era, courts across the country were explicit in noting the unique constitutional protection granted to open carry." Synthesizing this history, the panel held that "California's de jure ban on open carry in counties with a population above 200,000 people is inconsistent with the right to bear arms as applied to the states through the 14th Amendment."

This is a clean application of **Bruen**. Where the government cannot identify a historical analogue for its regulation — particularly one from the founding era — the regulation cannot stand. California produced no historical record of open carry restrictions at the founding. The panel's holding followed directly.

## The Concurrence: The "Unscrupulous Telemarketer" Rebuke

The concurring opinion by Judge Kenneth Lee, joined by Judge VanDyke, merits separate attention for its detailed examination of California's conduct in administering its nominal open carry licensing regime. Judge Lee wrote:

> "I write separately to highlight how California has apparently resorted to subterfuge to deny its citizens their Second Amendment rights. California insists that citizens in counties with populations fewer than [200,000] can apply for an open carry license. Yet, California admits that it has no record of even one open carry license ever being issued. How could this be? One potential reason is that California has misled its citizens about how to apply for an open carry license."

The concurrence then concluded with language that has justifiably attracted significant attention: "Our own state government must behave better than an unscrupulous telemarketer."

The comparison is apt and legally significant. An "unscrupulous telemarketer" is one who advertises a product or service that is in fact unavailable, uses confusing or deliberately misleading language to prevent customers from exercising rights they formally possess, and exploits information asymmetries to deny people what the law actually entitles them to. California's open carry licensing regime operated in precisely this fashion. The state maintained the formal structure of a licensing process — complete with application requirements, procedural steps, and nominal availability — while ensuring through information suppression and administrative obstruction that no license would ever actually issue.

This is constitutional bad faith. A government may not comply with the letter of a constitutional requirement while deliberately hollowing out its substance through administrative manipulation. Judge Lee's concurrence correctly identifies this practice for what it is, and the comparison to telemarketer fraud — sharp as it is — is analytically accurate.

## Significance of Judge VanDyke as a Potential Supreme Court Nominee

It is worth noting that Judge VanDyke has been widely discussed as a potential Supreme Court nominee. His track record in Second Amendment cases — consistently applying **Bruen**'s text-history-tradition methodology, refusing to import interest-balancing frameworks that **Bruen** explicitly rejected, and writing opinions that squarely confront rather than avoid Second Amendment questions — reflects precisely the jurisprudential approach that the Second Amendment community should hope to see on the Supreme Court. **Baird v. Bonta** is another data point in that record.

## Implications

**Baird v. Bonta** is significant for several reasons. First, it demonstrates that even the Ninth Circuit — historically among the most hostile circuits to Second Amendment claims — is capable of producing sound **Bruen** analysis when the historical record is as clear as it is here. Second, the concurrence's frank discussion of California's deliberate obstruction of the licensing process sets an important precedent for how courts should treat state actors who formally comply with constitutional requirements while covertly undermining them. Third, the decision reinforces the proposition that the right to bear arms is a right to carry, not merely to keep — that the Second Amendment protects both the home and the public square.

In my assessment, this is an important victory. The Ninth Circuit has now held that open carry is constitutionally protected, that California's population-based ban is unconstitutional, and that the state's conduct in administering its nominal licensing regime was worthy of comparison to unscrupulous commercial fraud. That is a significant development, and the Second Amendment community should recognize it as such.
