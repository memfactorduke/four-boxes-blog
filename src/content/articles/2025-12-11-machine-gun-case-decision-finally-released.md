## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit has issued a 2-1 decision in **United States v. Kitson**, upholding a conviction for allegedly unlawful transfer and possession of a pre-1986 unregistered machine gun. The case is legally remarkable — and, in my view, wrongly decided by the majority — because the defendant never physically possessed the machine gun, and the statutory exemption under 18 U.S.C. § 922(o) for transfers to the United States government squarely appears to cover the transaction at issue. Judge Lawrence VanDyke filed a powerful dissent that deserves careful attention.

*The following analysis is based on my review of the Ninth Circuit's majority opinion and the dissent in* United States v. Kitson*, decided December 2025.*

## Background

This case illustrates a principle I return to often on this channel: bad facts can make bad law, and judges who approach firearms cases through the lens of their emotions rather than the logic of the applicable statutes will reach unjust outcomes. The charges in **United States v. Kitson** arose from a federal sting operation, and the specific statutory language of 18 U.S.C. § 922(o) is critical to understanding why the majority's ruling is deeply problematic.

It is worth distinguishing this case from the more common scenario involving an ordinary citizen who illegally possesses or manufactures an unregistered machine gun. The facts here are dramatically different, and those differences matter at the highest levels of legal analysis.

## The Facts

In January 2020, federal agents learned that Mr. Daniel Kitson, a previously convicted felon, was willing to facilitate the sale of a World War II-era Russian machine gun. The seller of the weapon was a third party, Mr. Bonahan. An undercover federal agent, posing as a firearms and drug trafficker, engaged in communications with Kitson to arrange the transaction.

Here is the critical factual finding from the opinion itself: **Mr. Kitson never physically possessed the machine gun.** He never touched it. He never held it. What he did was arrange for a transfer of the firearm between Mr. Bonahan — who held the gun — and the undercover federal agent. After Kitson and the undercover agent drove to Bonahan's location, cash was exchanged for the machine gun, which passed from Bonahan directly to the federal agent. The United States Marshals Service subsequently arrested Kitson.

## The Statutory Issue

This is where the case becomes legally fascinating and, in my view, where the majority went plainly wrong. Section 922(o) of Title 18 provides, in relevant part: "Except as provided in paragraph (2) below, it shall be unlawful for any person to transfer or possess a machine gun."

However, § 922(o)(2) contains a critical exemption. That subsection provides that the general prohibition on transfer and possession of machine guns **does not apply** with respect to:

> a transfer to . . . the United States or any department or agency thereof.

Let those words sink in. The statute itself carves out an explicit exemption for transfers to the federal government. And what happened in this case? The machine gun was transferred from Mr. Bonahan to an undercover federal agent — meaning a representative of the United States government, acting on behalf of the United States government.

One cannot charge Mr. Kitson with the unlawful *possession* of the machine gun, because he never possessed it. And one cannot charge him with an unlawful *transfer* of the machine gun to the federal government, because Congress expressly exempted such transfers from criminal liability.

The statute is unambiguous. The exemption covers exactly what occurred here.

## The Majority Opinion

The majority was authored by a Barack Obama appointee, a former Ruth Bader Ginsburg law clerk, John Owen, with a Jimmy Carter appointee joining. The majority upheld the conviction by reasoning — in ways I find legally untenable — that Kitson's role in arranging the transfer brought him within the statutory prohibition, notwithstanding the fact that he never possessed the weapon and that the recipient of the transfer was the United States government itself.

The majority further attempted to characterize the NFA's registration scheme as analogous to a "shall-issue" licensing regime that they deemed presumptively constitutional under **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**. This characterization is, in my assessment, clearly false. A registration scheme under the NFA is not a shall-issue licensing system — it is a pre-1986 registration freeze that bars virtually all private citizens from ever registering new machine guns. These are fundamentally different regulatory frameworks.

The court was careful to frame its holding as an "as-applied" challenge — meaning it purported to rule only on the constitutionality of the NFA as applied to the specific circumstances of Kitson's case — rather than as a facial challenge to the suppressor and machine gun registration requirements of the NFA as a whole. I read between the lines here: the court appears to be reserving the right to reach a different outcome in a better-presented future case, while declining to rule properly for this defendant.

## Judge VanDyke's Dissent

Judge Lawrence VanDyke, widely regarded as one of the most intellectually rigorous Second Amendment jurists on the federal bench and a potential future Supreme Court Justice, filed what I consider a powerful and correct dissent. He recognized the statutory anomaly the majority glossed over and disagreed with the majority's characterization of the NFA's registration requirements as equivalent to a Bruen-compatible licensing regime.

VanDyke's dissent is important not only for what it says about this specific case, but for what it signals about the future direction of Second Amendment jurisprudence in the Ninth Circuit as that court's composition continues to change.

## Implications

The majority's reasoning in **United States v. Kitson** is troubling on two distinct levels.

First, on the pure statutory interpretation question: the majority effectively ignored an express congressional exemption for transfers to the United States government. This is a fundamental error in statutory analysis. When Congress writes an exemption into a criminal statute, courts are obligated to apply it. The outcome reached by the majority — upholding a conviction for a transfer that Congress explicitly declared non-criminal — is, in my view, a miscarriage of statutory justice driven by judicial distaste for the defendant's conduct rather than by fidelity to the text of the law.

Second, on the constitutional question: the majority's effort to analogize the NFA's registration scheme to a shall-issue licensing regime is analytically unsound and, if left uncorrected, could create harmful precedent for future Second Amendment challenges to machine gun registration restrictions.

The appropriate response from the Second Amendment legal community and the Trump DOJ is to monitor this case closely for further appellate development, including potential en banc reconsideration or a petition for certiorari to the Supreme Court, where the statutory exemption question could be squarely presented.

As I have noted repeatedly: bad facts make bad law. The fact that Mr. Kitson was a convicted felon with unsavory associations does not change what the statute says. The rule of law demands that courts apply statutory text faithfully, regardless of their feelings about a particular defendant.
