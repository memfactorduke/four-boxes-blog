> "Amtrak's lockbox proposal is not the right answer on the merits — the right answer is on-person carry — but it is another Overton-window shift in favor of the Second Amendment, and we win this war one battle at a time."
>
> — Professor Mark W. Smith, Four Boxes Diner Host

Major Second Amendment news from an unexpected place: Amtrak. According to CBS News, citing two sources who spoke to the Associated Press on condition of anonymity, the National Railroad Passenger Corporation is considering a policy change that would allow passengers to bring firearms onto roughly 1,500 trains a day — including the Northeast Corridor — by installing onboard lockboxes where the conductor holds the only key. CBS reported on May 1, 2026 that the proposal has been under consideration since early this year, after Trump administration officials pressured Amtrak to ease its firearm-transport restrictions. This is a meaningful expansion of the Overton window in favor of the right to keep and bear arms.

## What Amtrak Is Actually Proposing

The current rules date to the Wicker amendment, which Senator Roger Wicker (R-MS) attached to the Consolidated Appropriations Act of 2010, P.L. 111-117, signed December 2009 and implemented by Amtrak the following December. Under that regime, firearms are allowed only on the couple dozen long-distance trains with checked baggage service — unloaded, hard-sided case, declared 24 hours in advance, ammunition capped at 11 pounds. The Northeast Corridor has been off-limits entirely.

The proposed new policy expands this dramatically. Per CBS:

> "Amtrak's proposed rule change, which the railroad could begin testing soon, calls for adding lockboxes to its trains to enable passengers throughout the country to bring guns aboard, instead of allowing guns on trains that have locked bags."
>
> — CBS News, May 1, 2026

Is this a perfect outcome? No. The right answer, as I see it, is on-person carry for self-defense, just like in your car or on the street. But going from "checked baggage on a couple dozen long-distance trains" to "lockboxes on every Amtrak train, including the Northeast Corridor" is a real, structural expansion. Another battle won.

## Why the Constitution Reaches Amtrak

I have heard pushback on whether the Second Amendment even applies here. Technically, 49 U.S.C. § 24302 says Amtrak "is not a department, agency, or instrumentality of the United States Government." But the Supreme Court in *Department of Transportation v. Association of American Railroads*, 575 U.S. 43 (2015), held Amtrak IS a governmental entity for separation-of-powers purposes — the federal government owns its preferred stock, seven of its ten directors are presidentially appointed, and Congress funnels roughly $2.4 billion a year in appropriations into its operations. Federally-chartered, federally-subsidized common carrier. The Constitution reaches it.

## The Cole Allen Incident Does Not Change the Analysis

CBS notes Amtrak has not abandoned the proposal despite the recent arrest of a man who traveled by Amtrak from California to Washington, D.C. That is Cole Tomas Allen, 31, of Torrance, California, now charged with attempted assassination of President Trump after the April 25, 2026 incident at the White House Correspondents' Association Dinner security checkpoint. Allen rode Amtrak from Los Angeles to Chicago to D.C. carrying a 12-gauge pump-action shotgun and a Rock Island Armory 1911 .38 pistol. He shot a Secret Service officer in the chest — saved by a ballistic vest — and the officer returned fire.

Here is where my frustration boils over. The anti-gunners point to Allen and say, "See? This is why we cannot expand gun rights on trains." That is exactly backwards. We do not assess constitutional rights through the prism of misuse by a whackadoodle nut job psychopath. We assess them through the prism of LAWFUL use by ordinary citizens. You can misuse free speech and every other right in the Bill of Rights. We do not shrink the right because of the abuser. The Allen case is a criminal-prosecution problem, not a Second Amendment problem.

## The Comprehensive-Security Test — Which Amtrak Fails

I have argued for years, including in my Georgetown Journal of Law and Public Policy article on post-*Bruen* sensitive places, that a location can only be a constitutionally legitimate gun-free zone if the government provides comprehensive security: limited entry points, metal detectors and screening, and armed security on site. Think TSA checkpoints, federal courthouses, the DOJ. If the government takes away your right to defend yourself, it has to step in and do the defending.

Amtrak does none of that. Trains have countless doors at every station stop, no magnetometers, no Federal Air Marshal walking the aisles. A "no guns" rule here is not security — it is an unfunded promise. Even the lockbox proposal falls short of what the Constitution requires. But it moves the line in the right direction.

## Beccaria Saw This Two Centuries Before We Were a Country

Cesare Beccaria, the Italian Enlightenment criminologist whose 1764 treatise *On Crimes and Punishments* directly influenced Thomas Jefferson and John Adams, said it best:

> "The laws that forbid the carrying of arms... disarm those only who are neither inclined nor determined to commit crimes... Such laws make things worse for the assaulted and better for the assailants; they serve rather to encourage than to prevent homicides, for an unarmed man may be attacked with greater confidence than an armed man."

Two and a half centuries later, Dr. John Lott's research on gun-free zones makes the same empirical point: a posted "no guns" sign without comprehensive security is not a safe zone — it is a kill zone, a magnet for the very predators the rule pretends to exclude. Congress is reportedly considering bills to make assaulting Amtrak workers a federal felony, modeled on the airline-crew protection statute. Fine. But anyone willing to commit murder on a train is not deterred by a felony enhancement on assaulting the conductor. Beccaria explained that in 1764.

## Island Hopping Toward Restoration

Joseph Overton, the late Mackinac Center analyst who gave us the concept that bears his name, taught us that the range of politically acceptable policy moves over time. On the Second Amendment, the window keeps shifting in our direction. Wars are won island by island, the way the United States fought across the Pacific in World War II. The Wicker amendment took us from a total Amtrak gun ban to checked baggage on long-distance trains. This proposal would take us to lockboxes on every train in the system. The next battle is on-person carry. Encourage Amtrak to move forward — and keep pushing.
