A three-judge panel of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit has ruled that New York City's ban on stun guns survives constitutional challenge — not by meeting the government's burden under the *Bruen* framework, but by making sure that burden never shifts to the government at all.

The summary order in *Calce v. City of New York* holds that stun guns and tasers are not covered by the Second Amendment's plain text because the plaintiffs — the Firearms Policy Coalition, the Second Amendment Foundation, and several individual New Yorkers — failed to prove these arms are "in common use today for self-defense." That reasoning is wrong, and the panel appears to know it.

## The Burden-Shifting Game

Under the two-step *Bruen* methodology, step one asks whether the Second Amendment's plain text covers the individual's conduct. If it does, the Constitution presumptively protects that conduct, and the burden shifts to the government at step two to justify its regulation through the Nation's historical tradition of firearms law.

The critical question in *Calce* is where the "in common use" inquiry belongs. The Second Circuit placed it at step one, forcing the plaintiffs to affirmatively prove that stun guns are commonly owned before the plain text is even implicated. This is burden allocation by design. Everyone involved understands that if the case reaches step two, the government cannot demonstrate a historical tradition of banning non-lethal electronic arms — because no such tradition exists.

The Supreme Court in *Heller* defined "arms" as anything that can be used offensively or defensively. A stun gun plainly qualifies. The "in common use" language in *Heller* arose during the *historical* analysis — specifically, the longstanding tradition permitting bans only on "dangerous and unusual weapons." That is a government burden at step two, not a plaintiff burden at step one.

## Ignoring the Supreme Court — by Citation

Perhaps the most revealing feature of the panel's order is its authority. The Second Circuit did not cite *Heller*'s definition of arms. It did not grapple with *Caetano v. Massachusetts*, the 2016 per curiam decision in which the Supreme Court unanimously held that stun guns are constitutionally protected arms. Instead, the panel cited its own prior decisions — a circular authority game that ignores the court's status as an inferior tribunal bound by Article III to follow Supreme Court precedent.

The panel also conflated adjudicative facts with legislative facts. It rejected materials the plaintiffs introduced on appeal — newspaper articles, a Congressional Research Service report documenting surging civilian stun gun purchases — on the ground that they were not presented to the district court. But evidence of an arm's prevalence in American society is a legislative fact, not a disputed question requiring trial testimony. No witness needed to take the stand to establish that the American Revolution happened; likewise, no witness is needed to establish that millions of Americans own stun guns.

## The Summary Order Shield

The panel issued its ruling as a summary order with no precedential effect — a tell. A court confident in its reasoning publishes a precedential opinion. A court that knows the Supreme Court may intervene labels its work as minor housekeeping and hopes no one notices.

Whether the Firearms Policy Coalition and Second Amendment Foundation seek certiorari remains to be seen. If they do, the Supreme Court will confront a lower court that not only ignored *Caetano* but actively worked to ensure the government never had to carry the burden that *Heller* and *Bruen* assign to it.
