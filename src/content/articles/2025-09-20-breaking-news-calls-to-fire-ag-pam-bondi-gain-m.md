## Summary

The United States Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi, has filed a major brief before the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit in the consolidated cases of *Cheeseman v. New Jersey* and *Association of New Jersey Rifle and Pistol Clubs v. New Jersey Attorney General*, which challenge New Jersey's prohibition on AR-15 semi-automatic rifles and magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds. Those cases are scheduled for oral argument before the Third Circuit sitting en banc — meaning the entire active court — on October 15, 2025. The Department of Justice's brief sides fully with the plaintiffs, arguing that the Second Amendment protects all arms in common use for lawful purposes and that New Jersey's ban is unconstitutional. This filing is further evidence that the Trump administration has committed the full resources and authority of the federal executive branch to the defense of Second Amendment rights.

## Background

New Jersey maintains among the most restrictive firearms laws in the United States, including a categorical ban on AR-15 semi-automatic rifles and a prohibition on firearm magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds. These restrictions have been challenged as violating the Second Amendment under the frameworks established in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). The Third Circuit, which has jurisdiction over the federal courts of New Jersey, Delaware, and Pennsylvania, has taken both cases en banc — meaning that all active judges on the court will hear oral argument together rather than the matter being assigned to a three-judge panel.

The en banc posture is itself significant. The Third Circuit's decision to hear these consolidated cases with its full complement of active judges signals the court's recognition that the cases present questions of exceptional importance to Second Amendment jurisprudence. The outcome will bind the entire Third Circuit and will carry substantial weight in Supreme Court proceedings if — as appears likely — the losing party seeks further review.

The October 15, 2025 oral argument is occurring in an environment in which the Department of Justice has now weighed in on the side of the Second Amendment plaintiffs, adding the federal government's considerable institutional authority to the constitutional arguments against New Jersey's ban.

## The Department of Justice's Brief

The Department of Justice's brief in *Cheeseman* and the *Association of New Jersey Rifle and Pistol Clubs* case represents, in my view, one of the most important pro-Second Amendment filings the federal government has made in modern history. The brief takes the position that the Second Amendment protects all arms that are in common use by law-abiding Americans for lawful purposes — a principle drawn directly from *Heller* — and that the government bears the burden of demonstrating that any restriction on commonly owned arms is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in America.

The brief further argues that AR-15 semi-automatic rifles and magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds are manifestly in common use. The most popular rifle in the United States, the AR-15, is owned by tens of millions of law-abiding citizens for self-defense, sport shooting, hunting, and the common defense. Standard-capacity magazines are the factory configuration for the most widely sold centerfire pistols and rifles in the country. An argument that these items may be categorically banned fails at the first step of the *Heller* and *Bruen* analysis.

The brief also addresses the common defense rationale — the Second Amendment's prefatory clause, which identifies a well-regulated militia being necessary to the security of a free state, counsels in favor of protecting the same arms that citizens would bring to bear in the defense of the nation. Restricting civilians to firearms and configurations materially inferior to those maintained by military and law enforcement personnel runs contrary to the Founders' design.

## The Significance of En Banc Review in the Third Circuit

The decision by the Third Circuit to hear these cases en banc reflects the jurisprudential stakes involved. The Third Circuit sits in Philadelphia and its jurisdiction encompasses New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware — states with widely divergent firearms laws. A binding en banc ruling on the constitutionality of AR-15 bans and magazine restrictions will resolve the law across all three states simultaneously and will create or deepen the circuit split that will eventually compel the Supreme Court to address these questions directly.

The composition of the en banc Third Circuit at the time of oral argument is a matter of significant consequence. The confirmation of additional originalist jurists to the Third Circuit before October 15, 2025 — a matter I have separately analyzed — would shift the court's balance in a direction favorable to faithful application of the *Bruen* framework. Every vote on the en banc court matters, and the margin is likely to be narrow.

## Relationship to the *Barnett* Litigation in the Seventh Circuit

The *Cheeseman* and *Association of New Jersey Rifle and Pistol Clubs* cases are proceeding in parallel with *Barnett v. Raoul* in the Seventh Circuit, which involves Illinois's comparable ban on AR-15 rifles and standard-capacity magazines and which is also scheduled for oral argument in this period. The simultaneous consideration of AR-15 ban challenges in multiple circuits — with the federal government now filing briefs on the side of the Second Amendment — creates the conditions for a definitive Supreme Court resolution of the question.

The Department of Justice's brief in the Third Circuit case is, by some assessments, stronger than the brief the Department filed in the Seventh Circuit's *Barnett* proceedings. Both briefs are anchored in the common use test and the *Bruen* historical tradition framework, but the Third Circuit brief's articulation of the constitutional principles reflects the full weight of the federal government's institutional commitment to the Second Amendment claims of the plaintiffs.

## The Stakes of the October 15, 2025 Argument

The en banc argument on October 15, 2025 is among the most consequential Second Amendment proceedings of this decade. The court will be asked to determine whether the Second Amendment permits New Jersey to categorically prohibit the most popular rifle in America and the standard-configuration magazines for the most common defensive firearms. The answer that the full Third Circuit gives will shape the constitutional landscape for AR-15 bans across the country and will directly inform the Supreme Court's calculus regarding whether, and when, to grant certiorari in one of the many pending AR-15 ban cases.

In my analysis, the constitutional answer is clear: under *Heller* and *Bruen*, AR-15 rifles and standard-capacity magazines are protected arms. They are in common use for lawful purposes. The government cannot satisfy the burden of demonstrating that such a categorical ban is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation. The question is whether the en banc Third Circuit will apply the *Bruen* framework faithfully or will find reasons — as some courts have — to interpret the framework in ways that allow the ban to survive.

## Implications

The Department of Justice's brief in *Cheeseman* and *Association of New Jersey Rifle and Pistol Clubs* represents a major institutional statement in favor of Second Amendment rights. The federal government's position — that the Second Amendment protects AR-15 rifles and standard-capacity magazines, and that New Jersey's categorical ban is unconstitutional — places the full weight of the executive branch behind the most important firearms rights litigation of the current era.

The outcome of the October 15, 2025 argument will define the constitutional status of AR-15 bans in the Third Circuit and will materially advance the progression of these issues toward the Supreme Court. Regardless of how the Third Circuit rules, the combination of a developed trial record in *Barnett*, en banc proceedings in the Third Circuit, the federal government's pro-Second Amendment briefs, and the overall structure of circuit litigation on these issues strongly suggests that the Supreme Court will be called upon, in the near term, to provide the definitive constitutional ruling on AR-15 and semi-automatic rifle bans that the nation's gun owners require.
