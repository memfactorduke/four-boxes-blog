## Summary

President Trump has fired Attorney General Pam Bondi, raising immediate questions about the future of Second Amendment litigation strategy at the Department of Justice. While Bondi's tenure produced historically unprecedented pro-Second Amendment actions -- including the creation of a dedicated Second Amendment working group and the first-ever DOJ amicus briefs supporting gun rights against state laws -- her failure to rein in the Solicitor General's office on critical case-selection decisions exposed a significant strategic vulnerability. The next attorney general will need to maintain Bondi's affirmative posture while asserting greater control over which Second Amendment cases reach the Supreme Court.

## Background

Attorney General Pam Bondi's departure from the Department of Justice comes at a pivotal moment in Second Amendment litigation. Over the past several decades, the DOJ had never taken an affirmative pro-Second Amendment posture in cases challenging state firearms regulations. That changed under Bondi's leadership, and I have assessed her tenure to understand what her successor must do differently to sustain and advance the trajectory of gun rights law in the federal courts.

## Bondi's Second Amendment Record: Historically Strong

By any fair measure, Bondi's DOJ compiled the strongest pro-Second Amendment record of any attorney general in modern American history. Several actions stand out as genuinely unprecedented.

**The Second Amendment Working Group.** Pursuant to a presidential executive order, Bondi's DOJ established a dedicated Second Amendment working group within the Department -- the first of its kind -- tasked with identifying opportunities to expand and protect firearms rights through federal litigation and policy. This was a historic development, one that I view as essential to meaningful progress on Second Amendment issues.

**Amicus Briefs Supporting Gun Rights Against State Laws.** For the first time in history, the DOJ filed amicus briefs in cases challenging state gun control statutes, arguing *in favor of* Second Amendment rights. This represented a fundamental departure from the traditional DOJ posture of defending governmental power. Notable filings included briefs supporting challenges to so-called "assault weapon" bans, standard-capacity magazine prohibitions, and suppressor regulations. These filings reflected the kind of affirmative, principled stance that I have long advocated.

**Affirmative Litigation.** The DOJ under Bondi brought lawsuits challenging undue delays and excessive costs in firearms licensing regimes in multiple jurisdictions, including California. In the District of Columbia, U.S. Attorney Jeanine Pirro filed suit challenging the District's bans on semi-automatic rifles, including AR-15s and AK-47s. I have tracked these cases closely on The Four Boxes Diner.

**Active Courtroom Advocacy.** Harmeet Dhillon, head of the Civil Rights Division, personally argued before the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit in a case involving so-called "assault weapons." In *Wolford v. Hawaii* -- a challenge to Hawaii's firearms restrictions sometimes referred to as the "vampire rule" case -- Sarah Harris of the Solicitor General's office argued alongside private attorney Alan Beck in support of Second Amendment rights. The DOJ also played a supporting role in *Benson v. District of Columbia*, a 2-1 decision striking down D.C.'s magazine capacity ban that created a split of authority.

In the Third Circuit, the DOJ filed amicus briefs in both *Cheeseman* and the *Association of New Jersey Rifle and Pistol Clubs* cases, challenging New Jersey's bans on standard-capacity magazines and semi-automatic firearms.

## The Solicitor General Problem

Despite these achievements, Bondi's tenure exposed a critical weakness: the Solicitor General's office operated with excessive independence on Second Amendment case selection before the Supreme Court, and Bondi failed to intervene. As I have analyzed on my platform, this represents a significant strategic failure.

Solicitor General John Sauer holds the fourth-ranking position in the DOJ hierarchy, below the Attorney General, Deputy Attorney General Todd Blanche, and the Associate Attorney General. The Solicitor General's office is primarily responsible for deciding which cases the federal government brings to the Supreme Court via petitions for certiorari, and what positions the government takes in its Supreme Court briefing.

**The *Hammani* Decision.** The Solicitor General's office chose to seek certiorari in *Hammani v. United States*, a case arising under 18 U.S.C. Section 922(g) -- the federal statute defining categories of persons prohibited from possessing firearms. The defendant in *Hammani* had admitted to marijuana use and cocaine use while possessing firearms, and allegedly had connections to the Iranian Revolutionary Guard and drug trafficking. As a vehicle for establishing favorable Second Amendment precedent on prohibited-persons challenges, the case presented a deeply unsympathetic fact pattern -- the kind that risks producing a narrow or adverse ruling from a Court known to be skeptical of drug-related defendants. As I have argued, the strategic error mirrors the Biden administration's decision to push *United States v. Rahimi*, which involved a domestic violence defendant with egregious facts, as its preferred 922(g) vehicle.

**Cases the SG's Office Declined to Bring.** While pushing *Hammani*, the Solicitor General's office chose *not* to seek certiorari or change the government's position in two far more favorable cases:

- *Reese*, decided by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit, held that 18-, 19-, and 20-year-olds possess full Second Amendment rights. The opinion was thorough, well-reasoned, and built on a sympathetic fact pattern -- an ideal vehicle for establishing nationwide precedent on young adult gun rights.

- *Range v. Attorney General*, from the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit, presented perhaps the most sympathetic 922(g) fact pattern imaginable. Brian Range had been convicted decades earlier of a technical misdemeanor related to food stamp reporting that carried a potential five-year sentence under Pennsylvania law, triggering a lifetime firearms prohibition. He never served a day in prison. The en banc Third Circuit ruled in his favor, with opinions from respected jurists including Judges Bibas, Porter, and Hardiman -- judges well known to the Supreme Court.

The failure to bring *Range* or *Reese* while pushing *Hammani* reflects a Solicitor General's office that prioritized the institutional interests of federal prosecutors -- who prefer to win cases for the government -- over the administration's stated pro-Second Amendment policy objectives. I view this as a critical strategic mistake that any successor attorney general must correct.

**Consent Decrees and Remedies.** The Solicitor General's office also reportedly resisted entering into favorable consent decrees in cases brought by organizations such as Gun Owners of America, the Second Amendment Foundation, the California Rifle and Pistol Association, and the Firearms Policy Coalition. The resistance apparently stemmed from concerns about the scope of relief, particularly regarding universal injunctions -- a legitimate institutional concern, but one that the Attorney General should have been positioned to override in furtherance of the administration's Second Amendment priorities.

## Implications for the Next Attorney General

The central lesson of Bondi's tenure is that a pro-Second Amendment DOJ requires more than good instincts and willing subordinates. It requires an Attorney General prepared to exercise direct oversight of the Solicitor General's office on matters of constitutional litigation strategy. This is a point I have stressed repeatedly on The Four Boxes Diner.

The next attorney general must be willing to pick up the phone and direct the Solicitor General on case selection -- ensuring that the limited number of Second Amendment cases the Supreme Court will accept each term present strong fact patterns and favorable procedural postures. Supreme Court precedent, unlike regulatory changes that can be reversed by a subsequent administration, has the potential to shape Second Amendment law for generations.

Some commentators have suggested Harmeet Dhillon as a potential successor, but there is a strong argument against elevating her: her focused work leading the Civil Rights Division's Second Amendment efforts is too valuable to dilute with the broad responsibilities of the Attorney General's office. Lee Zeldin, currently serving as EPA Administrator and known for his willingness to challenge entrenched bureaucratic resistance, has also been mentioned as a candidate with a favorable Second Amendment record.

Whatever the choice, my view is that the Second Amendment community's priorities for the next attorney general should be clear: maintain the unprecedented affirmative posture that Bondi established, but pair it with the institutional authority and willingness to ensure the Solicitor General's office serves the administration's constitutional vision rather than the career instincts of federal prosecutors.
