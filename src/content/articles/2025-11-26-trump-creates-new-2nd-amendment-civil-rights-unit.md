## Summary

The Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi and President Donald J. Trump, has announced the creation of the Second Amendment Rights Section — a dedicated office within the Civil Rights Division focused exclusively on investigating and challenging state and local laws that unconstitutionally restrict the right to keep and bear arms. This development represents a historic institutional commitment to treating Second Amendment rights as genuine civil rights, on par with other fundamental constitutional guarantees, and signals an aggressive federal posture toward anti-gun outlier jurisdictions including California, New York, New Jersey, Hawaii, and Illinois.

## Background

For decades, the Civil Rights Division of the United States Department of Justice was deployed as an instrument of progressive legal advocacy — initiating litigation against conservative states over voter identification laws, redistricting maps, and other matters that the progressive left found politically objectionable. The Second Amendment, by contrast, was treated as a second-class right, unworthy of the institutional resources and litigation muscle that the federal government regularly brought to bear in support of other constitutional guarantees.

That era appears to be ending.

According to plans shared with Congress and reviewed by Reuters, the Department of Justice has announced the creation of the Second Amendment Rights Section within the Civil Rights Division, with an expected opening date of December 4, 2025. The office will be dedicated to investigating local laws and policies that limit gun rights and is expected to operate under the oversight of Harmeet Dhillon, the head of the Civil Rights Division.

The announcement implements a February 2025 executive order issued by President Trump directing Attorney General Bondi to assess ongoing infringements of gun rights across the entire federal government and to take corrective action.

## The Significance of Treating Second Amendment Rights as Civil Rights

The creation of a dedicated Second Amendment rights enforcement unit within the Civil Rights Division is significant for reasons that extend beyond the immediate litigation it will generate.

Since the Supreme Court's landmark decisions in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the constitutional status of the right to keep and bear arms has been formally elevated to that of a fundamental individual right. Yet the cultural and institutional treatment of the Second Amendment has lagged behind its legal status. Courts in anti-gun circuits have continued to apply watered-down scrutiny, legislatures in blue states have enacted wave after wave of post-**Bruen** circumvention laws, and the federal executive branch — until now — declined to bring the institutional weight of the Justice Department to bear on behalf of gun owners.

The Second Amendment Rights Section changes that calculus. By formally housing gun rights enforcement within the Civil Rights Division, the administration is making an institutional statement: the right to keep and bear arms is a civil right. Violations of that right by state and local governments will be investigated and challenged with the same resources and seriousness of purpose that the department has historically brought to racial discrimination, voting rights violations, and other civil rights infringements.

## The Overton Window and the Culture of Rights

One of the most significant long-term effects of this development may be cultural rather than immediately legal. For many decades, the Second Amendment was treated in the mainstream legal culture as a marginal, contested right — something that gun enthusiasts argued about but that serious lawyers and academics did not fully accept as a first-class constitutional guarantee. That perception has been shifting since **Heller** and accelerated substantially after **Bruen**.

The creation of a dedicated Second Amendment enforcement unit within the DOJ's Civil Rights Division continues that cultural shift. When the federal government treats a right as worthy of active federal protection, the broader legal culture tends to follow. State attorneys general, lower court judges, and legal academics are all influenced by the institutional signals that the Department of Justice sends.

When I was growing up, the State of Vermont was the only state in the Union with permitless carry. Today, twenty-nine states have enacted constitutional carry laws. That transformation did not happen overnight, and it did not happen by accident — it happened because the legal and political culture shifted to recognize that the right to bear arms is a genuine constitutional entitlement, not a legislative grace.

## Expected Targets and Immediate Legal Actions

The Second Amendment Rights Section is expected to focus initially on the most egregious anti-gun outlier jurisdictions: California, New York, New Jersey, Hawaii, and Illinois. These states have enacted comprehensive regulatory schemes that, in my assessment, are plainly incompatible with **Bruen**'s text-and-history framework.

The new section's first significant action is already visible. The Civil Rights Division has filed a lawsuit against the Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department for the systematic slow-walking of concealed carry permit applications — a practice that effectively denies law-abiding citizens the right to public carry that **Bruen** held is among the Second Amendment's most basic guarantees.

This litigation mirrors the approach the DOJ has taken in other civil rights contexts: identifying jurisdictions that are structurally and systematically violating a constitutional right and using the weight of federal enforcement to compel compliance.

## The Opportunity Cost Argument

There is a further dimension to this development that deserves attention. The resources of the Civil Rights Division are finite. Every attorney-hour spent investigating and litigating on behalf of Second Amendment rights is an hour not available for the left-wing policy litigation that has historically been the division's primary focus. This opportunity cost has real and immediate value.

By deploying the division's existing infrastructure in service of gun rights rather than progressive social policy, the administration is simultaneously advancing Second Amendment enforcement and reducing the institutional capacity available for the policy litigation that anti-gun progressives depend upon. The taxpayers who funded the division's infrastructure are now seeing those resources directed toward a genuinely constitutional purpose.

## Implications

The creation of the Second Amendment Rights Section is a development of major and lasting significance for the right to keep and bear arms. The institutional signal it sends — that the federal government will use the muscle of the Civil Rights Division to protect gun owners' constitutional rights — has the potential to reshape the litigation landscape, accelerate cultural acceptance of the Second Amendment as a full civil right, and impose meaningful compliance costs on anti-gun jurisdictions that have thus far treated **Bruen** as an inconvenience to be minimized rather than a binding constitutional command to be respected.

Whether this section's litigation proves decisive in any individual case will depend on the specific facts and the courts in which those cases are filed. But the broader institutional commitment it represents is, without qualification, the most significant administrative action in support of the Second Amendment since the Supreme Court decided **Bruen** in 2022.
