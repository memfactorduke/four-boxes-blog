Yesterday's oral argument in *Wolford v. Lopez*, No. 24-1046, gave Second Amendment supporters a great deal to think about. The case tests Hawaii's "vampire rule" — the requirement that gun owners obtain express authorization from a property owner before carrying on private property open to the public. I spent the argument taking notes on the specific quotes from the bench, and today I want to walk through the ones that matter most and explain what they signal about where this decision is headed.

## Kavanaugh: "No Broad Tradition" — and What That Means Legally

The most important sentence from yesterday's argument came from Justice Kavanaugh, who told Hawaii's counsel Neal Katyal that he just did not "see the kind of broad tradition here" that would be required for the law to survive under *Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). Let me explain why that phrasing is so significant.

*Bruen* requires the government to demonstrate that a challenged firearms regulation is "consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation." That is an affirmative obligation — the government must produce historical evidence. What Kavanaugh said, in plain terms, is that Hawaii failed to meet it. "No broad tradition" is not a question inviting a better answer. It is the *Bruen* standard applied and found lacking.

Kavanaugh is a vote-counter. He joined *Bruen* and has consistently applied it to reject novel gun restrictions. When he announces, at oral argument, that a challenged law has no sufficient historical support, he is telling you what he is writing in the opinion — or what he is signing onto.

## Gorsuch and the 1865 Louisiana Problem

Justice Gorsuch produced the most memorable moment of the argument when he pointed out that Hawaii's primary historical analogue — an 1865 Louisiana statute requiring property owners' consent before armed entry — was a law that many people treat like "garlic in front of a vampire." His point was sharper than it first appears.

The 1865 Louisiana statute was enacted during Reconstruction as part of the wave of Black Codes designed to disarm freedmen and leave them defenseless against the Ku Klux Klan. Civil rights advocates, scholars, and historians routinely treat these laws as monuments to racial subjugation — not as legitimate regulatory precedents. Yet Hawaii walked into the Supreme Court and asked the Justices to uphold a modern gun restriction by analogy to that law.

Gorsuch's comment was not merely witty. It exposed a fundamental problem in Hawaii's legal strategy: the government's strongest historical analogue is a statute designed to deny Black Americans their constitutional rights. Under *Bruen*, historical analogues must reflect a genuine and legitimate tradition of regulation, not a tradition of constitutional violation. Hawaii can't have it both ways.

## An En Banc Dissent from the Majority's Reasoning

Chief Justice Roberts added that the Second Amendment has operated as a "disfavored right" in the lower federal courts — a diagnosis that has been building since *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008). Justice Alito sharpened it: the 1865 Louisiana law was crafted specifically to prevent freed enslaved people from defending themselves against Klan violence and racist law enforcement. Describing Hawaii's reliance on that statute as "relegating the Second Amendment to second-class status," Alito connected the historical injustice to the contemporary constitutional problem with surgical precision.

Justice Barrett's exchange with Katyal on private homes — whether Hawaii could impose the same express-authorization requirement on carry in private residences — was revealing. Katyal said no. Barrett's one-word reply, "Really?" was not an endorsement. It was a signal that she found the constitutional distinction between commercial private property and residential private property thin.

## What Neal Katyal Was Working With

I want to be clear: Neal Katyal is an exceptional appellate advocate. He argued this case as well as it could be argued. The problem is that he was arguing a position the constitutional text and history do not support. When the strongest historical analogue available to defend your law is a Reconstruction-era statute designed to disarm Black Americans, and when six justices in a row ask you variations of "where exactly is the historical tradition for this?" — the case is not won on skill at the lectern. It is lost in the historical record.

The Ninth Circuit upheld Hawaii's law in *Wolford v. Lopez*, 116 F.4th 959 (9th Cir. 2024). Based on what I heard from the bench yesterday, that ruling is not going to survive.

## Reading the Roadmap

Six conservative justices expressed, in varying formulations, the same underlying concern: Hawaii's express-authorization requirement inverts the normal default for constitutional rights, has no meaningful historical precedent, and treats the Second Amendment worse than every other enumerated right. The decision, when it comes, will reverse the Ninth Circuit. The deeper question is how broadly the Court writes its holding — whether it simply disposes of Hawaii's specific statute or uses *Wolford* as the vehicle to clarify which "sensitive place" restrictions can and cannot pass muster under *Bruen*. Either way, today's argument told you which way this case is going.
