## Summary

The en banc panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit — comprising all active judges on the court — heard oral argument in the consolidated cases of **Coons v. Attorney General of New Jersey** and **Siegel v. Attorney General of New Jersey**, examining whether New Jersey's sweeping network of government-mandated gun-free zones can survive constitutional scrutiny under the Second Amendment. A central and revealing question that emerged during argument concerns which historical time period should govern the Bruen analysis: the Founding era of 1791, when the Second Amendment was ratified, or the post-Civil War Reconstruction era of the 1860s and 1870s. In my analysis, the answer is unambiguously 1791, a conclusion compelled by the text, structure, and jurisprudence of the Supreme Court itself.

## Background

Following the Supreme Court's landmark 2022 decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), New Jersey responded by enacting one of the most expansive sensitive-places regimes in the nation. The legislature designated approximately two dozen broad location categories as government-mandated gun-free zones — a formulation I prefer to the politically laundered phrase "sensitive places," which is simply a euphemism for government prohibition on the exercise of a constitutional right. These prohibited zones cover beaches, colleges, schools, childcare facilities, hospitals, nursery schools, youth sports events, museums, libraries, zoos, theaters, casinos, stadiums, healthcare facilities, and public transit, among many others. The practical effect is to leave law-abiding citizens disarmed across the vast majority of public space in New Jersey.

A federal district court, applying Bruen's text-and-history methodology, enjoined many of these restrictions, finding that New Jersey could not demonstrate a historical tradition of analogous firearm prohibitions rooted in the founding era. That ruling was appealed to the Third Circuit, where a three-judge panel, in a 2-1 decision issued in September 2025, reversed and upheld most of New Jersey's scheme. A powerful dissent by Judge David Porter questioned whether the majority had correctly applied Bruen. The full Third Circuit then voted to rehear the case en banc — a significant procedural development made more meaningful by the addition of two new Trump-appointed judges to the court, Judge Jennifer Mascott and Judge Emil Bove, giving the en banc panel a more balanced composition than the original three-judge panel.

## The Core Temporal Question: 1791 or 1868?

The most consequential methodological dispute in Second Amendment litigation today concerns which historical moment courts must examine when conducting the Bruen analysis. Gun-control advocates, including New Jersey's lawyers, argue that courts should look to the Reconstruction era — the period surrounding ratification of the Fourteenth Amendment in 1868 — because the Amendment incorporated the Second Amendment against the states, and because post-Civil War history contains far more gun-restriction laws than the Founding era. If courts credit Reconstruction-era restrictions as sufficient analogs, the practical effect is to validate a much broader set of modern prohibitions.

During oral argument, Chief Judge Michael Chagares posed the question directly to New Jersey's lawyers. Quoting from the court's exchange, the Chief Judge asked whether the state's contention — that reconstruction-era historical analogs should control when they conflict with founding-era evidence — was actually consistent with Bruen itself. He observed that Bruen explicitly directs courts to treat evidence from the late nineteenth century as having "minimal probative value" when it conflicts with earlier evidence. That is a direct quotation from the Supreme Court's own opinion, and it is devastating to New Jersey's position.

In my scholarly analysis published in the Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy in the wake of Bruen, I predicted precisely this litigation strategy and explained why it fails. I wrote that article immediately after Bruen was decided, foreseeing that anti-Second Amendment advocates would rush toward Reconstruction-era history precisely because the post-Civil War period generated a far denser corpus of gun restriction laws — many of them, I should note, motivated by racist animus. The Black Codes and their neutral-sounding legislative successors were specifically designed to disarm freed African-Americans while nominally applying to all citizens, with the tacit understanding that enforcement would be racially selective. It would be a profound historical and constitutional irony to permit that body of law to define the scope of a fundamental right that was codified in 1791.

## Why 1791 Is the Correct Answer

The Second Amendment codifies a pre-existing natural right. That formulation is significant. When the Founders wrote that "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed," they were not creating a new entitlement. They were recognizing and protecting a right that already existed — one rooted in English common law, the experience of colonial self-governance, and the fundamental principles of natural-rights philosophy that animated the entire constitutional enterprise.

If the right pre-exists 1791, then 1791 is necessarily the controlling interpretive moment. To look past the ratification date — to defer to laws enacted seventy or eighty years later, in a period of significant political dysfunction, racial oppression, and constitutional instability — is not originalism. It is a manipulation of the historical inquiry that produces a convenient but jurisprudentially unsound result. Bruen itself warns against exactly this move.

Furthermore, treating founding-era history as merely one data point to be "weighed" against later-period evidence transforms the Second Amendment into a less-than-fully-binding constitutional guarantee. No other fundamental right is construed this way. The First Amendment's protections are not diluted by post-Civil War restrictions on speech or press. The Fourth Amendment's protections against unreasonable searches are not reinterpreted in light of Reconstruction-era police practices. The Second Amendment deserves no less principled treatment.

## New Jersey's Sensitive Places Scheme Under Scrutiny

Even accepting for the sake of argument that some government-designated sensitive places can survive constitutional scrutiny, New Jersey's approach is constitutionally indefensible as a matter of scope. The state has not identified a narrow set of genuinely analogous historical restrictions. It has instead designated virtually every public location in the state as a gun-free zone, leaving the Second Amendment's guarantee of a right to bear arms in public — recognized in Bruen — as a hollow formality.

The state also retained its so-called "vampire rule," which prohibits a licensed carrier from bringing a firearm onto any private property open to the public — gas stations, grocery stores, laundromats — without specific, affirmative consent from the property owner. That provision, now pending before the Supreme Court in a related case, effectively nullifies the public-carry right by converting a constitutional entitlement into a series of individual opt-in negotiations with every private business operator a citizen might patronize.

## Implications

The en banc Third Circuit's decision in **Coons** and **Siegel** will be among the most significant Second Amendment rulings since Bruen. It will define the operative scope of the public-carry right across Pennsylvania, Delaware, and New Jersey. More broadly, it will signal whether the Third Circuit — as a newly reconstituted court — will faithfully apply Bruen's originalist framework or continue the pattern of result-oriented analysis that characterized much lower-court jurisprudence in the post-Bruen period.

The Chief Judge's pointed questions to New Jersey's counsel, pressing the state to reconcile its Reconstruction-era argument with Bruen's explicit textual instruction to treat late-nineteenth-century evidence as having minimal probative value when it conflicts with founding-era history, suggest that at least some members of the court are prepared to apply the methodology the Supreme Court actually prescribed. The outcome of this case, whatever it proves to be, will almost certainly be reviewed by the Supreme Court. The stakes for the Second Amendment right to bear arms in public could not be higher.
