## Summary

Two separate cases challenging state bans on so-called "assault weapons" — semi-automatic rifles including the AR-15 — are now pending before the United States Supreme Court, each arising from lower court decisions that got the Second Amendment analysis fundamentally wrong. One case arises from Cook County, Illinois, through the Seventh Circuit; the other involves Connecticut's assault weapon ban and comes through the Second Circuit. Both circuits upheld the bans. Both were wrong. I also address, at length, the critical but frequently misunderstood legal distinction between arms that are "in common use" and therefore protected by the Second Amendment, and arms that are "dangerous and unusual" and therefore subject to prohibition — with important implications for the National Firearms Act.

## Background

The Supreme Court's decision in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008) established the fundamental principle that the Second Amendment protects the right of law-abiding, responsible citizens to keep and bear arms, with the core purpose of self-defense. *Heller* also introduced key doctrinal concepts that continue to shape litigation over particular categories of firearms — most importantly, the concept of arms that are "in common use" for lawful purposes, and the corresponding category of arms that are "dangerous and unusual" and therefore unprotected.

The Court in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022) reaffirmed and elaborated on this framework, requiring courts to evaluate firearms regulations against the historical tradition of firearm regulation at the time of the Founding. Both the Seventh Circuit (in the Cook County AR-15 ban litigation) and the Second Circuit (in the Connecticut AR-15 ban litigation) have managed to apply this framework in a manner that is, in my view, clearly incorrect — and now the Supreme Court has an opportunity to set the record straight.

## The Two Cases Before the Court

**The Cook County / Seventh Circuit Case**

The case arising from Cook County, Illinois — coming through the United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit — involves a ban on AR-15s and other semi-automatic rifles. The Seventh Circuit upheld the ban under what I have called the "Bevis standard" — an analytical framework developed within the circuit that represents, in my assessment, a fundamental misapplication of the Supreme Court's Second Amendment jurisprudence. The Seventh Circuit's conclusion that banning the most popular rifle in America is constitutionally permissible under *Bruen* is not a good-faith application of that precedent.

It is worth noting that at the time I delivered this analysis, there was also a major oral argument pending before the Third Circuit sitting en banc on whether New Jersey could ban AR-15s and magazines holding more than ten rounds — further evidence of how pervasive this issue has become across multiple circuits.

**The Connecticut Cases**

Two related cases from Connecticut — **Grant v. Lamont** and **National Association for Gun Rights v. Lamont** — challenge Connecticut's assault weapon ban through the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit. The Second Circuit, like the Seventh Circuit, upheld the Connecticut ban, reaching a conclusion that is inconsistent with the plain logic of *Heller* and *Bruen*.

Both the Second Circuit and the Seventh Circuit got this wrong. The Supreme Court must now decide whether to take these cases and provide the definitive answer on AR-15 bans that lower courts have been unable or unwilling to deliver correctly.

## The Legal Test: What Arms Does the Second Amendment Protect?

The central question in all AR-15 ban litigation is the legal standard for determining which arms receive Second Amendment protection. Let me walk through this analysis carefully, because it is frequently distorted by opponents of the Second Amendment.

### Step One: What Is an "Arm"?

**Heller** defined "arms" as anything that can be used offensively or defensively. This is a broad definitional category that would include, in principle, machine guns, semi-automatic rifles, grenades, knives, axes, and any other implement capable of offensive or defensive use. The Second Amendment's text, however, is narrowed to "bearable" arms — those that can be carried — which eliminates from constitutional consideration items like battleships, fighter aircraft, and intercontinental ballistic missiles.

### Step Two: Not All Arms Are Protected

Not all bearable arms receive Second Amendment protection. **Heller** made clear that the Second Amendment protects only those arms "in common use at the time" for lawful purposes. The reciprocal principle — expressly recognized in *Heller* — is that arms that are "dangerous and unusual" are NOT protected by the Second Amendment and may therefore be regulated or banned by the government.

This distinction is critical and is frequently misused by gun control advocates. The "dangerous and unusual" category exists to permit the government to ban weapons that are not typically possessed by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes — think sawed-off shotguns, which the Court in **United States v. Miller** (1939) held were not ordinary military equipment and had no reasonable relationship to a well-regulated militia.

### Step Three: The "In Common Use" Test and Why AR-15s Are Clearly Protected

Here is where the analysis becomes dispositive for AR-15 cases: an arm cannot be both "in common use" for lawful purposes and "dangerous and unusual" at the same time. The two categories are mutually exclusive. The Supreme Court recognized this logical relationship in **Heller**.

The AR-15 is, by any objective measure, the most popular rifle in America. Estimates consistently place the number of AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles in civilian hands at somewhere between 20 million and 30 million. No firearm that is possessed by tens of millions of law-abiding Americans can plausibly be characterized as "unusual." It is, by definition, in common use.

The argument that such a weapon is nonetheless so uniquely "dangerous" as to fall outside Second Amendment protection is circular: all firearms are dangerous in some sense, which is precisely why the Second Amendment protects them for self-defense purposes. The relevant inquiry is whether a weapon is *unusually* dangerous in a manner that distinguishes it from other commonly owned arms. A semi-automatic rifle that fires one round per trigger pull — exactly like a common semi-automatic handgun — is not categorically more dangerous in its operating mechanism than the handguns that *Heller* unambiguously protects.

The lower courts that have upheld AR-15 bans have, in various formulations, tried to argue that these rifles' military lineage or their capacity for causing harm in mass shooting scenarios renders them "dangerous and unusual." This argument fails on both legal and logical grounds. *Heller* explicitly rejected the argument that the Second Amendment protects only weapons with no military utility; to the contrary, the Court recognized that the Second Amendment's historical purpose encompassed weapons "in common use at the time" — and at the Founding, the arms most protected were precisely those with military applications, because they were the arms citizens would need for militia service and collective defense.

## The National Firearms Act: A Nuanced Picture

This analysis also has important implications for the National Firearms Act (NFA), which regulates categories of firearms including machine guns, short-barreled rifles, short-barreled shotguns, silencers, and destructive devices.

Many people in the Second Amendment community assert, categorically, that the NFA is unconstitutional. In a theoretical world that bore no relationship to current Supreme Court case law, perhaps that assertion might have some merit. But in the real world in which we live, the answer is more nuanced, and intellectual honesty requires acknowledging that nuance.

The Supreme Court has made clear, repeatedly, that machine guns are not protected arms under the Second Amendment. The Court's reasoning, consistent with the "dangerous and unusual" framework, is that machine guns — automatic weapons capable of firing multiple rounds per single trigger pull — are not "in common use" for lawful self-defense purposes among law-abiding citizens, and their primary utility for lawful civilian purposes is substantially different from that of semi-automatic firearms. Whether one agrees with this analysis or not, it is the law as the Supreme Court has articulated it.

The proper conclusion is therefore not that the NFA is unconstitutional in its entirety, but that different provisions of the NFA must be evaluated separately:

- **Provisions regulating machine guns**: Likely constitutional under current Supreme Court doctrine, given the Court's repeated signals that machine guns fall outside Second Amendment protection.
- **Provisions regulating suppressors/silencers**: This is a closer question. Suppressors are items that reduce the sound of a firearm discharge; they do not alter the semi-automatic or automatic firing mechanism. Their regulation under the NFA is less clearly justified under the "dangerous and unusual" framework, and I believe this is an area where future litigation may produce favorable results for Second Amendment rights.
- **Short-barreled rifles and shotguns**: Also subject to ongoing debate. The historical tradition of regulating these items under the NFA is thinner than the machine gun regulation history.

The key principle is this: if an arm is not protected by the Second Amendment, the government has essentially unlimited regulatory authority over it. If an arm is protected — because it is "in common use" for lawful purposes and is not "dangerous and unusual" — then the government must justify any regulation of it by demonstrating consistency with the historical tradition of firearm regulation at the Founding.

## Why the Supreme Court Must Resolve This Now

The Third Circuit en banc argument on New Jersey's AR-15 ban, the Seventh Circuit's Cook County ruling, and the Second Circuit's Connecticut ruling collectively create precisely the kind of entrenched circuit-level error that demands Supreme Court intervention. The question of whether states may ban the most popular rifle in America is not one that can be left to circuit courts to answer differently based on geography.

I have laid out the proper legal analysis in my article published in the Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy, and I am highly confident that when the Supreme Court eventually resolves these questions, the analysis I have set forth — grounded in *Heller*'s text, history, and the "in common use" versus "dangerous and unusual" framework — will be vindicated. The history of this litigation will be written in the courts, and I believe the Second Amendment will prevail.

## Implications

The AR-15 ban cases now pending before or approaching the Supreme Court are among the most consequential Second Amendment disputes since *Heller* itself. A clear Supreme Court ruling that semi-automatic rifles in common use cannot be banned under the Second Amendment would resolve a question that has generated inconsistent and often defiant lower court rulings for years.

The answer, properly derived from *Heller* and *Bruen*, is clear: you cannot ban the most popular rifle in America. Period. The Supreme Court should say so, and the lower courts that have spent years pretending otherwise should be corrected once and for all.
