## Summary

There is a lot of good news coming out of the Trump administration on Second Amendment matters. But good intentions at the top do not excuse unforced errors at the brief-writing level, and the Department of Justice has just committed a serious one. In the case of *Brown v. ATF*, pending in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Missouri, the DOJ has filed a brief defending the National Firearms Act's application to suppressors and short-barrel rifles that is, in my professional judgment, clearly wrong on Supreme Court precedent, wrong on Bruen methodology, and wrong on the basic constitutional text.

This is not a partisan criticism. I am a strong supporter of this administration and of Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon. But when the DOJ files a brief that misapplies *Heller* and *Bruen* in ways that would embarrass a first-year constitutional law student, someone has to say so. Consider this a teaching moment — and a call for better internal quality control over the thousands of lawyers filing briefs under the Department's name.

## Background: What Brown v. ATF Is About

The case was brought by the Second Amendment Foundation, the Firearms Policy Coalition, the National Rifle Association, and the American Suppressor Association. The plaintiffs argue that suppressors and short-barrel rifles — items regulated under the NFA — are constitutionally protected arms under the Second Amendment, and that the NFA's regulatory framework as applied to these items is unconstitutional. One significant hook for the litigation is the elimination of the $200 NFA tax, which removes the taxing power justification that historically provided the constitutional basis for the NFA's registration and transfer scheme.

The conceptual argument is straightforward: suppressors and short-barrel rifles should be treated like any other ordinary firearm — a handgun, a hunting rifle, an AR-15 — rather than heavily regulated items requiring registration, a tax stamp, and months-long government approval. The plaintiffs argue these are protected arms, and I believe they are correct.

## Why the DOJ Is Defending the NFA

Before cataloguing the errors in the brief, fairness requires acknowledging the structural reason the DOJ is on the wrong side of this case. The federal government, when sued, must be represented by lawyers. Those lawyers almost universally come from the Department of Justice, and under the Take Care Clause of Article II, the executive branch has a historical obligation to defend federal law when challenged. This is not inherently unethical. Every litigant — including the United States — is entitled to a defense.

But that obligation has limits. DOJ lawyers swear an oath to the Constitution, not merely to the client. That oath does not require them to make arguments that are intellectually dishonest, methodologically backwards, or directly contradicted by controlling Supreme Court precedent. The problem here is not that the DOJ is defending the NFA — it is *how* it is doing so.

## Error One: Elevating Common Use to Plain-Text Analysis

The most significant error in the brief is the DOJ's treatment of the common-use test. The brief argues that at the first step of the *Bruen* analysis — the plain-text stage — the plaintiffs bear the burden of demonstrating that suppressors and short-barrel rifles are in common use by Americans for lawful purposes.

This is flatly wrong, and the error is not minor or technical. It inverts the entire analytical framework the Supreme Court established.

Under *Heller* and *Bruen* (which apply the same methodology, with *Bruen* simply providing more granular guidance because lower courts kept misapplying *Heller*), the plain-text analysis is definitional. You identify what the relevant terms of the Second Amendment mean, and you ask whether the challenged regulation burdens conduct that falls within those terms. The Supreme Court in *Heller* defined every critical word in the operative clause: "the people" means all Americans; "arms" means anything that can be used offensively or defensively, including body armor; "keep" means to possess; "bear" means to carry.

Critically, *Bruen* extended this definition explicitly. The Supreme Court stated that any instrument that facilitates armed self-defense qualifies as an "arm" for Second Amendment purposes. A suppressor is plainly an instrument. It is a physical object that you hold and attach to a firearm. And it facilitates armed self-defense — it protects the shooter's hearing during training (as recognized even in the Seventh Circuit's *Ezell* line of cases, which acknowledge a right to train), and it assists in a defensive situation. The same logic applies straightforwardly to short-barrel rifles, which are simply rifles.

At the plain-text stage, then, both suppressors and short-barrel rifles are arms. The regulation of them burdens the right to keep and bear arms. The plain-text analysis is complete. The burden then shifts to the government to justify the regulation through historical tradition.

The common-use test does not belong at step one. It derives from *Heller*'s discussion of the historical tradition of restricting "dangerous and unusual weapons." That is a step-two inquiry — part of the government's burden to demonstrate a historical tradition of analogous regulation. And note the conjunctive: *dangerous and unusual*, not dangerous or unusual. An arm in common use by Americans for lawful purposes is, by definition, not unusual. It therefore cannot satisfy the dangerous-and-unusual test. Which means the government cannot rely on that historical tradition to justify the restriction.

By attempting to relocate the common-use test to step one and assign the burden to the plaintiffs, the DOJ is trying to escape a burden it cannot meet. The government cannot show these items are not in common use — suppressors are owned by hundreds of thousands of law-abiding Americans, and short-barrel rifles are among the most popular firearms in the country. Rather than confront that reality at step two where it belongs, the brief tries to shift the analytical terrain entirely. That is not a legitimate legal argument. It is a methodological shell game.

The DOJ's authority for this maneuver? Dicta from an Eighth Circuit case, along with cases from the Fourth Circuit, the D.C. Circuit, and the Seventh Circuit. Here is a useful heuristic for any DOJ lawyer writing a Second Amendment brief: if your authority comes primarily from the Fourth, Seventh, D.C., or First Circuits, stop and reconsider. Those courts have been repeatedly and routinely reversed by the Supreme Court on Second Amendment questions. They are inferior courts — Article III uses that exact word — and where their precedents conflict with the Supreme Court's holdings, the Supreme Court controls. Full stop.

## Error Two: The Facial Challenge Argument

The brief's second major error involves the standard for facial constitutional challenges. The DOJ argues that because the NFA's suppressor regulations could theoretically be applied constitutionally — specifically, to criminals using suppressors in furtherance of unlawful activity — the statute cannot be struck down on its face.

This argument fails the moment you apply it to *Heller* itself.

The Supreme Court in *Heller* struck down the District of Columbia's handgun ban on a *facial* basis. Chief Justice Roberts' majority opinion voided the statute in its entirety. Now apply the DOJ's logic to *Heller*: couldn't DC's handgun ban be constitutionally applied to a criminal using a handgun to commit armed robbery? Of course it could. But the Supreme Court still struck it down facially. Why? Because the statute as written did not contain any element limiting its application to criminal misuse. It prohibited possession categorically, regardless of lawful purpose.

The NFA's suppressor and short-barrel rifle provisions work the same way. A federal prosecutor charging someone under the NFA does not have to prove that the suppressor was used for criminal purposes. Possession without proper registration and tax stamp is the offense, period. There is no criminal-misuse element in the statute. The DOJ cannot save a facially overbroad law by pointing to a constitutionally permissible subset of its applications when the statute itself does not limit its reach to that subset.

The argument proves too much. If accepted, it would mean that virtually no firearms law could ever be struck down facially, because virtually any firearms law could theoretically be applied to a criminal. That cannot be the rule, because the Supreme Court already told us otherwise in *Heller*.

## Error Three: Conflating Registration With Licensing

The brief also attempts to justify the NFA's registration requirements by analogizing them to historical shall-issue licensing regimes. This conflation is not new — it is a recurring error in government Second Amendment briefs — but it remains wrong.

The distinction between registration and licensing was addressed in detail by then-Judge Brett Kavanaugh in his dissent in *Heller II* at the D.C. Circuit in 2011. Kavanaugh explained that registration schemes — in which the government maintains a comprehensive list of who owns which firearms — represent a qualitatively different kind of regulation than licensing systems that simply confirm a person is not a prohibited possessor. Registration is, historically, a predicate step toward confiscation. It is not analogous to a background check or a shall-issue permit system. The two are categorically different animals.

The fact that a now-sitting Supreme Court Justice wrote at length about this distinction, and that his analysis is readily available, makes it especially puzzling that the DOJ continues to cite historical licensing analogues to justify modern registration requirements. These are not the same thing, and pretending otherwise does not make them so.

## The Broader Problem

This brief is not an isolated incident. The pattern — DOJ lawyers relying on anti-Second Amendment circuit court decisions, misapplying *Bruen* methodology, and making arguments inconsistent with the administration's stated positions elsewhere — suggests a systemic quality control failure. In the *Barnett* case before the Seventh Circuit, for instance, Harmeet Dhillon's DOJ is arguing against the application of *Caetano* and in favor of expansive Second Amendment rights. But in Missouri, a different set of DOJ lawyers is filing a brief that would undermine that same framework.

The inconsistency is not just embarrassing. It is tactically harmful. Courts notice when the government argues incompatible positions across circuits. And intellectually dishonest arguments — particularly ones that misstate what the Supreme Court actually held — can produce bad precedent that haunts future litigation for years.

The fix is not complicated. The Second Amendment methodology under *Heller* and *Bruen* is not obscure. It can be explained clearly in an hour. Someone at the DOJ needs to circulate that explanation to every attorney handling Second Amendment litigation, with explicit instructions on how to apply it correctly — and explicit prohibitions on the kinds of errors catalogued above.

This administration's instincts on the Second Amendment are sound. Its brief-writing, in too many cases, is not. Those two things need to be brought into alignment.
