## Summary

As my analysis of the recently filed supplemental briefs in **Duncan v. Bonta** and **Gator Custom Guns v. Walter Wence** demonstrates, the D.C. Court of Appeals' decision in **Benson v. United States** (March 5, 2026) has materially altered the certiorari calculus at the United States Supreme Court. The *Benson* ruling — holding that the District of Columbia's ban on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds is facially unconstitutional — has created a genuine split of authority between the D.C. Court of Appeals on one side and both the Ninth Circuit and the Washington State Supreme Court on the other. The supplemental briefs filed by counsel including Paul Clement, Aaron Murphy, and Chuck Michel present the Court with a compelling case that the time for plenary review has arrived, and that continued denial of certiorari will impose concrete, cognizable constitutional injuries on law-abiding citizens in California and Washington.

## Background

Prior to the *Benson* decision, the state of play in magazine capacity litigation was unfavorable in several important respects. The Ninth Circuit had upheld California's magazine ban in *Duncan v. Bonta*, and the Washington State Supreme Court had upheld Washington's magazine ban in *Gator Custom Guns v. Wence*. Both decisions were pending before the Supreme Court on petitions for certiorari. California's opposition to the *Duncan* petition rested substantially on the argument that there was no genuine conflict among courts of comparable authority on the magazine capacity question — an argument that, while contested, had some plausibility in the absence of a contrary ruling from a court of equivalent stature.

That argument is no longer available. The D.C. Court of Appeals — the highest court of the District of Columbia, functioning as the equivalent of a state supreme court — issued a thorough 50-page opinion on March 5, 2026, holding that the identical category of magazines is protected by the Second Amendment and cannot be banned. The decision was supported by a brief filed in September 2025 by United States Attorney Jeanine Pirro, acting pursuant to the Trump administration's Second Amendment enforcement policy under Attorney General Pam Bondi, which advanced the legal position that magazine capacity restrictions are constitutionally indefensible under *Bruen*. The D.C. Court of Appeals agreed. The split of authority that I had anticipated as an inevitable consequence of faithful application of the *Bruen* methodology has now materialized.

## The Benson Decision as the Predicate for Certiorari

The *Benson* decision is constitutionally significant at multiple levels. At the level of direct precedent, it protects individuals in the District of Columbia who possess magazines holding more than ten rounds from criminal prosecution under the local prohibition. But its significance for the pending Supreme Court petitions lies in what it establishes about the state of judicial disagreement on the constitutional question.

As I have examined the decision, the D.C. Court of Appeals did not merely reach a different bottom-line conclusion than the Ninth Circuit and the Washington State Supreme Court. It disagreed with those courts on every analytical step of the constitutional analysis. The court held that magazines meeting the prohibited capacity threshold are "arms" within the plain text of the Second Amendment — a conclusion the Washington State Supreme Court explicitly rejected. The court held that magazines in this category are in common use for lawful self-defense — a conclusion the Ninth Circuit declined to treat as dispositive. And the court held that the government could not identify a founding-era historical tradition of analogous prohibition — a conclusion that forecloses the interest-balancing approach several other courts have employed as an alternative to the *Bruen* methodology.

This multi-level disagreement is precisely what the Supreme Court's conflict-resolution jurisdiction is designed to address. The Court does not merely resolve bottom-line circuit splits; it resolves methodological disagreements that produce different outcomes in different jurisdictions on questions of constitutional law. The split created by *Benson* is of this more fundamental character.

## Duncan v. Bonta: The Case for Immediate Review

The supplemental brief filed in **Duncan v. Bonta** is styled, aptly, as presenting the argument that "the time is now." California's opposition to the petition had rested on the absence of a genuine conflict — a position untenable in light of *Benson* — and on the suggestion that the Court should await further percolation of the magazine capacity question in the lower courts before granting review.

As I have reviewed the supplemental brief, counsel for the petitioners have advanced a direct response to the percolation argument by framing it in terms of its human consequences. Thousands of Californians lawfully possess magazines holding more than ten rounds — items purchased in compliance with the law as it existed at the time of acquisition, held for the constitutionally protected purpose of self-defense, and now subject to criminal prohibition under a statute the *Bruen* framework cannot sustain. If the Court permits the Ninth Circuit's decision to stand while awaiting further lower-court developments, those citizens become criminals by operation of a law that, in my analysis, plainly violates the Second Amendment. The brief correctly characterizes this as a matter requiring "plenary review" not merely because of the legal significance of the question but because the human consequences of further delay are constitutionally intolerable.

The argument has force independent of its rhetorical appeal. The Supreme Court's certiorari jurisdiction is not purely ministerial — the Court has discretion to consider the practical consequences of deferring review of questions whose constitutional resolution is, in the Court's collective judgment, sufficiently clear. Where the legal question is ripe, the conflict is genuine, and the harm from delay is concrete and immediate, the case for granting certiorari is particularly strong.

## Gator Custom Guns: The Square Conflict

The supplemental brief in **Gator Custom Guns v. Walter Wence** presents what I regard as the most analytically powerful formulation of the split-of-authority argument. The Washington State Supreme Court's holding was not merely that magazine capacity restrictions are constitutionally permissible — it was that the Second Amendment is not implicated at all by such restrictions. The court held, in effect, that magazines do not fall within the plain text of the Second Amendment and are therefore outside its protections at the threshold.

The D.C. Court of Appeals held the opposite: that magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds are unambiguously "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment as interpreted by *Bruen*'s definition of an arm as any instrument facilitating armed self-defense. This is not a methodological disagreement about the weight of historical evidence or the appropriate level of historical analogy — it is a direct conflict about whether the constitutional provision at issue applies to the regulated items at all. When two courts of comparable stature disagree about whether a provision of the Bill of Rights even covers the subject matter of a challenged regulation, the Supreme Court cannot responsibly decline review. The petitioners' brief is correct that there is "no longer any reason to delay."

## Strategic Considerations: The AR-15 and Magazine Questions

As I have analyzed the litigation landscape, the magazine capacity and semi-automatic rifle questions present distinct but related constitutional issues that the Court will need to address, either together or sequentially. With respect to the AR-15 and comparable semi-automatic rifle platforms, I assess the constitutional case for Second Amendment protection as straightforward under current doctrine: these are among the most widely owned firearms in the United States, they are in unambiguous common use for lawful purposes including self-defense and hunting, and the government cannot identify a founding-era historical tradition of prohibiting arms with comparable characteristics. Justice Brett Kavanaugh's 2011 dissent in *Heller II* — which took the position that semi-automatic rifle bans are unconstitutional — reflects the correct constitutional analysis, and I assess the probability that the Court would uphold such a ban under the *Bruen* framework as negligible.

The magazine capacity question, while analytically stronger than it is sometimes credited, presents a more complex constitutional narrative. The core issue — whether a magazine constitutes an "arm" that facilitates armed self-defense — admits of a clean affirmative answer under *Bruen*'s text-based definition. The complicating factor is that the government's interest-balancing arguments, though foreclosed by *Bruen* as a doctrinal matter, have historically attracted more judicial sympathy in the magazine context than in the rifle context, and one cannot rule out the possibility that a Court majority would seek to distinguish between the two questions. My strategic preference would be for the Court to resolve the AR-15 question first, with the magazine cases held pending the outcome and then remanded for reconsideration in light of the rifle decision — a GVR disposition that would avoid the risk of a fragmented ruling that resolves the rifle question favorably while leaving the magazine question unsettled or adversely resolved. The optimal outcome, and the one I consider more probable than not, is that the Court grants certiorari in both a rifle case and a magazine case and resolves them together.

## Implications

The filing of supplemental briefs in *Duncan* and *Gator Custom Guns* within days of the *Benson* ruling reflects the responsiveness of the Second Amendment litigation bar to newly developed favorable authority. The practical effect of these filings is to ensure that the Court's law clerks, who will prepare the certiorari petitions for the justices' consideration, have before them a current account of the split of authority and its significance — framed not as an abstract doctrinal disagreement but as a question with immediate, concrete consequences for millions of law-abiding citizens.

In my analysis, the conditions for Supreme Court certiorari in at least one magazine capacity case are now fully satisfied. The split of authority is genuine and multi-dimensional. The petitions are well-developed. The supplemental briefs are legally sophisticated and factually compelling. The Department of Justice has staked out a position — that magazine capacity restrictions are constitutionally indefensible — that aligns with the correct constitutional analysis. And the human consequences of continued deferral are both real and cognizable. The *Benson* decision may reasonably be identified, in retrospect, as the development that made Supreme Court review of the magazine capacity question unavoidable.
