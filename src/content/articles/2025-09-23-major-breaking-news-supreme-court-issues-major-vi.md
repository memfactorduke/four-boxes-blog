## Summary

The Supreme Court of the United States has issued a significant order in the litigation concerning President Trump's termination of Rebecca Slaughter, a Democratic appointee to the Federal Trade Commission, dealing a decisive blow to those who argued that the President lacked constitutional authority to remove her from office. Chief Justice John Roberts entered an emergency order staying the lower court's reinstatement of Ms. Slaughter to her position pending further proceedings, and the full Court has taken additional steps signaling its imminent reconsideration of **Humphrey's Executor v. United States** — the 1935 precedent that has long been understood to limit the President's removal power over members of multi-member independent commissions. The potential overruling of **Humphrey's Executor** would represent one of the most consequential constitutional law decisions in a generation.

## Background

Article II of the United States Constitution vests the executive power of the federal government in a single individual: "The executive Power shall be vested in a President of the United States of America." This Vesting Clause is not merely ceremonial language — it is the constitutional foundation of the principle of unitary executive power, under which all executive functions of the federal government are ultimately accountable to and controllable by the President as the sole head of the executive branch.

The structural implications of this principle are straightforward. The President is accountable to the American people for the conduct of the executive branch. He alone bears ultimate political responsibility for the actions of every executive department, agency, and official operating within that branch. As a corollary of this accountability, the President must possess the authority to remove executive officers who fail to execute his lawful directives, who engage in misconduct, or whose continued service is inconsistent with his constitutional responsibilities.

This principle was called into question by the Supreme Court's 1935 decision in **Humphrey's Executor v. United States**, which held that Congress could limit the President's removal power over members of multi-member independent commissions — such as the Federal Trade Commission — to removal "for cause." **Humphrey's Executor** created what critics have long characterized as an unconstitutional "fourth branch" of government: administrative bodies that exercise executive functions but are insulated from presidential control and accountability.

## The Slaughter Termination and Lower Court Litigation

President Trump exercised his constitutional authority to terminate Rebecca Slaughter, a Democratic member of the FTC, whose policy views and regulatory philosophy were incompatible with the administration's agenda. Ms. Slaughter challenged her termination in the United States District Court for the District of Columbia, and the lower courts — relying on **Humphrey's Executor** as binding precedent — held that the President lacked authority to remove her and ordered her reinstated to her position.

The reasoning of the lower courts illustrates the constitutional absurdity that **Humphrey's Executor** has produced. Congress created the FTC as a multi-member commission with members from both political parties, and has specified that commissioners may only be removed for cause. President Trump's position — which I regard as constitutionally correct — is that this statutory structure violates the Vesting Clause of Article II by creating an agency that exercises executive functions while being shielded from the President's removal authority.

The President articulated his constitutional theory with clarity: under Article II, all executive power is vested in him; the FTC exercises executive power; therefore, the FTC must be subject to his direction and control, including his power to remove commissioners at will. Congress cannot by statute carve out a portion of the executive power and vest it in an agency that the President cannot control, because that would effectively create a "fourth branch" of government that the Constitution does not authorize.

## The Supreme Court's Action

Chief Justice Roberts' emergency order staying the lower court's reinstatement of Ms. Slaughter is itself a significant development. The standard for granting an emergency stay pending certiorari requires, among other things, a showing that the applicant has a reasonable probability of prevailing on the merits at the certiorari stage, that irreparable harm will result absent the stay, and that the balance of equities favors the stay applicant. Chief Justice Roberts' issuance of the stay — even before a full Court conference on the certiorari petition — signals his view that the administration has a substantial likelihood of success on the merits.

The broader Court has now taken additional steps indicating that it is prepared to grant certiorari and to reconsider **Humphrey's Executor**. The Court's recent decisions in **Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau** (2020) and **Collins v. Yellen** (2021) have already significantly narrowed **Humphrey's Executor**, holding that its reasoning does not extend to single-director independent agencies and expressing skepticism about the continued viability of the broader principle. A grant of certiorari in **Slaughter v. Trump** would give the Court the vehicle it needs to confront **Humphrey's Executor** directly.

## Constitutional Analysis: The Case for Overruling Humphrey's Executor

I submit that **Humphrey's Executor** was incorrectly decided and that its overruling is long overdue. The decision rested on a misreading of the constitutional text and structure, a mischaracterization of the functions performed by the FTC, and a failure to appreciate the systemic constitutional consequences of permitting Congress to insulate executive officers from presidential control.

The text of Article II could not be clearer. The executive power is vested in the President — not shared between the President and a collection of independent commissions beyond his control. The constitutional structure reinforces this conclusion: the President is the only member of the executive branch directly accountable to the American people through elections. If the President cannot control the officials who wield executive power in his name, the constitutional system of democratic accountability breaks down.

The 1935 Court's effort to distinguish between "purely executive" officers (who can be removed at will) and officers exercising "quasi-legislative" or "quasi-judicial" functions (who can be protected by for-cause removal requirements) has been recognized by subsequent courts and scholars as both theoretically incoherent and practically unworkable. Every function performed by a federal agency is, at bottom, an exercise of executive power — the implementation of law enacted by Congress. There is no principled constitutional distinction between "purely executive" and "quasi-legislative" functions that would justify subjecting them to different removal standards.

## Implications for Constitutional Governance

The potential overruling of **Humphrey's Executor** would have far-reaching consequences for the structure of the federal administrative state. Multi-member independent agencies that have operated for decades under the assumption of insulation from presidential control — including the FTC, the Federal Communications Commission, the Securities and Exchange Commission, and numerous others — would be rendered subject to presidential direction and removal authority.

This is, in my view, the constitutionally correct result. A federal administrative state in which powerful agencies exercising quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial functions over vast portions of the American economy are shielded from democratic accountability through presidential control is inconsistent with the constitutional design. The Constitution vests executive power in the President and makes him accountable to the people for its exercise. Independent agencies that operate outside that accountability structure are, in the framers' terms, a constitutional anomaly.

For readers of this publication who are primarily focused on Second Amendment issues, this development is relevant in a more specific sense as well. An executive branch that is genuinely unified under presidential control — one in which the President can effectively direct and remove every executive officer — is an executive branch better positioned to affirmatively protect constitutional rights, including Second Amendment rights, from infringement by subordinate officials who might otherwise operate without sufficient accountability. The rule of law benefits when constitutional accountability is clear, consistent, and enforced.
