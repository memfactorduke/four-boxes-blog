## Summary

Virginia's Democratic majority is advancing a suite of firearms restrictions, the most constitutionally significant of which is a proposal to prohibit individuals aged 18 to 20 from possessing so-called "assault firearms"—a statutory category that functions, in practice, as a ban on commonly owned semi-automatic centerfire rifles. In my analysis, this legislation is constitutionally untenable under the Second Amendment framework established in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, and it represents not an isolated policy choice but a deliberate incremental strategy to erode Second Amendment rights in the Commonwealth and, ultimately, to model that erosion for the rest of the nation.

## Background

Virginia sits within the jurisdiction of the United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit, a court that has historically been one of the most hostile circuits in the nation when it comes to Second Amendment claims. That institutional context matters when evaluating whether challenged Virginia firearms legislation will receive a fair hearing on the merits before reaching the Supreme Court.

The legislation under consideration—including House Bill 217 and companion measures—reflects a coherent anti-gun legislative agenda being driven by a new Democratic legislative majority and a governor whose background in intelligence work appears, if anything, to make her unsympathetic to private firearms ownership. The centerpiece of the current push is a prohibition on the importation, sale, manufacture, transfer, and for those under 21, possession of "assault firearms" and "large capacity ammunition feeding devices." Violations would be punishable as a Class 1 misdemeanor, with a three-year prohibition on purchasing, possessing, or transporting firearms following conviction.

## The Constitutional Problem with Age-Based Restrictions on Commonly Owned Firearms

The constitutional deficiency of the age-based restriction begins with a foundational question that **Bruen** now requires courts to answer: is there a historical tradition in the American legal order of categorically disarming law-abiding adults aged 18 to 20?

The answer is unambiguously no. At the founding, 18-year-olds were not merely permitted to keep and bear arms—they were expected to. The militia system that the Second Amendment was designed to protect assumed the participation of able-bodied men beginning in their teenage years. The Militia Acts of 1792 imposed an affirmative duty on free white male citizens between the ages of 18 and 45 to arm themselves and be ready for service.

Against that historical backdrop, a law that strips individuals in this age cohort of the ability to possess the quintessential modern militia-relevant firearm—a semi-automatic centerfire rifle—has no cognizable historical analogue. The legislature cannot manufacture one by attaching the label "assault firearm" to a category of weapons defined simply as semi-automatic centerfire rifles capable of accepting magazines of more than 15 rounds. That definition sweeps in the most commonly owned class of rifles in America.

The Virginia legislation is particularly difficult to defend because it targets a population of adults who are, by every other legal measure, fully enfranchised members of society. Individuals aged 18 to 20 may vote, enter binding contracts, marry, be conscripted for military service, and—notably—carry military-issued semi-automatic rifles in defense of the nation. The constitutional incoherence of simultaneously demanding military service while denying access to the civilian equivalent of service weapons is stark.

## The Camel's Nose: Understanding the Incremental Strategy

In my analysis, what makes this legislation particularly dangerous is not its immediate scope but its logic. The proponents of Virginia House Bill 217 are not genuinely arguing that the problem of firearm violence is meaningfully addressed by disarming 18- to 20-year-olds who have committed no offense. They are deploying an incremental strategy: establish a partial ban first, then expand it.

This pattern is visible in the legislative record. The bill's sponsors frame the measure in terms of "public safety," declining to engage with the Second Amendment text, its history, or its constitutional constraints. When a legislator states that gun violence is an "epidemic" requiring legislative action without once referencing the constitutional framework within which such action must operate, that legislator is not engaging in good-faith governance. That is advocacy dressed in legislative form.

The Fourth Circuit's track record on Second Amendment claims means that even unconstitutional Virginia legislation may survive for years in the lower courts. The circuit has historically been receptive to government rationales for firearms restrictions and has shown a persistent reluctance to apply **Bruen**'s text-and-history methodology with rigor. A Republican-appointed senior judge who has served as a dominant intellectual force on the court has stated in oral argument his belief that certain firearms can cut people in half—a claim that reflects neither the factual record nor a good-faith engagement with constitutional text.

## The National Significance of Virginia's Legislative Activity

Virginia functions as a legislative laboratory for anti-gun advocates precisely because it has the institutional infrastructure of a sophisticated state government, sits adjacent to the federal government in Washington, D.C., and has recently shifted its political composition in ways that make ambitious gun control legislation viable. What the anti-gun movement accomplishes in Virginia today is what it would implement nationally given sufficient federal power.

Understanding and contesting Virginia's current legislative push is therefore not merely a matter of vindicating the rights of Virginians—though that would be sufficient justification on its own. It is a matter of defeating a template before it can be replicated. Every feature of HB 217 that survives legal challenge in Virginia becomes a model that other blue-state legislatures will adopt and that federal legislators will eventually attempt to nationalize.

## Implications

The constitutionality of Virginia's proposed age-based assault weapons prohibition is, under any honest application of **Bruen**'s text-and-history test, highly questionable. There is no founding-era historical tradition of categorically disarming legal adults on the basis of age. There is no historical analogue for prohibiting the possession of commonly owned arms by the very cohort of citizens whom the founding generation expected to be armed and ready for militia service.

Litigation challenging this legislation—should it be enacted—will face a hostile Fourth Circuit, but the ultimate question will be decided by the Supreme Court, which has twice in recent years reaffirmed that the Second Amendment is not a second-class right. The Virginia legislature may pass this bill, the governor may sign it, and the Fourth Circuit may initially sustain it. But the constitutional analysis points in only one direction.
