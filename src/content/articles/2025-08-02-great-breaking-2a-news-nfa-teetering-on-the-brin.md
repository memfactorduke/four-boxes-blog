## Summary

A powerful new constitutional challenge to the National Firearms Act's registration regime has been filed in federal district court in Missouri by the National Rifle Association, the Second Amendment Foundation, the Firearms Policy Coalition, and the American Suppressor Association, among others. The case — **Chris Brown v. ATF** — advances two independent theories of unconstitutionality targeting the NFA's registration, fingerprinting, and personal-information disclosure requirements as applied to suppressors, short-barrel rifles, short-barrel shotguns, and any other weapons whose making and transfer taxes were eliminated by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed by President Trump on July 4, 2025. The first theory holds that by eliminating the $200 making and transfer tax on these items, Congress has destroyed the sole Article I constitutional hook that historically justified the NFA's registration scheme, rendering that scheme ultra vires as applied to the now-untaxed firearms. The second theory holds that the NFA's registration and regulatory regime violates the Second Amendment as construed by **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), because suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns are constitutionally protected arms for which no founding-era analog to comprehensive government registration exists. Both theories are meritorious, and the litigation represents the most direct and well-grounded assault on NFA restrictions to reach a federal court in the post-*Bruen* era.

## Background

The National Firearms Act of 1934, codified at 26 U.S.C. §§ 5801 et seq., has regulated certain categories of firearms — including machine guns, suppressors, short-barrel rifles, short-barrel shotguns, and any other weapons — through a dual mechanism of taxation and registration for nearly a century. Congress enacted the NFA explicitly premised on its Article I enumerated power to lay and collect taxes. The statute imposed a $200 tax on the making and transfer of covered items, and it established a comprehensive registration regime — requiring the submission of name, home address, photograph, date of birth, demographic information, fingerprints, and a detailed description of each covered firearm — as the administrative apparatus designed to ensure collection of that tax.

The Supreme Court upheld early challenges to the NFA on precisely this basis, describing the statute as an interrelated system for the taxation of certain classes of firearms and holding that the registration provisions were supportable as an aid to the revenue purpose. Under that rationale, the registration scheme derived its entire constitutional legitimacy from its function as a tax-enforcement mechanism. It existed to ensure that the $200 tax Congress was authorized to impose was actually paid.

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed into law on July 4, 2025, fundamentally altered this constitutional landscape. The Act eliminated the $200 making and transfer taxes on suppressors, short-barrel rifles, short-barrel shotguns, and NFA-defined any other weapons. Those taxes — the sole basis on which courts had sustained the NFA's registration requirements — no longer exist for these categories of items. The Act did not, however, repeal or modify the NFA's registration requirements. The result is a regulatory regime stripped of its constitutional foundation: the registration, fingerprinting, and personal-disclosure mandates continue to apply to firearms that are no longer subject to any tax that those mandates were designed to enforce.

## The Article I Constitutional Theory

The structural argument advanced in **Chris Brown v. ATF** is straightforward and, in my view, legally correct. Congress is a government of enumerated powers. It possesses only the authority Article I grants. For the NFA, Congress invoked the power to lay and collect taxes as the exclusive constitutional basis for the statute. The Supreme Court accepted that rationale and sustained the NFA as a taxing measure, with the registration provisions treated as ancillary to the revenue purpose.

Once the tax is eliminated for a given category of firearms, the taxing power provides no residual authority for continued regulation of those items. The NFA's registration requirements as applied to untaxed suppressors, short-barrel rifles, short-barrel shotguns, and any other weapons cannot be justified under the Commerce Clause, the Necessary and Proper Clause, or any other Article I grant of power independently invoked to sustain a scheme of individual firearms registration. No other Article I enumerated power supports the proposition that Congress may require private citizens to submit fingerprints, photographs, and home addresses to a federal database as a precondition to possessing constitutionally protected firearms.

As the complaint in **Chris Brown** correctly argues: while the NFA's regulations may have been permissible in support of the statute's taxes on making and transferring firearms, that justification no longer remains for items whose making and transfer are no longer taxed. To the extent the NFA imposes requirements on making, transferring, receiving, possessing, or otherwise using those untaxed firearms, it cannot be justified as an exercise of any other Article I power. Accordingly, the NFA is unconstitutional as applied to those untaxed firearms.

This is not a close question. The constitutional predicate for the NFA's registration regime was tax enforcement. Congress has now voluntarily surrendered the tax. A mechanism designed to enforce an obligation that no longer exists has no remaining legal justification.

## The Second Amendment Theory

The complaint also advances a parallel and independent Second Amendment claim, which provides an alternative and equally powerful basis for relief. **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), established that the Second Amendment protects instruments that facilitate armed self-defense, and that the government cannot ban arms that are in common use for lawful purposes. **Bruen** extended and formalized that framework, requiring the government to demonstrate that any regulation of Second Amendment-protected conduct is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation rooted in the founding era.

Suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns are constitutionally protected arms under these standards. They are firearms — or instruments that directly facilitate the use of firearms — capable of use offensively and defensively. Suppressors, in particular, are instruments that facilitate armed self-defense by protecting the hearing of a shooter and those in proximity; they are physical objects that serve an obvious defensive function. When a government restricts suppressors, it restricts a class of firearms capable of firing suppressed rounds, thereby limiting the manner in which constitutionally protected firearms may be used.

Short-barrel rifles and short-barrel shotguns are, at their core, firearms. Their status as "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment requires no elaborate argument: they are by definition weapons that can be wielded offensively and defensively. They are in common use across the United States by law-abiding citizens for home defense, hunting, and recreational shooting. Under *Heller* and *Bruen*, that is sufficient to trigger full Second Amendment protection.

Against that backdrop, the NFA's registration regime has no founding-era historical analog. The historical record contains no evidence of colonial, founding-era, or early republic legislation requiring private citizens to submit their names, addresses, photographs, fingerprints, and identifying information to a government registry as a precondition to possessing commonly owned weapons. Government registration of individual gun owners and their specific firearms is a twentieth-century invention — one that postdates the founding by more than a century and therefore cannot ground a tradition sufficient to sustain the NFA's requirements under *Bruen*'s methodology.

Justice Kavanaugh, writing in dissent as a judge on the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit in *Heller v. District of Columbia* (2011) — more than a decade before *Bruen* — reached precisely this conclusion. He observed that there is no historical tradition of registration of gun owners or firearms, that such requirements did not exist until the twentieth century, and that this historical absence was dispositive. That dissent, written before *Bruen* established the text-and-history framework as controlling law, anticipated the analytical approach the Supreme Court would subsequently mandate. It represents the considered judgment of the Justice who, more than any other member of the current Court, built his jurisprudential reputation on fidelity to the original public meaning of the Second Amendment.

## The Relationship Between the Two Theories

The two constitutional theories advanced in **Chris Brown v. ATF** operate independently and reinforce each other. The Article I theory is structural and categorical: Congress simply lacks the enumerated power to maintain the NFA's registration regime for untaxed firearms, regardless of what the Second Amendment might or might not permit. That theory can prevail without any resort to Second Amendment doctrine and without any historical analysis of founding-era firearms regulation.

The Second Amendment theory provides an alternative ground for relief that would apply even in a hypothetical universe where some other Article I hook existed. Because suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns are protected arms under the Second Amendment's text, the government must justify any restriction on those arms by reference to a historical tradition of analogous regulation. No such tradition exists for government firearms registration. The NFA's registration regime therefore fails the *Bruen* test independently of the Article I analysis.

A parallel challenge advancing the same theories has also been filed in federal district court in Texas by the Gun Owners of America. The concurrent pursuit of these claims in multiple federal circuits serves the Second Amendment community's strategic interests: it multiplies the venues in which these legal arguments will be tested, increases the probability of favorable early rulings, and creates a developed circuit record that will eventually reach the Supreme Court.

## Implications

The filing of **Chris Brown v. ATF** marks a pivotal moment in the ongoing constitutional recalibration of the NFA. The One Big Beautiful Bill has not merely reduced a tax — it has, as a matter of constitutional law, severed the statutory and structural basis on which the NFA's registration requirements were historically defended. What remains is a regulatory regime that imposes substantial burdens on the exercise of Second Amendment rights without any plausible constitutional justification.

The litigation will proceed through the district court on a factual and legal record, and the government will have an opportunity to articulate whatever constitutional theory it believes sustains the now-untaxed registration requirements. Based on my analysis of the governing precedent and constitutional structure, I do not believe the government can identify a tenable Article I power that supports the continued maintenance of the NFA's registration regime for items no longer subject to tax. Nor can it produce a founding-era analog to comprehensive firearms registration sufficient to satisfy *Bruen*.

The legislative path remains open as well. A subsequent reconciliation bill in 2026 could remove these items from the NFA entirely, achieving through legislation what the litigation seeks to establish through judicial adjudication. Both tracks — litigation and legislation — should be pursued with equal vigor. The constitutional infirmity exposed by the One Big Beautiful Bill's elimination of the NFA taxes is real, it is substantial, and the courts should say so.
