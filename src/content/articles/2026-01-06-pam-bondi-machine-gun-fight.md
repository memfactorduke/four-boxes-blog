## Summary

The arrest and indictment of Nicolás Maduro in New York federal court on charges including narco-terrorism conspiracy, cocaine importation conspiracy, and possession of machine guns and destructive devices has generated commentary within the Second Amendment community suggesting that the machine gun charges somehow threaten domestic firearms rights or reflect an anti-Second Amendment posture on the part of Attorney General Pam Bondi. In my assessment, this concern is unfounded. The machine gun charges against Maduro arise under a statutory framework entirely distinct from the National Firearms Act provisions that govern civilian ownership, and Maduro himself — a foreign national who illegally directed crimes against the United States — has no cognizable Second Amendment rights in any event. This analysis addresses both the statutory structure of the indictment and the constitutional question of who qualifies as "the people" protected by the Second Amendment.

## Background: The Indictment

The indictment against Nicolás Maduro and his wife Celia Flores, filed in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York, charges Maduro with narco-terrorism conspiracy, cocaine importation conspiracy, possession of machine guns and destructive devices, and conspiracy to possess machine guns and destructive devices against the United States. Attorney General Pam Bondi summarized the charges at a press conference, thanking President Trump "for having the courage to demand accountability on behalf of the American people" and the military for "the incredible and highly successful mission to capture these two alleged international narco traffickers."

The original indictment dates to 2020. The arrest in Caracas involved the deployment of approximately 150 aircraft and special forces units — an extraordinary exercise of military power pursuant to what Vice President JD Vance accurately described as the well-established legal authority to arrest and bring to the United States individuals who have committed or directed crimes against this country, regardless of their location abroad. As Vance stated: "You do not get to avoid justice for drug trafficking in the United States because you live in a palace in Caracas."

The legal authority for this arrest is well settled. In 1989, the United States entered Panama to arrest Manuel Noriega on drug trafficking charges. The principle established there — that the United States has legal authority to apprehend foreign nationals abroad who have directed crimes against the United States and to bring them before American courts — applies with full force here.

## The Statutory Framework: What the Indictment Actually Charges

The concern raised in some quarters of the Second Amendment community is that the machine gun charges in the Maduro indictment will somehow be used as precedent to expand restrictions on civilian machine gun ownership. This concern, while understandable given the current litigation environment around the National Firearms Act and the Hughes Amendment, does not survive scrutiny of the actual statutory provisions cited in the indictment.

The relevant provisions do not reference the National Firearms Act's registration requirements, nor do they invoke 18 U.S.C. § 922(o) — the Hughes Amendment provision prohibiting civilian possession of machine guns manufactured after May 19, 1986. The indictment is not a prosecution for violating the civilian regulatory framework that governs lawful American gun owners. It is a prosecution for using firearms — specifically machine guns and destructive devices — as instruments of narco-terrorism, drug trafficking conspiracies, and violent offenses directed against the United States.

The distinction is fundamental. Congress has always possessed the authority to prohibit the use of any weapon in the commission of federal crimes. That is categorically different from prohibiting law-abiding American citizens from possessing otherwise lawful firearms. A prosecution for using a firearm to advance a drug trafficking conspiracy is not a Second Amendment case. It is a criminal law case involving the use of a weapon as a tool of criminal enterprise. The two analytical frameworks do not intersect.

## The Constitutional Question: Who Are "The People"?

Even setting aside the distinct statutory framework, Maduro cannot invoke the Second Amendment's protections as a defense. The Second Amendment protects "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms." The Supreme Court, in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), has made clear that "the people" in the constitutional context refers to members of the national community — law-abiding citizens and those with a sufficient connection to the political community to warrant constitutional protection.

Maduro is a foreign national who has never been lawfully present in the United States, who was indicted for orchestrating crimes against the United States from the territory of a foreign state, and who has no connection whatsoever to the American political community. He is not part of "the people" as that term is used in the Second Amendment. The constitutional right simply does not attach to him, and no court would hold otherwise.

This point forecloses any argument that the machine gun charges in this indictment could serve as a vehicle for expanding restrictions on domestic firearms ownership through Second Amendment adjudication. There is no Second Amendment issue in this case because there is no Second Amendment claimant.

## The Hughes Amendment and Current NFA Litigation

It is worth noting, as background context, that the National Firearms Act was enacted in the 1930s and included machine guns within its regulatory scope. In 1986, Congress enacted the Hughes Amendment as part of the Firearm Owners Protection Act, prohibiting civilian transfer or possession of any machine gun manufactured after May 19, 1986. Under current law, a civilian may acquire a machine gun only if it was manufactured and registered before that date — a pool of weapons that is finite, increasingly expensive, and tightly regulated.

The constitutionality of the Hughes Amendment's post-1986 registration freeze is a live issue in Second Amendment litigation. I have elsewhere addressed the arguments that this ban is inconsistent with the text, history, and tradition analysis mandated by **Heller** and **Bruen**. That litigation is ongoing in various courts. But it proceeds entirely independently of the Maduro prosecution, which involves none of the statutory provisions relevant to that constitutional debate.

## Implications

The Maduro indictment, while significant as a matter of national security, foreign policy, and federal criminal law, poses no threat to the Second Amendment rights of law-abiding American citizens. The machine gun charges arise under criminal statutes governing the use of weapons as instruments of violent federal offenses — not under the NFA or Hughes Amendment provisions that govern civilian firearms ownership. And Maduro himself has no Second Amendment rights to assert.

In my assessment, Attorney General Bondi's prosecution of Maduro is entirely consistent with a commitment to both robust Second Amendment protection for American citizens and vigorous enforcement of federal criminal law against foreign actors who direct violence against the United States. Those two commitments are not in tension. Concerns that this prosecution somehow compromises Second Amendment rights conflate entirely distinct legal frameworks and should not be credited.
