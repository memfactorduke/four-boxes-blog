## Summary

With the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit sitting en banc scheduled to hear oral argument on October 15, 2025 in the consolidated New Jersey AR-15 and magazine ban cases — *Cheeseman v. New Jersey* and *Association of New Jersey Rifle and Pistol Clubs v. New Jersey Attorney General* — the composition of the en banc court is a matter of urgent practical consequence for Second Amendment rights. A pending judicial nomination of particular significance is that of Professor Jennifer Mascott, a former law clerk to Justice Clarence Thomas and Justice Brett Kavanaugh, who has been nominated by President Trump to a seat on the Third Circuit. Confirmation of Professor Mascott before October 15, 2025 could determine whether she participates in the en banc argument and, accordingly, could influence the outcome of what may be the most important AR-15 ban case currently in the federal courts.

## Background

The October 15, 2025 en banc argument in the New Jersey AR-15 and magazine ban cases is one of the most consequential Second Amendment proceedings of this decade. The Third Circuit, sitting with all its active judges, will determine whether New Jersey's categorical prohibitions on AR-15 semi-automatic rifles and magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds are constitutional under *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022) and *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008). The outcome will bind all federal courts within the Third Circuit's jurisdiction — New Jersey, Delaware, and Pennsylvania — and will carry substantial weight as the Supreme Court considers whether to grant certiorari in one of the many pending AR-15 ban cases.

En banc decisions are determined by vote of all active judges on the court. Because each judge has an equal vote and the margin of decision is frequently narrow, the confirmation of a single additional judge before the argument date can determine the outcome. Professor Mascott's pending nomination to the Third Circuit presents precisely this situation: her confirmation and participation in the October 15 argument could be the deciding factor in whether the Third Circuit upholds or strikes down New Jersey's ban.

## Professor Jennifer Mascott: Credentials and Judicial Philosophy

Professor Jennifer Mascott is one of the most credentialed originalist jurists available for federal appellate appointment. She served as a law clerk to Associate Justice Clarence Thomas — widely regarded as the intellectual leader of the Supreme Court's originalist wing — and also as a law clerk to Justice Brett Kavanaugh when he served on the D.C. Circuit. Her academic work has focused on originalist constitutional interpretation, administrative law, and the structural provisions of the Constitution.

Judges who clerked for Justice Thomas and have demonstrated commitment to the originalist methodology he has championed over decades of jurisprudence bring to the bench an especially rigorous analytical framework for Second Amendment cases. The *Bruen* framework, which requires courts to assess the constitutionality of firearms regulations against the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation, is an originalist methodology par excellence — it demands that courts consult the historical record of the founding era and the period of Reconstruction to identify what kinds of firearms regulations the Constitution was understood to permit. A judge with Professor Mascott's training and scholarly background is uniquely positioned to apply this framework faithfully.

## The Strategic Significance of the Third Circuit in AR-15 Ban Litigation

I have previously analyzed why the Third Circuit presents a structural advantage for Second Amendment claimants that is not present in the other circuits where AR-15 ban challenges are pending. The argument bears elaboration in the context of the October 15 proceeding.

In the Ninth Circuit — which covers California, where the most extensive gun ban laws exist — the court has a long history of decisions hostile to Second Amendment rights. The en banc Ninth Circuit's decision in *Duncan v. Bonta*, upholding California's magazine ban, was decided under the pre-*Bruen* framework, but even after *Bruen* the Ninth Circuit has shown reluctance to apply that framework in ways that invalidate firearms restrictions. The circuit's ideological composition makes a favorable outcome for Second Amendment plaintiffs structurally difficult.

In the Seventh Circuit — covering Illinois, where *Barnett v. Raoul* is currently being litigated — the en banc composition and the recent record of Second Amendment decisions also present challenges, as I have discussed separately.

The Third Circuit's composition at the time of the October 15 argument is more favorable. The circuit's geographical reach encompasses Pennsylvania, which has a strong tradition of firearms ownership and whose federal judges are drawn from a legal culture more balanced on Second Amendment questions than the cultures that dominate the Ninth and Seventh Circuits. The addition of a principled originalist such as Professor Mascott to the court before the October 15 argument would further improve the environment for faithful application of *Bruen*.

## The Role of the Senate in Protecting Second Amendment Rights

The confirmation of federal judges is a constitutional responsibility of the United States Senate. Senate Majority Leader John Thune and the Republican majority have the procedural tools necessary to confirm Professor Mascott before October 15, 2025 if they prioritize doing so. The confirmation of originalist judges to the federal courts of appeals is among the most durable and consequential actions available to a Republican Senate majority — federal appellate judges serve life tenure, and their decisions on constitutional questions affect millions of Americans for decades.

The urgency here is concrete and time-bound: October 15, 2025 is a fixed date, and a nomination that is not acted upon before that date cannot affect the composition of the en banc court for the New Jersey AR-15 ban argument. Gun owners and Second Amendment advocates who are aware of this procedural reality should make known to their elected representatives in the Senate the importance of acting on Professor Mascott's confirmation without delay.

## The Constitutional Stakes

The fundamental constitutional question in *Cheeseman* and the *Association of New Jersey Rifle and Pistol Clubs* case is whether New Jersey may categorically prohibit law-abiding citizens from possessing the most popular rifle in America and the standard-configuration magazines for the most common defensive firearms. Under *Heller* and *Bruen*, the answer is no: the Second Amendment protects arms in common use for lawful purposes, the government bears the burden of justifying any restriction, and there is no historical tradition of categorical semi-automatic rifle bans to which the government can point.

The practical importance of this case extends beyond New Jersey. A Third Circuit ruling striking down New Jersey's ban would be the first federal court of appeals decision invalidating a state-level AR-15 ban, creating or deepening a circuit split that would create strong pressure for Supreme Court review. A ruling upholding the ban would add to the weight of adverse circuit authority, potentially counseling the Supreme Court to defer action pending further lower court development.

## Implications

The confirmation of Professor Jennifer Mascott to the Third Circuit before October 15, 2025 is, in my assessment, among the most direct actions that can be taken to advance Second Amendment rights in the current litigation environment. The composition of the en banc court on that date is not fixed — it depends on Senate action — and each additional originalist judge who participates in the October 15 argument increases the probability of a decision that faithfully applies *Heller* and *Bruen* and holds New Jersey's AR-15 and magazine ban unconstitutional.

I urge gun owners and Second Amendment advocates to contact their Senators and communicate the importance of confirming Professor Mascott and other pending Trump judicial nominees to the Third Circuit without delay. The opportunity to shape the outcome of one of the most important Second Amendment cases currently in the federal courts is available, but it is time-limited. The clock is running toward October 15.
