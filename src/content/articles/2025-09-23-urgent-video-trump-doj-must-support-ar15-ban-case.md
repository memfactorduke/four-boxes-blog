## Summary

There exists a critically time-sensitive opportunity for the Trump administration's Department of Justice to file a brief in support of the certiorari petition pending before the Supreme Court of the United States in **Bevis v. City of Naperville** — a case arising from the Seventh Circuit that directly implicates the constitutionality of state and local bans on semi-automatic rifles, including AR-15s, and standard-capacity magazines. The DOJ has already demonstrated its commitment to this issue by submitting pro-Second Amendment briefs in related litigation in the Seventh and Third Circuits. A Solicitor General's brief in support of certiorari in **Bevis** would represent a powerful and potentially decisive signal to the Supreme Court that the federal executive regards this question as one of exceptional constitutional importance warranting the Court's plenary review.

## Background

**Bevis v. City of Naperville** is a challenge to Illinois's Protect Illinois Communities Act (PICA) — a sweeping ban on semi-automatic firearms commonly described as "assault weapons," including the AR-15, and on standard-capacity magazines. The case has traversed a complex and technically demanding procedural history in the Seventh Circuit that reflects the fundamental tension between controlling Supreme Court precedent and the circuit's earlier, pre-**Bruen** analytical framework.

Following the Supreme Court's landmark decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022), which rejected the means-ends balancing tests previously applied to Second Amendment claims and replaced them with a text, history, and tradition analysis, the Seventh Circuit was compelled to reconsider its prior disposition of challenges to the Illinois law. In a 2-to-1 decision, the Seventh Circuit remanded the case to the district court, finding that it could not resolve the constitutional question without additional factual findings.

On remand, United States District Judge Stephen McGlynn of the Southern District of Illinois — a jurist who, by all accounts, possesses a thorough command of both constitutional law and firearms — conducted what amounted to a full trial on the factual questions posed by the Seventh Circuit. The result was a comprehensive findings-of-fact opinion in which Judge McGlynn concluded, based on the evidence presented, that PICA's criminalization of the possession of semi-automatic firearms such as AR-15s and standard-capacity magazines is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The case is now before a Seventh Circuit three-judge panel for review of Judge McGlynn's ruling.

## The Certiorari Opportunity in Bevis

Simultaneously, **Bevis v. City of Naperville** — a companion case presenting substantially similar constitutional questions — has a certiorari petition pending before the Supreme Court of the United States. This petition presents the Court with an opportunity to resolve, once and for all, the question of whether the Second Amendment protects the right to keep and bear semi-automatic rifles such as the AR-15 — arguably the most popular and widely owned rifle in the United States.

I am highly confident that the Trump administration's DOJ genuinely wants this question resolved in favor of Second Amendment rights, because the DOJ has already committed itself to precisely that position in its amicus filings in related circuit court litigation. The DOJ's Civil Rights Division, under the leadership of Harmeet Dhillon, has submitted a powerful brief in the Seventh Circuit's **Barnett v. Raoul** litigation — the case that encompasses Judge McGlynn's proceedings — arguing that Illinois's assault weapons ban violates the Second Amendment. The DOJ has made the identical argument in **Cheeseman v. Platkin**, pending before the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit, where a full en banc argument is forthcoming.

Given this unambiguous record of executive branch commitment to the constitutional protection of semi-automatic rifles, the question is not whether the DOJ supports the Second Amendment position in **Bevis** — it plainly does — but whether the administration will take the additional and strategically significant step of filing a Solicitor General's brief in support of certiorari.

## The Solicitor General's Role in Certiorari

The Solicitor General of the United States holds a unique and institutionally significant position in the Supreme Court's certiorari process. The Court grants plenary review in only approximately 60 to 80 cases per Term, out of thousands of petitions. The Solicitor General's recommendation — whether through a brief in support of certiorari or in response to a "call for the views of the Solicitor General" (CVSG) invitation from the Court — carries extraordinary weight. The Court grants certiorari in cases where the Solicitor General recommends it at a rate dramatically higher than in cases where no such recommendation is made.

A Solicitor General's brief in support of certiorari in **Bevis** would communicate to the Court that the federal executive regards the resolution of this constitutional question as a matter of the highest national importance, that the current circuit landscape is creating uncertainty and inconsistency in the protection of a fundamental constitutional right, and that the Supreme Court's plenary review is both warranted and urgently needed.

## The Merits of the Second Amendment Question

On the merits, the constitutional case for protecting semi-automatic rifles under the Second Amendment is overwhelming. The AR-15 is the most popular rifle in the United States. Tens of millions of law-abiding Americans own one or more such rifles for a variety of lawful purposes, including home defense, competitive shooting, hunting, and recreational use. Under **Heller**'s "in common use" test — which asks whether a weapon is in common use by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes — the AR-15 plainly qualifies for constitutional protection.

The government's counterargument — that AR-15s are "weapons of war" that may be banned consistent with the Second Amendment — has been effectively dismantled by numerous courts and legal commentators. AR-15s are semi-automatic, not automatic, firearms. They fire one round per trigger pull, like any other semi-automatic pistol or rifle. The designation "assault weapon" is a political and rhetorical construction, not a technical or legal term of art with constitutional significance. Under the text, history, and tradition framework mandated by **Bruen**, there is no historical tradition of banning commonly owned semi-automatic firearms that would support PICA or analogous state laws.

## Implications

The certiorari petition in **Bevis** represents one of the most important Second Amendment opportunities currently pending before the Supreme Court. A grant of certiorari, followed by a ruling that AR-15s and standard-capacity magazines are protected by the Second Amendment, would constitute the most significant Second Amendment decision since **Bruen** and would effectively invalidate the assault weapons bans currently in force in Illinois, California, New York, New Jersey, Maryland, Connecticut, Massachusetts, and several other jurisdictions.

I urge the Solicitor General's office to act expeditiously on this opportunity. The DOJ has already committed itself to the Second Amendment position in the relevant circuit courts. Filing a brief in support of certiorari in **Bevis** would be entirely consistent with that commitment, and would represent a powerful use of the federal executive's unique institutional voice before the Supreme Court to advance the constitutional rights of tens of millions of law-abiding Americans.
