## Summary

President Donald Trump has issued a proclamation addressing the flow of immigration into the United States and its connection to the preservation of American institutions, traditions, and constitutional rights — including the Second Amendment. While much of the daily commentary on Second Amendment issues focuses on discrete legal questions such as the constitutionality of specific gun regulations or the progress of particular litigation, it is essential that the Second Amendment community not lose sight of a larger and in many respects more consequential dynamic: the long-term demographic transformation of the American electorate and its implications for the political and cultural foundation upon which Second Amendment rights depend. The terrorist attack in Washington, D.C., committed by an Afghan national brought to the United States during the Biden administration, provides a vivid and tragic illustration of the stakes involved.

## Background: The Broader Context of Second Amendment Defense

I want to begin by stepping back from the day-to-day analysis of cases, circuit splits, and certiorari petitions — the essential but sometimes granular work that occupies much of my commentary — to address a dimension of the Second Amendment challenge that does not receive sufficient attention from the legal commentariat: the relationship between the composition of the American electorate and the long-term survival of Second Amendment rights.

Every day on this channel, I analyze the granular details of Second Amendment jurisprudence: whether a particular court correctly applied the **Bruen** text-history-tradition framework; whether the Seventh Circuit properly handled the assault weapons ban question; whether a particular circuit has smuggled interest-balancing analysis into what is supposed to be a text-and-history inquiry. This analysis is critically important. Getting the legal doctrine right is mission-critical to protecting our right to keep and bear arms.

But doctrine does not exist in a vacuum. Constitutional rights, including the Second Amendment, ultimately depend on a political and cultural foundation that supports them. The Supreme Court's decision in **Heller** was a 5-4 decision; replace one Justice with a different individual and the right to keep and bear arms would have been effectively eliminated. The margin is thin, and the political conditions that produce Supreme Court justices sympathetic to the Second Amendment are themselves dependent on maintaining a voting majority that values constitutional liberty in the American tradition.

## The DC Attack and Its Implications

The terrorist attack in Washington, D.C., carried out by an Afghan citizen who had been brought to the United States during the Biden years as a purported American ally, and who proceeded to shoot and kill at least one National Guardsman, tragically illustrates the concrete human cost of the immigration policies pursued by the progressive movement over the past several decades. The attacker resided in Washington State with his wife and five children — individuals who, under birthright citizenship doctrines, may become American citizens and future voters regardless of their family's history.

I am not unmindful of the distinction between the individual perpetrator of this act of violence and the broader immigrant population. The vast majority of those who come to the United States seek a better life for themselves and their families, and the motivations driving migration are understandable in human terms. The critique I am articulating is directed not at individual immigrants but at a policy framework — the specific immigration policy advanced by the Democratic Party and the progressive movement — that has brought tens of millions of individuals into the United States without reference to their connection to or interest in American constitutional values, including the Second Amendment.

## The Electoral Mathematics of Constitutional Rights

The protection of Second Amendment rights in the long term depends on the ability to maintain political majorities that support those rights. This is not merely a practical observation but a constitutional one — the judges who interpret the Second Amendment are appointed by presidents who are elected by voters, and confirmed by senators who are elected by voters. The composition of the electorate is therefore directly relevant to the trajectory of Second Amendment jurisprudence.

The pattern that concerns me is well documented. Communities with large concentrations of recent immigrants from countries with no tradition of individual firearm ownership or Second Amendment culture tend to vote, at very high rates, for Democratic candidates and Democratic policy positions. This includes support for the comprehensive gun control agenda that the Democratic Party has consistently advanced — assault weapons bans, magazine restrictions, expanded background check requirements, red flag laws, and ultimately the kind of near-total civilian disarmament that prevails in Western Europe and the United Kingdom.

The importation of tens of millions of individuals from countries with no tradition of constitutional gun rights — countries where the government has historically maintained a monopoly on lethal force, where private arms are associated with criminality or military force rather than individual liberty, and where the concept of an armed citizenry as a check on government tyranny is culturally alien — has predictable electoral consequences for the Second Amendment.

## Trump's Proclamation in Constitutional Context

President Trump's proclamation on immigration, and his broader effort to reduce the flow of immigration and to remove individuals who are in the country unlawfully or who pose security threats, must be understood against this backdrop. It is not merely a law enforcement measure, though it is that. It is also a policy choice with long-term implications for the demographic and cultural composition of the American electorate and, therefore, for the future of constitutional rights that depend on that electorate.

The constitutional basis for executive action on immigration is well established. The president has broad authority under Article II and applicable statutes to manage the flow of immigration into the United States. Congress has enacted a comprehensive framework of immigration law that vests substantial discretionary authority in the executive branch to determine admission and removal policies. The Trump administration's exercise of this authority to prioritize border security and enforce immigration laws is legally uncontroversial as a general matter, even if specific applications may be challenged in court.

What is more significant for purposes of this analysis is the explicit connection President Trump has drawn between immigration policy and the preservation of American institutions, traditions, and constitutional rights. This connection is not rhetorical; it reflects a substantive understanding of the relationship between the composition of a democratic society and the values that its democratic institutions will express and protect.

## The Second Amendment as Part of the American Constitutional Tradition

The right to keep and bear arms is not a universal feature of liberal democratic societies; it is a specifically American institution rooted in a particular historical experience, a particular philosophical tradition about the relationship between citizens and their government, and a particular cultural understanding of the armed individual as the foundation of a free republic. This is why the Second Amendment exists in the American Bill of Rights but not in the constitutions of Western European democracies. It is why American gun culture differs so fundamentally from the gun cultures of countries that have adopted extensive civilian disarmament policies.

The preservation of this specifically American tradition requires a citizenry that understands, values, and identifies with it. The progressive project of mass immigration from countries with no connection to that tradition is, whether by design or as a predictable consequence, gradually diluting the cultural and political foundation on which Second Amendment rights rest.

## Implications

I want to be clear about what I am and am not arguing. I am not arguing that any individual immigrant cannot or will not come to embrace American constitutional values, including the Second Amendment. Many immigrants have come to America, assimilated deeply into American culture, and become strong defenders of constitutional liberty. The question is one of aggregate political effects, not individual character assessments.

The policy argument is about the scale and pace of immigration, and about the deliberate decision to forgo the kind of assimilation and vetting processes that historically produced immigrants who integrated into American constitutional culture rather than displacing it. A immigration system that is orderly, selective, and designed to admit individuals who can and will assimilate to American values produces a different electorate than one that brings in tens of millions of individuals in a short period with no filtering for connection to American constitutional traditions.

The Second Amendment community must understand that the legal and political battles we fight over gun rights are inextricably embedded in a larger set of cultural and political dynamics. Winning **Bruen** was essential; maintaining the political conditions that produced the Court that decided **Bruen** is equally essential. President Trump's immigration policies represent an attempt to preserve those conditions by restoring order and selectivity to the immigration system.

The protection of the Second Amendment is ultimately a political project as much as a legal one. The courts can defend the right to keep and bear arms, but only if the political conditions exist to appoint and confirm judges who will do so. Those political conditions, in turn, depend on a citizenry that values constitutional liberty in the American tradition. These considerations are all connected, and the Second Amendment community ignores their connection at its peril.
