## Summary

In a significant litigation development, the United States District Court for the Northern District of Texas has denied the federal government's motion to stay proceedings in a major constitutional challenge to the National Firearms Act's application to suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns. Gun Owners of America, the Silencer Shop Foundation, and co-plaintiffs argued that the enactment of the One Big Beautiful Bill — which eliminated the $200 NFA tax on these items — removed the only constitutionally cognizable basis for Congress's authority to require their registration, fingerprinting, and ongoing regulation. The court's refusal to pause the case in deference to a government shutdown is a significant procedural victory that will force expedited resolution of a constitutional question of the first importance.

## Background

Gun Owners of America, the Silencer Shop Foundation, and associated plaintiffs brought a major constitutional challenge in the United States District Court for the Northern District of Texas — before Judge James Wesley Hendrix, a Trump appointee — arguing that Congress lacks the constitutional authority to continue imposing the registration, fingerprinting, and regulatory requirements of the National Firearms Act on suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns.

The legal theory underlying this challenge is rooted in Article I of the United States Constitution. Article I enumerates the powers of Congress. Congress possesses only those powers granted to it by the Constitution; all other authority remains with the states or the people under the Tenth Amendment. To regulate a particular subject, Congress must identify a specific constitutional grant of power authorizing that regulation.

## The Constitutional Basis for the Original NFA

Since the National Firearms Act's enactment in 1934, Congress has justified its authority to impose registration, fingerprinting, and transfer restrictions on suppressors and short-barrel firearms through its Article I taxing power. The NFA imposed a $200 tax — a substantial sum in 1934 dollars — on the transfer of each covered item. The registration requirement, the fingerprinting requirement, the approval process, and the waiting period were all mechanisms designed to ensure that the $200 tax had been paid on each specific item.

The constitutional logic was simple: if Congress has the power to tax the transfer of these items, it has the incidental power to create administrative mechanisms to enforce and verify payment of that tax. The regulatory apparatus of the NFA was, in constitutional theory, subordinate to and dependent upon the taxing function.

## The One Big Beautiful Bill and Its Constitutional Consequence

President Trump's One Big Beautiful Bill, enacted through the congressional reconciliation process, eliminated the $200 NFA transfer tax as applied to suppressors and short-barrel firearms. This is a remarkable legislative development with profound constitutional implications.

If the sole constitutional basis for Congress's authority to regulate these items was the Article I taxing power — and that is, in my analysis, the only plausible constitutional basis — then the elimination of the tax removes the constitutional hook. Without a tax to enforce and verify, there is no longer a constitutionally adequate justification for the registration requirement, the fingerprinting requirement, the approval process, the transfer restrictions, or any other element of the NFA regulatory apparatus as applied to these items.

The plaintiffs in this case have made precisely this argument: Congress lacks the authority to require the fingerprinting and registration of suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns once the taxing power basis for those requirements has been eliminated by statute. Without a tax on these items, the mechanism to ensure the tax had been paid — the entire NFA regulatory structure — becomes constitutionally nugatory.

## The Government Shutdown and the Motion to Stay

When the federal government entered a partial shutdown, the lawyers representing the ATF and the Department of Justice filed a motion seeking to stay all proceedings. The government's argument was straightforward: DOJ civil attorneys are not required to work during a shutdown absent an emergency, and the government asked the court to pause the case until normal operations resumed.

The plaintiffs — Gun Owners of America and the Silencer Shop Foundation — opposed this motion vigorously. Their position was that they had brought this case in good faith, that the legal question presented was important, and that they wanted it resolved promptly. Plaintiffs who sue generally want their cases to proceed as quickly as possible because they believe they will prevail.

Judge Hendrix denied the government's stay motion. The court's ruling was grounded in a procedural mechanism that illustrates the government's exposure. Under the rules governing the shutdown, DOJ civil attorneys who are ordinarily furloughed may be required to continue working if a court orders them to act in a pending civil matter. By denying the stay and ordering the government to continue litigating, Judge Hendrix effectively converted the case into an "emergency" for shutdown purposes — obligating the government's attorneys to continue working on the matter. The government must now defend the NFA's constitutionality with respect to these items, or lose by default.

Judge Hendrix specifically noted that the case implicated Second Amendment concerns — an acknowledgment that the right to keep and bear arms is at stake in this litigation — and that important constitutional questions warranted expedited resolution.

## The Broader Constitutional Stakes

The argument advanced by Gun Owners of America in this case is not merely a tactical opportunity arising from the elimination of the NFA tax. It reflects a principled constitutional analysis that has been available to Second Amendment scholars and litigators for years but has rarely been litigated in this specific form.

The NFA has been defended as a taxing measure since 1934. If the tax is eliminated — as it now has been for suppressors and short-barrel firearms — the regulatory apparatus that was justified as incident to that tax loses its constitutional foundation. This is not a close question. It is an application of the elementary principle that congressional power must be traced to a specific constitutional grant, and that when the basis for that grant disappears, so does the authority it supported.

The argument extends beyond suppressors and short-barrel items. If the underlying constitutional principle is correct — that NFA regulation depends on the taxing power — then any future elimination of NFA taxes on other items, such as machine guns, would raise identical constitutional questions about the continued validity of registration and transfer restrictions on those items as well.

## Implications

The district court's denial of the government's stay motion is a procedural victory of real significance. It forces the federal government to defend the constitutional basis for NFA regulation of suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns now, under conditions where the only available constitutional justification — the Article I taxing power — has been eliminated by the One Big Beautiful Bill. Gun Owners of America and the Silencer Shop Foundation have brought a powerful constitutional challenge that goes to the heart of Congress's authority to maintain the NFA regulatory structure in the absence of the tax that originally provided that authority. Second Amendment practitioners and advocates should follow this litigation closely. A favorable ruling from Judge Hendrix would represent one of the most significant Second Amendment victories since **District of Columbia v. Heller**.
