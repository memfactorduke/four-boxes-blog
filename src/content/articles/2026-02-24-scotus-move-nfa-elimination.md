## Background

The National Firearms Act of 1934 was enacted under Congress's Article I taxing authority, a constitutional characterization confirmed by the Supreme Court in *Sonzinski v. United States*, 300 U.S. 506 (1937). The NFA's regulatory apparatus — including mandatory registration, transfer tax payment, fingerprinting, and background check submission — was structured as the administrative machinery necessary to collect and enforce the $200 transfer tax. Congress did not ground the NFA's regulatory requirements in the Commerce Clause or any other enumerated power; the taxing authority provided the sole constitutional hook for the entire statutory scheme.

In 2025, Congress enacted legislation through the budget reconciliation process — colloquially referred to as the "One Big Beautiful Bill" — that eliminated the $200 transfer tax on suppressors, short-barreled rifles, and short-barreled shotguns. Following enactment, the administration took the position in lower court proceedings that the elimination of the tax did not disturb the remaining regulatory requirements. In my analysis, that position raises a foundational constitutional question: once the taxing authority that justified the regulatory apparatus is removed, what enumerated power supports continued enforcement of requirements whose sole constitutional justification was the facilitation of tax collection?

Several Second Amendment litigation organizations filed suit challenging this position, and the Supreme Court's intervening decision in *Learning Resources v. Trump* has now provided courts with authoritative constitutional guidance on the relationship between the taxing power and the regulatory power.

## The Court's Holdings in *Learning Resources v. Trump*

*Learning Resources v. Trump* arose from a challenge to executive tariff authority under the International Emergency Economic Powers Act, presenting the Court with the question of whether the President possessed statutory authority to impose tariffs on imported goods. In resolving that question, the Court addressed the constitutional character of the taxing and regulatory powers — and its analysis carries significant implications beyond the trade law context.

### The Taxing Power and Regulatory Power Are Constitutionally Distinct

The Court grounded its analysis in the text of Article I, Section 8, observing that the taxing power and the commerce power are enumerated separately and serve distinct constitutional functions. The majority quoted Alexander Hamilton's characterization of the taxing power as "the most important of the authorities proposed to be conferred upon the Union" and as "the power to destroy" — language that underscores the independent, self-sufficient nature of fiscal authority as distinct from the power to prescribe conduct. The Court's analysis of tariffs illuminates this distinction:

> "The power to impose tariffs is very clearly a branch of the taxing power. A tariff after all is a tax levied on imported goods and services. And tariffs raise revenue, the defining feature of a tax."

Critically, the Court cited *Sonzinski v. United States* in the course of its analysis — a direct acknowledgment that the NFA itself has long been understood as an exercise of the taxing power, not the regulatory power.

### Taxation and Regulation Are "Different in Kind, Not Degree"

The more consequential holding for NFA litigation appears in the Court's treatment of the distinction between a tax and a regulation. Addressing the argument that tariffs could be understood as a subset of regulatory authority, the Court rejected that characterization in explicit terms:

> "The word regulate, as that term is ordinarily used, means to fix, establish or control, to adjust by rule, method or established mode, to direct by rule or restriction, to subject to governing principles or law. This definition captures much of what a government does on a day-to-day basis. But the facial breadth of 'to regulate' places in stark relief what the term is not usually thought to include: taxation."

The Court further observed: "The U.S. Code is replete with statutes granting the executive the authority to regulate someone or something. Yet the government cannot identify any statute in which the power to regulate includes the power to tax." The majority's conclusion was unambiguous: tariffs — and by extension, taxes generally — are "different in kind, not degree" from regulatory measures, falling "outside of the spectrum entirely" of regulatory authority.

As I have reviewed the opinion, this holding is not dicta; it is central to the Court's resolution of the statutory and constitutional question before it.

## Application to NFA Regulatory Requirements

The administration's post-repeal enforcement position rests on the implicit premise that the NFA's regulatory requirements possess an independent constitutional foundation that survives the elimination of the transfer tax. In my analysis, *Learning Resources* forecloses that argument.

The registration, fingerprinting, and background check requirements of the NFA were not enacted as independent exercises of the commerce power, the necessary and proper power, or any other enumerated authority. They were designed and enacted as the administrative infrastructure necessary to collect the $200 transfer tax and deter tax evasion. Congress's constitutional authority to impose those requirements derived entirely from its taxing power. As the Court has now held, the taxing power and the regulatory power are "different in kind" — a legislature that possesses authority to tax does not thereby possess authority to regulate, and vice versa.

This distinction is structurally decisive. If the authority to require registration, fingerprinting, and background checks arose exclusively from the taxing power, and if Congress has now eliminated the tax that conferred that authority, the regulatory requirements no longer rest on any identifiable constitutional foundation. The administration cannot invoke the commerce power as a substitute, because Congress made no findings of a commerce nexus when enacting the NFA and no such nexus was part of the statutory design. As I have argued, this is precisely the analytical framework that courts should apply to challenges currently working through the lower federal courts.

## Originalist Analysis

As I have examined this question from an originalist perspective, the Court's reasoning in *Learning Resources* reflects a commitment to the foundational principle that different constitutional powers enumerated in Article I, Section 8 have different scope, different purposes, and different limits. The power to "lay and collect Taxes, Duties, Imposts and Excises" is enumerated separately from the power to "regulate Commerce." An originalist reading of the constitutional text gives independent meaning to each enumeration and does not permit one power to be collapsed into another.

The administration's enforcement position effectively treats the NFA's regulatory apparatus as an independent regulatory regime that merely coexisted with the transfer tax. That characterization contradicts both the statutory history of the NFA and the Supreme Court's holding in *Sonzinski*, which characterized the Act as a revenue measure. An originalist analysis, faithful to the constitutional text and the Court's own historical characterization of the statute, compels the conclusion that the regulatory requirements fall with the tax.

## Implications

In my assessment, *Learning Resources v. Trump* provides Second Amendment litigants with an authoritative basis for arguing that the NFA's remaining regulatory requirements are constitutionally unsupported following the elimination of the transfer tax. Courts adjudicating these challenges will now confront an on-point Supreme Court holding that the taxing power and the regulatory power are categorically distinct — a holding that directly answers the administration's implicit argument that the regulatory apparatus can persist independently of the tax it was designed to administer.

The scope of this development should not be understated. The NFA's registration requirements, fingerprinting mandates, and background check provisions affect millions of Americans seeking to acquire suppressors, short-barreled rifles, and short-barreled shotguns. If lower courts apply *Learning Resources* as I believe the precedent requires, those requirements fall as applied to items for which the transfer tax has been repealed. The administration will likely seek further appellate review, and the question may ultimately return to the Supreme Court. However, the constitutional trajectory established by *Learning Resources* — and by the Court's originalist commitment to bounded, enumerated powers — points toward a conclusion that the regulatory structure cannot survive the elimination of its sole constitutional justification.
