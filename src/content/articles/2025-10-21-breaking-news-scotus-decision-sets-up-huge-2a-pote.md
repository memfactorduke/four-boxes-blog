## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has granted certiorari in **United States v. Hammani**, agreeing to decide whether 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3) — the federal statute that prohibits the possession of firearms by any person who is an unlawful user of or addicted to a controlled substance — is consistent with the Second Amendment. This is now the second Second Amendment case the Court has agreed to hear in the current term, following the earlier grant of certiorari in the Ninth Circuit's so-called "vampire rule" case, **Wolford v. Bonta**. In my analysis, while the Second Amendment challenge in **Hammani** will almost certainly fail on the outcome, the Court's reasoning and methodology will matter enormously for the future of firearms jurisprudence — and there are real opportunities within this case for the Court to advance the Second Amendment in significant ways.

## Background

The case involves Ali Daniel Hammani, a dual citizen of Pakistan and the United States, who was allegedly found in possession of a firearm while also in possession of marijuana and, reportedly, cocaine. Hammani has alleged connections to the Iranian Revolutionary Guard and has been indicted on federal charges. The United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit dismissed the indictment, holding that § 922(g)(3) — as applied to Hammani's circumstances — violated the Second Amendment. The government then sought, and the Supreme Court has now granted, certiorari to resolve the constitutional question.

## The Statute at Issue

Section 922(g)(3) of Title 18 makes it a federal crime for any person who "is an unlawful user of or addicted to any controlled substance" to possess a firearm. The prohibition is not permanent. If an individual ceases to be an unlawful user of controlled substances and is no longer addicted, the statutory disability is lifted and Second Amendment rights are restored. The controlled substances at issue encompass marijuana, cocaine, heroin, and a range of other federally prohibited drugs.

This structure is consistent with what the Supreme Court recognized in **United States v. Rahimi** — that temporary disarmament of individuals found to pose a physical danger to themselves or others, following due process, can survive Second Amendment scrutiny. Unlike the domestic violence restraining order context addressed in **Rahimi**, however, § 922(g)(3) imposes a categorical prohibition based on drug use status rather than an individualized judicial determination of dangerousness.

## Why the Second Amendment Claim Will Fail

In my assessment, the probability that the Supreme Court will invalidate § 922(g)(3) on its face is essentially zero. There are not five votes on this Court — nor could there plausibly be — for the proposition that individuals who are regularly and unlawfully using controlled substances such as cocaine, heroin, or marijuana retain an unfettered constitutional right to possess firearms during that period of active drug use. That outcome would be untenable as a matter of history, tradition, and the basic logic of the Second Amendment itself.

The historical tradition test established in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** requires courts to determine whether a challenged firearms regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States. As I see it, there is substantial historical support for the proposition that the founding era recognized the legitimacy of disarming individuals who were not considered "law-abiding, responsible citizens" — a category that historically included intoxicated persons and those regarded as a present threat to public order. The government's brief will rely heavily on these historical analogues.

Separately, I have publicly expressed concern — at events hosted by the Second Amendment Foundation and the Gun Owners of America — that the Trump Department of Justice sought certiorari in this case. With other, more favorable Second Amendment cases available for the Court's attention — including challenges to assault weapons bans out of Chicago's Cook County, young adult firearms prohibition cases, and the Vermont case — § 922(g)(3) was not the vehicle I would have chosen to advance Second Amendment rights at the Supreme Court level. Nevertheless, the government made its strategic decision, the circuit split on this question made cert virtually inevitable once the Solicitor General sought it, and we must now work within the resulting landscape.

## Why the Reasoning Matters More Than the Outcome

In my view, the most consequential dimension of **Hammani** is not who wins the case but how the Court explains its ruling. Every Supreme Court opinion contains two critical components: the judgment — who wins and who loses — and the rationale, the legal reasoning and methodology that will govern future cases. In a case where the outcome is nearly predetermined, the methodology is everything.

There are two principal ways the Court could uphold § 922(g)(3), and the choice between them will significantly affect the trajectory of Second Amendment law.

The first approach would be narrow and case-specific: the Court upholds the statute as applied to Hammani given the particularly compelling facts — alleged ties to a foreign terrorist organization, possession of multiple controlled substances, and a criminal indictment. This approach would offer little guidance to lower courts in future § 922(g)(3) cases involving more sympathetic defendants, such as state-legal marijuana users in states that have decriminalized or legalized cannabis.

The second approach — and the one the government is likely pushing — would involve a broader doctrinal statement about the permissibility of disarming individuals who are actively engaged in unlawful drug use. The concern here, from a Second Amendment perspective, is that an overly broad ruling could introduce analytical flexibility that weakens the **Bruen** framework in ways that could be exploited in future gun control litigation.

## The Potential Silver Lining

Despite my reservations about the timing and vehicle, I acknowledge that there are genuine opportunities for the Second Amendment in **Hammani**, even in a case the Court is likely to decide against the defendant. The Court's opinion may:

Reaffirm that the **Bruen** historical tradition test is the exclusive governing methodology for Second Amendment challenges, foreclosing any revival of the interest-balancing approaches that circuit courts employed prior to 2022.

Clarify the precise scope of the "law-abiding, responsible citizen" category — specifically, that the Second Amendment's core protections apply to the full universe of law-abiding Americans, not merely a government-defined subset.

Emphasize the temporary and contingent nature of § 922(g)(3) — that the disarmament applies only during the period of active drug use and ceases upon cessation — in a manner that may limit the government's ability to argue for more permanent categorical disarmament of other disfavored groups.

Provide important guidance on as-applied challenges, potentially signaling that a defendant who is merely a casual or infrequent marijuana user — as opposed to a habitual user or addict — might succeed on an as-applied challenge to the statute.

## Implications

**United States v. Hammani** arrives at the Supreme Court as a case that the Second Amendment community did not choose and, in my view, did not need at this moment. The outcome — upholding § 922(g)(3) — is almost certainly foreordained. But the reasoning, the language, and the doctrinal architecture the Court constructs to support that outcome will shape Second Amendment litigation for years to come. The question for those of us who care deeply about the right to keep and bear arms is not whether we will win **Hammani**, but whether we can emerge from it with a methodological framework that continues to protect and advance Second Amendment rights in the cases that truly matter.

I will continue to monitor briefing in this case and will provide analysis of the oral arguments and the ultimate decision as they unfold.
