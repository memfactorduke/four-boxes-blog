## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has issued a landmark 6-3 ruling holding that lower federal courts may not enjoin the executive branch's immigration enforcement operations in the manner attempted by federal district courts and the Ninth Circuit. In the specific matter before the Court, district courts in California had issued injunctions purporting to prohibit ICE agents from conducting brief investigatory stops of individuals whom agents had reasonable suspicion to believe might be present in the United States unlawfully. The Supreme Court stayed and effectively reversed those lower court orders, reaffirming the executive branch's statutory and constitutional authority to enforce federal immigration law. Justice Brett Kavanaugh wrote a significant concurrence elaborating on the legal framework governing immigration stops.

## Background

The controversy arose from the Trump administration's immigration enforcement operations in the Los Angeles metropolitan area and elsewhere in California. ICE agents, operating under the direction of the Department of Homeland Security, conducted operations at locations including Home Depot parking lots and similar venues — places where individuals seeking day labor frequently congregate — and made brief investigatory stops of individuals whom agents had reasonable suspicion to believe might be unlawfully present in the United States.

These stops were not arrests. They were brief investigatory detentions, during which agents would ask a small number of questions to ascertain whether the individual was a United States citizen or was otherwise lawfully present. If the individual produced satisfactory documentation or otherwise demonstrated lawful presence, the encounter ended. If the individual was found to be unlawfully present, a further enforcement action might follow.

Federal plaintiffs — including advocacy organizations — challenged these operations in federal district court in California, arguing that the stops violated the Fourth Amendment's prohibition on unreasonable searches and seizures, and sought a preliminary injunction prohibiting ICE from conducting them. A federal district court judge granted the injunction. On appeal, the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit affirmed, holding that the district court had not abused its discretion in enjoining the stops.

The Trump administration then sought an emergency stay of the Ninth Circuit's decision from the Supreme Court.

## The Supreme Court's Ruling

The Supreme Court, in a 6-3 decision, granted the stay — effectively halting the lower courts' injunctions and permitting ICE to resume its enforcement operations. The Court held that the federal government had demonstrated the likelihood of success on the merits and that the balance of equities favored allowing the enforcement operations to proceed pending further litigation.

The majority's analysis rested on the straightforward proposition that the federal government possesses plenary authority, under both federal statute and the Constitution, to enforce the nation's immigration laws. The Immigration and Nationality Act specifically authorizes immigration officers to interrogate any alien or person believed to be an alien as to his right to be or to remain in the United States. This statutory authority, the Court found, encompasses the brief investigatory stops at issue — stops based on reasonable suspicion that an individual might be unlawfully present.

The Court further held that a lower court may not enjoin the executive branch from exercising its enforcement authority before any violation has occurred. If an individual federal agent acts improperly in a specific encounter — for example, by conducting a search or seizure without adequate legal justification — that individual may be held accountable in subsequent proceedings. But a prospective, system-wide injunction prohibiting the entire federal immigration enforcement apparatus from conducting reasonable-suspicion stops is categorically different and constitutionally impermissible.

## Justice Kavanaugh's Concurrence

Justice Kavanaugh wrote separately to articulate the standing and Fourth Amendment analysis in greater detail. His concurrence begins with the standing issue: the plaintiffs who brought the lawsuit lacked standing to seek injunctive relief because they could not demonstrate an imminent, concrete risk that they personally would be subjected to an unlawful stop. The speculative possibility that a lawfully present person might be briefly questioned by an ICE agent does not constitute the kind of impending harm that supports injunctive relief under established Article III doctrine.

On the Fourth Amendment merits, Justice Kavanaugh's concurrence lays out the governing legal framework with precision. The Fourth Amendment's protection against unreasonable searches and seizures does not prohibit brief investigatory stops where officers have reasonable suspicion, based on specific articulable facts, that the person being questioned is engaged in unlawful activity or, in the immigration context, is an alien unlawfully present in the United States.

Justice Kavanaugh quoted directly from the applicable statutory text: "Immigration officers may briefly detain an individual for questioning if they have a reasonable suspicion based on specific articulable facts that the person being questioned is an alien illegally in the United States. The reasonable suspicion inquiry turns on the totality of the circumstances."

Importantly, the concurrence notes the factual context that makes reasonable suspicion for immigration enforcement readily understandable in the Los Angeles area specifically: the government estimates that there are at least fifteen million people unlawfully present in the United States, and that illegal immigration is especially pronounced in the Los Angeles area, where approximately two million of the twenty million total population may be unlawfully present. In this environment, an immigration officer who encounters individuals exhibiting behavioral, documentary, or other contextual indicators of unlawful presence has ample basis for the kind of brief, minimally intrusive stop that the injunction purported to prohibit.

Justice Kavanaugh analogized immigration stops to ordinary traffic stops: just as a police officer may briefly stop a motorist who the officer has reasonable suspicion has violated traffic law, an immigration officer may briefly stop an individual who the officer has reasonable suspicion may be unlawfully present. Neither encounter involves probable cause. Both are constitutionally permissible under the Fourth Amendment's reasonableness standard as articulated in **Terry v. Ohio** and its progeny.

## The Dissent

The three liberal justices — Sotomayor, Kagan, and Jackson — issued a dissent taking strong issue with the majority's analysis. The dissent characterized the government's enforcement operations as constitutionally problematic and argued that the lower courts had acted within their authority in issuing injunctive relief. I find the dissent unpersuasive as a matter of constitutional law, for reasons the majority adequately addresses, but its existence confirms that these questions are genuinely contested and that the Court's 6-3 alignment on immigration enforcement questions reflects a fundamental division in constitutional interpretation.

## Implications

This decision has significance on two levels. At the immediate practical level, it restores the federal government's ability to conduct the immigration enforcement operations that the Ninth Circuit and district courts had purported to prohibit. The administration will be able to enforce federal immigration law in California and elsewhere without being subjected to the kind of prospective, categorical injunctive relief that the lower courts had imposed.

At the broader constitutional level, the decision reaffirms the principle of executive branch supremacy in the conduct of immigration enforcement. The executive branch, under Article II of the Constitution, is charged with faithfully executing federal law, including the immigration laws. Federal courts — including district courts in California and the Ninth Circuit — may not substitute their policy preferences for the executive's enforcement decisions by issuing injunctions that effectively prevent the government from enforcing the law at all. Retroactive judicial review of specific enforcement actions remains available; prophylactic injunctions against entire categories of lawful enforcement activity do not.

For those of us who follow Second Amendment law, this ruling has an important parallel significance. The same constitutional principles that protect the executive branch's authority to enforce immigration law also protect its authority to decline enforcement of unconstitutional firearms regulations, to take pro-Second Amendment positions in litigation, and to direct federal resources toward the vindication of constitutional rights rather than their suppression. A strong executive branch, operating within constitutional limits, is essential to the protection of the Second Amendment and all other individual rights.
