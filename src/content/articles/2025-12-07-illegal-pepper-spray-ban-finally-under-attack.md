## Summary

A powerful new Second Amendment lawsuit has been filed in the United States District Court for the Southern District of California. Byrna Technologies, Inc. — a publicly traded company specializing in non-lethal defensive arms, also known as pepper projectile launchers — has sued, alongside several individual plaintiffs, challenging California's categorical ban on the possession and sale of these devices. The complaint is excellent, the legal theory is sound, and this case has strong potential to generate favorable Second Amendment precedent under **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**. I have reviewed the complaint carefully and want to analyze its strengths and one area where I would suggest a refinement as the litigation proceeds.

---

*The following analysis is based on my review of the complaint filed in* Byrna Technologies v. California *in the United States District Court for the Southern District of California, December 2025.*

## Background

I have long maintained on this channel that the Second Amendment's protection extends to the full range of arms that Americans commonly use for self-defense — including non-lethal and less-lethal alternatives to conventional firearms. This view is not merely a policy preference; it is the conclusion demanded by the text of the Second Amendment, the Supreme Court's precedents in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, **McDonald v. City of Chicago**, **Caetano v. Massachusetts**, and **Bruen**, and the historical tradition of American arms regulation.

The Supreme Court's unanimous decision in **Caetano v. Massachusetts** (2016) is particularly relevant here. In **Caetano**, the Court summarily reversed a Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court decision that had held stun guns were not protected by the Second Amendment. The Supreme Court made clear that the Second Amendment's protection extends to arms that are "in common use" for lawful purposes, and that the Second Amendment is not limited to weapons that existed at the time of ratification. Stun guns are, without question, "arms" protected by the Second Amendment, even though they are non-lethal. The logic of **Caetano** extends directly to pepper projectile launchers.

## What Byrna Technologies Produces

Byrna Technologies is a publicly traded company that manufactures and sells non-lethal defensive projectile launchers. These devices fire projectiles containing pepper compound — a chemical irritant that temporarily incapacitates attackers without inflicting lethal force. They are designed and marketed specifically as a self-defense alternative for individuals who, for various reasons, prefer not to use or cannot use conventional firearms.

I view these devices as what might fairly be called "gateway" defensive arms — not in a pejorative sense, but in the sense that they provide meaningful self-defense capability for individuals who are fearful of or unfamiliar with conventional firearms, or who cannot legally possess firearms for various reasons. A person who begins with a Byrna launcher for home or personal defense may eventually become comfortable with conventional firearms; or they may continue to rely on the Byrna as their primary defensive tool. Either outcome is consistent with the individual's constitutional right to self-defense.

California has criminalized both the possession and the sale of these devices. The state's ban is categorical and admits no exceptions for law-abiding citizens seeking to exercise their right to self-defense.

## The Legal Theory: Arms, Common Use, and the Bruen Framework

The complaint's introduction elegantly captures the core constitutional argument:

> "The Second Amendment protects law-abiding citizens' rights to possess and, as an incident to [possession], acquire arms for lawful purposes, including self-defense. Plaintiffs bring this lawsuit to challenge California's ban on the possession and sale of pepper projectile launchers. Pepper projectile launchers are a commonly used arm that many Americans choose as a non-lethal alternative to firearms. California does not just deny citizens this choice. It criminalizes the act of selling or possessing such an arm. This inexplicable ban violates the Second Amendment."

The complaint correctly cites **Caetano v. Massachusetts** for the proposition that non-lethal stun guns are protected "arms" under the Second Amendment, and then argues that pepper projectile launchers are "similarly protected."

Under the **Bruen** framework, the analysis proceeds as follows: the threshold question is whether the challenged device — here, the pepper projectile launcher — is an "arm" that falls within the plain text of the Second Amendment. If it is, then the regulation is presumptively unconstitutional, and the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that the regulation is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation.

**Step One: Is a pepper projectile launcher an "arm"?** Under **Heller** and **Caetano**, "arms" include "weapons of offence, or armour of defence," and the category is not limited to lethal weapons. **Caetano** confirmed that non-lethal weapons used for self-defense fall within the Second Amendment's protection. There is no principled basis for treating a pepper projectile launcher differently from a stun gun for purposes of the Second Amendment's textual scope.

**Step Two: Is this device in common use?** The **Heller** Court held that arms that are "in common use at the time" for lawful purposes are protected. Byrna Technologies' devices are commercially manufactured, publicly sold (in most states), and owned by well over a million Americans. They are not rare, exotic, or newly invented weapons. They are commonplace defensive tools.

**Step Three: Are they "dangerous and unusual"?** The one historical category of arms that **Heller** identified as unprotected is "dangerous and unusual weapons." Pepper projectile launchers are explicitly *non-lethal*. They are, by design and operation, less dangerous than virtually any conventional firearm. And as noted above, they are owned by well over a million Americans — meaning they cannot, by any sensible definition, be considered "unusual." California cannot satisfy either prong of the "dangerous and unusual" standard.

## A Refinement Worth Considering

I want to flag one area where the complaint's framing, while not wrong, might benefit from refinement as the litigation proceeds. The complaint relies on the "dangerous and unusual" standard as though it is the primary constitutional test that the *plaintiff* must satisfy — i.e., that the plaintiff must demonstrate the weapon is *not* dangerous and unusual.

That framing is technically correct under **Heller**, but under **Bruen**, the better formulation — and the one that more precisely allocates the constitutional burden — is to note that the government bears the burden of demonstrating that pepper projectile launchers fall within the historical tradition of weapons that governments have permissibly regulated or excluded from Second Amendment protection. California's ban on a non-lethal defensive arm is almost certainly outside any cognizable historical tradition. The government cannot point to a single historical analogue from the founding era or even from the late 19th century banning the civilian possession of non-lethal defensive devices.

This is a minor refinement, not a fatal flaw. The complaint is well constructed and makes excellent points. Plaintiffs should simply ensure they are consistently leveraging the full **Bruen** burden-shifting framework as the case develops.

## Why This Case Is Important

This litigation is important for reasons beyond the specific issue of pepper projectile launchers. If the Ninth Circuit — which covers California and other anti-gun states — is compelled to rule that a state cannot ban a non-lethal self-defense arm that is in common use, that ruling will have significant implications for future challenges to bans on a wide range of defensive tools.

More broadly, the case challenges one of California's most transparently illogical gun-control positions: banning a weapon that is *less dangerous* than the firearms the state purportedly tolerates. If California cannot sustain a ban on a device that fires pepper compound, it becomes significantly harder for the state to defend bans on other defensive arms.

## Implications

This lawsuit should be a victory for the plaintiffs. The legal theory is sound, the **Caetano** precedent is directly on point, the **Bruen** framework strongly favors the challengers, and the practical absurdity of California banning a non-lethal defensive arm while claiming to permit conventional firearm ownership makes the state's position difficult to defend on any principled basis.

I will be following this case closely as it proceeds through the Southern District of California and, ultimately, through the Ninth Circuit. A favorable ruling at either level would be an important contribution to the development of Second Amendment law governing the protection of non-lethal defensive arms.
