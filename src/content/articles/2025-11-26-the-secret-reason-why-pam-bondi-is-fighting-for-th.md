## Summary

Following the enactment of the "One Big Beautiful Bill," which eliminated the $200 transfer tax on suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns, a legal challenge arose in the United States District Court for the Southern District of Texas asserting that the NFA's remaining registration and fingerprinting requirements are now constitutionally unsupportable. The Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi, filed a brief defending those requirements — a position that surprised many Second Amendment advocates. After careful analysis of the brief and the broader pattern of this administration's litigation conduct, I believe there is a plausible and encouraging strategic explanation for the DOJ's posture, one rooted in a litigation doctrine that has become known as "pulling a Windsor."

## Background

The National Firearms Act of 1934 imposes registration requirements, a $200 transfer tax, and fingerprinting obligations on persons seeking to acquire certain regulated items, including suppressors (silencers), short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns. For decades, this framework was justified as an exercise of Congress's Article I taxing power. The $200 tax, collected at each transfer, served as the constitutional peg on which the entire regulatory scheme was hung.

The One Big Beautiful Bill, signed into law by President Donald J. Trump, eliminated that $200 transfer tax on suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns. The consequence of that legislative change is now being litigated in **Silencer Shop Foundation v. BATF**, pending in the United States District Court for the Southern District of Texas. The plaintiffs in that case argue, correctly in my view, that once Congress removed the taxing peg, the remainder of the NFA's registration and fingerprinting requirements — as applied to those items — loses its Article I constitutional foundation and is therefore unconstitutional as applied.

## The DOJ Brief and Its Weaknesses

The Trump-Bondi Department of Justice submitted a brief in the Southern District of Texas defending the continued enforceability of the NFA's registration and fingerprinting requirements even in the absence of the $200 tax. Having analyzed that brief extensively, I find the legal arguments advanced therein to be deficient on multiple grounds.

First, the brief's Article I analysis is inadequate. With the taxing power justification removed by Congress's own act, the government would need to establish an independent Commerce Clause basis for mandating the registration and fingerprinting of private firearms transfers. The brief's Commerce Clause analysis does not satisfactorily make that case, particularly in light of the Supreme Court's post-*Lopez* limitations on Congress's commerce power.

Second, the brief's Second Amendment analysis is weak. Under **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), any firearms regulation must be consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. The government's brief fails to identify adequate historical analogues that would support maintaining a national registry of lawfully owned firearms in the absence of a revenue-generating tax.

Third, the assertion by some DOJ officials — that challengers who dislike the NFA should simply return to Congress rather than pursue judicial review — is constitutionally untenable. Since **Marbury v. Madison**, 5 U.S. (1 Cranch) 137 (1803), the federal judiciary has possessed the power to strike down legislation that conflicts with the Constitution. That power exists precisely because Congress, like all political actors, can err. The Second Amendment community did return to Congress — and Congress acted, removing the tax. The logical consequence of that legislative act is now properly before the courts.

## The "Windsor" Theory: A Possible Strategic Explanation

Having acknowledged the brief's legal deficiencies, I want to offer what I believe is a plausible strategic explanation for the DOJ's conduct — one that I find distinctly encouraging rather than alarming.

The doctrine I am calling "pulling a Windsor" derives from the Obama administration's handling of the Defense of Marriage Act in **United States v. Windsor**, 570 U.S. 744 (2013). In that case, the DOJ declined to defend DOMA's constitutionality on the merits while nonetheless continuing to enforce the law and defend it in litigation for a specific and technical procedural reason: to preserve an Article III case or controversy.

Under Article III of the Constitution, federal courts may only adjudicate genuine cases or controversies. If the executive branch simply declines to enforce a statute and refuses to appear in court to defend it, there may be no adverse party and therefore no justiciable controversy. The case could be dismissed for lack of standing or mootness, which would mean the court could never definitively invalidate the statute.

By defending the NFA at the trial court level — even with arguments I consider substantively weak — the DOJ ensures that there remains a live case and controversy that can travel up the appellate ladder to the Fifth Circuit and, potentially, to the United States Supreme Court. This is not merely plausible; it is precisely the mechanism by which significant constitutional precedent gets made.

## The Broader Pattern of Pro-Second Amendment Conduct

This strategic reading of the DOJ's NFA brief does not require me to abandon skepticism entirely. Rather, it should be evaluated against the administration's broader litigation record on Second Amendment issues, which has been substantially favorable.

The Trump-Bondi DOJ declined to seek certiorari in the Fifth Circuit's *Reese* case, which held that young adults have a constitutional right to keep and bear arms. It similarly declined to challenge the Third Circuit's *Range* ruling permitting as-applied challenges to federal firearm disabilities by non-violent felons. It has declined to enforce Washington D.C.'s large-capacity magazine ban. It has filed amicus briefs supporting the Second Amendment in the **Barnett** case challenging Illinois's assault weapons and large-capacity magazine ban, in the *Cheeseman* case in the Third Circuit, and in the **Wolford v. Lopez** case before the United States Supreme Court. The Civil Rights Division of the DOJ has also just created the Second Amendment Rights Section — a dedicated unit charged with investigating and challenging state and local laws that infringe upon the right to keep and bear arms.

This pattern suggests an administration that is broadly and consistently pro-Second Amendment. The NFA brief in the Southern District of Texas may therefore represent not a betrayal of that posture but a calculated procedural move designed to ensure that the constitutional question presented ultimately reaches the Supreme Court in a properly postured case.

## Implications

I maintain that the substantive legal arguments in the DOJ's NFA brief are incorrect and that the registration and fingerprinting requirements, once stripped of their taxing power justification, are unconstitutional as applied. I am disappointed that the brief was filed in its current form and believe it required closer attention from senior DOJ leadership before submission.

At the same time, the "Windsor" framework offers a charitable and plausible reading of what may be a deliberate litigation strategy. If the DOJ is defending the NFA at the trial court level precisely to preserve the constitutional question for Supreme Court resolution, then the weakness of the brief's arguments may ultimately serve the Second Amendment community better than a strong defense — because weak arguments from the government tend to produce favorable precedent for the challenger.

The Second Amendment community should continue to press this challenge vigorously. The removal of the $200 transfer tax by the One Big Beautiful Bill created a constitutional moment that should not be squandered. Whether the DOJ is engaged in sophisticated litigation strategy or has simply filed an inadequate brief, the courts remain open and the constitutional arguments in favor of the challengers are strong.
