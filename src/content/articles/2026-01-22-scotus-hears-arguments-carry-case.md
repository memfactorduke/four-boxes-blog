## Summary

The Supreme Court of the United States has heard oral argument in **Wolford v. Lopez**, the case presenting the question whether Hawaii's presumptive prohibition on the carry of licensed handguns onto private property open to the public — absent express permission from each individual property owner — violates the Second Amendment. Based on a careful reading of the oral argument transcript, I assess that the Second Amendment will prevail in this case by a margin of six to three. More consequential than the outcome, however, is the question of how the majority opinion will reason its way to that result — the methodology and rationale laid out in the opinion will govern Second Amendment litigation in the lower courts for years to come, and it is the shape of that reasoning, not merely the disposition, that demands the closest scrutiny.

## Background: The Question Presented

The certified question in *Wolford v. Lopez* asks whether the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit erred in holding that the State of Hawaii may presumptively prohibit the carrying of handguns by licensed concealed carry permit holders on private property open to the public, unless the property owner affirmatively and expressly grants prior permission to each individual carry holder.

The practical operation of Hawaii's law is worth dwelling upon, because the absurdity of its application illuminates the constitutional defect. A licensed carrier who drives to a gas station, grocery store, or restaurant in Hawaii is in technical violation of state law from the moment they enter the property — unless they have somehow obtained advance permission from the property owner or manager of that specific establishment. The identities and contact information of property owners are not posted at the entrance of commercial establishments, nor is there any mechanism by which a carry holder can efficiently obtain blanket permission across the dozens of privately owned but publicly accessible spaces they encounter in the course of an ordinary day. Hawaii's law thus creates a legal regime in which the right to carry, though nominally recognized, is practically unavailable.

The Ninth Circuit, consistent with its historical posture on Second Amendment claims, upheld the law. The Supreme Court granted certiorari, and oral argument has now occurred.

## Two Dimensions of Every Supreme Court Decision

Before addressing the substance of the oral argument, I wish to articulate a framework that I have advanced consistently in prior analysis and that bears directly on how one ought to assess the significance of *Wolford v. Lopez*.

Every Supreme Court decision has two distinct dimensions that Second Amendment advocates must attend to separately.

The first dimension is the outcome — which party wins the case. In *Wolford*, I am confident the Second Amendment will prevail. The outcome for the named plaintiffs, and by extension for licensed carry holders throughout Hawaii and the Ninth Circuit, will be a victory. That is necessary and important, but it is not sufficient.

The second dimension — and in my assessment the more consequential one for the long-term trajectory of Second Amendment jurisprudence — is the reasoning and methodology set forth in the majority opinion. It is that reasoning, not the specific disposition, that lower courts will cite in hundreds of future cases. A broad ruling that firmly establishes the default presumption in favor of carry in public spaces, draws clear limits on the sensitive places exception, and explicitly repudiates the use of racially discriminatory post-Civil War statutes as historical analogues would have enormous downstream effects across every federal circuit. A narrow ruling that merely disposes of the specific Hawaii law on its particular facts, without providing clear methodological guidance, would be a far lesser victory — even if the outcome for the named plaintiff is identical.

The question that matters most in *Wolford* is therefore not whether Hawaii loses. It will. The question is how the Court explains *why* Hawaii loses, and whether that explanation is written with sufficient breadth and clarity to guide the lower courts toward fidelity to *Bruen*'s demanding standard.

## Chief Justice Roberts and the Default Presumption

The most instructive exchange of the oral argument, in my assessment, involved Chief Justice John Roberts, who directed Hawaii's counsel to a foundational question about the direction of the constitutional presumption at issue.

Chief Justice Roberts posited the scenario of a political candidate canvassing door-to-door — walking up the driveway of a private residence, approaching the front door, and distributing campaign literature. Under First Amendment doctrine, this conduct is constitutionally protected; it proceeds by right unless the property owner specifically withdraws permission by instructing the canvasser to leave. The law does not require a political candidate to first identify and contact each homeowner, obtain advance authorization, and produce documentation of that authorization before approaching the front door. The exercise of the First Amendment right is the default; exclusion from it must be affirmatively imposed by the property owner.

Chief Justice Roberts's challenge to Hawaii was direct: if we would not require a citizen exercising a First Amendment right on private property to obtain advance permission before doing so — and that private property is not even open to the general public — how on earth would we sustain a law requiring a citizen exercising a Second Amendment right to obtain advance permission before entering property that is *affirmatively open* to the public? The asymmetry is legally indefensible, and the Chief Justice's pointed articulation of it reveals an understanding that Hawaii's consent-default regime inverts the constitutional presumption that should govern the exercise of any enumerated right.

That Chief Justice Roberts — who does not always align with the Court's most expansive Second Amendment readings — was "hot to trot," as the colloquial expression goes, on this point, is a significant signal. It suggests that even those justices who might otherwise be inclined toward doctrinal caution found Hawaii's position constitutionally untenable on first principles.

## Justice Gorsuch and the Rejection of Black Codes

The second most consequential exchange at oral argument involved Justice Neil Gorsuch and the question of what historical analogues Hawaii could offer in support of its statute under *Bruen*'s text-and-history framework.

Hawaii presented the Court with two categories of purported historical analogues. The first consisted of founding-era anti-poaching laws — statutes that prohibited the discharge of firearms on privately owned improved land, enacted to prevent trespassing hunters from interfering with private agricultural and commercial activity. These laws bear no meaningful resemblance to Hawaii's modern consent-default regime: they restricted *hunting* on improved private land, not the carry of arms by licensed individuals entering commercial establishments open to the public. I do not believe the Court will find this category of laws analogous in either justification or burden, as *Bruen* requires.

The second category is more troubling in its legal genealogy and more revealing about the poverty of Hawaii's historical case. Hawaii and its supporting amici pointed to a Louisiana statute enacted in 1865 — at the end of the Civil War, before Louisiana had been readmitted to the Union — that restricted the carrying of firearms onto private property. That statute is a Black Code. It was enacted by the unreconstructed Louisiana legislature for the express purpose of disarming the newly freed African American population and denying them the practical ability to hunt, provide for themselves, and exercise the rights of free citizens. Its purpose was to replicate, through legal disarmament, the functional dependency that chattel slavery had previously imposed through ownership.

Justice Gorsuch pressed Hawaii's counsel directly on this point. His inquiry captured the fundamental problem with using Black Code statutes as historical analogues under *Bruen*: a law enacted in one state, designed to target a distinct minority of the American population for the purpose of suppressing their constitutional rights, cannot by definition constitute evidence of a "long-standing national tradition of firearms regulation." It is, at most, evidence of a single jurisdiction's unconstitutional practice of targeted disarmament. As Justice Brett Kavanaugh noted in *United States v. Rahimi*, the Constitution has left such laws behind; they do not represent a tradition that modern firearms regulation may draw upon for support.

In my assessment, Justice Gorsuch will write substantially on this issue in the majority opinion or a concurrence. The rejection of Black Codes as historical analogues — not merely in *Wolford* but as a general doctrinal matter — would be among the most significant contributions this decision could make to the long-term coherence of Second Amendment jurisprudence.

## Justice Jackson's Counter-Argument

A notable feature of the oral argument was an exchange involving Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson, who advanced an argument in support of the continued use of Black Code statutes as historical evidence. Her argument, as I understand it, is essentially that by rejecting these laws as permissible historical analogues, the Court is selectively discarding portions of the historical record — and that this selective discarding reveals a flaw in *Bruen*'s text-and-history methodology itself.

I submit that this argument, though internally consistent, is unpersuasive and ought to be squarely repudiated by the majority. The *Bruen* framework does not require the Court to treat every historical statute as equally valid evidence of a constitutional tradition, regardless of the circumstances of its enactment or the purposes it served. Laws enacted for the express purpose of violating the constitutional rights of a minority population represent a tradition of constitutional transgression, not a tradition of constitutional permission. Excluding them from the historical record is not selective — it is precisely what fidelity to the Constitution demands.

That the argument was advanced by the only Black woman on the Court, with respect to laws specifically designed to disarm freed Black Americans, renders the exchange historically poignant. But the analytical error remains regardless of who advances it.

## What the Opinion Must Accomplish

*Wolford v. Lopez* presents the Court with an opportunity to accomplish several specific doctrinal tasks that would materially strengthen Second Amendment jurisprudence going forward.

First, the majority opinion should establish, clearly and broadly, that the default presumption under the Second Amendment runs in favor of the right-holder. Law-abiding citizens with valid carry permits may carry in ordinary public spaces — including privately owned spaces open to the public — until specifically excluded. The burden runs the other way: those who would restrict the exercise of the right must affirmatively justify the restriction, not the other way around. Hawaii's inversion of this presumption should be rejected as a general constitutional matter, not merely as applied to this specific law.

Second, the opinion should address the scope of the sensitive places exception, drawing a clear line between historically grounded exclusions — legislative assemblies, courthouses, polling places — and categorical prohibitions on carry in ordinary commercial spaces. The former have a genuine historical pedigree. The latter do not.

Third, the opinion should explicitly address, and reject, Black Codes and Reconstruction-era statutes enacted by unreconstructed Confederate governments as permissible historical analogues under *Bruen*. This rejection would provide clear guidance to lower courts that have, in some cases, relied on precisely these sources to sustain restrictive firearms regulations.

## Implications

The oral argument in *Wolford v. Lopez* provides substantial basis for confidence that the Second Amendment will prevail. Chief Justice Roberts's articulation of the default presumption problem, Justice Gorsuch's sustained engagement with the Black Code question, and the apparent skepticism of the Court's six-justice conservative majority toward Hawaii's historical case all point toward a favorable outcome.

Oral argument attorney Alan Beck, arguing his first case before the Supreme Court, acquitted himself commendably, as did Sarah Harris arguing on behalf of the Trump administration through the Solicitor General's office in support of the Second Amendment position. The Trump administration's engagement in *Wolford* on the side of the Second Amendment is itself a consequential institutional signal.

The decision is expected by the end of the current term, most likely in June. I remain of the view that the outcome will be a six-to-three Second Amendment victory. The greater question — the one that will determine how much lasting doctrinal good this decision achieves — is whether the majority opinion embraces the broadest and most clearly reasoned path to that result. I am cautiously optimistic that it will.
