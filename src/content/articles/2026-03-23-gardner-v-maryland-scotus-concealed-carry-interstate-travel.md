## Summary

The law firm Cooper & Kirk has filed a reply brief on behalf of petitioner Eva Marie Gardner in *Gardner v. Maryland*, urging the United States Supreme Court to grant certiorari in a case that could establish whether the Second Amendment protects the right of licensed concealed carry holders to bear arms while traveling through states where they lack a local permit. As I've analyzed, the brief argues that Maryland's conviction of Gardner -- a Virginia concealed carry licensee who was arrested while traveling through Maryland en route to Pennsylvania -- is irreconcilable with the Supreme Court's precedent in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen* and unsupported by any historical tradition of restricting armed interstate travel.

## Background

Eva Marie Gardner, a Virginia resident holding a valid Virginia concealed carry permit, was traveling by car through Maryland on her way to visit her mother in Pennsylvania. During the trip, another driver forced her off the road in what she perceived to be a police-type maneuver. When that driver exited his vehicle and approached Gardner in a manner she found threatening, she displayed the handgun she was lawfully licensed to carry in Virginia -- a common, non-lethal self-defense measure. Maryland authorities subsequently arrested and convicted Gardner not for any inappropriate conduct during the encounter, but solely for possessing a firearm without a Maryland concealed carry permit.

Gardner initially sought certiorari from the Supreme Court on a pro se basis. At least one justice found the petition sufficiently meritorious to direct the state of Maryland to file a response -- a procedural signal that the Court is taking the case seriously. Cooper & Kirk, one of the nation's leading Second Amendment litigation firms, has now entered an appearance on Gardner's behalf and filed the reply brief at issue.

## The Cooper & Kirk Brief

### A Compelling Fact Pattern

The reply brief opens with a striking characterization of the case. It describes the history of Gardner's prosecution as reading "like a how-to guide on the way to flout the right to bear arms and this Supreme Court['s] Second Amendment precedent as thoroughly as possible." The brief frames Gardner's conduct -- carrying a firearm for self-protection while traveling and displaying it when confronted by a threatening stranger -- as falling squarely within both the Second Amendment's plain text and America's tradition of legitimate armed self-defense.

The factual parallels to *Caetano v. Massachusetts* (2016) are notable. In that case, Jaime Caetano had been convicted under Massachusetts law for possessing a stun gun she used to fend off a threatening ex-boyfriend. The Supreme Court issued a per curiam opinion effectively directing the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court to reconsider the conviction under a proper Second Amendment analysis. The Massachusetts court ultimately dismissed the case. Gardner's situation presents a similarly sympathetic set of facts: a law-abiding individual using a weapon for precisely the purpose the Second Amendment was designed to protect, only to face criminal prosecution for doing so.

### Maryland's May-Issue Problem

Perhaps the most devastating argument in the brief concerns the timing of Gardner's arrest relative to the Supreme Court's 2022 decision in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*. Gardner's arrest and conviction occurred before *Bruen* was decided. At that time, Maryland operated a "may-issue" concealed carry licensing regime -- the very type of regime the Supreme Court struck down in *Bruen* as unconstitutional. The *Bruen* Court specifically identified Maryland as one of the jurisdictions operating under an unconstitutional permitting framework similar to New York's.

This creates what the brief describes as a situation that "goes beyond irony to farce." Maryland convicted Gardner for carrying a firearm without a Maryland license, yet even if Gardner had applied for a Maryland permit, she would have been legally barred from obtaining one. Under Maryland's then-existing may-issue regime, individuals like Gardner who cited ordinary self-defense needs were "entirely prevented from exercising their right to [bear] arms in public." The brief notes that Maryland does not dispute that its pre-*Bruen* regime was -- and under the Court's reasoning in *Bruen*, always had been -- "patently unconstitutional."

### The Bruen Framework Analysis

Under the two-step framework established in *Bruen*, a court must first determine whether the challenged conduct falls within the Second Amendment's plain text. If it does, the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that the regulation is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation.

On the first step, Gardner's case is straightforward. She was keeping and bearing a handgun for self-defense -- conduct at the core of the Second Amendment's protections. She had a valid license from her home state. She displayed the weapon to protect herself from a perceived threat. It is difficult to identify conduct that more directly implicates the right to keep and bear arms.

On the second step, the brief argues that Maryland has failed entirely. Maryland "identifies no evidence of any historical tradition restricting the right of out-of-state travelers to carry firearms for self-defense while in transit. Not from the founding and not even from the Reconstruction era." The Maryland appellate court that upheld Gardner's conviction likewise pointed to no such historical evidence.

### Historical Tradition Favors Travelers

The brief goes further, arguing that the historical record affirmatively supports Gardner's position. During the colonial period, at least four colonies required travelers to carry arms for their safety. From the founding through the end of the nineteenth century, American jurisdictions "routinely exempted travelers from firearm restrictions otherwise enforced, in recognition of the acute importance of armed self-defense while on the road."

This silence in the historical record is fatal to Maryland's position under *Bruen*. The government bears the burden of proof to establish a longstanding historical tradition of firearms regulation sufficiently analogous to the challenged law. Maryland has not identified -- and apparently cannot identify -- any historical precedent for prohibiting licensed out-of-state travelers from carrying firearms through the state.

### Structural Constitutional Arguments

The brief also invokes structural constitutional principles beyond the Second Amendment. It points to the Full Faith and Credit Clause, which requires each state to give effect to another state's public acts, and to the foundational premise that the Constitution was designed to "form a more perfect union" in which citizens of one state possess the right "to pass through any other state for the purpose of trade, agriculture, professional pursuits, or otherwise."

The argument is that a legal regime under which states can effectively nullify another state's concealed carry permits at their borders is inconsistent with the federalist structure the Constitution was designed to establish. Licensed carriers do not forfeit their fundamental constitutional rights upon crossing a state line.

## Broader Implications

The stakes in *Gardner v. Maryland* extend well beyond the individual case. The brief emphasizes that approximately 20 million Americans currently hold concealed carry licenses, and many of them regularly engage in interstate travel. Yet states such as Maryland, New York, and Illinois -- which contain some of the principal passageways of interstate travel -- demand that out-of-state travelers obtain separate in-state licenses to carry firearms for self-protection while transiting through their jurisdictions. The brief characterizes this burden on the Second Amendment right to armed self-defense as "immense."

If the Supreme Court grants certiorari, the case could establish a constitutional framework addressing whether and to what extent states may prohibit licensed out-of-state travelers from bearing arms in transit. In my view, a ruling in Gardner's favor would represent a significant step toward what Second Amendment advocates have long sought: constitutional recognition of a right to concealed carry that extends across state lines without requiring permits from every jurisdiction through which a citizen travels.

## What to Watch

The case is currently at the certiorari stage. The fact that the Court called for a response from Maryland is a positive procedural signal, though it does not guarantee that certiorari will be granted. The entry of Cooper & Kirk -- a firm with extensive Supreme Court experience in Second Amendment litigation -- adds substantial credibility and legal firepower to the petition. In my analysis, the Court's decision on whether to take the case will be a significant indicator of the justices' appetite for extending *Bruen*'s reach to the question of interstate concealed carry.
