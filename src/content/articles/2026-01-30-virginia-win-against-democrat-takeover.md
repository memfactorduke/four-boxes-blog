## Summary

A Virginia state court has enjoined a Democratic-led effort to amend the Commonwealth's constitution during the current legislative session — an amendment that would have transferred congressional redistricting authority from an independent commission to the Democratic-controlled General Assembly, potentially shifting Virginia's congressional delegation from a six-to-five Democratic advantage to an overwhelming ten-to-one Democratic majority. The court identified two independent procedural defects fatal to the effort: an initial legislative vote taken without unanimous consent during a special session whose scope was expressly limited to budget matters, and the fact that that vote occurred after over one million Virginians had already cast early ballots, disqualifying it as a vote occurring "before" the intervening election that Virginia's constitutional amendment procedure requires. In my analysis, this ruling has significant implications not only for Virginia's congressional map but for the broader effort to preserve a Republican House majority — and with it, the legislative environment in which Second Amendment rights are defended and advanced.

## Background: Why Redistricting Matters to the Second Amendment

I have repeatedly explained that the right to keep and bear arms does not exist in a vacuum. It is protected or eroded through the full apparatus of American constitutional government — including elections, the composition of Congress, and the Senate confirmation of federal judges. In 2016, had a different president taken office, **New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen** would not have been decided as it was; indeed, it would not have been decided at all in the way favorable to the Second Amendment. Justices Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, and Barrett were confirmed because Republicans held the Senate and the White House. The legislative and electoral dimensions of this fight are not peripheral; they are foundational.

Virginia's current congressional delegation stands at six Democrats and five Republicans. Speaker Mike Johnson holds a narrow House majority going into the November 2026 midterms — a majority whose loss would slow or reverse much of the current administration's agenda, including its posture on Second Amendment issues. The Democratic effort to redraw Virginia's congressional lines to produce a ten-to-one delegation would have represented a net gain of four or five Democratic seats, potentially decisive in determining which party controls the House after the midterms.

## Virginia's Constitutional Amendment Procedure

The Virginia Constitution imposes a specific sequence for constitutional amendments: (1) the General Assembly votes in favor of the proposed amendment; (2) a general election for the legislature intervenes; (3) the newly elected General Assembly votes again in favor of the same amendment; and (4) if both legislative votes pass, the amendment goes to the voters in a referendum. The purpose of the intervening election requirement is transparent — it ensures that at least one vote on the proposed amendment reflects the will of voters who knew about it when they chose their legislators.

Virginia Democrats argued that they had satisfied this sequence: a first vote in October 2025, the November 4, 2025 general election, and a second vote in January 2026 by the newly seated legislature, with a referendum targeted for April 2026. The state court found two independent reasons why this chronology fails.

## The First Defect: Lack of Unanimous Consent in Special Session

Governor Glenn Youngkin called a special session in the fall of 2025 for the limited purpose of addressing Virginia's budget and financial matters. The rules governing that session specified that no substantive business beyond budgetary issues could be taken up without unanimous consent of the General Assembly's members. Republicans declined to consent to adding the constitutional amendment proposal to the special session's agenda. Democrats nevertheless voted — on a party-line majority — to approve the proposed amendment.

The court correctly found this vote a nullity. Special session rules in Virginia are not aspirational; they are jurisdictional constraints on what the legislature may lawfully do within that session. A vote taken on an item outside the session's authorized scope, without the required unanimous consent, is void ab initio. Because the October 2025 vote was the first of the two required legislative acts, its invalidity destroyed the sequence upon which Democrats were relying. There was no lawful first vote. The January 2026 vote is now, at earliest, the first vote — meaning there must be another election before a second vote can occur, and a referendum cannot be placed before Virginia voters in April 2026.

## The Second Defect: The Election Had Already Begun

The court identified an additional, independent defect. Even setting aside the unanimous-consent problem, the October 2025 vote could not function as a pre-election legislative act because the election had already started. By the time the legislature voted, more than one million Virginians had already cast early ballots in the November 4, 2025 general election.

The rationale for the sequencing requirement — vote, then election, then vote again — is that citizens should have the opportunity to evaluate incumbents' positions on the proposed amendment when deciding whom to elect to the legislature that will cast the second vote. That rationale is undermined when a million citizens have already voted before the legislature acts. Those voters had no opportunity to weigh the proposed amendment in making their electoral choices, because the amendment had not yet been proposed when they cast their ballots. The court therefore held that the October 2025 vote did not occur "before" the intervening election in any meaningful sense. The election had commenced, and the constitutional sequencing requirement was not satisfied.

## The Injunction and Its Implications

The court enjoined the planned April 2026 referendum. The earliest a compliant constitutional amendment could reach Virginia voters is after the next regularly scheduled general election — a timeline that effectively removes the redistricting threat for the 2026 midterm cycle.

This is a significant outcome. Had the redistricting scheme succeeded, Virginia's congressional map would have been redrawn to maximize Democratic representation in ways that could have determined control of the House of Representatives. The procedural defects identified by the court were not technicalities in the pejorative sense; they were structural protections built into Virginia's constitution specifically to prevent a temporary legislative majority from entrenching its power by unilaterally rewriting the rules between elections.

## Broader Context: The National Redistricting Fight

I have reviewed the national context of this litigation, and it is important to understand it clearly. Multiple states — California, Illinois, New York, and now Virginia — have pursued or considered mid-decade redistricting to offset Republican-controlled states' efforts to redraw their own maps. The fight is symmetrical in that both parties engage in it, but the constitutional and statutory constraints differ by state. Virginia's constitutional amendment procedure proved to be a meaningful check on the Democratic effort here. Other states with less stringent amendment procedures may not provide equivalent protection.

For Second Amendment advocates, the stakes are direct. A House of Representatives controlled by a party committed to federal gun control legislation presents a fundamentally different legislative environment than one in which even a narrow Republican majority can block such measures. The Virginia ruling buys time and preserves the current balance of power through at least the 2026 cycle. The fight continues.
