## Summary

The United States Department of Justice Civil Rights Division, under the leadership of Assistant Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon, has formally announced a sequenced enforcement strategy for advancing Second Amendment rights across the country. The announcement marks the first time the DOJ's Civil Rights Division has prioritized the right to keep and bear arms as a core civil rights mission, and it outlines a prioritized, methodical approach to challenging unconstitutional gun-control regimes in anti-gun states.

---

*The following analysis is based on my review of Assistant Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon's public statements and the Civil Rights Division's announced priorities as of December 2025.*

---

## Background

Personnel is policy. I have said this on this channel repeatedly throughout 2025, and the formal announcement by Harmeet Dhillon regarding the DOJ's Second Amendment enforcement priorities is precisely the kind of development that vindicates that observation.

When President Trump took office in January 2025, I explained that until the Solicitor General — now John Sauer — was confirmed, and until Harmeet Dhillon was confirmed to lead the Civil Rights Division, it would be very difficult to achieve practical progress for Second Amendment rights in the federal courts. The machinery of the executive branch simply was not in place. Now that both officers are confirmed and installed, we are beginning to see meaningful movement.

## The DOJ's Announced Priorities

In public statements, Assistant Attorney General Dhillon has been unusually candid about the sequencing of the Civil Rights Division's Second Amendment work. She acknowledges that the Division cannot do everything at once — a concession to practical reality that I believe demonstrates sound strategic thinking — and that the team intends to work through priorities in sequence, starting with what she describes as "low-hanging fruit" before moving to more complex challenges.

The priorities as she has articulated them include:

**First: Prompt processing of permits for law-abiding citizens.** The Division has already filed suit against the City of Los Angeles for imposing unreasonable multi-year delays on concealed carry permit applications. This is an easy win on the merits. The Supreme Court's decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** makes clear that the Second Amendment protects the right of law-abiding citizens to carry firearms in public for self-defense, and a jurisdiction that weaponizes delay as a de facto denial cannot withstand scrutiny.

**Second: Challenging unreasonable restrictions on commonly used firearms.** Dhillon personally argued the **Barnett v. Raoul** case before the Seventh Circuit in Chicago, defending the position that Illinois's ban on semi-automatic rifles, including AR-15s, is unconstitutional. The DOJ also filed a powerful amicus brief in the Third Circuit's en banc consideration of **Cheeseman v. Platkin**, asserting that New Jersey's similar ban cannot survive constitutional scrutiny. The DOJ has likewise filed challenges to Hawaii's unreasonable firearms restrictions.

**Third: Challenging high-cost permit schemes.** Several jurisdictions impose application costs running into the thousands of dollars — a transparent attempt to price law-abiding citizens out of their constitutional rights.

## The "Sequencing" Question

One aspect of Dhillon's approach that I want to address directly is the sequencing debate. There are members of the Second Amendment community who are frustrated that the DOJ is not immediately moving to undo every federal gun-control law simultaneously. I understand that frustration, but I think it reflects a misunderstanding of how institutional litigation strategy works.

The DOJ operates under the "Take Care" clause of Article II of the Constitution, which obligates the President and the executive branch to faithfully execute the laws passed by Congress. Until those laws are declared unconstitutional by a court, the executive has an institutional obligation to defend them — even laws the administration may privately believe are constitutionally questionable. The practical consequence is that the DOJ is likely to focus first on state-level anti-gun regimes (where it can take an offensive posture as plaintiff or amicus) before addressing federal statutory questions.

This means the current strategy makes legal and institutional sense: target California's laws, Illinois's laws, New York's laws, New Jersey's laws, and Hawaii's laws first, while building the jurisprudential foundation for future federal challenges.

## The Departure of Anti-Gun Personnel

The left-wing media has spent considerable energy reporting on the departure of hundreds of DOJ Civil Rights Division lawyers who resigned or were removed following the transition. In my view, this is unambiguously good news. The Civil Rights Division had for decades been staffed with ideological opponents of the Second Amendment. Their departure removes friction from the Division's new mission and allows Dhillon and her team to staff up with attorneys who actually believe in the right to keep and bear arms.

## Implications

The formal establishment of a Second Amendment section within the DOJ Civil Rights Division is a structural change of potentially lasting significance. Whether this infrastructure survives a future administration change is, of course, uncertain. But the precedent of treating Second Amendment rights as a core civil rights priority — worthy of the same institutional attention that the Division gives to other constitutional rights — is significant.

The key metrics by which I would assess this Division's performance over time are straightforward: Is it winning cases? Is it securing injunctions against unconstitutional state laws? Is it building favorable appellate precedent that will outlast this administration? And is it doing so in a methodical, legally sound manner that will withstand Supreme Court scrutiny?

Based on what I have observed so far, the trend is our friend. That does not mean every decision the DOJ has made with respect to Second Amendment litigation has been correct — there are areas where I believe improvements are warranted, and I will continue to note them on this channel. But the overall direction is unmistakably positive, and the formal announcement of these priorities confirms that the DOJ is serious about advancing the right to keep and bear arms as a matter of civil rights enforcement.
