## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has issued a significant victory for the executive branch, staying orders entered by U.S. District Judge John McConnell of the District of Rhode Island — and affirmed by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the First Circuit — that would have commanded President Donald J. Trump to disburse approximately four billion dollars in Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) benefits during a government shutdown, when no congressional appropriations existed to fund such payments. Notably, the administrative stay was first referred to Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson, who granted an initial stay, later extended by the full Court in **Rollins v. Rhode Island Council of Churches**. That even the Court's most reliably left-leaning Justice paused Judge McConnell's order speaks to how constitutionally untenable the lower court rulings were.

## Background

Arising out of the federal government shutdown, a group of plaintiffs filed suit in the U.S. District Court for the District of Rhode Island — a jurisdiction within the First Circuit — challenging the executive branch's inability to distribute SNAP benefits when Congress had failed to appropriate the necessary funds. The strategic choice of Rhode Island as the forum was not accidental: the First Circuit, in my assessment, is among the most constitutionally unfaithful appellate courts in the nation.

Judge John McConnell, a Barack Obama appointee to the federal bench in Rhode Island, entered an order commanding President Trump and his administration to pay out billions of dollars in SNAP welfare benefits despite the reality that Congress had shut down the government and had not allocated those funds. In essence, an Article III district court judge was ordering the Article II executive to spend money that the Article I legislature had never appropriated.

## The Separation-of-Powers Problem

The constitutional infirmity here is stark. Our constitutional structure divides governmental power among three co-equal branches. Congress, under Article I, holds the power of the purse — it is Congress, and Congress alone, that appropriates funds. The President, under Article II, administers and executes those appropriations but has no independent authority to conjure money that Congress has not made available. The courts, under Article III, adjudicate disputes; they do not appropriate funds.

Judge McConnell's order asked the impossible: that the executive branch spend money the federal government did not possess. The SNAP statute itself, as the Trump administration correctly argued before the Supreme Court, conditions benefit payments on congressional appropriation of funds. When no such appropriation exists — as was plainly the case during the shutdown — the statutory obligation to pay simply does not arise.

The administration's petition to the Supreme Court, filed by Solicitor General D. John Sauer, articulated the problem with precision: the orders required the federal government to transfer an estimated four billion dollars by a date certain, notwithstanding that Congress had failed to appropriate SNAP funds for the fiscal year and the executive branch had exhausted its entire SNAP contingency reserve of over five billion dollars. As the Solicitor General correctly stated, the funding lapse is a crisis of congressional origin — and one that only Congress, through legislative action, can remedy.

## The First Circuit's Failure

After the initial stay was granted, the case returned to the First Circuit for expedited consideration. With a panel composed of Obama and Biden appointees, the circuit court did precisely what one would expect: it affirmed Judge McConnell's order, holding that Article III courts possess authority to command the Article II executive to spend money that Article I never appropriated. This reasoning is constitutionally backwards.

The First Circuit's willingness to enforce such an order reflects an institutional disposition toward judicial supremacy over the coordinate branches that I have long criticized. The willingness of that court to assert plenary authority over executive spending decisions — particularly when no legislative appropriation underlies the purported obligation — represents the kind of judicial overreach that undermines the constitutional design.

## The Supreme Court's Intervention

The Trump administration immediately sought relief from the Supreme Court. The application was presented to Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson, in her capacity as Circuit Justice for the First Circuit. Justice Jackson granted an initial administrative stay, a development that itself reflects how far outside constitutional norms the lower court orders had ventured. When a justice with Justice Jackson's known jurisprudential commitments independently halts a district court's spending mandate, the impropriety of that mandate is essentially beyond serious dispute.

Subsequently, the full Supreme Court extended the stay in **Rollins v. Rhode Island Council of Churches**, continuing the pause through the end of the week — effectively until the government could reopen and the case would become moot. The Court's per curiam order directed that the administrative stay remain in effect, with Justice Jackson noting that she would have denied the extension. The full Court's decision to extend over that dissent demonstrates that a majority of the justices recognized the constitutional gravity of the lower court's order.

## Implications

This episode illustrates several important principles that citizens and attorneys should understand.

First, the separation of powers is not merely a structural nicety — it is an operational safeguard. A federal district court judge cannot manufacture a constitutional obligation to spend money that does not exist. The power of the purse resides in Congress; courts may not transfer that power to themselves through injunctive fiat.

Second, the First Circuit continues to demonstrate why forum selection in politically charged litigation matters enormously. Plaintiffs who filed in Rhode Island rather than in Texas, Louisiana, or Florida did so with full awareness that the First Circuit would be the reviewing court. The Supreme Court's intervention is a corrective, but the cost in litigation uncertainty and potential executive disruption is real.

Third, even the Court's most progressive justices, when confronted with orders so plainly beyond judicial authority, will act to restrain lower court excess. Justice Jackson's initial administrative stay — before the full Court extended it — is a telling indicator of just how constitutionally unsustainable Judge McConnell's order was.

The underlying dispute became moot upon the government's reopening. But the precedent established here — that district courts may not order executive spending in the absence of congressional appropriations — remains important for future confrontations between an assertive lower judiciary and a coordinate executive branch.
