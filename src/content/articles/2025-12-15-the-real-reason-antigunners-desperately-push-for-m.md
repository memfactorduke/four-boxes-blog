## Summary

The movement to disarm law-abiding Americans does not pursue its agenda because gun control saves lives — the empirical record on that question is at best contested and at worst flatly contradictory to that claim. Rather, the anti-gun movement pushes most aggressively in the wake of mass violence precisely because mass violence creates the emotional and political conditions most favorable to restricting constitutional rights. Understanding this dynamic — the strategic exploitation of tragedy — is essential to mounting an effective defense of the Second Amendment and the broader framework of individual liberty from which it springs.

*Analysis by Mark W. Smith, constitutional attorney and member of the United States Supreme Court Bar.*

## Background

The right to keep and bear arms does not exist in isolation. It is part of a broader constitutional inheritance rooted in Western civilization, Judeo-Christian ethics, and the tradition of ordered liberty — the idea that individuals possess inherent rights that exist prior to and independently of the state, and that government's role is to protect those rights rather than suppress them. This context matters because the assault on the Second Amendment is not merely a policy dispute about guns. It is part of a larger project to reshape the relationship between the individual and the state — one that is playing out not only in American courtrooms but across the entire Western world.

## The Strategic Logic of Exploiting Mass Violence

The most significant advantage possessed by the gun control movement is not its arguments, which are generally weak when subjected to empirical or historical scrutiny. Its greatest advantage is the occurrence of high-profile violent events — mass shootings, targeted attacks on civilians, acts of terrorism. When these events occur, they create a temporary but powerful public demand for action, a demand that the anti-gun movement is organized and prepared to channel into legislation and litigation.

The logic is straightforward. Social disorder, high-profile violence, and the perception of breakdown in public safety generate calls for more government control — over firearms, over speech, over financial transactions, over the full range of individual freedoms. The anti-gun movement does not cause these events, but it has developed a sophisticated capacity to exploit them, converting grief and fear into legislative momentum before the public has had an opportunity to think clearly about what is actually being proposed and whether it would accomplish anything.

The key question that is never adequately answered in the immediate aftermath of a mass shooting is: would the proposed gun control measure have prevented this particular event? In virtually every case, the honest answer is no. The perpetrators of mass shootings have obtained their weapons through legal channels, through theft, or through methods that no licensing regime would have obstructed. The restrictions proposed — universal background checks, assault weapons bans, magazine capacity limits, red flag laws — consistently fail to address the specific circumstances of the attack being invoked to justify them.

## Australia as a Case Study in Failure

Consider the example of Australia, which undertook one of the most sweeping civilian disarmament programs in modern history following the Port Arthur massacre in 1996. Australia confiscated hundreds of thousands of legally owned firearms and imposed among the most restrictive gun laws in the world. The anti-gun left in America, including former Secretary of State Hillary Clinton during her presidential campaign, explicitly held up Australia as a model for the United States to follow.

Yet in recent years, Australia has experienced a series of mass violence events, including terrorist attacks carried out by individuals who were permitted to enter the country despite known connections to extremist networks. Far from rendering the country safe from mass violence, Australia's gun confiscation program has proven that disarming law-abiding citizens does not eliminate the threat posed by individuals who are determined to commit violence by whatever means are available.

The Australian government's response to this reality is not to reconsider the premise of civilian disarmament. It is to call for even more gun control — a response that reflects not empirical evaluation but ideological commitment to a predetermined conclusion.

## What Gun-Free Zones Actually Accomplish

The concept of a gun-free zone is the domestic application of the same logic. Placing a sign at the perimeter of a property declaring that firearms are prohibited does not reduce the incidence of violence. It disarms the law-abiding and has no effect whatsoever on those who intend to commit violence. A person planning a mass shooting reads a "no guns" sign not as a deterrent but as an assurance that his intended victims will be unable to resist.

This is not a theoretical proposition. It is confirmed by the overwhelming preponderance of mass shootings in the United States, which occur in designated gun-free zones. Brown University's gun-free zone policy, which I discussed separately in connection with the December 2025 shooting on that campus, is a paradigm case: a comprehensive firearms prohibition policy that does not protect the people it purports to protect and instead creates precisely the conditions — a defenseless population — that make mass violence possible.

Consider the contrast: the President of the United States, the Vice President, religious leaders, financial executives, and virtually every other person of consequence in our society protects themselves with armed security. Not with signs. Not with policies. With firearms in the hands of trained personnel. The wealthy and powerful understand instinctively that the presence of armed individuals capable of responding to violence is what deters and stops violence. They do not rely on declarations. They rely on guns.

Why, then, would we structure the environment of ordinary citizens — students and faculty members at universities, shoppers in grocery stores, riders on public transit — to deny them the same protection?

## The Deeper Purpose

I believe the answer lies in the political utility of maintaining a vulnerable civilian population. A populace that is accustomed to depending on the state for its physical security is more likely to accept dependence on the state in other domains. The erosion of the individual's capacity for self-defense is not incidentally connected to the broader project of expanding state power — it is an essential feature of it.

This is why the anti-gun movement is most active in the wake of tragedy. It is not that they welcome violence — they do not. But they understand that tragedy creates political opportunity that ordinary argumentation does not. The emotional weight of mass violence overwhelms rational analysis of what gun control can and cannot accomplish.

## Implications

The defense of the Second Amendment requires an understanding of this dynamic. We must be prepared to resist the exploitation of tragedy — not by minimizing the horror of violence, but by insisting that policy responses to violence be evaluated on the basis of evidence and logic rather than emotion. We must ask, consistently and persistently, whether the specific measure being proposed would have prevented the specific event being invoked. And we must resist the systematic conflation of the law-abiding gun owner with the violent criminal.

The right to keep and bear arms is the individual's ultimate safeguard of ordered liberty. Its erosion does not make society safer. It makes the individual more dependent, more vulnerable, and ultimately less free.
