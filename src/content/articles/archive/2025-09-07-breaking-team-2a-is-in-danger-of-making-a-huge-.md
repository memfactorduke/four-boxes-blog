## Summary

Reports have emerged that the Trump administration's Department of Justice has been internally discussing ways to restrict individuals who identify as transgender — on the theory that gender dysphoria constitutes a mental disorder — from possessing firearms under existing federal law. The Second Amendment community has reacted with considerable agitation, much of it kneejerk and some of it poorly reasoned. I want to take this opportunity to think through the issue carefully, identify the correct constitutional framework, and offer what I believe to be a strategically sound perspective — one that protects the Second Amendment community's long-term interests while engaging honestly with the genuine complexity of the question.

## Background

The Daily Wire reported that DOJ officials have been discussing whether individuals who identify as transgender and are diagnosed with gender dysphoria might be restricted from purchasing or possessing firearms under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4), the federal statute that prohibits a person who "has been adjudicated as a mental defective or has been committed to a mental institution" from possessing firearms. The discussions reportedly center on the DOJ's view that gender dysphoria — recognized as a mental disorder in the current Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders — might qualify as a basis for applying that prohibition.

Simultaneously, the recent mass shootings at the Annunciation Church in Minnesota and the Nashville school shooting, among other incidents, have involved individuals identified as transgender, raising public questions about any connection between gender dysphoria and violent behavior.

## The Correct Constitutional Framework

Before engaging with the specific policy question, it is essential to establish the correct constitutional framework for evaluating any proposed restriction on firearms possession.

The Second Amendment protects the right of the people to keep and bear arms. That right is not absolute — no constitutional right is. But under the framework established by **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008) and elaborated by **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022), any regulation that restricts the exercise of Second Amendment rights must be consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in America.

The Supreme Court's decision in **United States v. Rahimi** (2024) addressed one category of permissible restriction: individuals who have been found by a court — through an individualized adjudicative process with procedural safeguards — to pose a credible threat of physical violence to another person may be temporarily disarmed. This is a narrow holding, tied to specific factual circumstances and requiring judicial determination in a specific case involving a specific individual.

The key words are "individualized" and "adjudicated." What the Constitution permits is a targeted, case-by-case determination that a particular individual presents a demonstrated and imminent danger, arrived at through a legal process with adequate procedural protections. What the Constitution does not permit is a categorical, status-based prohibition that sweeps in all members of a group without any individualized determination of dangerousness.

## What the Existing Law Actually Requires

Federal law under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4) already prohibits firearm possession by individuals who have been adjudicated as mental defectives or committed to mental institutions. This prohibition applies to any person, of any demographic background, who has gone through a judicial or administrative process resulting in such an adjudication or commitment.

The question the DOJ is apparently considering is whether gender dysphoria, as a diagnosed mental disorder, can form the basis for applying this existing prohibition. The legal answer requires distinguishing between two different propositions.

The first proposition — that a specific individual who has been formally adjudicated as a mental defective, or who has been formally committed to a mental institution, may be prohibited from possessing firearms even if the underlying basis for that adjudication or commitment was gender dysphoria — is legally sound. If a court or administrative body with appropriate authority has made an individualized determination that a particular person is a mental defective or requires civil commitment, the fact that gender dysphoria was among the conditions considered does not disqualify the resulting prohibition from applying.

The second proposition — that all individuals who have received a diagnosis of gender dysphoria may categorically be prohibited from possessing firearms, without any individualized adjudicative process — is constitutionally untenable. A categorical prohibition based solely on a diagnostic label, without any finding of dangerousness or any individualized proceeding, would be precisely the kind of status-based restriction that neither **Rahimi** nor the historical tradition of firearms regulation supports.

## The Strategic Concern for the Second Amendment Community

I have observed that a significant portion of the Second Amendment community has responded to this news with immediate, blanket opposition — condemning any possibility of restricting any individual from possessing firearms in connection with gender dysphoria, full stop. I understand the instinct. The Second Amendment community has good reason to be suspicious of any expansion of the category of prohibited persons, because gun-control advocates have historically used any such expansion as a precedent and a template for further restrictions.

However, this instinctive opposition risks missing the strategic opportunity that this situation presents. Consider the following.

The Second Amendment community correctly insists that the greatest threat to our rights comes from mass shootings carried out by individuals who ought never to have had access to firearms — and that these tragedies are then used by gun-control advocates to justify restrictions on the law-abiding majority. This is precisely how Second Amendment rights have been eroded over decades: one terrible act, perpetrated by one genuinely dangerous individual, triggers an emotional wave of legislation that disarms millions of innocent people who bear no responsibility for the crime.

If the existing statutory framework for restricting firearm access by genuinely dangerous, mentally ill individuals were actually enforced — if individuals who have been formally adjudicated as dangerous mental defectives were actually reported to the NICS background check system and actually prohibited from purchasing firearms — the result would be fewer mass shootings, less political pressure for broad-based gun-control legislation, and greater safety for the law-abiding majority.

The argument is not that transgender individuals as a group are dangerous. The vast majority of individuals who experience gender dysphoria are not violent and pose no threat to anyone. The argument is that the existing legal framework already provides a mechanism — a constitutionally sound mechanism, involving individualized adjudication — for restricting firearms access by individuals who have been found by a court to be mentally ill and dangerous. That framework should be applied rigorously and consistently to all individuals who meet its criteria, regardless of their identity or background.

## The Dangerous Proposal: Categorical Restriction

What would be constitutionally and strategically impermissible — and what the Second Amendment community should vigorously oppose — is any proposal to impose a categorical prohibition on all transgender individuals, or on all individuals diagnosed with gender dysphoria, without an individualized judicial determination of dangerousness. Such a proposal would be unconstitutional under **Rahimi** and **Heller**, it would expose the administration to significant litigation risk, and it would hand gun-control advocates a narrative that would be devastating to the Second Amendment community's standing in the broader public debate.

The constitutional principle is clear: the government may disarm an individual who has been found — through a legal process with appropriate procedural safeguards — to be a credible threat of physical violence. The government may not disarm a category of individuals based on a demographic or diagnostic characteristic alone, without any individualized determination of dangerousness.

## Implications

The correct approach for the Second Amendment community is to insist on the constitutionally sound framework and to apply it consistently. We should support rigorous enforcement of the existing mental health disqualification provisions of federal firearms law, including improved reporting to the NICS system. We should oppose any categorical, status-based prohibition that lacks the individualized adjudicative foundation that the Constitution requires. And we should make the affirmative argument that keeping genuinely dangerous individuals — of whatever background — away from firearms serves the interests of both public safety and the Second Amendment community.

The Second Amendment is for all Americans. It protects the law-abiding. It is not served by kneejerk reactions that fail to distinguish between constitutional and unconstitutional applications of existing law. The strength of our position lies in the rigor of our constitutional analysis, and that analysis demands that we engage with complexity rather than retreat to slogans.
