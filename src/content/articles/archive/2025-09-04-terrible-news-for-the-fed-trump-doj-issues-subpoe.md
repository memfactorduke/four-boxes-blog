## Summary

On September 4, 2025, the Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi and the direction of President Donald J. Trump, opened a formal criminal investigation into Federal Reserve Board Governor Lisa Cook, issuing grand jury subpoenas as part of an inquiry into whether Governor Cook submitted fraudulent information on mortgage applications. According to United States officials familiar with the matter, initial scrutiny has centered on properties owned by Cook in Ann Arbor, Michigan, and Atlanta, Georgia, with investigators employing grand juries as part of the ongoing probe. This development follows two criminal referrals from the Trump-appointed Director of the Federal Housing Finance Agency, who publicly alleged that Cook engaged in mortgage fraud. The investigation also intersects with President Trump's effort to terminate Cook's appointment and reassert executive control over monetary policy at the Federal Reserve System.

## Background

Lisa Cook is one of the seven governors of the Federal Reserve System's Board of Governors, and as such is one of the Federal Open Market Committee's voting members on matters of monetary policy, including the setting of short-term interest rates. She was nominated by President Joseph Biden and confirmed by the Democratic-controlled Senate during the Biden administration. As a member of the Board of Governors, she serves a fourteen-year term pursuant to federal statute.

President Trump terminated Governor Cook's appointment on grounds that she either committed mortgage fraud or, at minimum, engaged in conduct sufficiently close to the line to embarrass the Federal Reserve System — an institution whose credibility, by definition, must be above reproach when its governors participate in decisions that directly affect the nation's mortgage and financial markets. It is axiomatic that an official responsible for setting short-term interest rates — decisions that directly influence the cost of mortgages across the United States — cannot be permitted to occupy that post while under credible investigation for conduct involving the fraudulent acquisition of federally backed mortgage financing.

## The Statutory Framework for Removal

The federal statute governing the terms of Federal Reserve Board governors provides for a fourteen-year term subject to one critical carveout: the president of the United States may terminate a governor for cause. The statute explicitly references the president and explicitly references the "for cause" standard. This is not a blanket prohibition on removal — it is a conditional tenure provision.

The central legal dispute is therefore not whether the president possesses removal authority, but rather whether the accumulated evidence of alleged mortgage fraud constitutes "cause" sufficient to justify termination. In my view, the answer is clearly affirmative. The existence of authenticated documentation — documentation that even the Washington Post has reported as verified — combined with two formal criminal referrals from the Federal Housing Finance Agency, and now the issuance of grand jury subpoenas, provides a robust factual basis for a finding of cause under any reasonable legal standard.

Governor Cook responded by filing suit against the Trump administration, claiming the president lacked authority to terminate her appointment. This litigation position, while predictable, is legally untenable for reasons that extend beyond the specific "for cause" question.

## The Separation of Powers Dimension

Even setting aside the "for cause" provision, there is a powerful constitutional argument that the president possesses inherent Article II authority to remove a principal officer of the United States. The Federal Reserve Board governors are not mere employees; they exercise significant governmental authority over the nation's monetary policy. The Federal Reserve System, whatever its historical claims to independence, is not a constitutionally sanctioned fourth branch of government. Article II of the Constitution vests the executive power of the United States in the president alone. That vesting clause is not decorative — it carries genuine legal weight.

The Supreme Court's jurisprudence on presidential removal power, while complex, has consistently recognized that Congress may not insulate principal officers from executive supervision in ways that fundamentally impair the president's ability to execute the laws and exercise constitutional authority. An argument that a governor of the Federal Reserve — an official whose votes directly shape the economic environment in which the president must govern — is beyond the reach of presidential removal authority faces serious constitutional headwinds.

## The Criminal Investigation

The grand jury subpoenas issued by the Department of Justice represent a significant escalation. Unlike a preliminary inquiry or administrative review, grand jury proceedings carry the full compulsory power of the federal courts. Grand juries may compel testimony, demand the production of documents, and lay the foundation for a formal criminal indictment. The issuance of subpoenas targeting Cook's Ann Arbor and Atlanta properties signals that investigators have identified specific transactions they believe warrant scrutiny under federal criminal statutes governing mortgage fraud and misrepresentation to federally backed institutions.

Mortgage fraud — the submission of materially false information on applications to borrow money from federally insured or federally backed financial institutions — is a federal crime carrying substantial penalties. The involvement of the Federal Housing Finance Agency, which oversees Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, underscores that the transactions at issue involve the very federal financial infrastructure that Governor Cook's policy decisions at the Federal Reserve directly affect.

## Implications

The Cook investigation and the attendant legal dispute over presidential removal authority carry implications that extend well beyond the immediate matter.

First, the outcome of the "for cause" litigation will help define the boundaries of presidential authority over the Federal Reserve System for decades to come. If the courts uphold the president's removal of Cook — either on the "for cause" statutory grounds or on broader Article II removal power principles — it will fundamentally alter the conventional understanding of Federal Reserve independence and bring monetary policy more firmly within the domain of democratic accountability.

Second, the criminal investigation itself, if it produces an indictment, will represent an extraordinary moment in American institutional history. No Federal Reserve Board governor has ever faced criminal prosecution for conduct related to transactions before federally backed institutions while serving in that capacity.

Third, and most practically, the resolution of these disputes — whether through litigation, criminal proceedings, or political negotiation — will determine the composition of the Federal Reserve's Board of Governors and, by extension, the direction of United States monetary policy in the near term. If President Trump ultimately succeeds in removing both Governor Cook and Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell, he will possess the authority to appoint a majority of the Board of Governors aligned with his economic policy preferences. The legal battles now unfolding in federal court are therefore not merely of jurisprudential interest — they will directly shape the economic fortunes of the American people.
