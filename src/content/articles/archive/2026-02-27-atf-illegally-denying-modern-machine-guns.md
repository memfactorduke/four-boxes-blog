## Summary

In my analysis, the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives has, since 1989, applied an administrative interpretation of the Hughes Amendment — codified at 18 U.S.C. § 922(o) — that is unsupported by the statute's plain text and that has effectively foreclosed an avenue of machine gun ownership that Congress expressly preserved. As several states now consider legislation to authorize the transfer of post-1986 machine guns to private citizens under state authority, the legal and regulatory basis for the ATF's longstanding interpretation warrants careful re-examination. As I have reviewed the relevant statutory text, the ATF's position rests on language the agency invented, not language Congress enacted.

## Background

The Firearm Owners Protection Act of 1986, Pub. L. 99-308, added § 922(o) to Title 18, creating a general prohibition on the transfer or possession of machine guns manufactured after May 19, 1986. The effect of this provision — in combination with the pre-existing National Firearms Act requirement that all transferable machine guns appear in the BATFE registry — has been to restrict civilian ownership of fully automatic firearms to those registered prior to the statutory cutoff date. That closed registry of pre-1986 machine guns has appreciated dramatically in value as a result, placing practical ownership beyond the reach of most citizens.

The constitutional and statutory validity of § 922(o) has been questioned in both scholarly commentary and judicial opinions. As I have examined, then-Judge Samuel Alito raised serious Article I Commerce Clause objections to the provision in his dissent in *United States v. Rybar*, 103 F.3d 273 (3d Cir. 1996). Beyond those constitutional questions, there is a distinct and more immediately tractable issue: whether the ATF's 1989 regulatory interpretation of § 922(o)(2)(A) — the provision establishing exceptions to the general prohibition — correctly reflects the statute's meaning. In my assessment, it does not.

## The Statutory Text: What § 922(o) Actually Says

Section 922(o)(1) provides: "Except as provided in paragraph (2), it shall be unlawful for any person to transfer or possess a machine gun." The general prohibition is thus subject to the exceptions enumerated in paragraph (2), and an accurate analysis of those exceptions is essential to evaluating the legality of state-level machine gun programs.

Section 922(o)(2)(A) provides that the prohibition "does not apply with respect to — (A) a transfer to or by, or possession by or under the authority of, the United States or any department or agency thereof or a State, or a department, agency, or political subdivision thereof." Section 922(o)(2)(B) separately preserves the pre-1986 grandfather status for machine guns "lawfully possessed before the date this subsection takes effect."

The text of § 922(o)(2)(A) is unambiguous on its face. The prohibition does not apply to: (1) a transfer *to* a state or its agencies; (2) a transfer *by* a state or its agencies; or (3) possession *under the authority of* a state or its agencies. These are three grammatically distinct categories of exempt conduct. The phrase "under the authority of" a state is not limited to transfers directly from state government to an individual — it encompasses possession that occurs pursuant to the state's authorization, an interpretation consistent with how the same phrase is used elsewhere in federal law (driver's licenses, permits, licenses generally constituting authorization from state authority).

## The ATF's 1989 Regulatory Overreach

In 1989, the ATF issued an open letter and subsequent regulatory guidance stating that post-1986 machine gun registrations would only be accepted when the weapon was for "the benefit of and use of a government entity." This language does not appear in § 922(o)(2)(A). The statute specifies transfers *to or by* a state and possession *under the authority of* a state; it says nothing requiring that the ultimate beneficiary of possession be the state itself.

As I have examined this history, the ATF's 1989 interpretation was not a reasonable construction of ambiguous statutory text — it was the substitution of new, judicially unreviewed language for the language Congress actually enacted. The practical effect was to eliminate the "under the authority of" prong of § 922(o)(2)(A) as an operative legal category, rendering it a nullity. Under standard rules of statutory interpretation, courts disfavor constructions that render statutory provisions surplusage, and the canon of surplusage counsels against reading "under the authority of" as merely coextensive with transfers to or by government entities.

The redundancy problem is compounded by the pre-existing provision at 18 U.S.C. § 925(a)(1), which separately exempts government agencies and states from substantially all firearms regulations under Chapter 44 of Title 18 — including the NFA's transfer restrictions. If § 922(o)(2)(A) merely duplicated the government-use exemption already established in § 925(a)(1), the separate enumeration of the "under the authority of" category in § 922(o)(2)(A) would be without purpose. The canon of surplusage strongly favors reading those words as adding something the pre-existing exemptions do not cover — namely, private citizen possession conducted pursuant to state-issued authorization.

## The Path Forward Under a Correct Statutory Reading

In my assessment, a correct reading of § 922(o)(2)(A) supports the following conclusions. First, a state may lawfully acquire, manufacture, and possess post-1986 machine guns. Second, a state may lawfully transfer such firearms — including to private citizens acting under state authority. Third, private citizens who possess post-1986 machine guns pursuant to state-issued authorization are exempt from the federal prohibition, because their possession occurs "under the authority of" the state within the plain meaning of § 922(o)(2)(A).

This framework is directly applicable to the state-level machine gun legalization programs now under consideration in West Virginia and other jurisdictions. A state that enacts legislation authorizing qualified citizens to receive and possess post-1986 machine guns through a state-administered registration and permitting process would, on the proper reading of the statute, be operating within the scope of the § 922(o)(2)(A) exemption. The ATF's 1989 position to the contrary lacks statutory authority.

## The Constitutional Dimension

As I have argued, this analysis operates at the intersection of statutory interpretation and constitutional principle. The Second Amendment protects the right of law-abiding citizens to keep and bear arms, and as the Supreme Court held in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), firearms regulations must be consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. Whether the post-1986 machine gun prohibition — particularly as extended by the ATF's extratextual 1989 interpretation — survives scrutiny under the *Bruen* framework presents an independent constitutional question that courts will eventually need to address.

In the interim, however, the more tractable path for states seeking to authorize machine gun ownership is the one provided by § 922(o)(2)(A) itself. Congress expressly preserved state authority over this category of firearms. The ATF's 1989 interpretation contracted that authority without statutory warrant. States that choose to exercise their preserved authority under the correct reading of the statute are not challenging federal law — they are complying with it.

## Implications

In my assessment, the ATF's decades-long misinterpretation of § 922(o)(2)(A) has denied American citizens access to a category of firearms ownership that Congress explicitly preserved when it enacted the Hughes Amendment. As states now move to test and challenge that interpretation, the statutory argument for state authority over post-1986 machine gun transfers is, in my view, well-grounded in the text, structure, and canons of construction applicable to § 922(o). If the ATF declines to honor state-authorized programs under the correct reading of the statute, the resulting litigation will present federal courts with a straightforward question of statutory interpretation — and the text answers that question in favor of state authority.
