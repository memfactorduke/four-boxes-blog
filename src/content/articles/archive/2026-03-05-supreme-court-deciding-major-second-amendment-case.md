## Summary

*United States v. Harmani* presents the Supreme Court with a question that follows directly from its 2024 decision in *United States v. Rahimi*, 602 U.S. 680 (2024): whether the federal prohibition on firearm possession by unlawful users of controlled substances under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3), as applied to a defendant whose only documented disqualifying conduct is regular marijuana use, satisfies the Second Amendment's requirement that disarmament be grounded in a demonstrated finding of dangerousness. In my analysis, the constitutional defect in the government's case is not incidental but structural — Congress, in drafting § 922(g)(3), incorporated a categorical drug-user disqualification without establishing, as *Rahimi* now appears to require, that the class of conduct at issue correlates with the violent dangerousness that the historical tradition of disarmament was designed to address. The case is now before the Court for a decision, and the oral argument record suggests the justices are acutely aware of this constitutional mismatch.

## Background

Ali Harmani was charged under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3), which prohibits a person who is "an unlawful user of or addicted to any controlled substance" from possessing a firearm or ammunition. Harmani admitted to regular marijuana use — approximately twice weekly. He was found in possession of a handgun. The government's charging theory rested entirely on his marijuana use as the disqualifying conduct; allegations that he was involved in cocaine distribution or drug dealing were not substantiated through conviction or factual finding in the underlying criminal proceeding.

The threshold question — whether a marijuana user who has never been found dangerous through any civil or criminal adjudication may be categorically disarmed under the Second Amendment — was left open by the Court's narrowly framed holding in *Rahimi*. That decision addressed whether a person subject to a domestic violence civil protective order, who had judicially been found to pose a credible threat to an intimate partner's physical safety, could be disarmed pending the order's duration. The *Rahimi* majority held that such disarmament was consistent with the nation's historical tradition of temporarily disarming individuals found to be "physically dangerous." *Harmani* now requires the Court to determine how broadly — or narrowly — that dangerousness standard applies.

## The *Rahimi* Framework

In *United States v. Rahimi*, the Supreme Court held that the Second Amendment permits the temporary disarmament of individuals who have been found, through a judicial proceeding, to pose a credible threat of physical violence to another person. The case facts were notably clear: Zackey Rahimi had acknowledged in court proceedings that he had engaged in serious physical violence — conduct that formed the basis of the restraining order at issue. The Court's opinion, authored by Chief Justice Roberts, grounded the constitutional permissibility of the disarmament in historical analogues — surety statutes and going-armed laws from the founding era — that authorized disarming individuals who presented a demonstrable threat to public peace or personal safety.

As I have analyzed *Rahimi*, its holding is best understood as establishing that the Second Amendment permits disarmament of a discrete, historically grounded category: persons who have been individually adjudicated to pose a credible threat of physical violence. What *Rahimi* did not hold — and what the *Harmani* Court must now address — is whether a categorical statutory disqualification, applied without any individualized dangerousness finding, is consistent with that historical tradition when the predicate conduct has no established correlation with violent behavior.

## The Constitutional Mismatch in § 922(g)(3)

In my analysis, the central constitutional defect in the government's application of § 922(g)(3) to Harmani is the absence of any finding — at any level of the judicial or administrative system — that he poses a violent danger to third parties. Consider what the record does not contain: no civil commitment proceeding in which Harmani was found dangerous; no family or domestic relations court finding of a threat to another's physical safety; no conviction for any offense with a violent element; no individualized governmental determination of any kind that Harmani is physically dangerous.

The statute's prohibition is categorical rather than individualized. Section 922(g)(3) applies to any "unlawful user of or addicted to any controlled substance" — a class defined by a pattern of conduct, not by a finding of dangerousness. This structural feature distinguishes the statute from the restraining order provision upheld in *Rahimi*, where the restraining order itself was the product of a judicial proceeding that had evaluated the specific respondent's conduct and found a credible threat. No analogous process precedes disqualification under § 922(g)(3).

The constitutional mismatch is compounded by the statute's incorporation of the Controlled Substances Act's schedule of prohibited substances. Congress, in drafting § 922(g)(3), did not assess whether particular substances on that schedule cause or correlate with violent behavior. Marijuana — the substance at issue in *Harmani* — has not been associated, through any legislative finding or established empirical record, with the kind of violent conduct that the historical disarmament tradition addressed. The statute, as written, treats a marijuana user identically to a user of substances that might have some correlation with violent behavior — a categorical equivalence that the Second Amendment's individually grounded dangerousness inquiry would appear to preclude, absent some additional showing.

## The Oral Argument Record

As I have reviewed the oral argument, the justices appeared focused on precisely this question: what has the government actually established about Harmani specifically? The Court appeared unimpressed by the government's invocation of unsubstantiated allegations about cocaine dealing or distribution. What remained in the record after those uncharged allegations were set aside was a single factual predicate: regular marijuana use. The government was placed in the position of arguing that this predicate alone — without any adjudicatory finding of dangerousness — suffices to disarm an individual under the Second Amendment.

This is the question *Rahimi* left open, and the difficulty the government faces in answering it reflects the structural problem in the statute's design. Congress enacted § 922(g)(3) as a prophylactic categorical bar, not as a mechanism for individualized dangerousness assessments. Under the *Bruen* framework as elaborated by *Rahimi*, courts assess whether a challenged firearms regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation — a tradition that, on the record developed by *Rahimi*, appears to have required individualized conduct-based findings rather than status-based categorical prohibitions as the predicate for disarmament.

## Predictive Assessment

I want to offer a candid assessment of the likely outcome, because intellectual honesty requires acknowledging the tension between the legal merits and the probable vote distribution on this Court. In my analysis, the constitutional argument that the government has failed to establish the dangerousness predicate *Rahimi* requires is legally sound. The statute's categorical design and the absence of any individualized finding in Harmani's case create a genuine constitutional deficiency under the framework the Court has articulated.

That said, I regard a pro-Second Amendment outcome in *Harmani* as uncertain. The Court's composition includes members who are likely to give substantial weight to the government's interest in disarming drug users as a class, independent of any particularized showing of violence — a policy judgment that may influence their reading of the historical tradition, even if the doctrinal case for the defendant is strong. A 5-4 decision upholding the conviction, with the constitutional question left partially unresolved, remains a plausible outcome. What the case will produce, at minimum, is a more precise articulation of what the government must demonstrate to disarm an individual under *Rahimi*'s dangerousness standard — and that clarification will shape the constitutional analysis of § 922(g)(3)'s applications well beyond the facts of this particular case.

## Implications

Whatever the outcome in *Harmani*, the case focuses the Court on a constitutional principle of broad significance: the government's power to disarm a citizen is not coextensive with its power to regulate conduct. The Second Amendment, as interpreted in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), and *Rahimi*, recognizes a fundamental individual right that the government may restrict only through regulations consistent with the nation's historical tradition. That tradition, as the Court has described it, required individualized assessments of dangerousness — not categorical status-based disqualifications applied to broad classes of persons whose conduct has not been linked to violent behavior.

The statute's overbreadth, if recognized by the Court, would not necessarily invalidate § 922(g)(3) in all its applications. A holding might be limited to the as-applied question: whether Harmani's marijuana use, absent any finding of violence or dangerousness, satisfies the *Rahimi* standard. But even a narrow as-applied ruling would clarify the constitutional floor that § 922(g)(3) must meet in future cases — and that clarification is itself of substantial doctrinal importance for the ongoing development of Second Amendment jurisprudence in the lower courts.
