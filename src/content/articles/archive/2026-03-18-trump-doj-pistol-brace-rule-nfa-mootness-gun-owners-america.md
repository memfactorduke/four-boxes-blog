## Summary

The Trump Department of Justice recently filed a brief in *State of Texas v. BATFE* indicating that the Biden-era pistol brace rule has been vacated and that the case is now moot. The filing also stated that the DOJ will continue to enforce the National Firearms Act as it exists on the books. While some commentators interpreted this as a betrayal of gun owners, my analysis shows that the filing is better understood as a routine procedural statement reflecting the executive branch's constitutional obligation to enforce existing federal statutes -- and a signal that the major victory against the Biden pistol brace regulation remains fully intact.

## Background: The Biden Pistol Brace Rule

In 2023, the Biden administration's Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives promulgated a final rule that dramatically expanded the definition of "short-barreled rifle" under the National Firearms Act. The rule established a multi-factor test designed to reclassify pistols equipped with stabilizing braces as NFA-regulated short-barreled rifles, thereby requiring their registration and payment of the $200 tax stamp. The practical effect was to bring millions of lawfully owned firearms under the NFA's regulatory umbrella overnight -- without any act of Congress.

Gun Owners of America, the State of Texas, and other plaintiffs filed suit in the Northern District of Texas, challenging the rule under the Administrative Procedure Act. The complaint, spanning well over a hundred pages, raised numerous claims -- each one directed squarely at the final rule. The plaintiffs argued that the ATF had exceeded its statutory authority, that the rule was arbitrary and capricious, and that it violated the Second Amendment, the Fifth Amendment's takings clause, and due process protections.

## The Victory: Vacatur of the Final Rule

The central relief sought by the plaintiffs was vacatur of the Biden administration's final rule. Under the APA, when a federal court determines that an agency has exceeded the scope of its delegated authority, the appropriate remedy is to vacate the unlawful regulation -- effectively erasing it as though it had never been promulgated. In Latin legal parlance, the rule is vacated *nunc pro tunc*, meaning from its inception.

That is precisely what occurred here. The Biden pistol brace rule has been vacated. It is gone. Both the plaintiffs and the government agree on this point. The multi-factor test that the Biden ATF devised to sweep stabilizing braces into the NFA's definition of short-barreled rifles no longer exists as a matter of law. This represents an unqualified victory for the Second Amendment community and the organizations that litigated the challenge.

## The DOJ Filing: What It Actually Says

The recent Trump DOJ filing that generated alarm in some corners of the firearms community was considerably more mundane than social media reactions suggested. Let me break down what the Department of Justice actually argued in the brief.

First, the government argued that the case is now moot. Because every cause of action in the original complaint was directed at the final rule, and the final rule has been vacated, there is no remaining controversy for the court to adjudicate. Under Article III of the Constitution, federal courts may exercise jurisdiction only over live cases or controversies. When the relief a plaintiff sought has been fully obtained, the case ordinarily becomes moot and the court loses jurisdiction to proceed further.

Second, the DOJ included what amounts to a boilerplate reservation: the executive branch intends to continue enforcing the National Firearms Act as written by Congress. This statement is not a new policy position. It is a recitation of the executive branch's obligation under Article II of the Constitution, which directs the President to "take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed." The NFA remains a valid federal statute. Whether or not one agrees with it as a policy matter, the executive branch is constitutionally obligated to enforce it until Congress repeals it or a court strikes it down.

## Why Procedure Matters as Much as Substance

Understanding the distinction between substance and procedure is essential to evaluating this filing correctly. The legal tool used to challenge the Biden pistol brace rule was the Administrative Procedure Act. The APA is the mechanism by which courts review whether a federal agency's regulations exceed the authority delegated to it by Congress. The APA challenge succeeded: the rule was struck down because the ATF exceeded its statutory mandate.

But an APA challenge is, by its nature, limited in scope. It addresses the lawfulness of a specific regulation -- not the constitutionality of the underlying statute. The plaintiffs in *State of Texas v. BATFE* asked the court to invalidate a regulation. They did not ask the court to declare the National Firearms Act itself unconstitutional. Having obtained the precise relief they requested, the case has served its purpose.

This does not mean the NFA is immune from challenge. It means that a different legal vehicle is required. Constitutional challenges to federal statutes are typically brought under 42 U.S.C. Section 1983 or its federal analogue, *Bivens*, or through direct constitutional claims. These are different tools in the litigation toolbox -- analogous to using a wrench rather than a hammer. The APA was the right tool to dismantle an unlawful regulation. A constitutional challenge under Section 1983 or the Second Amendment directly is the right tool to attack the statute itself.

## The Path Forward Against the NFA

The vacatur of the pistol brace rule does not foreclose future challenges to the National Firearms Act. Multiple avenues remain open, as I've analyzed. Courts across the country are already considering cases that challenge the NFA's application to short-barreled rifles, short-barreled shotguns, and suppressors on Second Amendment grounds. Additionally, the elimination of the $200 NFA tax stamp through the reconciliation process -- a provision included in what has been described as the "One Big Beautiful Bill" -- could undermine the statutory basis for NFA registration requirements by severing the connection to Congress's taxing power.

The plaintiffs in the Texas case could also seek leave to amend their complaint to add constitutional claims under 42 U.S.C. Section 1983, arguing that the NFA as applied to pistols with stabilizing braces violates the Second Amendment or exceeds Congress's enumerated powers. However, given that such challenges are already pending in other jurisdictions, this may prove unnecessary.

## Conclusion

The Trump DOJ's filing in the pistol brace case does not represent a retreat on the Second Amendment. The Biden-era pistol brace rule has been vacated in its entirety -- a significant and durable victory. The DOJ's statement that it will continue to enforce the NFA as written is neither surprising nor alarming; it is a restatement of the executive branch's constitutional duty. My take is that the appropriate response is not alarm but strategic focus: the tools to challenge the NFA itself exist, the litigation is already underway in multiple courts, and the legislative landscape continues to shift in favor of reform. The victory has been won. The next battle requires a different approach -- and it is already being fought.
