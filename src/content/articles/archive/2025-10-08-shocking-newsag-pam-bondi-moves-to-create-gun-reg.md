## Summary

The Trump administration's Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi, has taken a position in the ongoing litigation over young adults' Second Amendment rights — specifically the federal prohibition on 18-, 19-, and 20-year-old Americans purchasing handguns from federally licensed dealers — that I find both surprising and, on careful analysis, legally problematic. In **Reese v. ATF**, which arose out of the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit, the Second Amendment plaintiffs secured a landmark ruling that 18-to-20-year-olds possess full Second Amendment rights. The case has now been remanded to the district court in the Western District of Louisiana for the remedy phase. It is at that remedial stage where the Department of Justice, representing the interests of the United States government, has taken a position that diverges from what the Second Amendment plaintiffs and supporting organizations believe is constitutionally required. I want to examine that position with care, explain where I believe the DOJ has gone wrong, and outline what the proper constitutional remedy should look like.

## Background

**Reese v. ATF** has a complex procedural history that is essential to understanding what is now at stake. The case began as a challenge to 18 U.S.C. § 922(b)(1) and related federal regulations prohibiting federally licensed firearms dealers — FFLs — from transferring handguns to persons under 21 years of age. The plaintiffs, including the Second Amendment Foundation and the Firearms Policy Coalition along with several individual young adults, argued that this categorical exclusion of adults between the ages of 18 and 20 from handgun purchases is incompatible with the Second Amendment as interpreted by the Supreme Court in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**.

At the district court level in the Western District of Louisiana, the trial judge initially ruled against the Second Amendment plaintiffs, finding that 18-to-20-year-olds had no Second Amendment right to purchase handguns. That decision was appealed to the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit. In a significant pro-Second Amendment ruling, the Fifth Circuit reversed the district court, holding that 18-, 19-, and 20-year-olds are members of "the people" within the meaning of the Second Amendment and therefore possess full Second Amendment rights. The federal prohibition on handgun purchases by those adults was held unconstitutional.

With the liability phase resolved in favor of the Second Amendment plaintiffs, the Fifth Circuit remanded the case back to the same district court — the same trial judge who had originally ruled against the plaintiffs — for the remedy phase. It is now the district court's responsibility to enter a final judgment crafting the appropriate remedy consistent with the Fifth Circuit's constitutional holding.

## The Remedy Phase: What Is at Stake

In constitutional litigation of this nature, the remedy phase is not merely a formality. The plaintiffs and their supporting organizations — the Second Amendment Foundation and the Firearms Policy Coalition — sought injunctive relief that would prohibit the federal government from enforcing the handgun purchase restriction against young adult members of those organizations nationwide. That is the logical consequence of a ruling that the federal statute is unconstitutional: the government must be enjoined from enforcing it.

The question of the scope of the injunction is where the controversy lies. Should the injunction be limited to the named plaintiffs and the specific organizational members who were parties to the litigation? Or should it extend more broadly, applying nationwide to all 18-to-20-year-old Americans?

The Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi, has argued for a narrow injunction — one that covers only the named plaintiffs and the identified members of the Second Amendment Foundation and Firearms Policy Coalition. In order to identify those members for the purpose of applying the injunction, the DOJ sought access to the membership lists of those organizations.

## The Membership List Controversy

The district court judge — acting, it appears, on his own initiative rather than at the express request of any party — entered an order requiring the Second Amendment Foundation and Firearms Policy Coalition to disclose their membership lists to the court. This extraordinary demand raised immediate and serious First Amendment concerns.

The right of association is a foundational constitutional right. The Supreme Court has repeatedly recognized that the government cannot compel disclosure of membership in advocacy organizations without a compelling justification, particularly where such disclosure would expose members to potential harassment or adverse government action. The landmark case of **NAACP v. Alabama** established this principle decades ago, and it remains fully applicable today.

It is worth noting that both the Second Amendment plaintiffs and the Department of Justice opposed this membership disclosure requirement — they were aligned in objecting to it. The district court ultimately vacated the membership disclosure order, recognizing that it was constitutionally problematic. But the episode illustrates the kinds of procedural hazards that arise when Second Amendment cases are remanded to district courts that are less than fully sympathetic to Second Amendment rights.

## The DOJ's Position on Remedy: Where It Goes Wrong

While the DOJ and the Second Amendment plaintiffs were aligned on opposing the membership disclosure order, they diverged on the fundamental question of remedy scope. The DOJ's position — that the injunction should be limited to identified members rather than applying broadly — reflects an institutional preference for narrow relief that, in this context, I believe is inconsistent with the constitutional holding of the Fifth Circuit.

The Fifth Circuit did not hold that the federal handgun purchase restriction is unconstitutional only as applied to members of the Second Amendment Foundation or the Firearms Policy Coalition. The court held that 18-, 19-, and 20-year-olds possess Second Amendment rights — full stop. That is a constitutional ruling of general application. It applies to all Americans in that age group, not merely to those who happen to belong to particular advocacy organizations.

When a law is held facially unconstitutional — meaning that it cannot be constitutionally applied to the class of persons it affects — the proper remedy is an injunction against enforcement of the law, not an injunction that covers only a subset of the people whose rights are being violated. The Second Amendment plaintiffs, in my view, are correct that the injunction should be broad in scope, covering all 18-to-20-year-old Americans within the Fifth Circuit's jurisdiction at a minimum.

## The Broader Implications for Second Amendment Litigation

The **Reese** remedy phase is not occurring in isolation. The Supreme Court has granted certiorari in **Wolford v. Lopez**, the Hawaii no-carry default rule case, and I have addressed that grant extensively. There is also significant pending litigation in the **West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF** case raising the young-adult purchase question in the Fourth Circuit context. The Trump DOJ has taken the position in **West Virginia Citizens Defense League** that the Supreme Court should not grant cert at this time but should instead wait for **Wolford** and other pending decisions, then issue a GVR — a grant, vacate, and remand order — sending the case back to the lower courts.

I have concerns about that approach in the **West Virginia** context as well, but the **Reese** remedy dispute is distinct and more immediately pressing. It involves a case where the constitutional holding has already been established, and where the question is simply how broadly to apply the relief that flows from that holding.

The Second Amendment community deserves — and the Constitution requires — that when a federal court holds a gun law unconstitutional, the resulting injunction actually protects the rights of all the people whose rights were violated, not merely a narrow subset of organizational members who can be identified from a list. The DOJ's preference for narrow, list-based relief is administratively convenient but constitutionally inadequate.

## Implications

The **Reese v. ATF** remedy phase represents one of the most consequential stages of young-adult Second Amendment litigation currently before the federal courts. The Fifth Circuit's holding on the merits was a resounding victory — one of the strongest constitutional rulings in favor of young adults' Second Amendment rights in the post-**Bruen** landscape. What remains is to ensure that the remedy is equal to the holding.

I call on the Department of Justice to reconsider its position on the scope of the injunction. Attorney General Pam Bondi and Solicitor General John Sauer have shown genuine commitment to the Second Amendment in many contexts this term. The remedy phase of **Reese** is an opportunity to demonstrate that commitment in a concrete and meaningful way — by supporting broad injunctive relief that actually vindicates the rights of all the young Americans the Fifth Circuit found to be constitutionally entitled to purchase handguns. Anything less falls short of what the Constitution demands.
