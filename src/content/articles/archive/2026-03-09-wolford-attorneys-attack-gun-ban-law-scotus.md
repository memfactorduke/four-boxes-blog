## Summary

Attorneys Alan Beck and Kevin O'Grady — counsel of record in *Wolford v. Lopez*, the landmark concealed carry case argued before the United States Supreme Court — have filed a new federal complaint challenging Hawaii's categorical exclusion of non-residents from its concealed carry permit system. The case, **Sinsky v. Lopez** (U.S. District Court for the District of Hawaii), presents a direct constitutional challenge under the Second Amendment's plain text, which protects the right to "bear arms" — a right the Supreme Court confirmed in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), extends to public carry for self-defense. In my analysis, Hawaii's residency restriction is constitutionally indefensible: the complaint amasses 35 pages of historical documentation establishing that the founding-era tradition uniformly protected the right of non-resident travelers to carry arms, the precise opposite of what Hawaii's statute requires.

## Background

Hawaii's firearms licensing scheme requires that an applicant for a concealed carry permit be a resident of the state. As a result, any individual who is not a Hawaii resident — regardless of their qualifications, training, demonstrated need, or lawful firearm ownership — is categorically ineligible to obtain a permit and cannot lawfully carry a firearm for self-defense while present in Hawaii. The state has not maintained this restriction on the basis of any individualized determination that non-residents pose heightened risk; the restriction is categorical and admits no exceptions.

The *Bruen* decision fundamentally altered the analytical framework courts must apply to such regulations. Under *Bruen*, once a plaintiff demonstrates that the regulated conduct — carrying a firearm in public for self-defense — falls within the plain text of the Second Amendment, the burden shifts entirely to the government. The government must then affirmatively demonstrate that the challenged regulation is "consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation." *Bruen*, 597 U.S. at 17. If the government cannot identify a founding-era tradition of analogous regulation, the restriction fails. The question in **Sinsky v. Lopez** is whether Hawaii can identify any such tradition supporting a categorical prohibition on non-resident carry — and in my examination of the record, the answer is plainly no.

## The Complaint: Text, History, and the Non-Resident Tradition

The complaint in **Sinsky v. Lopez** runs 52 pages, of which approximately 35 are devoted to historical analysis. This level of historical documentation is both appropriate and strategically significant. While technically the plaintiff bears no burden to affirmatively demonstrate a historical tradition of protection — that burden rests entirely with the government under *Bruen* — counsel for the plaintiffs have marshaled historical evidence establishing the affirmative case: that the founding-era tradition, tracing from pre-revolutionary practice through the late nineteenth century, consistently recognized and protected the right of travelers and non-residents to carry arms for self-defense.

The historical record documented in the complaint is, in my analysis, dispositive. The tradition that emerges is not one of neutrality toward non-resident carry — it is one of affirmative protection. Colonial-era vagrancy and traveling regulations uniformly assumed that persons traveling through a jurisdiction would be armed. Post-founding state statutes that addressed the carrying of arms by non-residents typically treated such carry as lawful and expected. There is no historical analog to Hawaii's categorical prohibition — no founding-era state required residency as a condition precedent to the exercise of the right to bear arms. As the complaint correctly notes, the plaintiffs do not technically bear the burden of proving this positive tradition, but having demonstrated it, Hawaii faces an essentially insurmountable task: it must produce historical evidence of the opposite, and no such evidence exists.

## The Plaintiff: Jonathan Sinsky

In my analysis of Second Amendment litigation strategy, the selection of a named plaintiff is among the most consequential decisions counsel can make. Poor factual predicates produce constrained opinions; strong factual predicates provide courts the analytical latitude to write broad, principled rulings. The attorneys representing the plaintiffs in **Sinsky** have selected an exceptionally well-situated lead plaintiff.

Jonathan Sinsky served eight years on active duty in the United States Marine Corps as an infantryman and sniper, including a tour of duty stationed at Marine Corps Base Hawaii between 2011 and 2015. Following his military service, he has worked as a senior shooting instructor for the U.S. State Department's Diplomatic Security Service. His firearms training and proficiency substantially exceed that of the overwhelming majority of civilian permit applicants in any jurisdiction. He has documented connections to Hawaii, visits the state regularly, and has presented his application for a concealed carry permit meeting all substantive qualifications the state has established. Hawaii has effectively conceded the point: the state does not dispute that Sinsky meets every criterion for permit issuance other than the residency requirement. That concession is legally significant — it removes from the case any argument that the denial rests on individualized grounds rather than the categorical statutory bar.

This factual posture is the optimal platform from which to mount a constitutional challenge. The court is presented with a plaintiff of unimpeachable qualifications, a denial that is conceded to be solely a function of the residency statute, and a constitutional question that admits of a clean answer under *Bruen*.

## The Licensing Framework Reservation

The complaint contains an important litigation preservation device that warrants specific attention. Footnote 3 provides that the plaintiffs "do not concede that any permit, preapproval, license, or permission is necessary for the exercise of any aspect of Second Amendment rights." This reservation is legally significant beyond its technical function as a preservation placeholder.

While the immediate prayer for relief seeks to require Hawaii to issue concealed carry permits to qualified non-residents — a remedy that, if granted, would require the state to extend its existing licensing regime rather than dismantle it — the reservation forecloses any argument that the plaintiffs have accepted the constitutional legitimacy of the licensing framework as such. Second Amendment litigation is by necessity incremental: the doctrinal edifice is rebuilt precedent by precedent, and no single case can be expected to resolve every question implicated by a state's firearms regulatory scheme. The appropriate strategic approach is to secure the available relief in each case while preserving the broader arguments for future proceedings. The footnote accomplishes precisely this.

## Hawaii in the National Context

As I have observed in my analysis of Second Amendment litigation patterns, Hawaii occupies an extreme position on the spectrum of state firearms regulatory regimes. At present, twenty-nine states have adopted permitless carry, and reciprocity agreements among shall-issue states mean that a significant portion of American territory is accessible to individuals holding carry permits issued in any one of dozens of jurisdictions. Federal courts have already issued rulings against categorical non-resident exclusions in New York and California — jurisdictions that resisted extending concealed carry permit access to non-residents and were ordered to do so under the *Bruen* framework.

Hawaii's residency restriction presents materially identical legal issues. The state's position — that it may categorically exclude all non-residents from the exercise of the right to bear arms within its territory — is not supported by historical tradition, is not required by the text of the Second Amendment, and is in direct tension with the pattern of federal court rulings that have emerged since *Bruen*. In my assessment, the case presents a strong constitutional challenge on facts that are as favorable as any currently in the federal court system.

## Implications

**Sinsky v. Lopez** advances Second Amendment jurisprudence on two related but distinct fronts. First, it squarely presents the question of whether a state may condition the exercise of the right to bear arms on residency — a categorical restriction with no founding-era historical analog. As I have examined the record, the answer the *Bruen* methodology compels is that it may not. Second, the case contributes to the developing body of post-*Bruen* precedent addressing the outer limits of state discretion to impose procedural and substantive restrictions on public carry. Each favorable ruling in this line of cases strengthens the foundation for subsequent challenges, in Hawaii and elsewhere, to restrictions that cannot survive genuine historical scrutiny.

The depth and quality of the historical analysis in the complaint — prepared by counsel who have argued the non-resident carry question before the Supreme Court of the United States in *Wolford v. Lopez* — reflects the seriousness with which this challenge is presented. In my analysis, the constitutional law is clear, the historical record is one-sided, and the factual predicate is optimal. The case merits close attention as it proceeds through the District of Hawaii.
