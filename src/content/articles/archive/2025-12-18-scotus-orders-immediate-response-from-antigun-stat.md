## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has ordered the State of Illinois to file a response brief explaining why the Court should not grant certiorari in **Schoenthal v. Raoul**, a Second Amendment challenge to Illinois's ban on carrying firearms on public transit. This call for response is a significant procedural step that frequently signals heightened judicial interest in a petition. I want to provide a comprehensive assessment of where the Second Amendment docket stands at the Supreme Court — which cases have been granted, which have been denied, and which are positioned as strong candidates for review in the coming term.

## Background

The Second Amendment landscape at the Supreme Court is dynamic and, at times, difficult to track. My purpose here is to give you the clearest possible picture of where things stand — the altitude and airspeed of the litigation, if you will — so that you can understand not just which cases exist, but which ones matter and why.

Following the Supreme Court's landmark decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen** (2022), which established the text-and-history methodology for evaluating Second Amendment claims, lower courts have been working through an enormous backlog of cases applying that framework. The Court's docket reflects this ongoing process.

## Cases Currently Granted Certiorari

There are two Second Amendment cases that the Supreme Court has already granted certiorari and will hear this term.

The first is **United States v. Hmani**, out of the Fifth Circuit. This case presents a challenge to 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3), the federal statute that prohibits firearms possession by persons who are unlawful users of, or addicted to, controlled substances. The defendant in **Hmani** will almost certainly lose on the merits — the historical record supporting disarmament of those who cannot lawfully possess firearms is well-established. What will be significant, however, is the *methodology* the Court uses in reaching that conclusion. The framework articulated in **Hmani** will guide lower courts for years, perhaps decades.

The second granted case is **Wolford v. Lopez**, arising out of the Ninth Circuit. This case challenges Hawaii's so-called vampire rule, which provides that even a licensed concealed carry permit holder cannot bring a firearm onto private property that is open to the public — a gas station, a grocery store, a convenience store — without first obtaining express affirmative permission from the property owner. This rule effectively makes concealed carry meaningless in practice, because it is impossible for a traveler to obtain advance permission from every business establishment they might enter. The Court is very likely to strike down Hawaii's vampire rule. The real question, again, is the breadth and methodology of the opinion.

## Certiorari Denied: Notable Cases

Several cases have been denied certiorari this term, though none of these denials should be viewed as surprising or as affirmations of the lower court decisions. Cert denial is not a ruling on the merits.

**Gustafson v. Springfield, Inc.** presented a Tenth Amendment commerce clause challenge to the Protection of Lawful Commerce in Arms Act. The Pennsylvania courts rejected the challenge, and the Supreme Court had no interest in review. This is a favorable outcome — the PLCAA remains intact.

## The Call for Response in Schoenthal v. Raoul

The most significant development I want to highlight is the Supreme Court's call for response in **Schoenthal v. Raoul**. This case involves Illinois's prohibition on carrying firearms on public transit — buses, trains, and similar conveyances. The State of Illinois has been directed to file a brief addressing why certiorari should not be granted.

This procedural posture — a CVSG, or call for the views of the state respondent — strongly suggests that at least some Justices view this petition as certiorari-worthy. It elevates the case considerably above the ordinary petition.

The legal question is important. Public transit is one of the most common means of travel for millions of Americans, and a categorical ban on carrying firearms on public transit implicates the core Second Amendment right to bear arms for self-defense in public that **Bruen** recognized. The anti-gun jurisdictions have been systematically designating virtually every public space a "sensitive place" in an effort to gut **Bruen**. Illinois's public transit ban is a prime example of that strategy.

## The Importance of Methodology Over Outcome

I want to emphasize a point that is essential to understanding appellate jurisprudence: in Second Amendment cases, how a court reaches its conclusion is frequently as important as what the conclusion is. The methodology used — whether a court properly applies the text-and-history test from **Bruen**, whether it correctly identifies the relevant historical tradition, and whether it resists importing means-ends balancing through the back door — will shape the landscape of Second Amendment litigation for years.

This is why cases like **Hmani**, where the second amendment claim is almost certainly going to fail on the merits, still matter enormously. If the Court articulates a broad, protective methodology in upholding the drug-user prohibition, that same methodology will constrain lower courts from upholding other, far more problematic restrictions.

## Implications

The Supreme Court's active engagement with the Second Amendment docket signals continued commitment to enforcing **Bruen**'s text-and-history framework. The call for response in **Schoenthal v. Raoul** suggests the Court may be prepared to address sensitive-places doctrine head-on. The outcome in **Wolford v. Lopez** will significantly curtail the ability of states to use permitting rules as de facto carry prohibitions. And the methodological guidance in **Hmani** will provide additional structure for lower courts navigating the post-**Bruen** landscape.

For Second Amendment advocates, the message is clear: the Supreme Court remains the essential battleground, and the cases now in the pipeline have the potential to substantially expand — or, if poorly decided, substantially contract — the practical scope of the right to keep and bear arms in public.
