## Summary

Solicitor General John Sauer, acting on behalf of the United States Department of Justice under Attorney General Pam Bondi, has filed a brief in the United States Supreme Court opposing the petition for a writ of certiorari in **Rush v. United States**. The case raises the question of whether the National Firearms Act's registration requirements as applied to short-barrel rifles are constitutional under the Second Amendment. The DOJ's brief argues that **Rush** presents a poor vehicle for resolving this important constitutional question, citing the unfavorable factual record involving the petitioner and the availability of better-suited cases pending before the Court. While some in the Second Amendment community have criticized this filing, I believe the DOJ's position reflects sound litigation strategy and is not cause for alarm.

## Background

### The National Firearms Act and Short-Barrel Rifles

The National Firearms Act of 1934, codified at 26 U.S.C. Chapter 53, imposes a registration and tax scheme on certain categories of firearms deemed particularly dangerous or unusual, including machine guns, suppressors, destructive devices, and short-barrel rifles. A short-barrel rifle is defined as a rifle with a barrel shorter than sixteen inches or an overall length shorter than twenty-six inches. Possession of an unregistered short-barrel rifle is a federal felony.

Whether short-barrel rifles are constitutionally protected arms under the Second Amendment is a question of increasing importance. Under **Heller**, arms in common use for lawful purposes by law-abiding citizens are presumptively protected. Short-barrel rifles have become increasingly popular among law-abiding gun owners for defensive use and recreational shooting, which raises the question of whether they now meet the common use threshold.

### The Rush Petitioner and His Factual Record

Jaman Rush is the petitioner seeking Supreme Court review. According to the record, Rush was stopped by police while driving with a revoked license to a court appearance on an existing citation for driving without a license. Upon stopping Rush, officers detected the odor of marijuana and observed marijuana in the vehicle. During the ensuing search, law enforcement discovered an unregistered short-barrel rifle. Rush fled from officers, resulting in a physical confrontation before he was restrained. He was convicted of possessing an unregistered short-barrel rifle in violation of the NFA and sentenced to thirty months in federal prison. He now seeks Supreme Court review on the Second Amendment constitutionality of his conviction.

## The DOJ's Brief: Vehicle Concerns

The Solicitor General's brief opposing certiorari raises several concerns about **Rush** as a vehicle for resolving the Second Amendment question it presents.

### The Petitioner's Unfavorable Record

The most significant concern is the petitioner's own conduct and character. I have consistently cautioned the Second Amendment community that the Supreme Court is not indifferent to the facts of the cases it decides. When the Court is evaluating whether an arm is in common use by law-abiding citizens, a petitioner whose factual record is saturated with criminal behavior is a poor standard-bearer. A petitioner who flees from police, resists arrest, and is found in possession of contraband while driving with a revoked license is not the law-abiding citizen whose rights **Heller** was designed to protect.

The object lesson of **United States v. Rahimi** (2024) should be fresh in mind. In that case, the Biden administration's Department of Justice pushed to the Supreme Court a case involving a petitioner who had, among other things, bashed his girlfriend's head against a dashboard and then fired at witnesses. The Court upheld the federal prohibition on firearms possession by persons subject to domestic violence restraining orders, and the unfavorable facts unquestionably shaped the Court's analysis. When a case involving a genuinely sympathetic, law-abiding plaintiff is brought to the Court, the Second Amendment community can expect a better result.

### Mootness Concerns and Better-Suited Vehicles

The DOJ's brief also notes that other pending cases present the same or related legal questions in superior procedural postures. **Wolford v. Lopez** has already been granted certiorari and is before the Court. **United States v. Hammani** — the marijuana-user gun possession case — is also before the Court. Both cases will provide guidance on Second Amendment methodology that will bear on the question of NFA restrictions.

Additionally, the Solicitor General identifies two other pending petitions — **West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF** and **NRA Inc. v. Glass** — that address the Second Amendment rights of adults between the ages of 18 and 20, a related question of whether certain federally mandated restrictions on otherwise law-abiding young adults are constitutional. Those cases present better factual records and procedural postures for litigating these questions.

## Why I Am Not Alarmed

Some commentators in the Second Amendment community have characterized the DOJ's opposition to certiorari in **Rush** as evidence that the Trump administration opposes Second Amendment rights. I disagree strongly with that assessment.

The decision to oppose certiorari in a particular case is not a statement about the constitutional merits of the underlying legal question. It is a strategic judgment about whether a particular case is the right vehicle for resolving that question in the most favorable way. The Trump administration's DOJ is on record as supporting the constitutional protection of commonly owned firearms and opposing restrictions that cannot be justified under the **Bruen** historical tradition test. Nothing in the **Rush** brief contradicts that position.

The appropriate analogy is a chess player who declines to advance a particular piece not because the move is inherently bad, but because the timing is wrong and a better opportunity will present itself shortly. Forcing a constitutional question to the Supreme Court on bad facts — with a petitioner whose record undermines sympathy and whose conduct is difficult to defend — risks producing adverse precedent that will bind the Second Amendment community for a generation.

## Implications

The DOJ's brief in **Rush v. United States** reflects sophisticated strategic thinking about how to advance Second Amendment rights before the Supreme Court. The administration is not abandoning the question of NFA constitutional limitations on short-barrel rifles. It is declining to litigate that question in a case where the petitioner's conduct makes him an unappealing vehicle for constitutional expansion.

Second Amendment advocates should not conflate opposition to certiorari in a specific case with opposition to the constitutional right. The administration has demonstrated, through its actions in **Ree v. ATF**, in **Harrel v. Raoul**, and in its general litigation posture, that it is genuinely committed to advancing Second Amendment rights. Its decision to seek better factual vehicles for presenting important constitutional questions to the Supreme Court is not evidence of retreat — it is evidence of the kind of strategic discipline that produces enduring constitutional victories.
