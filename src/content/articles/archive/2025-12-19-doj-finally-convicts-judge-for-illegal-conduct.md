## Summary

A federal jury in the Eastern District of Wisconsin has convicted Milwaukee County Circuit Court Judge Hannah Dugan of obstruction of federal law enforcement for her role in helping an illegal alien evade ICE agents who were waiting to arrest him outside her courtroom. The conviction carries a maximum sentence of up to five years in federal prison. This case marks a significant and historic moment in the accountability of members of the judiciary for conduct that crosses the line from judicial independence into active criminal obstruction of federal law.

## Background

The question I hear constantly from viewers and concerned citizens alike is: why don't judges who break the law go to prison? It is a fair question, and for too long the answer has been, essentially, that they do not. That calculus may now be changing.

The facts in this case are as follows. Eduardo Flores Ruiz, an illegal alien, appeared before Milwaukee County Circuit Court Judge Hannah Dugan on multiple counts of violent battery, including an assault on a woman severe enough to require hospitalization. At the time of that court appearance, federal ICE agents were stationed outside Judge Dugan's courtroom, armed with an administrative warrant, waiting to take Flores Ruiz into custody for immigration violations.

## Judge Dugan's Alleged Conduct

According to the government's case, when Judge Dugan became aware that federal immigration officials were present in the courthouse waiting to arrest the defendant before her, she did not simply proceed with the matter before her court. Instead, she took active steps to frustrate the federal enforcement operation.

First, she approached the ICE agents and falsely represented to them that their administrative warrant was deficient and that they would need to proceed down the hall to speak with the chief judge about the matter. This representation had the effect — whether intended or not, though the jury clearly concluded it was intentional — of diverting the federal agents away from the courtroom long enough for Dugan to act.

Second, and more critically, while the ICE agents were sent on this errand, Judge Dugan facilitated the movement of Flores Ruiz through a private entrance to the courthouse, away from public view, and directed him to flee down the street. She also reportedly told him he would not need to return to the courthouse in person, suggesting future hearings could be conducted by video conference — thereby eliminating any further opportunity for ICE to apprehend him at that location.

The ICE agents, to their credit, were not fully deceived. They gave chase and ultimately tackled and arrested Flores Ruiz in the street outside the courthouse. He was subsequently deported.

## The Federal Prosecution

Judge Dugan was thereafter charged by a federal grand jury on multiple counts, including obstruction of federal law enforcement — effectively obstruction of justice. On December 19, 2025, a federal jury in the Eastern District of Wisconsin returned a guilty verdict on that charge.

The prosecution was overseen by Brad Schimmel, the interim United States Attorney for the Eastern District of Wisconsin. Mr. Schimmel is a name familiar to Second Amendment advocates — I covered his candidacy for the Wisconsin Supreme Court extensively, explaining why it was critical that a constitutionalist occupy that seat. The significance of a constitutionalist U.S. Attorney bringing this prosecution to successful conclusion should not be underestimated.

## Legal Analysis

What makes this case legally significant is not simply the fact of conviction, but what it signals about the limits of judicial conduct. Judges, of course, possess substantial independence. That independence is a structural feature of our legal system designed to protect the rule of law from political interference. But judicial independence is not a license to commit federal crimes.

The charge of obstruction of a federal proceeding — at its core, the active interference with ICE's lawful enforcement of immigration law — does not become defensible merely because the person committing it wears a black robe. The jury rejected any such argument. The government proved beyond a reasonable doubt that Judge Dugan's conduct was not a judicial act protected by any doctrine of immunity, but rather deliberate criminal interference with federal officers carrying out their duties.

The facts that Dugan actively misdirected federal agents, physically facilitated the defendant's movement through a non-public exit, and affirmatively told him not to return to the courthouse in person collectively establish a pattern of intentional obstruction that no claim of judicial authority can insulate.

## Implications

Several implications flow from this conviction.

First, this case demonstrates that sitting judges who commit federal crimes are not immune from prosecution. Judicial robes confer authority within the courtroom; they do not confer immunity from the criminal law outside it.

Second, the case illustrates the broader importance of prosecutorial will. For years, the question of judicial misconduct has been treated as a matter for bar associations and judicial conduct commissions — bodies that rarely impose serious consequences. This prosecution reflects a different approach: treating criminal conduct by judicial officers as exactly what it is, a federal crime, and bringing it before a jury of citizens.

Third, from a rule-of-law perspective, the verdict sends an unambiguous message that selective enforcement of federal immigration law by state court actors — however motivated — will not be tolerated. The federal government's authority to enforce immigration statutes cannot be vetoed by a single state judge who disagrees with that policy.

The sentencing phase will determine whether Judge Dugan faces actual imprisonment, but the conviction itself represents a meaningful assertion that accountability under law extends to all individuals, regardless of the office they hold.
