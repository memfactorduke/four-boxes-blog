## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has issued an emergency order reinstating a preliminary injunction entered by Judge Roger Benitez of the U.S. District Court for the Southern District of California in *Mirabelli v. Bont*, a case challenging California school policies that required teachers and administrators to withhold from parents information about their minor children's gender identity transitions at school. In my analysis, the Court's order reflects a determination that the plaintiff parents are likely to succeed on their Free Exercise Clause claims and that California's policies will likely fail strict scrutiny — the highest standard of constitutional review. The decision simultaneously vindicates Judge Benitez's underlying constitutional analysis and signals the Supreme Court's willingness to scrutinize state educational policies that systematically exclude parents from decisions about their minor children.

## Background

California implemented policies directing public school teachers and administrators not to disclose to parents when a student had adopted a different gender identity at school — including using different names and pronouns — without the student's explicit consent. These policies applied even to minor students in middle school. Under the challenged framework, a student might present under a different name and different pronouns in the school environment while parents remained entirely uninformed, regardless of their religious beliefs or parental authority over their child's medical and psychological welfare.

Judge Benitez enjoined the policies, concluding that they violated the Free Exercise Clause of the First Amendment, the substantive due process rights of parents under the Fourteenth Amendment, and potentially the Equal Protection Clause. California appealed, and the Ninth Circuit stayed Judge Benitez's injunction pending appeal — prompting the Supreme Court application that led to the order now under review.

## The Factual Record

As I have reviewed the Court's order, the factual record in *Mirabelli* is particularly significant to the constitutional analysis. Among the plaintiffs are parents identified as "John and Jane Poe" who held religious objections to gender transitioning and were not informed when their daughter began presenting as male at school during seventh grade, using a male name and male pronouns. At parent-teacher conferences, school officials used only female pronouns and provided no disclosure of the student's gender-related conduct at school.

At the start of eighth grade, the student attempted self-harm and was hospitalized. Only at that point did her parents learn from medical personnel that she had been experiencing gender dysphoria and presenting as male at school for the preceding year. Months after discharge, she was again involuntarily hospitalized due to ongoing risk of self-harm. At a subsequent school, despite the parents' explicit instructions to teachers and administrators, school personnel continued using the student's chosen male name and pronouns, citing their obligations under California state policy.

These facts matter both legally and institutionally. Courts, including the Supreme Court, respond to concrete factual records that illustrate the human consequences of challenged policies. The record in *Mirabelli* presents a direct causal chain between the state's non-disclosure mandate and serious harm to a vulnerable minor — precisely the kind of showing that undermines the government's assertion of a compelling state interest in student safety.

## The Constitutional Framework

The Supreme Court's order rests on the Free Exercise Clause standard established in *Church of Lukumi Babalu Aye, Inc. v. City of Hialeah*, 508 U.S. 520 (1993), and more recently reinforced in *Fulton v. City of Philadelphia*, 593 U.S. 522 (2021). Laws that burden religious exercise and are not neutral and generally applicable must satisfy strict scrutiny — the government must demonstrate a compelling interest pursued through narrowly tailored means.

In my analysis, California's policies are facially non-neutral with respect to religion: they create a system in which parents who hold religious views about gender identity receive no accommodation, while the student's personal preferences take precedence over parental religious exercise. The Court's order states that the plaintiff parents "are likely to succeed on the merits" of their free exercise claim and that "California's policies will likely not survive the strict scrutiny that the Free Exercise Clause demands." The order further observes that California's asserted interest in student safety — while cognizable — is undermined by the very policies at issue, which "cut out the primary protectors of children's best interest, their parents." On narrow tailoring, the Court noted that the state's safety interest could be served through a policy permitting religious exceptions while separately addressing cases involving genuine risk of parental abuse.

## The Parental Rights Dimension

The substantive due process component of the challenge implicates a well-established line of Supreme Court precedent recognizing parents' fundamental liberty interest in the upbringing and education of their children. *Meyer v. Nebraska*, 262 U.S. 390 (1923), and *Pierce v. Society of Sisters*, 268 U.S. 510 (1925), established the foundational principle; *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000), reaffirmed that parental rights are among the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized under the Due Process Clause. California's policies place the state in the position of actively concealing from parents information relevant to their children's psychological health and medical history — an intrusion that, in my view, implicates rather than merely regulates parental rights.

The tension here is not between parental rights and student autonomy in the abstract; it is between the state's substitution of its own non-disclosure judgment for the parental authority that constitutional law has long recognized as primary. The state's invocation of student privacy as a justification fails to account for the fact that parental access to information about a minor child's welfare is not ordinarily treated as a privacy intrusion — it is a function of the parent-child relationship that the Constitution protects.

## Significance for Judge Benitez's Jurisprudence

As I have observed throughout his Second Amendment jurisprudence, Judge Benitez applies constitutional text and precedent with methodological consistency — a quality that has attracted both appellate scrutiny and, increasingly, appellate vindication. The Supreme Court's emergency order in *Mirabelli* reinstating his injunction tracks the same pattern visible in his Second Amendment cases: a district court applying rigorous constitutional analysis, a Ninth Circuit panel staying or reversing the ruling, and a Supreme Court ultimately crediting the district court's underlying analysis.

The pattern is significant because it reflects not merely outcomes in individual cases but a broader dynamic in which the Supreme Court has been correcting the Ninth Circuit's constitutional methodology across multiple doctrinal domains. Judge Benitez's opinions in the Second Amendment context — including his rulings on California's assault weapon ban and magazine restrictions — are pending in that same appellate pipeline, and the Supreme Court's demonstrated willingness to credit his constitutional analysis in *Mirabelli* warrants consideration when assessing the likely trajectory of those cases.

## Implications

The Supreme Court's emergency order in *Mirabelli v. Bont* carries implications that extend beyond California's specific policies. As I have analyzed, the order signals that the Court takes seriously both the Free Exercise Clause's protection of religiously motivated parenting decisions and the substantive due process framework protecting parental authority over children's welfare. It also reinforces the principle — visible in the Court's standing and injunction jurisprudence — that judicial relief must be responsive to the actual constitutional violation, not calibrated to minimize disruption to challenged government programs.

For practitioners and legal scholars, the case also illustrates the strategic importance of factual records in constitutional litigation. The specific and documented harm suffered by the Poe family provided a concrete basis for the Court to assess both the constitutional merits and the equitable considerations bearing on emergency relief. Cases with well-developed factual records demonstrating concrete injury tend to produce opinions with broader doctrinal reach — and *Mirabelli*, as it proceeds through the Ninth Circuit and potentially returns to the Supreme Court on the merits, may prove to be among them.
