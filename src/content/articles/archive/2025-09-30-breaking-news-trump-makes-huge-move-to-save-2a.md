## Summary

The Trump administration has taken decisive action to dismantle and revoke the Biden administration's interim final rule governing civilian firearm exports — a regulatory scheme I characterized at the time of its promulgation as a deliberate attempt to destroy the American gun industry through administrative obstruction rather than legislation. The Department of Commerce, through its Bureau of Industry and Security, has now formally revoked the burdensome export licensing requirements that Biden administration officials imposed in 2024, restoring the ability of American firearms manufacturers and dealers — large and small alike — to compete in global markets and secure the economic vitality of the domestic gun industry.

## Background

The Biden administration, confronted with its repeated inability to achieve significant gun control through the legislative process, turned to administrative regulation as a surrogate mechanism for suppressing the American firearms industry. In 2024, the Department of Commerce — operating through the Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS), the sub-agency responsible for issuing export licenses for civilian firearms — promulgated an interim final rule that fundamentally restructured the licensing framework governing the export of civilian firearms and ammunition to overseas markets.

The practical effect of the rule was as follows. American firearms manufacturers and dealers, particularly smaller companies that depend on export revenue for their economic survival, were required to obtain individual export licenses for each transaction or category of transactions — and to renew those licenses on an annual basis. The licensing process was deliberately designed to be burdensome, time-consuming, and expensive, with the predictable consequence that many smaller gun companies simply could not maintain export operations at a profitable level. The rule thus functioned, in my view, as an indirect mechanism to drive American gun companies out of business by depriving them of tens of millions of dollars in export revenue.

The constitutional and legal infirmities of this approach were manifest. The Second Amendment protects not merely the right of individuals to keep and bear arms but, as a necessary corollary, the commercial ecosystem that makes that right practicable. An administrative rule designed to cripple the arms industry — not through direct prohibition but through regulatory attrition — is precisely the kind of indirect assault on constitutional rights that the courts have recognized as constitutionally suspect.

## The Trump Administration's Regulatory Action

Immediately upon taking office, the Trump administration moved to address this regulatory overreach. In January 2025, the Department of Commerce placed a pause on all export licenses and related proceedings under the Biden rule while the administration conducted a full review. That pause provided immediate relief to American gun companies that had been operating under the cloud of the Biden-era requirements.

Following that review, the administration has now taken the further and conclusive step of revoking the Biden interim final rule in its entirety, restoring the prior regulatory framework and eliminating the annual licensing requirements that had functioned as a practical embargo on civilian firearms exports. This action benefits not only the largest firearms manufacturers in the country but also the small and medium-sized businesses — machinists, gunsmiths, specialty manufacturers, and dealers — whose livelihoods depend on access to global markets.

## Administrative Law Considerations

I wish to draw attention to a significant point of administrative law that arises from this sequence of events. The Biden administration's interim final rule, while deeply misguided in its policy objectives, did proceed through a regulatory process. The Trump administration's revocation of that rule must similarly comply with the procedural requirements of the Administrative Procedure Act (APA) — principally, the notice-and-comment rulemaking requirements of 5 U.S.C. § 553.

The lesson of the Trump administration's first term is instructive here. When administrative actions are taken without strict adherence to APA procedural requirements, courts may invalidate those actions on procedural grounds even when the substantive policy judgment is entirely sound. The administration's careful and deliberate approach — pausing the rule, conducting a review, and then proceeding to revocation through proper channels — reflects an understanding of this lesson and augurs well for the durability of the revocation.

## Industry and Economic Impact

The economic significance of this action cannot be overstated. The global civilian firearms market represents a substantial and growing commercial opportunity for American manufacturers, who produce some of the most sought-after civilian firearms and accessories in the world. The Biden administration's export restrictions threatened to cede this market to foreign competitors — particularly European and Israeli manufacturers — who faced no equivalent regulatory burden in their own jurisdictions.

By removing these restrictions, the Trump administration has restored the competitive position of the American gun industry, preserved jobs across the manufacturing sector, and affirmed that the federal government will not employ administrative regulation as a back-door mechanism to undermine an industry whose products are protected by the Second Amendment.

## Implications

This action carries implications that extend beyond the immediate regulatory and economic context. It reflects a broader principle that I have advocated consistently: the Second Amendment and the commercial ecosystem that supports it are entitled to affirmative protection by the federal government, not merely the tolerance of the federal courts. An administration that takes seriously its oath to uphold the Constitution must protect constitutional rights from administrative encroachment with the same vigor it employs in their defense against legislative assault.

The revocation of the Biden firearms export regulations is, in that sense, a companion to the DOJ's affirmative lawsuit against Los Angeles County's obstructionist CCW permitting practices. Together, these actions signal a governing philosophy in which the Second Amendment is treated as a genuine, enforceable constitutional right deserving of the full resources and authority of the federal executive.
