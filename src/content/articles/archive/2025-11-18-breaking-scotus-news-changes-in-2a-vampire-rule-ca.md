## Summary

The petitioners in **Wolford v. Lopez** — the case challenging Hawaii's so-called "vampire rule" or no-carry default law — have filed their opening brief before the United States Supreme Court, explaining in detail why Hawaii's Act 52 violates the Second Amendment. The brief lays out a powerful constitutional argument that Hawaii's law, which presumes that concealed carry permit holders may not bring their firearms onto any private property open to the public unless the property owner affirmatively grants express permission, effectively eviscerates the right to bear arms in public that *Bruen* unambiguously secured. I expect a major win for the Second Amendment when this case is decided.

## Background

The Supreme Court's 2022 decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** was a watershed moment for the right to keep and bear arms. The Court held, in no uncertain terms, that law-abiding Americans have a constitutional right to carry firearms in public for self-defense — and that any law burdening that right must be consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation at the time of the Founding.

Five states — New York, New Jersey, California, Maryland, and Hawaii — responded to *Bruen* not by complying with its holding, but by constructing an elaborate statutory architecture designed to make the exercise of the newly vindicated right effectively impossible in practice. These states rushed to enact what I have called "Bruen-reaction laws": statutes that formally acknowledged they could no longer maintain their unconstitutional may-issue permitting systems, but that sought to render the resulting shall-issue permits nearly useless by banning their exercise in virtually every conceivable location.

## Hawaii's Vampire Rule Explained

Hawaii's response to *Bruen* was Act 52, signed by the Governor of Hawaii on June 2, 2023. The core provision at issue — which I have long called the "vampire rule" — establishes a presumption that concealed carry permit holders may **not** bring their firearms onto any private property that is open to the public, such as gas stations, convenience stores, supermarkets, restaurants, rest areas, laundromats, and virtually every other place of business a law-abiding citizen might visit in the course of daily life.

The only way to overcome this presumption is for the permit holder to first obtain **express affirmative permission** from the property owner — either in writing or orally — before entering with a firearm. The practical consequence is staggering: a permit holder driving across Oahu who stops for gas, then picks up groceries, then grabs a bite to eat would need to secure the personal permission of three separate property owners or their authorized agents before exercising the right *Bruen* guaranteed.

As the petitioners' brief correctly observes, this is a completely unrealistic requirement. The average person does not know who owns the gas station, supermarket, or restaurant they patronize — and even if they did, asking a cashier or shift manager for permission to carry a concealed firearm before entering is not how law-abiding gun owners want to interact with the world. That, of course, is precisely the point. Hawaii designed its law to make carry so practically burdensome as to be effectively nonexistent, while maintaining the fig leaf of formal compliance with *Bruen*.

According to the petitioners' brief, Act 52 prohibits or creates a presumption against carrying in **96.4 percent** of the publicly accessible land in Hawaii. That figure alone should end the constitutional analysis. The Second Amendment's guarantee of the right to bear arms cannot be reduced to a right exercisable on 3.6 percent of the state's publicly accessible territory.

## The Ninth Circuit's Flawed Analysis

The Ninth Circuit upheld Hawaii's law, distinguishing it from California's analogous statute — which the same court found unconstitutional — on the thin basis that Hawaii's law permits oral permission in addition to written permission, while California required only signage. The Ninth Circuit's determination that this procedural distinction renders the two nearly identical laws constitutionally different is difficult to defend. Both laws impose the same practical burden on permit holders seeking to exercise their right to carry, and the oral permission mechanism provides no meaningful relief given the realities of commercial life.

The Ninth Circuit decision in *Wolford* prompted sharp dissents from judges including Judge Lawrence Van Dyke — well known within the Second Amendment community for his principled analysis of gun rights cases — and Judge Bumatay. Those dissenting voices were correct.

## The Question Presented and the Petitioners' Argument

The question the Supreme Court has agreed to consider is: "Whether the Ninth Circuit erred in holding, in direct conflict with the Second Circuit, that Hawaii may presumptively prohibit the carry of handguns by licensed concealed carry permit holders on private property open to the public unless the property owner affirmatively gives express permission to the handgun carrier."

The petitioners — Jason Wolford, Allison Wolford, and Adam Capriccioso — are represented by attorneys Alan Beck, Mark Pennock, and Kevin O'Grady. Their opening brief argues, correctly in my view, that Hawaii's law represents an unabashed effort to nullify the practical exercise of a constitutional right. The brief opens by tracing the sequence of events precisely: before *Bruen*, Hawaii virtually never granted concealed carry permits; after *Bruen*, rather than issue permits freely, Hawaii enacted a law designed to ensure that even those who obtained permits could carry almost nowhere.

The Trump administration's Department of Justice filed a brief supporting the petitioners, urging the Court to grant certiorari and strike down Hawaii's law. The Court did grant certiorari, and the petitioners' opening brief has now been filed.

## The Circuit Split and Why It Matters

The Court's decision to take this case is in part motivated by the direct conflict between the Ninth Circuit's ruling in *Wolford* and the Second Circuit's analysis of analogous laws. When federal circuits disagree on a fundamental constitutional question — here, whether states may impose a default prohibition on carry in all public accommodations, subject to individualized express permission — the Supreme Court must resolve the conflict. Gun owners in Hawaii and California face a categorically different legal landscape than gun owners in states where courts have applied *Bruen* faithfully.

## Implications

**Wolford v. Lopez** is one of the most consequential Second Amendment cases to reach the Supreme Court since *Bruen* itself. A ruling striking down Hawaii's vampire rule would not only vindicate the rights of Hawaiian gun owners; it would send an unmistakable signal to all five Bruen-reaction states — New York, New Jersey, California, Maryland, and Hawaii — that their efforts to circumvent *Bruen* through sensitive-places designations and default-prohibition schemes are constitutionally untenable.

The *Bruen* framework demands that the government justify its firearm regulations by demonstrating a historical tradition of analogous regulation. There is no Founding-era tradition of requiring law-abiding citizens to obtain individualized express permission from private property owners before exercising their right to carry in the ordinary course of daily life. The historical record simply does not support Hawaii's law, and I expect the Supreme Court to say so.

The filing of the petitioners' brief marks a significant milestone in this litigation. Big days are coming, and they are coming fast.
