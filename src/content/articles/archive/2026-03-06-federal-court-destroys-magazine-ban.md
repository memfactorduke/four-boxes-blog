## Summary

The District of Columbia Court of Appeals has issued a 2-1 decision in *Benson v. United States* holding that the District's ban on firearm magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. In my analysis, this decision is jurisprudentially significant on two grounds: it represents the first ruling from a court of last resort — the DC Court of Appeals is functionally equivalent to a state supreme court — that a large-capacity magazine ban violates the Second Amendment under the framework established in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), and it creates a split in authority that materially strengthens the case for Supreme Court review of *Duncan v. Bonta*, the California magazine ban case currently before the Court on a petition for certiorari.

## Background

The District of Columbia has long prohibited the possession of magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds of ammunition. This restriction, among the most restrictive in any American jurisdiction, was challenged as a violation of the Second Amendment following the Supreme Court's decisions in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and *Bruen*. The legal questions presented — whether magazines constitute "arms" protected by the Second Amendment, and whether a magazine capacity restriction can survive the *Bruen* historical tradition analysis — are the same questions presented by magazine ban challenges in California (*Duncan v. Bonta*), Massachusetts, New Jersey, and other states.

Prior to *Benson*, no federal court of appeals, and no state supreme court or equivalent tribunal, had held that a magazine capacity restriction violates the Second Amendment. The Ninth Circuit, sitting en banc, upheld California's magazine ban in *Duncan v. Bonta*, 19 F.4th 1087 (9th Cir. 2021), before the case returned to the district court following *Bruen*. The First and Seventh Circuits had similarly declined to invalidate magazine restrictions. *Benson* breaks from this pattern and, for the first time, produces a highest-court ruling on the merits that the Second Amendment is violated by a ten-round magazine capacity limit.

## The Constitutional Framework Applied

The *Bruen* framework requires a two-step analysis. First, courts assess whether the challenged regulation burdens conduct protected by the Second Amendment's plain text. Second, if the text is implicated, the government bears the burden of demonstrating that the regulation is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. The government cannot satisfy this burden through mere analogy; it must identify historical regulations that are relevantly similar to the challenged restriction in both justification and burden.

As I have examined the analytical structure applicable to magazine bans, the government's position faces difficulties at both steps. On the text, magazines are instrumentally necessary to the operation of the firearms they feed — a magazine ban that renders a firearm inoperable for the self-defense purposes *Heller* recognized as the core of the Second Amendment guarantee functions as a partial ban on the arm itself. Magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds are, by any reasonable metric, in common use for lawful purposes: they are standard-configuration components of the most widely owned centerfire pistols and rifles in the United States.

On the historical tradition question, the government has been unable to identify founding-era or Reconstruction-era regulations that imposed capacity limitations on firearms or their components. The *Bruen* Court was explicit that the government must produce more than isolated historical examples — it must demonstrate a tradition of analogous regulation. No such tradition exists for magazine capacity limits, which are a late twentieth-century legislative development with no historical precedent in the periods the Supreme Court has designated as the relevant reference points for Second Amendment analysis.

## The Judicial Architecture and the Circuit Split

An understanding of the DC Court of Appeals' institutional position is essential to assessing the significance of *Benson*. The DC Court of Appeals is the highest court in the District of Columbia for cases arising under DC law, and its decisions on federal constitutional questions carry the same weight, for certiorari purposes, as a state supreme court decision. The U.S. Court of Appeals for the DC Circuit — a separate tribunal — has jurisdiction over federal agency matters and federal criminal cases in the district; the DC Court of Appeals handles local law, including DC's criminal code under which the magazine ban operates.

A split between the DC Court of Appeals and the Ninth, First, and Seventh Circuits on the constitutionality of magazine bans creates precisely the kind of unresolved conflict in constitutional interpretation that traditionally warrants Supreme Court review. The Court grants certiorari to resolve such conflicts, and a split of this character — with courts of equivalent precedential authority reaching opposite conclusions on the same constitutional question — is among the most compelling grounds for certiorari. The timing of *Benson* is particularly significant given that *Duncan v. Bonta* is currently pending before the Court on a petition for certiorari; the DC decision strengthens the argument that immediate Supreme Court intervention is warranted.

## The Common Use Analysis

As I have argued throughout my analysis of Second Amendment jurisprudence, the *Heller* Court's holding that the Constitution protects arms "in common use at the time" for lawful purposes — and that the government may not ban entire categories of arms that are commonly possessed by law-abiding citizens — is the constitutional provision most directly implicated by magazine capacity restrictions. Magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds are not exotic or unusual items; they are the standard configuration for the most common defensive firearms in America. Standard magazines for the Glock 17, for example, hold seventeen rounds; standard magazines for the AR-15 hold thirty rounds. A prohibition on these configurations functionally restricts the most widely owned defensive firearms to modified, reduced-capacity variants.

The *Heller* Court expressly held that the Second Amendment is not limited to the arms available at the founding — it extends to modern arms in common use for self-defense. Under this framework, the relevant inquiry is not whether founding-era citizens owned magazines (they did not, in the modern sense), but whether the arms these magazines feed are in common use for lawful purposes today. They plainly are. And as I have noted, an argument that the government may separate a firearm from its standard-capacity magazine and prohibit only the latter while permitting the former is constitutionally untenable: the practical effect is a restriction on the self-defense utility of a protected arm.

## Relationship to *Duncan v. Bonta*

The Supreme Court's consideration of *Duncan v. Bonta* — the Ninth Circuit's en banc decision upholding California's magazine ban — provides the most immediate context in which *Benson* will be felt. Prior to *Benson*, the landscape of magazine ban decisions was uniformly adverse to Second Amendment claimants at the appellate level, which could have counseled the Court to await further lower court development before granting review. *Benson* changes that calculus. A genuine split between a highest-court ruling in DC and the Ninth Circuit's contrary holding in *Duncan* gives the Supreme Court a direct circuit conflict — precisely the kind of conflict its Rule 10 identifies as a consideration for certiorari.

In my analysis, the Supreme Court will eventually need to address magazine capacity restrictions directly. The Court's hesitation to do so before now has allowed states with restrictive magazine laws to maintain those laws in defiance of what the *Heller* and *Bruen* frameworks would appear to require if applied straightforwardly. The *Benson* decision creates the legal infrastructure for the Court to accept review: a developed factual record, a clear constitutional ruling, and a conflict in authority that demands resolution.

## Implications

The DC Court of Appeals' ruling in *Benson v. United States* represents the most significant development in magazine ban litigation since *Bruen*. As I see it, the decision is constitutionally sound — it applies the *Bruen* framework faithfully to a restriction that lacks both textual support in the Second Amendment and any analogue in the historical tradition of firearms regulation. The decision's institutional origin — a court that has historically been receptive to firearms restrictions and that operates in the jurisdiction where the federal government itself is headquartered — gives it particular salience as evidence that the *Bruen* framework, applied rigorously, leads courts to invalidate magazine bans regardless of their political context.

The split in authority between *Benson* and the contrary federal circuit decisions means that gun owners' Second Amendment rights depend, at present, on the jurisdiction in which they reside. That kind of geographic variance in the scope of a fundamental constitutional right is precisely the circumstance the Supreme Court's certiorari jurisdiction exists to remedy. The pressure on the Court to grant review in *Duncan v. Bonta* or a similar case has increased materially, and the prospect of a definitive constitutional resolution of magazine capacity restrictions — one that applies uniformly across all jurisdictions — is closer than it has been at any point since *Heller*.
