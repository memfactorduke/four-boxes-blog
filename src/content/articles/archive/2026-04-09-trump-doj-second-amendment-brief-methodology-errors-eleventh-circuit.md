I've been watching the Trump DOJ's Second Amendment briefs closely, and I have to be direct: someone in Washington is not doing their job.

The Eleventh Circuit is currently considering *United States v. Alsenat*, a federal prosecution under 18 U.S.C. § 922(o) — the federal machine gun ban. The defendant, Maxon Alsenat, was convicted of selling Glock switch machine gun conversion devices. Ugly facts, straightforward prosecution. But the DOJ couldn't resist botching the constitutional analysis on the way to the finish line.

In its brief, the DOJ argued that machine guns fall outside the Second Amendment's plain text because they are "dangerous and unusual." Read that again. The government is claiming that the *plain text* of the Second Amendment excludes machine guns — and its reason is that they are dangerous and unusual.

That is not what *Bruen* says. That is not what *Heller* says. And any constitutional attorney worth his bar card knows it.

Here is the correct methodology, stated plainly. Step one under *Bruen*: does the plain text of the Second Amendment cover the conduct at issue? *Heller* defined "arms" as "any thing that a man wears for his defence, or takes into his hands, or useth in wrath to cast at or strike another." A machine gun is, unambiguously, a bearable arm. It is a weapon a person can carry. The plain text inquiry ends there — machine guns are "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment. Full stop.

Step two — and only step two — asks whether the government can justify its regulation by pointing to a historical tradition of analogous firearms regulations. *This* is where "dangerous and unusual" lives. The dangerous-and-unusual concept is a historical tradition argument, not a plain text exclusion. And critically, at step two, the burden is on the *government*, not the rights claimant, to produce historical evidence.

By collapsing these two distinct analytical steps into one, the DOJ is effectively asking the Eleventh Circuit to short-circuit *Bruen* before the historical analysis even begins. That is a methodological error — and it is not an innocent one.

What makes this especially damning is the contrast with *Barnett v. Raoul* in the Seventh Circuit, where the DOJ is challenging Illinois's PICA assault weapons ban. In that brief — the one carrying Harmeet Dhillon's imprimatur from the Civil Rights Division — the methodology is applied correctly. AR-15s and similar rifles are first identified as "arms" under the plain text. Then, and only then, does the brief argue that they are in common use and therefore not dangerous and unusual under the historical tradition analysis.

The *Barnett* brief is how you do it. The *Alsenat* brief is how you don't.

I have a theory about why we keep seeing this pattern. When Dhillon and her team at the Civil Rights Division are driving the brief, the analysis is sound. When career DOJ attorneys — many of whom spent years arguing the other side of these cases under the Biden administration — are left to their own devices, the analysis reverts to pre-*Bruen* thinking. Whether that is deliberate resistance or simple institutional inertia, the effect is the same: the Trump administration is undermining its own Second Amendment credibility in federal courts.

The Solicitor General's office, under D. John Sauer, needs to implement a mandatory review protocol for every Second Amendment brief filed by the DOJ. Every single one. The stakes are too high to let career holdovers quietly muddy the legal waters in cases that will shape Second Amendment doctrine for a generation.

*United States v. Alsenat* may be a machine gun case with unsympathetic facts, but bad facts make bad law — especially when your own lawyers are handing the courts a methodologically defective framework to work with. If the Eleventh Circuit adopts the DOJ's conflated plain text / dangerous-and-unusual analysis, that error will ripple through Second Amendment litigation for years.

Someone in the Trump DOJ needs to get control of this. The clock is ticking.
