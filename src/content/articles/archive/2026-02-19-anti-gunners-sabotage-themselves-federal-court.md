## Summary

A certiorari petition in **Shoenthal v. Ralph**, currently pending before the United States Supreme Court, challenges Illinois's categorical prohibition on bearing firearms on public transportation—a restriction the Seventh Circuit upheld despite the absence of any meaningful founding-era analogue. In opposing certiorari, Cook County State's Attorney Eileen O'Neill Burke filed a brief that, on close examination, makes a series of concessions highly damaging to the broader project of defending sensitive places legislation across the country. Most significantly, Cook County's brief expressly concedes that parks, sidewalks, and cities cannot be designated as sensitive places—a concession that directly contradicts the litigation positions of New York, California, Maryland, and Massachusetts, among others. Cook County's brief also attempts to attack the "comprehensive security" theory as novel and unsupported, without apparent awareness that the Supreme Court itself cited the precise sources articulating that doctrine in *Bruen*.

## Background

**Shoenthal v. Ralph** arises from Illinois's blanket prohibition on carrying a firearm on public transportation, including buses and trains, even by individuals holding valid concealed carry permits issued by the state. Benjamin Shoenthal, the Second Amendment plaintiff, prevailed initially in federal district court on the grounds that the restriction lacked historical foundation under the framework established in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). The Seventh Circuit reversed, holding that public transportation could be treated as a sensitive place under *Bruen* notwithstanding the absence of any founding-era restriction on armed travel in conveyances of any kind. Shoenthal subsequently petitioned the Supreme Court to grant certiorari and resolve the proper scope of the sensitive places doctrine—an issue on which the lower courts have produced conflicting and methodologically divergent decisions.

The Seventh Circuit's record on Second Amendment cases following *Bruen* has been notably unfavorable to Second Amendment claimants. The circuit has repeatedly upheld restrictions that other courts have found constitutionally infirm, and its sensitive places analysis in this case exemplifies the methodology *Bruen* was designed to foreclose: identifying broad functional categories of regulation rather than specific historical analogues with a genuine pedigree. Shoenthal's cert petition invited the Supreme Court to intervene and provide the definitional clarity the *Bruen* Court explicitly declined to supply.

## Cook County's Concession on Parks and Public Spaces

Among the various defendants in **Shoenthal**, Cook County filed a brief in opposition to the cert petition that warrants careful examination. On pages 15 through 16 of that brief, Cook County wrote:

> "Sensitive places recognized by the Supreme Court tend to be discreet confined places, usually specific buildings. As *Bruen* explained, larger open places like cities, sidewalks, and parks cannot be considered sensitive places because it would effectively nullify the Second Amendment right to categorically exclude them from its coverage."

The legal significance of this passage cannot be overstated. Cook County—the jurisdiction that encompasses Chicago and one of the most consistent proponents of broad firearms regulation in the United States—has conceded before the Supreme Court that cities, sidewalks, and parks fall outside the scope of permissible sensitive place designations. This concession is not a mere rhetorical flourish; it is a formal legal argument submitted in a Supreme Court filing. Other jurisdictions advancing litigation positions that depend on treating parks and public outdoor spaces as sensitive places will now face this concession as adverse precedent from a fellow government litigant.

As I have reviewed the post-*Bruen* litigation landscape, New York, California, Massachusetts, and Maryland have each enacted or defended statutes that effectively prohibit firearms in parks, public squares, and outdoor gathering spaces under various sensitive places theories. Cook County has now, in attempting to distinguish *Shoenthal*'s situation from the broader sensitive places problem, provided Second Amendment plaintiffs with a powerful admission from a sympathetic government source. The strategic damage to anti-firearms litigation postures in those jurisdictions is substantial.

## The Comprehensive Security Doctrine and Its Origins in *Bruen*

Cook County's brief also challenged the "comprehensive security" theory advanced by Shoenthal, characterizing it as a "novel" invention unsupported by law or the historical record. Cook County wrote that "Shoenthal's novel comprehensive security theory is not supported by the law or the facts" and predicted that "Shoenthal has no hope of prevailing on the merits" on this basis.

This characterization is demonstrably incorrect, and the error is of a particularly notable kind: Cook County failed to trace the sources that the *Bruen* Court itself expressly cited. The *Bruen* majority, in discussing historically recognized sensitive places, cited to pages 11 through 17 of an amicus brief filed by the Independent Institute. Those pages articulate the comprehensive security doctrine with precision. The Independent Institute brief states:

> "From the time of the Statute of Northampton through the American founding, the principle that weapons-free zones must be accompanied by increased sovereign security remained consistent."

The brief further explains that arms-free zones were "limited to areas in which the government provided the requisite security to compensate for the deprivation of the self-defense right." The doctrine is not Shoenthal's invention. It is the organizing principle that the founding generation applied to determine when the government could constitutionally disarm the citizenry in a particular location—and it is the principle the *Bruen* Court thought sufficiently important to incorporate by citation into its landmark decision.

The structure of the founding-era sensitive places tradition is clear from the historical record. Legislative chambers were secured by a sergeant-at-arms—a position created by the First Congress. Courthouses were protected by bailiffs, marshals, and the United States Marshals Service, established in 1789. Polling places were guarded by sheriffs. In each case, the government's power to disarm citizens at a particular location was conditioned on—and justified by—the government's assumption of responsibility for their protection within that space. Public transportation systems in modern American cities bear none of these characteristics.

## The Metal Detector Question and Heller's Technological Neutrality Principle

A secondary argument Cook County might advance, and one I have encountered in other litigation contexts, is that the founding era's lack of metal detection technology renders the "comprehensive security" requirement anachronistic. This objection misunderstands the nature of constitutional interpretation as the Supreme Court has practiced it in both *Heller* and *Bruen*.

**District of Columbia v. Heller** recognized that constitutional principles apply to modern circumstances using modern means. The Court applied the Second Amendment to handguns—weapons not in widespread use at the founding—because the constitutional protection extends to "arms" generally, including those that technology has made common and useful for self-defense. The same logic applies to comprehensive security: at the founding, a long gun could not be concealed, and visual inspection sufficed to identify armed individuals in controlled spaces. The modern handgun can be concealed under ordinary clothing, making visual inspection ineffective. The technological equivalent of visual inspection—the only means by which the founding generation could have enforced a disarmament requirement—is a modern screening apparatus: magnetometers, controlled entry points, and armed security personnel. This is not an expansion of the doctrine; it is a faithful application of the founding principle using means adapted to contemporary technology.

## The Beccaria Footnote

Cook County's brief included a footnote questioning why the plaintiffs relied on the writings of Cesare Beccaria, the eighteenth-century Italian philosopher and criminologist. The implicit suggestion was that Beccaria was an obscure or irrelevant source for Second Amendment analysis.

Page 17 of the same Independent Institute amicus brief that the *Bruen* Court cited addresses precisely this question. That page discusses the careful balance the founding generation drew in designing limited arms-free zones, describing the underlying principle as sounding "in the philosophy of Caesar Beccaria, an Italian philosopher who was highly regarded by the founding generation." The brief notes that Thomas Jefferson copied extensive passages from Beccaria's *On Crimes and Punishments* into his commonplace book, and that Beccaria's influence on the founding generation's understanding of the relationship between arms regulation and self-defense is well-documented in the secondary literature. The Independent Institute brief cites, among other sources, scholarship by Mark W. Smith on Beccaria's influence on the founders' understanding of the Second Amendment.

In short, Shoenthal cited Beccaria because the Supreme Court cited, in *Bruen*, a source that itself cited Beccaria. Cook County's footnote criticizing this citation demonstrates an incomplete engagement with the *Bruen* decision's own intellectual architecture.

## Implications

**Shoenthal v. Ralph** presents the Supreme Court with a genuine opportunity to provide the definitional clarity that *Bruen* left open. The lower courts have fragmented on the proper scope of the sensitive places doctrine, and that fragmentation has enabled a range of state legislative responses that differ dramatically in their fidelity to historical methodology. A Supreme Court ruling that adopts the comprehensive security framework—holding that a location may be designated sensitive only where the government provides armed security, controlled access, and weapons screening as a condition of disarmament—would provide a coherent and historically grounded limiting principle.

Cook County's brief, whatever its strategic intent, has advanced the constitutional analysis in an unanticipated direction. By conceding that parks, sidewalks, and cities cannot be sensitive places, Cook County has effectively established a ceiling on sensitive place designations that forecloses some of the most expansive post-*Bruen* legislative responses. By attacking the comprehensive security theory without adequately engaging the sources that the Supreme Court itself relied upon, Cook County has underscored the weakness of the historical case for designating public transportation—or any comparably unprotected space—as a zone from which law-abiding armed citizens may be excluded. The anti-firearms litigation posture in this case has, in attempting to hold a narrow line, sacrificed ground on a much broader front.
