## Summary

The New York Times published a prominent Sunday story headlined "Federal Judges Warning of a Judicial Crisis: Fault Supreme Court's Emergency Orders," presenting anonymous commentary from a small number of federal district court judges who expressed displeasure with the Supreme Court's use of the emergency docket — also called the shadow docket — to reverse lower court decisions in cases involving the Trump administration's policy agenda. In my analysis, the Times story has, entirely by accident, produced the most effective possible advertisement for Attorney General Pam Bondi's Department of Justice: it documents, through the complaints of those being defeated, that the government's legal arguments are prevailing at the Supreme Court with remarkable consistency against lower court rulings that have exceeded the proper bounds of judicial authority. The story also provides a timely opportunity to explain the shadow docket, why it exists, and how the oral argument the prior day in **Louisiana v. Callais** connects to the broader pattern of the Supreme Court reasserting the proper limits of lower court power.

## Background: The New York Times Story

The story reports that more than three dozen sitting federal judges — described in the article's subtitle as sharing "concerns about risk to the court's legitimacy" — responded anonymously to a Times survey focused on the Supreme Court's emergency orders in cases involving Trump administration policies. These judges, drawn from jurisdictions that the Times selected — New York, San Francisco, Chicago, the District of Columbia, Boston, and similar localities — expressed frustration that the Supreme Court's emergency orders are "brief" and "opaque," meaning the Court does not always explain its reasoning at length when it reverses a lower court on the emergency docket.

Several observations about this story are warranted before analyzing its substance.

First, the sample is not representative. The approximately three dozen judges who responded represent something in the range of two to three percent of all federal judges in the United States. The jurisdictions from which they were drawn are the same jurisdictions where anti-administration plaintiffs have chosen to file their lawsuits against Trump administration policies precisely because those venues are most likely to produce favorable results. These are the jurisdictions where, as I have observed, lower courts have been most aggressive in issuing nationwide injunctions and restraining orders against constitutionally grounded executive actions — and therefore the same jurisdictions that have been most frequently reversed by the Supreme Court. The survey is, in other words, biased toward the exact population of judges who have the most reason to complain about being reversed.

Second, the judges responded anonymously. I find it notable that members of the federal judiciary — officials who hold lifetime appointments under Article III, who cannot be removed except by impeachment, and who face no electoral accountability whatsoever — declined to attach their names to criticism of the Supreme Court. The suggestion that their views were too controversial to state on the record is difficult to reconcile with the institutional independence the federal judiciary is supposed to possess.

## What the Shadow Docket Actually Is

Because the Times story uses the phrase "shadow docket" with a pejorative connotation that I do not accept, it is worth taking a moment to explain the emergency docket accurately.

There are two types of proceedings at the United States Supreme Court. The merits docket involves cases accepted for full review through the certiorari process — full briefing by both sides, oral argument before all nine justices, extended deliberation, and written opinions that may run to dozens of pages explaining the Court's reasoning. This is the process that produced landmark decisions like **District of Columbia v. Heller** in 2008, **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** in 2022, and **Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization** in 2022.

The emergency docket — the so-called shadow docket — is something different. It is the mechanism through which the Supreme Court acts in real time on requests for emergency relief, principally stays of lower court injunctions and other time-sensitive orders. When a lower court issues an injunction that unlawfully restrains a federal executive action, the party harmed by that injunction cannot wait the months or years that full merits review would require. The emergency docket allows the Supreme Court to step in promptly to correct the error.

Justice Amy Coney Barrett has explained, in a Fox News Sunday interview, the institutional reason why the Court does not typically write extensive opinions in emergency docket cases: these are preliminary determinations, not final judgments on the merits. Writing a long opinion on the emergency docket risks giving the public and the lower courts the false impression that the issue has been definitively resolved when in fact it has not. The Court is deciding who bears the burden pending final resolution, not necessarily how the case will ultimately come out.

## The Real Meaning of the Story

Having established what the shadow docket is, the real import of the Times story becomes clear. The anonymous judges who participated in the survey are unhappy not because the Supreme Court is acting improperly, but because the Supreme Court is consistently telling them that they are acting improperly. Every time the Supreme Court grants an emergency stay in a case involving the Trump administration, it is implicitly or explicitly finding that the lower court order being stayed was legally erroneous.

Attorney General Pam Bondi's Department of Justice — specifically, Solicitor General John Sauer and the team at the Office of the Solicitor General — has been bringing these emergency applications to the Supreme Court throughout 2025 with extraordinary success. The Times story, by documenting the frustration of the judges who are being reversed, inadvertently confirms the scale of that success. If Bondi's team were losing these emergency applications, there would be nothing for these judges to complain about. They are complaining precisely because they are losing.

The constitutional principle that underlies these results is straightforward. President Trump was elected under Article II of the United States Constitution. The executive power of the United States vests in the President. Federal judges appointed under Article III do not have the constitutional authority to invade the province of the Article II executive branch on matters of core presidential authority, including immigration enforcement, border security, the deployment of military forces, and the management of the executive branch workforce. The lower courts have been repeatedly attempting to do exactly that, and the Supreme Court has been repeatedly telling them to stop.

## The Connection to Louisiana v. Callais

The Times story and the Supreme Court's emergency docket activity provide useful context for understanding what is happening in **Louisiana v. Callais**, which was argued before the Supreme Court the day before the Times story ran. As I have analyzed in detail, **Callais** presents the question whether Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act, as applied to require the creation of majority-minority congressional districts, violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.

The connection between the two subjects is structural. In **Callais**, as in the emergency docket cases, the question is whether an inferior federal enactment — in **Callais**, a statute; in the emergency docket cases, a lower court injunction — must give way to the superior commands of the Constitution. The Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause is part of the Constitution. The Voting Rights Act is a statute. Statutes that violate the Constitution are invalid.

If the Supreme Court rules in Louisiana's favor in **Callais**, holding that Section 2's requirement of race-conscious redistricting violates the Equal Protection Clause, the political consequences will be significant: upward of nineteen majority-minority congressional districts drawn under Section 2 compulsion could be redrawn on race-neutral criteria, likely resulting in a meaningful shift in the composition of the House of Representatives toward larger and more durable Republican majorities. That structural political shift would provide substantial protection for Second Amendment rights in the legislative arena for years, if not decades.

## Implications

The Times story and the **Callais** argument together illustrate a single underlying reality: the Supreme Court, under the leadership of its current majority, is consistently enforcing the hierarchy of American law — the principle that the Constitution is supreme, that inferior enactments cannot contradict it, and that courts exceeding their authority will be corrected. That is not a judicial crisis. That is the judiciary functioning as designed.

The anonymous judges who participated in the Times survey appear to believe that federal courts should be entitled to restrain presidential action without effective check from the Supreme Court. That view is constitutionally untenable. The emergency docket exists precisely to prevent the lower courts from becoming unaccountable veto points over the democratic process. Its use under current conditions is not an aberration — it is a correction.

I will continue to monitor both the ongoing emergency docket litigation and the pending decision in **Louisiana v. Callais** and provide analysis as the Court moves forward.
