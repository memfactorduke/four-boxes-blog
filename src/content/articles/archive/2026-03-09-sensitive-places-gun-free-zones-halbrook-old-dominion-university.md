## Summary

Following the murder of an ROTC instructor at Old Dominion University -- a state school in Virginia with a weapons-free zone policy -- Second Amendment scholar Stephen Halbrook joins me on The Four Boxes Diner to examine the constitutional boundaries of government-mandated gun-free zones. The discussion centers on the "sensitive places" doctrine that emerged from *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and was refined in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association, Inc. v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). Halbrook argues that the historical record supports a narrow reading of the sensitive-places exception: only locations where the government provides comprehensive armed security -- such as courthouses, legislative chambers, and polling places -- qualify as places where firearms may constitutionally be prohibited. The conversation also addresses the pending Supreme Court case *Wolford v. Lopez*, which challenges Hawaii's expansive prohibition on carrying firearms in premises open to the public.

## The Old Dominion University Tragedy and the Gun-Free Zone Problem

I was prompted to explore this issue by the killing of an ROTC instructor at Old Dominion University, a public university in Virginia. The assailant -- a Sierra Leone-born immigrant who reportedly supported ISIS -- carried out the attack in a campus environment that maintained not merely a gun-free zone policy but a comprehensive weapons-free zone policy, prohibiting all weapons on the premises. I noted a grim irony: the students and ROTC personnel who ultimately subdued and killed the attacker did so with a knife -- a weapon that itself would have violated the university's prohibition.

The incident illustrates the central tension in the gun-free zone debate, as I see it. Policies that disarm law-abiding citizens do not prevent those with violent intent from carrying out attacks. They instead ensure that potential victims lack the means of self-defense during the critical minutes before law enforcement can respond. This observation frames the legal question at the heart of my discussion: what does the Constitution actually permit when governments designate certain locations as firearms-prohibited zones?

## The Origins of the Sensitive-Places Doctrine

As I've discussed with Halbrook, the phrase "sensitive places" entered Second Amendment jurisprudence through Justice Antonin Scalia's majority opinion in *Heller*. In that case, the central question was whether the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms and whether the District of Columbia could ban the possession of handguns in the home. The Court answered both questions in favor of individual rights.

In the course of the opinion, however, Justice Scalia included dicta stating that "nothing in our opinion should be taken to cast doubt on" certain "longstanding" regulatory measures, including bans on firearms possession "in sensitive places such as schools and government buildings." Halbrook emphasized that this language was dicta -- not a holding of the Court -- and that the word "presumptively" appeared in the relevant passage, indicating that such restrictions are probably lawful but not necessarily so. Presumptions, he noted, can be rebutted.

When the Supreme Court took up the issue more directly in *Bruen*, the Court identified three specific historical examples of places where firearms prohibitions had been imposed at or near the founding: polling places, legislative assemblies, and courthouses. In my analysis, the Court did not hold that these were the only permissible sensitive places, but it established these as the paradigmatic examples and provided a framework for analyzing any claimed sensitive-places restriction.

## The Common Thread: Comprehensive Government-Provided Security

Halbrook argued that the key characteristic uniting the three categories identified in *Bruen* is that each involved comprehensive security provided by the government. This is not merely a coincidence of history; it reflects the constitutional logic of the sensitive-places doctrine.

**Courthouses** provide the clearest illustration. They are secured by sheriff's deputies or other law enforcement personnel. Individual courtrooms have bailiffs. Entry is controlled, typically through screening. The government has, in effect, taken over the security function that individuals would otherwise provide for themselves through the exercise of their right to bear arms. Halbrook traced this tradition back to English law and the Statute of Northampton of 1328, under which one could not appear before the king's justices while armed. The rationale was straightforward: courts conduct serious business involving charged emotions -- criminal sentencing, divorce proceedings, civil disputes -- and the combination of these tensions with the authority of the judicial process demanded controlled conditions.

**Legislative assemblies** operate on the same principle. Armed security has historically been provided through sergeants-at-arms and other officers of the legislature. Halbrook referenced the 1950 incident in which Puerto Rican nationalists opened fire in the United States Congress, underscoring the reality that legislative bodies attract political passions that can turn violent and therefore require professional security measures.

**Polling places** present a more historically specific case. Halbrook pointed to Delaware as a particularly instructive example. During and immediately after the American Revolution, violent disputes between patriots and loyalists -- including killings and the burning of homes -- created an urgent need for security at locations where political activity occurred. States such as Maryland required the county sheriff to be present at each polling place. The firearms prohibitions at polling places thus arose in the context of comprehensive law enforcement presence, not as standalone disarmament mandates.

Smith added an important historical observation regarding the nature of voting at the founding. Prior to the adoption of the Australian ballot system in the late nineteenth century, voting in America was a public act. Voters declared their choices openly rather than casting secret ballots. This public character of voting heightened the potential for intimidation and violence, reinforcing the need for both security and restrictions on firearms at the polling place. It also distinguishes the founding-era polling place from the modern-day elementary school library where many Americans now cast their votes -- often without any security presence at all.

## The Critical Distinction: Security Versus Mere Disarmament

In my analysis of the historical record, the central point that emerges is a constitutional principle: the government may prohibit firearms in locations where it has affirmatively assumed the obligation of providing armed security for those present. Where the government merely declares a location to be a gun-free zone without providing any alternative means of protection, it has not established the conditions that historically justified such restrictions, as I see it.

Halbrook drew a sharp distinction between a courthouse with deputies, screening, and bailiffs and a 300-acre public park where the only available security response is a call to 911. The latter, he argued, cannot credibly be called a "sensitive place" within the meaning of the constitutional tradition. Yet jurisdictions have enacted gun bans covering precisely such locations -- large wooded parklands, beaches, and other outdoor spaces where no comprehensive security apparatus exists.

The same criticism applies to indoor commercial spaces. This concern is directly implicated in *Wolford v. Lopez*, the pending Supreme Court case involving Hawaii's prohibition on carrying firearms into any premises open to the public unless the business owner affirmatively posts a sign inviting armed entry. Halbrook observed that few businesses wish to involve themselves in the political controversy of posting such signage, meaning the law operates as a de facto prohibition on carry in virtually all commercial establishments -- without any corresponding obligation on the part of the state to provide security.

## The Historical Record and the Bruen Framework

Under the analytical framework established in *Bruen*, once a modern regulation implicates conduct protected by the Second Amendment's plain text, the burden shifts to the government to identify a historical tradition of analogous firearms regulation. Halbrook noted that the historical research -- including a study by Dr. Angus McClellan examining the original states during the period of the Bill of Rights' adoption -- confirms that the places identified by the Supreme Court as sensitive all shared the characteristic of comprehensive government-provided security.

This historical evidence matters because it constrains the sensitive-places doctrine. If the only founding-era firearms bans at specific locations were implemented alongside comprehensive security measures, then modern governments cannot invoke the sensitive-places exception to justify firearms prohibitions at locations where no such security exists. The doctrine is not a license for wholesale disarmament of public spaces; it is a narrow exception premised on the government's affirmative assumption of the security obligation that armed citizens would otherwise fulfill for themselves.

## Conclusion

In my analysis, the sensitive-places doctrine, properly understood, is far narrower than many jurisdictions have assumed. The historical record supports firearms prohibitions only at locations where the government provides comprehensive armed security -- courthouses, legislative chambers, and polling places at the founding being the paradigmatic examples. Expansive bans covering parks, beaches, commercial businesses, and university campuses -- locations where the government provides no meaningful security alternative -- find no support in the constitutional tradition. As the Supreme Court considers *Wolford v. Lopez*, the scope of this doctrine may receive its most definitive treatment yet, with significant implications for gun-free zone policies across the country.
