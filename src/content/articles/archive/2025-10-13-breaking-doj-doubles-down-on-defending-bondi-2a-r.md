## Summary

Assistant Attorney General Harlan Dillon, head of the Civil Rights Division of the United States Department of Justice, has issued a detailed public statement on the social media platform X defending Attorney General Pam Bondi's Second Amendment record and describing the affirmative litigation steps the Department has taken under her leadership to advance the constitutional right to keep and bear arms. The statement came in response to continued controversy within portions of the Second Amendment community regarding the membership list provision in **Ree v. ATF** and broader questions about whether the Bondi DOJ has been sufficiently committed to Second Amendment rights. I find the statement to be largely accurate and, on balance, a strong affirmation of the administration's commitment to protecting the Second Amendment.

## Background

Following a district court's sua sponte order in **Ree v. ATF** directing Second Amendment organizations to disclose their membership lists to the federal government — an order the DOJ itself promptly moved to vacate — a vocal segment of the Second Amendment community called for Attorney General Bondi's resignation. I have consistently maintained that this criticism is misplaced. The DOJ neither sought nor desired the membership list disclosure, and it acted swiftly and cooperatively with the plaintiff organizations to have that provision struck. The broader record of the Bondi Department of Justice on Second Amendment matters is, in my assessment, positive and in many respects historically significant.

Against this backdrop, Assistant Attorney General Harlan Dillon — the Senate-confirmed head of the DOJ's Civil Rights Division and the principal administrator of the department's Second Amendment Task Force — took the unusual step of issuing a public video statement specifically defending the administration's Second Amendment record. The statement is notable both for what it reveals about the DOJ's ongoing litigation posture and for the candor with which it acknowledges the scope of the administration's pro-Second Amendment activity.

## The Dillon Statement: The Ree Case

On the central controversy, Dillon's statement is unequivocal. She confirms that the DOJ never requested the membership lists at issue in **Ree v. ATF**. She confirms that when the district court entered its order sua sponte directing disclosure, the DOJ and the plaintiffs jointly moved to vacate it. She confirms that the court granted that motion. She further explains that compelled disclosure of private membership lists is inconsistent with longstanding DOJ policy — policy rooted in the constitutional precedent of **NAACP v. Alabama** (1958), which recognized that forcing advocacy organizations to reveal their membership to the government chills the First Amendment right of free association. The DOJ's position, as Dillon articulates it, is that the government has no right to know the names of people who belong to Second Amendment organizations.

Dillon also explains the broader injunctive relief obtained in **Ree v. ATF**. The Fifth Circuit district court's order enjoins the federal prohibition on federally licensed dealers selling handguns to adults between the ages of 18 and 20. The DOJ worked cooperatively with the plaintiffs to ensure that this injunction was as broad as possible consistent with the Supreme Court's decision in **Trump v. CASA**, which imposed limitations on the use of universal injunctions. The result is circuit-wide injunctive relief supplemented by coverage for the plaintiff organizations' members across the country — a significant expansion of Second Amendment rights for young adults.

## Affirmative Second Amendment Litigation

Beyond defending against criticism, Dillon's statement describes the affirmative litigation steps the DOJ has taken to advance Second Amendment rights. Of particular note is the DOJ's intervention in **Harrel v. Raoul**, the challenge to Illinois's ban on semi-automatic rifles including the AR-15 and other popular firearms. Dillon personally argued on behalf of the United States in the Seventh Circuit, advancing the position that Illinois's ban is unconstitutional under **Bruen** and **Heller**. This represents an extraordinary use of DOJ resources — the head of the Civil Rights Division appearing personally before a federal circuit court to argue for the invalidation of a state assault weapons ban.

The constitutional basis for the DOJ's position in the Illinois case is straightforward. Under **Heller**, arms in common use by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes are presumptively protected by the Second Amendment. The AR-15 is the most popular rifle in the United States, with tens of millions in civilian ownership. Justice Thomas, writing for the Court in **Bruen**, further emphasized that the Second Amendment's text covers all bearable arms and that the government bears the burden of justifying restrictions through historical analogues. Illinois's categorical ban on an entire class of commonly owned semi-automatic firearms cannot survive this standard.

The DOJ has also indicated it is prepared to file similar briefs in other circuits where states have enacted comparable assault weapons bans. This represents a systematic, affirmative deployment of the department's litigation resources in favor of Second Amendment rights — a stark contrast to the posture of prior administrations.

## Originalism and the Second Amendment

Dillon's statement also addresses the jurisprudential framework that animates the DOJ's approach to Second Amendment litigation. The administration has adopted originalism — the interpretive methodology that seeks to understand constitutional text according to its original public meaning at the time of enactment — as its governing approach to constitutional interpretation. This is directly relevant to the Second Amendment because **Bruen's** historical tradition test is itself an originalist methodology. A DOJ committed to originalism is a DOJ that will consistently press for the application of the **Bruen** framework, rather than seeking to water it down or evade it.

I have consistently argued that the most important thing the Second Amendment community can do is to ensure that originalist judges are confirmed to the federal courts and that the executive branch advances originalist positions in its litigation. The Dillon statement reflects an administration that understands this and is acting accordingly.

## Assessment

I want to be candid about my assessment of Dillon's statement. I agree with virtually everything she says. Where I have methodological disagreements, they are nuanced and do not undermine my overall positive evaluation of the Bondi DOJ's Second Amendment record. Attorney General Bondi has been in office since approximately mid-February 2025 — roughly eight months at the time of this statement. In that period, the DOJ has achieved a record of approximately 25 wins and zero losses at the United States Supreme Court across all areas of its litigation portfolio. Nobody fires the head coach when the team is 25 and 0.

The calls for Attorney General Bondi's resignation that emerged from portions of the Second Amendment community were, in my view, both factually uninformed and strategically counterproductive. The political reality is that replacing Attorney General Bondi would require a new Senate confirmation process in a chamber where several moderate Republican senators would need to be held in line. The risk of a worse outcome — a recess appointment or a prolonged vacancy — is real. The case for demanding Bondi's removal is weak. The case for holding her accountable when mistakes occur — as I have done and will continue to do — is sound. The case for calling for her removal based on a mischaracterized judicial order is not.

## Implications

The Dillon statement, taken together with the vacatur of the membership list provision in **Ree v. ATF** and the DOJ's active intervention in the Illinois assault weapons ban litigation, presents a coherent picture of a Department of Justice that is genuinely and substantively committed to advancing Second Amendment rights. That picture is not without blemish — no large institution operates perfectly — but it reflects serious, high-level engagement with the Second Amendment as a constitutional priority. For Second Amendment advocates, the appropriate posture is continued engagement, informed accountability, and strategic support for an administration that is, on balance, doing more for the right to keep and bear arms than any prior administration in modern history.
