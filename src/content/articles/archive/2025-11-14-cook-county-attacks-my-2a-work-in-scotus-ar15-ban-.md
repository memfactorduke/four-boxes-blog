## Summary

In the **Viramontes v. Cook County** AR-15 ban case now pending before the United States Supreme Court, Cook County filed its opposition brief urging the Court to deny certiorari — and in the process took a pointed shot at my published legal scholarship and at The Four Boxes Diner. In footnote one of Cook County's brief, the respondents attempted to discredit two law review articles published in the *Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy* — one of which is my own work — by dismissing them as submissions to a "prerurem web page with no discernible acceptance criteria" carrying no more persuasive weight than "a blog post or personal website." As I shall explain, this tactic backfired spectacularly.

## Background: If You're Taking Flak, You're Over the Target

There is an old saying among aviators: if you're taking flak, you know you're over the target. Cook County's decision to specifically attack my scholarship in a brief to the United States Supreme Court is, I submit, a confirmation that the arguments I have developed and published on the "in common use" test and its application to semi-automatic rifle bans are doing exactly what good legal scholarship should do — influencing the debate where it matters most.

The **Viramontes v. Cook County** case arises from Cook County, Illinois, which banned AR-15s and semi-automatic rifles. The Seventh Circuit upheld the ban. The Second Amendment claimants — those challenging the ban — petitioned the Supreme Court for certiorari, arguing that the Seventh Circuit's decision is wrong and that the Court must intervene. The case is before the Court, and Cook County filed its opposition brief arguing that certiorari should be denied.

## What Cook County Did in Footnote One

In footnote one of Cook County's opposition brief, the respondents wrote the following — which I will quote directly because it warrants careful attention:

> "While Viramontes offers two citations to supposed authority on the proper analysis of common use, those citations are not to formal academic journals, but rather to submissions to a prerurem web page with no discernible acceptance criteria discussed in the prerurem submissions for the *Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy*. Needless to say, such submissions carry no more persuasive weight here than a blog post or personal website."

Cook County was attacking two articles published in the *Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy* per curium, including one authored by me — Mark W. Smith — and one by Peter R. Patterson, both of which analyze the "in common use" test and its correct application to firearms ban cases. The petitioners in **Viramontes** cited to these articles in their brief to the Supreme Court in support of their Second Amendment arguments.

## Why This Argument Is Meritless

Cook County's attack is a classic *ad hominem* fallacy: rather than engage with the substance of the scholarship, it attacks the publication format. Let me explain why this is both legally and intellectually bankrupt.

### The Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy Is a Highly Respected Publication

The *Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy* is among the most cited law journals in the country. It has published articles by accomplished attorneys, federal judges, and legal academics for decades. The per curium section of the journal publishes shorter pieces and commentary that undergoes the same institutional editorial process of the broader journal. Courts regularly cite articles from the *Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy*, and my work has appeared alongside distinguished contributors.

### The Supreme Court Itself Has Cited Per Curium Articles from This Journal

Here is the critical fact that Cook County appears to have overlooked — or chose to ignore. Supreme Court Justice Samuel Alito, in a concurrence joined by Justice Clarence Thomas in **VanDerStok v. Garland**, cited an article published in the per curium section of the *Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy*. That article was authored by a federal district court judge — one who happens to be a former Clarence Thomas clerk — who has issued notable Second Amendment rulings, including a ruling that banning guns in post offices is unconstitutional. If the per curium section of the *Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy* is good enough to be cited by Supreme Court Justices Alito and Thomas in an official opinion of the United States Supreme Court, Cook County's characterization of such publications as the equivalent of blog posts is not merely wrong — it is embarrassing advocacy.

Cook County chose to attack the source of the argument rather than the argument itself. In logic, this is known as an *ad hominem* attack, and it is a recognized logical fallacy precisely because the validity of an argument has nothing to do with the identity or platform of the person making it. The fact that Cook County felt compelled to specifically address my scholarship in a Supreme Court brief is, if anything, evidence that the arguments I have made are sufficiently powerful to require rebuttal — and that the only rebuttal available is an attack on the messenger rather than the message.

## The Substance of the Scholarship Cook County Attacked

The articles cited by the **Viramontes** petitioners — including my piece in the *Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy* — analyze the proper legal framework for evaluating whether a particular firearm is protected by the Second Amendment under the "in common use" versus "dangerous and unusual" dichotomy established in **Heller**.

My core argument, which I am highly confident will ultimately be vindicated by the Supreme Court, is as follows:

1. **Heller** established that the Second Amendment protects arms that are "in common use" for lawful purposes by law-abiding citizens.
2. Arms that are "dangerous and unusual" are not protected.
3. These two categories are mutually exclusive: an arm cannot be both in common use and dangerous and unusual simultaneously.
4. The AR-15 and other semi-automatic rifles are, by every available metric, in common use in America — with between 20 and 30 million such rifles in civilian hands.
5. Therefore, the AR-15 cannot be "dangerous and unusual" within the meaning of **Heller**, and any ban on these rifles is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment.

This argument is not fringe legal scholarship. It is a straightforward application of **Heller**'s text and logic, and it has been cited by both the petitioners seeking to vindicate Second Amendment rights before the Supreme Court and, now that they have felt compelled to respond to it, by Cook County in its opposition brief.

## What Cook County's Attack Really Tells Us

When the government respondent in a Supreme Court case finds it necessary to specifically call out a legal scholar's work in a footnote and attempt to discredit it by attacking the publication format rather than the substance of the argument, it tells us something important: the argument is strong. A weak argument that poses no threat gets ignored; an argument that must be explicitly neutralized is one that the government fears might actually influence the Court.

The Bevis standard that the Seventh Circuit applied to uphold the Cook County AR-15 ban is a bastardization of the Supreme Court's Second Amendment rules. Cook County knows it, which is why rather than defend the merits of the Seventh Circuit's analysis on firm historical grounds, it resorts to attacking the credibility of the scholars who have exposed its flaws.

## Implications

The **Viramontes v. Cook County** case before the Supreme Court — and the scholarship that Cook County saw fit to attack in its brief — sit at the center of the most important Second Amendment question pending in federal courts: may the government ban the most popular rifle in America?

The answer, under **Heller**'s "in common use" framework, is clearly no. And I am, as I have stated before, highly confident that when the Supreme Court ultimately resolves this question, the analysis I have developed and published — the analysis Cook County took a pot shot at in footnote one of its Supreme Court brief — will be vindicated.

Taking flak means you're over the target. I'll take that as encouragement.
