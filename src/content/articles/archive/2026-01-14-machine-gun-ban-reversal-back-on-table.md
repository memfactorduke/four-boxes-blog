## Summary

Two prominent judges on the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit — Judge James Ho and Judge Don Willett, both widely regarded as leading candidates for potential elevation to the Supreme Court — have publicly flagged serious constitutional concerns about 18 U.S.C. § 922(o), the so-called Hughes Amendment, which prohibits private citizens from possessing machine guns manufactured after May 19, 1986. Their intervention reignites a constitutional debate first significantly joined by then-Judge Samuel Alito in his dissent in **United States v. Rybar**, and raises foundational questions under both the Second Amendment and the Enumerated Powers doctrine.

## Background

### The Hughes Amendment: 18 U.S.C. § 922(o)

The Hughes Amendment, enacted as part of the Firearm Owners Protection Act of 1986, added subsection (o) to 18 U.S.C. § 922, prohibiting private citizens from possessing or transferring any machine gun manufactured or transferred after May 19, 1986. The practical effect is that the transferable machine gun registry was closed as of that date; only those fully automatic weapons already registered with the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives (ATF) under the National Firearms Act (NFA) prior to the cutoff date are legally transferable to private citizens.

It bears emphasis that the Hughes Amendment does not categorically prohibit civilian ownership of all machine guns. A citizen may lawfully acquire and possess a pre-1986 registered machine gun, provided the purchaser completes the NFA registration process — including the payment of a $200 transfer tax and submission to a background investigation — and the weapon is one of those already in the transferable registry. The prohibition applies exclusively to post-1986 manufactured machine guns.

### Justice Alito's Dissent in United States v. Rybar

The constitutional infirmity of § 922(o) was first prominently identified by then-Judge Samuel Alito, dissenting in **United States v. Rybar**, 103 F.3d 273 (3d Cir. 1996). The Third Circuit majority upheld § 922(o) as a valid exercise of Congress's commerce power under the Commerce Clause. Judge Alito dissented, arguing that the statute lacked any connection to interstate commerce and therefore exceeded Congress's enumerated powers — a dissent that, viewed through the lens of subsequent Supreme Court jurisprudence, has proved remarkably prescient.

## The Enumerated Powers Problem

### Congress's Authority to Enact § 922(o)

To understand why § 922(o) presents a fundamental constitutional question, one must begin with the structural architecture of federal legislative authority. Unlike states, which possess plenary police power, the federal government possesses only the powers enumerated in the Constitution. As Article I makes clear, Congress may legislate only pursuant to a granted power.

Historically, federal gun regulations have been justified under two principal enumerated powers: the Taxing Power (Article I, § 8, cl. 1) and the Commerce Clause (Article I, § 8, cl. 3). The NFA itself, enacted in 1934, was structured as a taxing statute — it imposed a $200 tax on the transfer of machine guns and required registration with the Treasury Department. The constitutional validity of the NFA as a taxing measure has long been accepted by the courts precisely because it operates as a genuine revenue measure, however small the revenue collected.

### No Taxing Nexus for § 922(o)

The Hughes Amendment presents a categorically different constitutional posture from the NFA. Section 922(o) does not impose a tax. It does not generate any revenue. Indeed, and critically, the ATF specifically refuses to accept applications to register post-1986 machine guns — meaning that the government will not even accept the $200 NFA tax for such weapons. There is, in short, no taxing nexus connecting § 922(o) to Article I's taxing power.

This point is not academic. If the taxing power cannot sustain § 922(o), the government must rely on the Commerce Clause — and that reliance, as Judges Ho and Willett have now flagged, presents severe constitutional difficulties.

### The Commerce Clause Defect

When Congress enacted the Hughes Amendment, it did not include in the statutory text any reference to interstate commerce. The statute, as written, contains no jurisdictional element — no requirement that the prohibited machine gun traveled in interstate commerce, affected interstate commerce, or bore any connection whatsoever to commerce among the states.

The significance of this drafting decision cannot be overstated. In **United States v. Lopez**, 514 U.S. 549 (1995), the Supreme Court struck down the Gun-Free School Zones Act of 1990 in part because that statute lacked a jurisdictional element requiring a connection to interstate commerce. The Court held that Congress cannot simply invoke the Commerce Clause without demonstrating that the regulated activity substantially affects interstate commerce. Where no jurisdictional hook exists in the statutory text, the court is left to evaluate whether the regulated class of activity — here, intrastate possession of a post-1986 machine gun — constitutionally satisfies that standard.

I submit that a purely intrastate possession of a firearm — a machine gun never transported in interstate commerce, manufactured in-state, and possessed entirely within one state — presents the same Commerce Clause vulnerability the Supreme Court identified in **Lopez**. The fact that Congress omitted any interstate commerce element from § 922(o) compounds this problem: a jury presented with a § 922(o) charge is under no instruction, and the statute imposes no requirement, to find that the machine gun in question touched interstate commerce.

## The Second Amendment Dimension

### Heller and the Common Use Standard

Beyond the Enumerated Powers question, § 922(o) faces mounting constitutional pressure under the Second Amendment itself. In **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), the Supreme Court held that the Second Amendment protects firearms "in common use at the time" for "lawful purposes like self-defense." Justice Scalia's majority opinion suggested, in dicta, that particularly dangerous and unusual weapons could be regulated — but the Court tied this exception to weapons not in "common use."

The question of whether machine guns fall within "common use" is one courts have wrestled with post-**Heller**. Most circuits have held that machine guns, precisely because they are so heavily regulated that very few are in civilian hands, are not "in common use" for Second Amendment purposes. But this reasoning is circular: the scarcity of transferable machine guns is itself the product of the Hughes Amendment's registry closure. Accepting this argument is to bootstrap the constitutionality of the restriction from the restriction itself.

### Bruen and the Text-History-Tradition Framework

**New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), significantly complicates the government's defense of § 922(o). Under **Bruen**'s text-history-tradition standard, the government must identify a historical analog from the founding era or Reconstruction era supporting the type of restriction at issue. The question becomes: was there any founding-era tradition of categorically prohibiting private citizens from possessing the most militarily effective small arms of the day?

The historical record suggests no such tradition existed. To the contrary, the Founders were well aware that armed citizens with military-grade weapons were essential to the preservation of liberty — a principle embodied in the Second Amendment's reference to "a well regulated Militia." A founding-era government that had just secured independence through armed citizen-soldiers would have found deeply alien the notion that those same citizens could be categorically prohibited from possessing the most effective personal weapons available.

### The Significance of Judges Ho and Willett

Judges James Ho and Don Willett are not peripheral figures in the federal judiciary. Both are highly regarded jurists with significant intellectual influence on Second Amendment jurisprudence. Both have been prominently mentioned as potential Supreme Court nominees. Their public identification of § 922(o)'s constitutional vulnerabilities signals that these arguments are now squarely within the mainstream of serious federal judicial thought — not merely academic exercises.

When judges of this caliber and visibility flag constitutional concerns about a longstanding federal statute, the legal community takes notice. Their intervention marks the beginning of what may be a sustained judicial reexamination of the Hughes Amendment.

## Implications

### The Path to Litigation

The constitutional arguments against § 922(o) have been developed over decades, beginning with Justice Alito's dissent in **Rybar** and continuing through the post-**Heller** and post-**Bruen** academic and judicial literature. What has been lacking, until now, is a sufficiently compelling judicial signal that these arguments are viable before the courts.

Judges Ho and Willett's public statements change the calculus. They provide both the intellectual credibility and the judicial endorsement necessary to encourage Second Amendment litigants to press these arguments vigorously. A well-constructed challenge to § 922(o) — one that carefully develops both the Commerce Clause and the Second Amendment arguments — would have a realistic prospect of success before a circuit court willing to apply **Bruen** rigorously.

### Congressional Authority and Structural Limits

This episode also illustrates a broader constitutional principle that I believe deserves far more attention than it typically receives: the federal government's power to regulate firearms is structurally constrained not only by the Second Amendment but by the entire architecture of enumerated powers. When Congress acts without a legitimate constitutional basis — when it cannot point to a taxing power nexus, a genuine interstate commerce connection, or some other grant of authority — the statute is ultra vires regardless of its policy merits.

The Hughes Amendment may ultimately be vulnerable on two independent constitutional grounds: first, because Congress lacked enumerated-powers authority to enact it; and second, because even if Congress had such authority, the Second Amendment prohibits the result. Either ground, standing alone, would be sufficient to strike the statute down.

In my assessment, the constitutional momentum building around § 922(o) represents yet another brick in the wall of Second Amendment protection. The arguments are sound, the judicial interest is real, and the legal landscape created by **Bruen** has never been more favorable for pressing them.
