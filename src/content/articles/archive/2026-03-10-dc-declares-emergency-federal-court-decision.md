## Summary

The District of Columbia Court of Appeals has issued a decision in **Benson v. United States** holding that the District's ban on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds of ammunition is facially unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The District has responded by filing an emergency motion seeking to suspend the panel opinion's precedential effect pending a petition for en banc review — a procedural posture that, in my analysis, reflects the ruling's substantial legal and institutional significance. The *Benson* decision directly conflicts with the United States Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit's earlier ruling in **Hansen v. District of Columbia**, which upheld the same prohibition, creating a split of authority that substantially increases the probability of Supreme Court review.

## Background

The D.C. Court of Appeals and the United States Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit are distinct institutions with overlapping but separate jurisdictions. The D.C. Court of Appeals is the highest court of the District of Columbia for purposes of local law; it functions as the equivalent of a state supreme court. The D.C. Circuit is the federal appellate court for the same geographic area. Both courts have now addressed the constitutionality of the District's large-capacity magazine prohibition under the Second Amendment, and they have reached opposite conclusions.

The D.C. Circuit, in **Hansen v. District of Columbia**, upheld the prohibition. The D.C. Court of Appeals, in **Benson v. United States**, struck it down. As the District itself has acknowledged in its emergency filings, *Benson* is the only appellate decision in the country to invalidate a large-capacity magazine ban. This positions the case as a significant outlier in the current jurisprudential landscape — and, critically, as an outlier that creates the kind of conflict among courts that tends to attract Supreme Court attention.

## The Benson Decision: Constitutional Analysis

The D.C. Court of Appeals applied the two-step analytical framework the Supreme Court established in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). The court's analysis proceeded in the prescribed sequence: first determining whether the regulated item falls within the plain text of the Second Amendment, and second, if so, whether the government can demonstrate a historical tradition of analogous regulation sufficient to justify the restriction.

On the first question, the court held that magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds constitute "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment. The operative definition derives from *Bruen* itself: the Court characterized an "arm" as any instrument that facilitates armed self-defense. A detachable magazine is an instrument — a physical object designed to enable a firearm to discharge multiple rounds without manual reloading between each shot. As such, it unambiguously facilitates armed self-defense and falls within the Amendment's textual scope.

On the second question, the court applied the *Heller* formulation that arms "in common use" by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes are presumptively protected and cannot be characterized as "dangerous and unusual." *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570, 627 (2008). The evidentiary record established that magazines holding more than ten rounds exist in the hundreds of millions in civilian hands and are among the most widely owned firearms accessories in the United States. By any applicable measure, they are in common use. The District could not demonstrate otherwise, and accordingly could not satisfy its burden under the *Bruen* framework. The prohibition fell.

## The Emergency Motion: An Institutional Reaction

The District's emergency motion requesting suspension of the panel opinion pending en banc consideration is revealing in its stated rationale. The motion characterizes the ruling as creating "uncertainty and chaos" and describes its consequences as affecting the District's ability to enforce its laws and protect public safety. In my analysis, the procedural urgency the District asserts reflects not a genuine emergency in the traditional legal sense — imminent irreparable harm that cannot be addressed through the ordinary appellate process — but rather institutional anxiety about the precedential implications of allowing the opinion to take effect.

The emergency motion's reference to "blast radius" — a colloquial term describing the scope of a ruling's effects on related cases and enforcement postures — is instructive. It signals that the District's concern is not primarily about immediate operational disruption but about the downstream litigation consequences of a published appellate opinion holding that magazine restrictions violate the Second Amendment. Those consequences are real: the opinion is now available as persuasive authority in every federal and state court in the country that is currently adjudicating a magazine ban challenge. By seeking to suspend the opinion before it can be cited more widely, the District seeks to limit its precedential velocity.

## The Role of the United States Attorney

An additional dimension of *Benson* complicates the District's position. United States Attorney Jeanine Pirro, acting pursuant to the Trump administration's Second Amendment enforcement policy under Attorney General Pam Bondi, filed a brief in *Benson* in September 2025 arguing that the District's large-capacity magazine prohibition violates the Second Amendment. The federal government's brief effectively aligned the prosecutorial authority of the United States with the constitutional position that the D.C. Court of Appeals ultimately adopted.

This creates an unusual institutional alignment: the District of Columbia is seeking en banc reversal of a ruling that the federal government — which has concurrent jurisdiction over many criminal matters in the District — argued was correct. If the federal government is already declining to enforce the magazine prohibition on constitutional grounds, the District's assertion that the *Benson* ruling disrupts "law enforcement" has limited practical force. The primary disruption the District appears to seek to prevent is doctrinal rather than operational: it is the establishment of a favorable precedent in a published appellate opinion.

## The En Banc Petition: Prospects

The D.C. Court of Appeals has seven active judges. The *Benson* ruling was issued by a three-judge panel in a 2-1 decision. En banc review in the D.C. Court of Appeals is infrequent; examining the court's recent history, en banc consideration has been granted in a modest number of cases, primarily involving technical questions of criminal procedure, jury selection practice, and District-specific statutory interpretation. I have not identified a pattern of the court granting en banc review in cases presenting broad constitutional questions of first impression at the federal appellate level.

There is also an institutional consideration that bears on the analysis. The *Benson* ruling aligns with the position taken by the United States Attorney's Office, an office within the executive branch of the federal government. The D.C. Court of Appeals, as a D.C. government court, operates in a relationship with the federal government that differs from that of a purely independent state judiciary. Whether that relationship influences the en banc calculus is a matter on which I decline to speculate with precision, but it is a contextual factor that the relevant institutional actors will presumably weigh.

## Implications

In my analysis, the *Benson* decision is constitutionally significant for two reasons that operate at different levels of generality. At the case-specific level, it holds that a specific large-capacity magazine prohibition is facially unconstitutional — a holding that directly benefits any individual in the District of Columbia who possesses or seeks to possess such a magazine. At the systemic level, the decision creates the kind of genuine split of authority between high-level courts that tends to attract Supreme Court review.

As I have argued in connection with the supplemental briefs filed in *Duncan v. Bonta* and *Gator Custom Guns v. Wence*, the Supreme Court now has before it in multiple petitions a question on which the D.C. Court of Appeals, the Ninth Circuit, and the Washington State Supreme Court have reached materially conflicting conclusions. The conflict is not merely on the bottom-line question of whether magazine bans are constitutional, but on the preliminary question of whether magazines are protected arms at all — with the D.C. Court of Appeals saying yes and the Washington Supreme Court saying no. That kind of foundational disagreement on a question of constitutional text is precisely the kind of issue the Supreme Court exists to resolve. The District's emergency motion, whatever its ultimate outcome, cannot change that structural reality.
