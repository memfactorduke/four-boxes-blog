## Summary

On January 15, 2026, the Department of Justice's Office of Legal Counsel issued a memorandum opinion concluding that 18 U.S.C. Section 1715 -- the longstanding federal statute prohibiting the mailing of handguns through the United States Postal Service -- is unconstitutional as applied to constitutionally protected firearms. In response, the USPS has now published a proposed rule that would allow lawful handguns to be mailed under the same terms and conditions as rifles and shotguns, marking a significant expansion of Second Amendment rights through executive branch action.

## Background

For decades, federal law has drawn a sharp distinction between handguns and long guns when it comes to the U.S. mail. Under 18 U.S.C. Section 1715, private individuals have been prohibited from mailing handguns through the United States Postal Service, while rifles and shotguns could be sent under certain conditions. Only federal firearms licensees (FFLs) and certain other authorized persons were exempt from the handgun prohibition. The practical consequence was that ordinary, law-abiding Americans could not use the postal system to ship a handgun -- for example, to themselves at a second home or for purposes of repair -- even though the same system could carry long guns.

The USPS implemented Section 1715 through its internal regulations in Publication 52, Subchapter 43, which classified handguns as "non-mailable" items. This regulatory framework remained largely unchallenged for years, even as the Supreme Court's Second Amendment jurisprudence evolved dramatically.

## The OLC Opinion

The January 15, 2026, OLC memorandum opinion was issued at the request of Attorney General Pam Bondi, who asked the Office of Legal Counsel to evaluate the constitutionality of Section 1715 in light of current Second Amendment doctrine. I have reviewed this opinion carefully, and the OLC's conclusion is unequivocal: the statute is unconstitutional as applied to constitutionally protected firearms, including handguns.

The opinion's reasoning rested squarely on the analytical framework established in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*. Under *Bruen*, a firearms regulation must be "consistent with the nation's tradition of firearm regulation" to survive constitutional scrutiny. The OLC found that Section 1715's purpose and burden "find no analog in this nation's history and tradition of firearm regulation." In other words, there is no historical tradition of prohibiting the transport of constitutionally protected arms through government mail systems.

The opinion also drew on *District of Columbia v. Heller*, which established that handguns are constitutionally protected arms. Since the Supreme Court in *Heller* specifically identified handguns as the "quintessential self-defense weapon," treating them as non-mailable items while permitting the mailing of rifles and shotguns amounted to an unjustifiable burden on a core constitutional right. I find this reasoning sound and well-grounded in precedent.

The OLC reached three key conclusions:

1. Section 1715 is unconstitutional as applied to constitutionally protected firearms, including handguns, because it "serves an illegitimate purpose" and fails the *Bruen* historical-tradition test.
2. The Department of Justice may not, consistent with the Constitution, enforce Section 1715 with respect to constitutionally protected firearms.
3. The Postal Service should modify its regulations to conform with the scope of the Second Amendment as described in the opinion.

## The USPS Proposed Rule

Acting on the OLC's directive, the United States Postal Service has now published a proposed rule revising its mailability regulations. The key elements of the proposed rule are straightforward: lawful handguns would become mailable under the same terms and conditions that currently govern the mailing of lawful rifles and shotguns.

In its Federal Register notice, the USPS stated that it "defers to OLC's judgment as to the lawful scope of the criminal statute" and worked in consultation with the OLC to develop the proposed revisions. The revised regulations in Publication 52 would establish clear definitions of "mailable firearms" and "non-mailable firearms" consistent with the OLC opinion. The proposed revisions also include detailed definitions for National Firearms Act items, machine guns, shotguns, handguns, and other categories of firearms.

Certain conditions would still apply. Mailed firearms must be unloaded, and otherwise non-mailable handguns (such as NFA items) would remain mailable only between authorized persons consistent with Section 1715's existing exceptions. The proposed rule is currently in a public comment period before final promulgation.

## The Administrative Process

The sequence of events in this matter illustrates how executive branch action can advance constitutional rights through established administrative processes. The Attorney General requested the OLC's legal opinion. The OLC -- staffed largely by former Supreme Court clerks and regarded as one of the most intellectually rigorous offices in the federal government -- issued a formal memorandum concluding that the statute was unconstitutional. That opinion, which carries significant weight throughout the executive branch, then prompted the Postal Service, a separate executive branch agency, to initiate a rulemaking to conform its regulations to the Constitution. As I have analyzed on The Four Boxes Diner, this represents a significant and proper use of executive authority to vindicate constitutional rights.

This is the standard process by which OLC opinions influence executive branch conduct. When the OLC concludes that a statute is unconstitutional, the executive branch treats that determination as binding on its agencies. The result is not a judicial ruling -- Section 1715 remains on the books as a matter of statutory law -- but the executive branch will no longer enforce the provision as applied to constitutionally protected firearms.

## Implications

The practical impact, once the proposed rule is finalized, is that law-abiding Americans will be able to mail handguns through the USPS for lawful purposes -- including shipping firearms to themselves at another location, sending handguns for repair, or facilitating lawful transfers -- under the same regulatory framework that has long governed long-gun shipments. I view this as a meaningful expansion of Second Amendment protections in practice.

More broadly, the OLC opinion represents a significant application of *Bruen*'s historical-tradition test outside the judicial context. The executive branch's willingness to apply *Bruen* to invalidate a federal statute -- rather than waiting for a court to do so -- signals an aggressive posture in favor of Second Amendment rights. It also establishes a precedent within the executive branch that could be applied to other federal firearms regulations that similarly lack historical analogs. As I have emphasized, this is the kind of proactive, constitutionally grounded approach we should expect from executive officials.

The opinion further demonstrates the downstream consequences of the *Heller* and *Bruen* decisions. *Heller* established that handguns are constitutionally protected; *Bruen* provided the analytical framework for evaluating regulations that burden that right. Together, they supplied the doctrinal foundation for the OLC's conclusion that discriminating against handguns in the postal system is constitutionally untenable.

Whether future administrations would maintain this interpretation remains an open question. OLC opinions can be withdrawn or superseded by subsequent administrations. However, the underlying constitutional analysis -- grounded in *Heller* and *Bruen* -- would be difficult to reverse without contravening established Supreme Court precedent. I expect this will become a standard interpretation moving forward.
