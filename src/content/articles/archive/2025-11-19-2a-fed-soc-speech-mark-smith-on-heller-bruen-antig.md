## Summary

I recently delivered a presentation at the 2025 National Lawyers Convention of the Federalist Society in Washington, D.C. — an event attended by thousands of lawyers, scholars, judges, and journalists, including Supreme Court Justices Amy Coney Barrett and Brett Kavanaugh. The speech addressed the state of the Second Amendment battle in the post-*Bruen* era, with particular focus on two strategies that lower courts are employing to evade the Supreme Court's clear directives, and why those strategies will ultimately fail.

## Background

The fight to vindicate the Second Amendment in American courts has played out in two distinct acts since the Supreme Court's landmark decision in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008). Understanding the arc of both acts is essential to understanding where we are today and where this battle is headed.

## Act One: Post-Heller Resistance

The period from 2008 through 2022 was characterized by what can only be described as massive, organized judicial resistance to *Heller*. Lower courts — the Article III inferior courts — simply pretended they could not understand a text as plain as "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms shall not be infringed." They feigned confusion about the most basic constitutional commands and, as a predictable consequence, the government won nearly every Second Amendment case during that fourteen-year span. The Second Amendment was relegated, again and again, to what Justice Thomas famously called a second-class right.

## Act Two: Bruen and the Paint-by-Numbers Mandate

The Supreme Court's 2022 decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** was designed to end Act One by providing lower courts with what I have described as a "paint-by-numbers" methodology for Second Amendment analysis. *Bruen* told the lower courts, in explicit terms, how to apply *Heller*: a law that burdens conduct covered by the Second Amendment's plain text is presumptively unconstitutional unless the government can demonstrate that the regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation in this country at the time of the Founding.

*Bruen* was the Supreme Court's attempt to leave no room for further evasion. The methodology was deliberately clear and step-by-step, so that inferior courts could not plausibly claim they "couldn't figure it out" a second time.

## The New Evasion Strategies

And yet, the lower courts have found new ways. In my presentation at the Federalist Society, I identified two primary tactics through which anti-Second-Amendment courts are now attempting to circumvent both *Heller* and *Bruen*.

### The Facial Challenge Game

The first tactic is what I call the "facial challenge game." It is grounded in a misapplication of the Supreme Court's 1987 decision in **United States v. Salerno**, which established the foundational principle for facial constitutional challenges. Under *Salerno*, a party challenging a statute on its face — as opposed to challenging a specific application of the statute to their particular circumstances — must demonstrate that the statute is unconstitutional in all of its applications. This is the "no set of circumstances" standard, and it is a high bar.

The facial challenge game works as follows. A Second Amendment claimant challenges a broad gun control statute on its face, arguing that the statute is unconstitutional as written. A lower court hostile to the Second Amendment then engages in what I can only call "fantasy judging": the court imagines a hypothetical, narrower version of the challenged statute — one that does not actually exist — and finds that this imaginary, narrower law might have at least one constitutional application. Having conjured this phantom constitutional application, the court then uses it as a hook to dismiss the facial challenge entirely.

To illustrate the absurdity: suppose someone challenges a blanket DC handgun ban — a law that prohibits all persons from possessing handguns — on its face. Under *Salerno*, a properly applied analysis would ask whether there is any set of circumstances under which such a ban is constitutional. Since *Heller* held that law-abiding citizens have the right to keep handguns in their homes, the answer is clearly no — such a ban cannot be constitutional in all applications.

But a court playing the facial challenge game does something different. It says: "Well, it would be constitutional to ban felons from possessing handguns. Therefore, a handgun ban has at least one constitutional application — the felon scenario — and therefore the facial challenge fails." The problem, of course, is that the actual statute being challenged does not contain a felon limitation. The court has rewritten the statute in the abstract to include a limiting principle the legislature never enacted, and used that phantom limitation to insulate the actual broad prohibition from constitutional scrutiny. This is not legal analysis; it is judicial legislation dressed up as constitutional adjudication.

### The As-Applied Workaround and Post-Rahimi Maneuvering

The second tactic has evolved in the wake of the Supreme Court's 2024 decision in **United States v. Rahimi**, which held that disarming individuals subject to domestic violence restraining orders is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of disarming dangerous individuals. Some lower courts have read *Rahimi* far too broadly, treating it as a license to uphold sweeping gun control statutes on the theory that any law with some historical analogue is constitutionally permissible — without engaging seriously with whether the modern law is actually "relevantly similar" to the historical regulation in its burden and justification.

## The Salerno Principle Properly Understood

The proper application of the *Salerno* facial challenge standard in the Second Amendment context requires courts to analyze the actual statute before them — not an imagined, narrower version of it. Where a gun control law on its face burdens conduct that *Heller* and *Bruen* protect, and where the government cannot demonstrate that the law's entire sweep is consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation, the statute fails a facial challenge. Courts that manufacture constitutional applications out of thin air to rescue unconstitutional statutes are not applying *Salerno*; they are abusing it.

## The Federalist Society Audience and the Stakes

Speaking at an event alongside Justices Barrett and Kavanaugh, as well as Sixth Circuit Judge Amul Thapar, underscored for me the importance of this audience understanding these technical but crucial doctrinal games. The lawyers, scholars, and judges who attend the Federalist Society's National Lawyers Convention are precisely the people who will litigate, decide, and shape the Second Amendment cases of the coming decade.

The lower courts' resistance to *Heller* and *Bruen* is not a random phenomenon. It is, in many respects, an organized rear-guard action by courts in blue-state jurisdictions — precisely those jurisdictions where, as I will discuss separately, the "blue slip" process has historically limited the appointment of genuine originalists to the federal district court bench.

## Implications

The two evasion strategies I identified — the misuse of facial challenge doctrine and the selective over-reading of *Rahimi* — will ultimately be corrected by the Supreme Court. The Court has already demonstrated, through *Bruen* itself, its willingness to step in when lower courts persistently misapply its Second Amendment jurisprudence.

The critical question is timing. Each year these tactics succeed in the lower courts is another year in which Second Amendment rights are treated as a second-class guarantee. The cases that will force the Court's hand are already working their way through the system — the *Wolford* case challenging Hawaii's so-called "vampire rule," the AR-15 ban cases in the Second and Seventh Circuits, and others.

The framework of *Heller* and *Bruen* is correct and will endure. The only question is how long the lower courts can delay the inevitable reckoning.
