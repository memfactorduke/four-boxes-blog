## Summary

The inauguration of Zohran Mamdani as Mayor of New York City — with Senator Bernie Sanders administering the oath, Representative Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez speaking, and Governor Kathy Hochul in attendance — marked the arrival in the nation's most prominent municipal office of a politician who has explicitly called for replacing "the fragility of rugged individualism" with "the warmth of collectivism." While Mamdani's inauguration address did not specifically mention the Second Amendment or firearms, the ideological framework he articulated — one that rejects individual rights in favor of state-directed collective solidarity — is fundamentally hostile to the constitutional architecture that protects the right to keep and bear arms. In my assessment, this is not simply a New York City story; it is a national story about the ongoing conflict between the founding principles of individual liberty and a resurgent collectivist ideology that, if implemented, would reshape the constitutional landscape against which all of our rights — including the Second Amendment — are secured.

## Background: The Inauguration and Its Ideological Content

Mayor Mamdani's inauguration was notable both for the company he kept and for the content of his address. Senator Bernie Sanders, a self-described democratic socialist, administered the oath. Representative Alexandria Ocasio-Cortez — among the most prominent voices for progressive economic redistribution in American politics — delivered remarks. Governor Kathy Hochul, whose record on the Second Amendment is sharply antagonistic, attended in a position of prominence. The assembled coalition represented a clear ideological statement about the direction Mamdani intends to take the city.

The substantive content of the address is more important than the personnel. Mamdani stated, in relevant part: "For too long, these communities have existed as distinct from one another. We will draw the city closer together. We will replace the fragility of rugged individualism with the warmth of collectivism." He further declared that "the words that define us are the two we all share: New Yorkers" — a formulation notably silent on American identity, the United States Constitution, or the individual rights that the federal founding documents protect.

The address also invoked, approvingly, the South African Freedom Charter — a document associated with the African National Congress's platform during the anti-apartheid struggle that has been linked to collectivist economic prescriptions. The invocation of the South African Freedom Charter, rather than the United States Constitution, as an inspirational touchstone is itself an ideological signal. It is worth recalling that the late Justice Ruth Bader Ginsburg, in a widely noted interview, expressed the view that the South African Constitution — rather than the United States Constitution — was a preferable model for 21st-century governance. Mamdani's address falls within the same tradition of looking to non-American constitutional frameworks as alternatives to the founding documents.

## The Philosophical Stakes: Individualism Versus Collectivism

The conflict between "rugged individualism" — which Mamdani treats as a fragility to be overcome — and "collectivism" — which he presents as warmth and solidarity — is not merely a policy debate. It is a constitutional debate with direct implications for the Second Amendment and for every other individual right protected by the Bill of Rights.

The American constitutional system is built on the premise that certain rights inhere in individuals, not in groups, and that those rights exist prior to and independent of the state. The state does not grant these rights; it is constitutionally obligated to respect them. The Second Amendment protects the individual's right to keep and bear arms. The First Amendment protects individual speech, press, religion, assembly, and petition. The Fourth Amendment protects the individual's person, house, papers, and effects against unreasonable government search and seizure. Each of these rights is an individual entitlement — a sphere of autonomy that the government may not enter without justification meeting the appropriate constitutional standard.

Collectivism, by contrast, subordinates individual rights to the judgment of the collective — ultimately the judgment of the state acting in the collective's name. Under a collectivist framework, no right is truly individual; every right is conditional on its consistency with the public good as defined by the governing authority. This is precisely the framework that the American founding rejected. The Framers had observed what collectivist governance looked like under a tyrannical monarchy. They designed a system of enumerated, individual rights protected against majoritarian and governmental overreach precisely because they understood that the "warmth of collectivism" can become, very quickly, the cold tyranny of the state.

## The Second Amendment in Context

I have consistently emphasized that the Second Amendment is not merely about guns. It is about the broader framework of individual liberty. The right to keep and bear arms is one strand in a web of liberties — the "spiderweb of liberties," as I have described it — that the founders recognized as essential to free citizenship. Remove one strand, and others weaken. Embrace collectivism as the organizing principle of government, and all individual rights become contingent entitlements subject to revision whenever the collective determines that individual autonomy is inconvenient.

Mayor Mamdani did not mention guns in his inauguration address. But his ideological framework — replace rugged individualism with collectivism, define identity by city rather than by constitutional citizenship, invoke foreign constitutional models rather than the American founding — is precisely the framework under which individual rights erode. The Second Amendment does not exist in isolation. It exists within a constitutional structure predicated on the primacy of individual liberty over state authority.

None of Mamdani's supporters on that inauguration stage are friends of the Second Amendment. Governor Hochul has been among the most aggressive anti-Second Amendment executives in the country. Representative Ocasio-Cortez has consistently opposed legislation that would expand the right to keep and bear arms. Senator Sanders has spent his career advocating for restrictions on firearms that are inconsistent with the constitutional framework. The coalition that elevated Mamdani to the mayoralty of New York City is, in its entirety, hostile to the individual rights framework that the Second Amendment embodies.

## Why This Is a National Story

New York City may seem like a local matter, but the mayoralty carries national influence disproportionate to its formal authority. The mayor of New York City commands a platform from which to shape national political discourse, propose policy models that other progressive municipalities emulate, and set the terms of debate in ways that extend far beyond the five boroughs. When the mayor of America's largest and most prominent city articulates a vision of replacing individual liberty with collective solidarity, that vision does not stay in New York.

Moreover, the ideological trends that produced Mamdani's election — dissatisfaction with the "fragility" of individual autonomy, embrace of state-directed solidarity as a remedy for social fragmentation, preference for collective identity over constitutional citizenship — are not confined to New York. They represent a broader movement that will contest elections, seek judicial appointments, and advance legislation in venues across the country.

## Implications

The election of Mayor Mamdani is a signal that the conflict between individual liberty and collectivism — between the constitutional framework of the American founding and ideological alternatives that subordinate individual rights to the collective — is entering a new phase. The Second Amendment community should understand this not merely as a gun issue but as a constitutional culture issue. The right to keep and bear arms is best protected not only in courts but in the broader political and cultural contest over whether the United States will remain a republic of individual rights or become, over time, something more closely resembling the "collectivist" model that Mamdani celebrated at his inauguration. In my assessment, that contest has never been more consequential than it is today.
