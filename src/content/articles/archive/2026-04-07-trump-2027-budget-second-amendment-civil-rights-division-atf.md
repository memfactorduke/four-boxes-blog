## Summary

President Trump's Office of Management and Budget has transmitted a proposed fiscal year 2027 federal budget to Congress that contains several significant provisions advancing Second Amendment rights. The proposal allocates $1.4 million to establish a dedicated Second Amendment office within the Civil Rights Division of the Department of Justice, $4.8 million to fund a firearms rights restoration initiative through the Office of the Pardon Attorney under 18 U.S.C. § 925, and directs the ATF to continue reversing Biden-era regulations targeting pistol braces, homemade firearms, and federally licensed dealers. While a presidential budget proposal is not law — Congress controls appropriations — this document represents the clearest formal statement yet of the Trump administration's legislative priorities on gun rights, and it deserves close attention from anyone who cares about the Second Amendment's future.

It is worth being precise about what a presidential budget proposal is and is not. Under the Budget and Accounting Act, the executive branch submits a budget request to Congress each year that serves as the president's opening bid — a declaration of priorities, not a binding appropriation. All spending bills originate in the House, must pass both chambers, and require the president's signature. The filibuster in the Senate means that even a nominally Republican Congress must negotiate with the minority to pass spending legislation. So while I am genuinely encouraged by what I see in this proposal, the proof will be in what Congress actually enacts. With that caveat firmly in place, let me walk through what the administration is asking for and why it matters.

## A Dedicated Second Amendment Office in the Civil Rights Division

The most structurally significant provision in the budget is the $1.4 million allocation to create a new office within the Civil Rights Division of the Department of Justice "solely dedicated to protecting Second Amendment rights from unlawful infringement on the right to bear arms and pursuing cases to definitively enshrine those rights in perpetuity."

This is not a small thing. The Civil Rights Division was created in 1957 to enforce federal civil rights laws, and it has historically focused on voting rights, employment discrimination, housing, and law enforcement misconduct. The Second Amendment has never had a dedicated institutional home within that division — or anywhere else in the DOJ, for that matter. Anti-gun administrations have used DOJ resources to attack gun owners; this proposal would flip that dynamic and create an affirmative prosecutorial and litigation function specifically tasked with defending the right to keep and bear arms.

Attorney General Pam Bondi and Assistant Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon deserve significant credit for laying the groundwork for this initiative. If Congress funds it and it becomes a permanent institutional fixture within DOJ, gun owners will have a known point of contact within the federal government — a place to report Second Amendment violations, a unit that can bring affirmative civil rights litigation on behalf of gun owners, and an office whose existence creates accountability and visibility around whether the government is actually defending the right.

The comparison to other civil rights enforcement structures is instructive. The Voting Section of the Civil Rights Division employs dozens of attorneys and has litigated hundreds of cases over its history. A well-resourced Second Amendment section could do the same — filing suit against state and local governments that enact unconstitutional restrictions, intervening in cases where gun owners' civil rights have been violated by federal or state actors, and building a body of favorable precedent. $1.4 million is a modest starting figure, but a foot in the door matters enormously in institutional terms.

## Rights Restoration Under 18 U.S.C. § 925: A Long-Overdue Fix

The second major provision is $4.8 million for the Office of the Pardon Attorney's firearms rights restoration initiative, which the budget describes as providing "a pathway for law-abiding citizens to regain their Second Amendment rights after fulfilling their obligations to the justice system."

Some background is essential here. Federal law under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g) prohibits a range of individuals from possessing firearms — convicted felons, persons subject to domestic violence restraining orders, those adjudicated mentally defective, and others. The prohibition is sweeping, and it applies for life absent affirmative restoration. Congress created a safety valve: 18 U.S.C. § 925(c), which authorizes the ATF to review individual applications from prohibited persons seeking restoration of their rights, with judicial review available if the ATF denies or ignores a petition.

The problem is that for decades, Congress — through a bipartisan agreement that is genuinely shameful — has included a budget rider prohibiting any federal funds from being spent to process § 925(c) applications. Republicans who are hostile to giving rights back to convicted criminals and Democrats who are hostile to guns in any form found common ground in defunding the program entirely. The result has been that § 925(c) has been a dead letter since 1992. Courts have held that because the ATF has no funding to act, denial by inaction is unreviewable — effectively eliminating the judicial safety valve as well. See *United States v. McGill*, 74 F.3d 64 (5th Cir. 1996), among other circuit decisions reaching similar conclusions.

The Trump administration, under the DOJ rather than the ATF, has been working to revive some form of administrative rights restoration. Proposed regulations have been published, public comments have been received, and the agency is now in the process of reviewing and responding to those comments — a legally required step before a final rule can issue. The $4.8 million in this budget proposal is designed to fund that infrastructure: staff, systems, and processing capacity to handle what could be an enormous volume of applications once a final rule is in place.

I believe the demand will be extraordinary. There are hundreds of thousands — possibly millions — of Americans who were convicted of non-violent offenses years or decades ago, have lived law-abiding lives since, and have never been able to regain their Second Amendment rights because no mechanism existed to do so. This program, if properly funded and administered, is potentially one of the most consequential expansions of gun rights since *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008). It does not expand who has rights; it restores rights to people who should never have lost them permanently in the first place.

## ATF Regulatory Rollbacks: Pistol Braces, Ghost Guns, and FFL Revocations

The budget also directs the ATF to continue its work reversing Biden-era regulations that, in the administration's words, "effectively criminalized law-abiding gun ownership." Three specific regulatory areas are named.

**Pistol braces.** The Biden ATF's 2023 rule reclassifying pistol-brace-equipped firearms as short-barreled rifles under the National Firearms Act — requiring registration or modification under threat of up to ten years' imprisonment — was one of the most punitive regulatory overreaches in the agency's history. It targeted a device specifically designed to enable disabled veterans and others with limited hand strength or upper-body mobility to operate handguns. The Trump ATF has been unwinding this rule, and the budget signals continued commitment to that effort. To my knowledge, no one is currently being prosecuted for unregistered pistol braces, which is as it should be.

**Homemade firearms ("ghost guns").** The Biden administration's 2022 rule redefining "firearm" under the Gun Control Act to capture certain parts kits and unfinished frames — effectively regulating items that were not manufactured firearms under any prior interpretation of the statute — was a regulatory stretch of the first order. The Supreme Court addressed related statutory interpretation questions in *Garland v. VanDerStok*, 144 S. Ct. 2539 (2024), though the full implications of that decision continue to be worked out in the lower courts. The president's budget reflects a policy commitment to pulling back from aggressive regulatory expansion in this area and respecting the historical tradition of Americans making their own firearms — a practice with roots extending well before the founding era.

**FFL revocations.** The Biden ATF pursued an aggressive policy of revoking Federal Firearms Licenses from dealers for regulatory violations that, in many cases, were minor paperwork errors. FFLs are the backbone of the commercial firearms market; their widespread revocation does not reduce crime but does reduce lawful access to firearms for ordinary Americans. The budget directs the ATF to revisit these revocations and, where appropriate, restore licenses to dealers who were targeted through disproportionate enforcement.

## The Broader Enforcement Priority: Prosecuting Violent Actors

One element of the budget that does not get enough attention in Second Amendment discussions is its emphasis on prosecuting actual violent criminals. The proposal prioritizes resources for prosecuting illegal firearms traffickers, MS-13 gang members, violent drug cartel operatives, and street criminals. This is not window dressing.

From a purely strategic standpoint, every high-profile mass casualty event involving a firearm creates political conditions that are deeply unfavorable for Second Amendment advocates. The reflexive response is to demand gun control legislation — and those demands gain traction precisely because the perpetrators of the most sensationalistic crimes are not in prison where they belong. Robust prosecution of violent actors is not merely a public safety good; it is structurally protective of the Second Amendment itself. A government that locks up violent criminals rather than harassing law-abiding gun owners is one that gives the gun-control lobby far less political ammunition.

The president's budget reflects an understanding of this dynamic: defend the constitutional rights of the law-abiding, and prosecute vigorously those who actually commit violence. That is not a complicated philosophy, but it is one that has been conspicuously absent from federal law enforcement priorities in recent years.

## What Comes Next

As the budget moves to Congress, the relevant questions are whether the specific Second Amendment provisions survive the appropriations process and whether they are funded at the levels proposed or cut. The filibuster in the Senate means that no appropriations bill will clear on a party-line vote; the administration will need Democratic cooperation or must pursue these priorities through reconciliation, continuing resolutions, or other vehicles.

I remain cautiously optimistic. The structural proposal here — a named, funded Second Amendment office within the Civil Rights Division — is the kind of institutional foothold that, once established, tends to persist. Bureaucratic structures develop constituencies, case law, and internal expertise. If this office is created and funded even once, it becomes significantly harder to eliminate in future budgets. That institutional durability is precisely why this proposal matters beyond the dollar amounts involved.

The FY2027 budget proposal is a meaningful data point in the ongoing effort to restore Second Amendment rights to their proper constitutional standing. It is not a final victory, but it represents the administration putting its fiscal priorities on the record in favor of gun owners — and that record, whatever Congress ultimately does with it, will have lasting significance.
