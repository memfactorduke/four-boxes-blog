## Summary

In my analysis, the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit has issued a decision in **Kellogg v. Nichols** that I believe is clearly wrong, and I will explain precisely why. The case concerns whether individuals whose applications for concealed carry permits were denied by a New York state court judge, acting in an administrative licensing capacity, may sue that judge for violating their Second Amendment rights under 42 U.S.C. § 1983. The Second Circuit held, unanimously, that such judges are protected by absolute judicial immunity and cannot be sued. As I will demonstrate, this conclusion misapplies the doctrine of judicial immunity by extending it to officials performing purely administrative, non-judicial functions.

## Background: New York's CCW Licensing System

Under New York state law, the possession and carry of a handgun is generally prohibited unless the individual obtains a license. Following the Supreme Court's landmark 2022 decision in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, which held that the Second Amendment protects the right of law-abiding citizens to carry handguns in public for self-defense, New York was required to establish an objective, shall-issue licensing regime that does not require applicants to demonstrate a particularized need for self-protection.

New York's licensing system employs two distinct categories of officials to evaluate applications for concealed carry permits. The first category consists of law enforcement officials -- sheriffs, police departments, and the NYPD -- who review applications and apply the statutory criteria to determine whether an applicant qualifies for a permit. The second category consists of state court judges, who in certain jurisdictions, including Columbia County, New York, serve as the licensing authority responsible for making the initial determination on permit applications.

When a state court judge sits as a licensing authority under New York's concealed carry permit system, that judge is not performing a judicial function in any meaningful constitutional sense. The judge is not resolving a dispute between adverse parties, applying law to facts presented in adversarial proceedings, or exercising the judicial power of the state in any traditional sense. The judge is, instead, performing exactly the same administrative task as a police officer or sheriff: reading an application, applying statutory criteria, and issuing or denying a permit accordingly.

## The Facts of Kellogg v. Nichols

In **Kellogg v. Nichols**, two individuals applied for concealed carry permits in Columbia County, New York. Their applications were reviewed and denied by a state court judge acting in the licensing authority capacity. The plaintiffs subsequently brought suit under 42 U.S.C. § 1983, alleging that the denial of their permit applications violated their Second Amendment rights as recognized by *Bruen*.

The central question before the Second Circuit was not the merits of whether the plaintiffs' applications should have been granted. Rather, the question was whether the judge who denied the applications could be sued at all, given the doctrine of absolute judicial immunity.

## Why the Second Circuit's Decision Is Wrong

The doctrine of absolute judicial immunity shields judges from civil liability for acts performed in their judicial capacity. The doctrine's purpose is to protect the independence of the judiciary by ensuring that judges need not fear personal liability for the decisions they make in the course of adjudicating disputes. But the immunity is not boundless. The Supreme Court has consistently held that judicial immunity extends only to the performance of genuinely judicial acts -- it does not protect officials from liability for purely administrative or ministerial functions, even when those functions are performed by someone who also serves as a judge.

The key question is not whether the defendant holds the title of "judge" but whether the specific act at issue was a judicial act. A judicial act is one that involves the resolution of a controversy through the exercise of judicial power. By contrast, an administrative act involves the application of predetermined criteria to an application or request in the absence of any adversarial proceeding.

In my analysis, when a New York state court judge reviews a concealed carry permit application, the judge is performing the identical administrative function that a police officer, a sheriff, or an NYPD official performs when reviewing the same application. There is no adversarial proceeding. There are no adverse parties presenting competing arguments. There is no invocation of judicial power in any meaningful constitutional sense. The judge is simply administering a licensing program as required by New York state law.

Because the act of reviewing and ruling on a concealed carry permit application is not a judicial act but rather an administrative act, the doctrine of absolute judicial immunity should not apply. A sheriff who unconstitutionally denies a concealed carry permit application can be sued under § 1983. There is no principled reason why a judge performing the identical administrative function should be immune from the same liability.

## The Practical Consequences of the Second Circuit's Error

The Second Circuit's ruling creates a perverse and constitutionally problematic result. In jurisdictions where law enforcement officials serve as licensing authorities, applicants who are wrongfully denied permits can seek redress through § 1983 suits against the responsible officer. But in jurisdictions where judges serve as licensing authorities -- performing the same administrative function -- applicants who are wrongfully denied permits have no recourse against the decision-maker.

This disparity is constitutionally unjustifiable. The Second Amendment right recognized in *Bruen* does not vary based on whether the licensing authority happens to hold a judicial title. A constitutional right that can be denied without remedy is, in practical effect, no right at all.

## The Broader Stakes: Accountability in the CCW Process

Beyond the specific question of judicial immunity, **Kellogg v. Nichols** raises fundamental questions about accountability in the administration of concealed carry licensing systems. *Bruen* requires that shall-issue permit systems be administered in a non-discriminatory manner, applying objective criteria without undue discretion. When licensing officials -- whether judges or law enforcement officers -- exercise that discretion in a manner that violates applicants' Second Amendment rights, there must be a meaningful mechanism for redress.

The Second Circuit's decision, if allowed to stand, effectively insulates a category of licensing officials from any accountability under federal civil rights law, even when those officials act in a purely administrative capacity. That outcome is inconsistent with the principle, fundamental to § 1983 jurisprudence, that constitutional rights must have effective remedies.

## Looking Ahead

In my assessment, the Second Circuit's decision in **Kellogg v. Nichols** is a candidate for en banc rehearing or certiorari review before the Supreme Court. The question of whether judicial immunity extends to judges acting in administrative licensing capacities is one of significant importance for Second Amendment rights and for the administration of concealed carry systems across New York and other states within the Second Circuit's jurisdiction. The error committed by the panel majority -- treating an administrative act as a judicial act solely because it was performed by a judge -- is a conceptual error that the Supreme Court has the tools and the precedent to correct.
