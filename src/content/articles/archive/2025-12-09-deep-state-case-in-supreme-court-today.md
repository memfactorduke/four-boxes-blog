## Summary

The United States Supreme Court today heard oral argument in **Trump v. Slaughter**, a landmark separation-of-powers case with profound implications not only for presidential authority over the executive branch, but for the broader constitutional framework within which Second Amendment rights — and all constitutional rights — are protected and enforced. The question presented is whether President Trump has the constitutional authority to remove Rebecca Slaughter, a Biden-appointed commissioner on the Federal Trade Commission, or whether a congressional statute purporting to limit the President's removal power shields her from termination. I believe the President will prevail, and I want to explain why this case matters far beyond the FTC.

## Background: The Three Branches and the Problem of Independent Agencies

To understand **Trump v. Slaughter**, one must begin with a basic but essential civics lesson that I emphasize repeatedly on this channel: the United States Constitution creates three — and only three — branches of the federal government.

Article I of the Constitution creates the Congress — the legislative branch. Its function is to make laws, enact statutes, and appropriate funds. Article II of the Constitution creates the Presidency — the executive branch. It vests all executive power in a single individual: the President of the United States. One person. Not a commission, not a board, not a group of bipartisan experts. The President is, as the text states, the singular head of the executive branch with full responsibility for faithfully executing the laws. Article III of the Constitution creates the federal judiciary — the courts — with the power to adjudicate cases and controversies under federal law.

There is no Article IV. There is no constitutional provision creating a fourth branch of government populated by "independent agencies" — entities that exercise executive power but answer to no branch of government in any meaningful sense. Yet since the New Deal era of the 1930s, beginning under President Franklin D. Roosevelt, Congress has created scores of such agencies. The Federal Trade Commission is among the oldest and most prominent.

## The FTC Removal Restriction and Humphrey's Executor

The FTC was created by Congress, and the statute establishing it contains a provision limiting the President's power to remove FTC commissioners. Under the statute, a commissioner may be removed only for specified causes — not simply because the sitting President disagrees with the commissioner's policy positions or wants to install someone more aligned with the current administration's priorities.

Rebecca Slaughter was appointed to the FTC by President Joe Biden. When President Trump won the 2024 election and assumed office, he terminated her commission — and she sued, claiming the statutory removal protection shielded her from removal at the President's discretion.

Her argument relies primarily on the Supreme Court's 1935 decision in **Humphrey's Executor v. United States**, which upheld Congress's authority to restrict presidential removal of FTC commissioners. For decades, that precedent has been used to justify the proliferation of independent agencies throughout the federal government.

## The Unitary Executive Theory

President Trump's position rests on the "unitary executive" theory of Article II: a single President, duly elected by the American people, must be able to control and direct all officers of the executive branch who exercise executive power. If the President cannot hire and fire the personnel who administer executive power, then the people's choice of President becomes a fiction. The bureaucracy becomes permanent and unaccountable — a "deep state" that governs regardless of election outcomes.

The unitary executive theory has deep roots in the constitutional text and structure. The President takes an oath to faithfully execute the laws of the United States. He cannot faithfully execute those laws if he is forced to retain officers appointed by political opponents who are committed to undermining his agenda. As the Framers understood, and as the Constitution's structure reflects, control over executive personnel is essential to democratic accountability.

I want to be direct: the concept of "independent agencies" that answer to no one is constitutionally anomalous. The idea that unelected commissioners — serving multi-year terms, appointed by different presidents of different parties — can exercise binding executive authority without being subject to removal by the sitting President is fundamentally inconsistent with Article II's vesting of executive power in a single President.

## What Happened at Oral Argument Today

Today's oral argument before the Supreme Court was, by all reports, a substantial confrontation between the competing constitutional theories. The justices pressed both sides on the line between permissible congressional limitations on removal power and unconstitutional infringement on the President's core Article II authority.

The critical question the Court must answer is whether the line drawn in **Humphrey's Executor** — which upheld removal restrictions for multi-member commissions exercising "quasi-legislative" and "quasi-judicial" functions, as distinct from purely executive functions — remains viable after more recent precedents such as **Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau** (2020), in which the Court held that Congress could not restrict the President's ability to remove the director of the CFPB.

**Seila Law** was a significant step toward reaffirming the unitary executive. **Trump v. Slaughter** may be the next step — and potentially a larger one, since it invites the Court to reconsider **Humphrey's Executor** directly.

Based on the composition of the current Court and the trajectory of its separation-of-powers jurisprudence, I believe President Trump will prevail. The logic of **Seila Law** and the broader text-and-history methodology the Court has employed in recent cases strongly supports the conclusion that Congress cannot insulate executive officers from presidential removal simply by labeling their agency "independent."

## Why This Case Matters for the Second Amendment

I have spoken on this channel frequently about the importance of understanding constitutional law broadly — not just Second Amendment-specific jurisprudence — because all of our rights are interconnected. The Second Amendment does not exist in isolation. It depends on the same constitutional framework of separated powers, democratic accountability, and limited government that protects all constitutional rights.

Here is the direct connection: if the President cannot control the personnel of executive agencies, then the Second Amendment enforcement priorities I discussed in connection with the DOJ Civil Rights Division's new Second Amendment unit are at perpetual risk. A future President could install an anti-gun Commissioner at the ATF, an anti-gun Secretary of Defense, or anti-gun officials throughout the executive branch who would be insulated from removal even as they systematically undermined the right to keep and bear arms.

More broadly, the same "deep state" mentality that produces anti-gun bureaucratic overreach — agency personnel acting in defiance of Second Amendment principles regardless of which party controls the White House — is precisely what the unitary executive theory is designed to prevent.

## Implications

A Supreme Court ruling in favor of President Trump in **Trump v. Slaughter** would represent a significant step toward restoring the constitutional architecture of separated powers and democratic accountability over executive branch personnel. It would empower future presidents — including future pro-Second Amendment presidents — to ensure that the officials who administer firearms law and enforcement are aligned with the constitutional understanding that the Second Amendment guarantees an individual right to keep and bear arms.

I am cautiously optimistic about the outcome. The oral argument today was encouraging, and I believe the current Court is prepared to reaffirm the core of the unitary executive theory. That result would be good for the Constitution, good for democratic accountability, and good for the Second Amendment.
