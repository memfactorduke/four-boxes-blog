## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit has issued a unanimous three-judge panel decision in **United States v. Hembry**, holding that 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) — the federal felon-in-possession statute — as applied to a defendant whose sole prior conviction was a single 2018 Mississippi count of methamphetamine possession, violates the Second Amendment under the **Bruen** methodology. The court found no sufficient historical tradition of firearms regulation that would justify a lifetime disarmament predicated exclusively on a nonviolent drug-possession offense. In my analysis, the decision is doctrinely sound, but it also surfaces a strategic concern that Second Amendment advocates and the Trump Department of Justice should weigh carefully before seeking Supreme Court review.

## Background: The Federal Felon-in-Possession Statute

Section 922(g)(1) of Title 18 provides that any person "who has been convicted in any court of[] a crime punishable by imprisonment for a term exceeding one year" may not possess firearms. The statutory definition sweeps broadly: the felony need not have resulted in any period of incarceration, and it encompasses offenses that the relevant legislature has denominated felonies regardless of their relationship to violence or dangerousness.

Charles Hembry was convicted in 2018 of a single count of possessing methamphetamine under Mississippi law — a nonviolent offense involving no allegations of trafficking, distribution, or violence. Several years later, he was found in possession of a firearm. The federal government charged him under § 922(g)(1) on the theory that the Mississippi drug conviction rendered him a prohibited person. He reserved his Second Amendment challenge for appeal.

The district court rejected the challenge, but a unanimous Fifth Circuit panel reversed.

## The Three-Circuit Framework for § 922(g)(1) Challenges

I have reviewed the circuit landscape on this question, and it is useful to map the three competing frameworks that have emerged in the **Bruen** era:

**Theory One — Categorical Validity:** The majority of circuits — including the Second, Fourth, Eighth, Ninth, Tenth, and Eleventh — hold that any conviction for an offense punishable by more than one year categorically satisfies § 922(g)(1), regardless of the offense's relationship to violence or dangerousness. Under this view, Martha Stewart's conviction for making false statements to the FBI — a paradigmatically nonviolent offense — would permanently disqualify her from firearm possession. I do not believe this categorical approach will survive ultimate Supreme Court scrutiny under the **Bruen** methodology.

**Theory Two — Individualized Dangerousness Assessment:** The Third and Sixth Circuits require the sentencing court to make an individualized determination whether the specific defendant, in light of the offense of conviction, poses a violent danger to society. This approach treats the dangerousness inquiry as a case-by-case factual exercise at the time of the predicate conviction.

**Theory Three — Elemental Analysis:** The Fifth Circuit's approach, applied in **Hembry**, asks whether the essential elements of the offense of which the defendant was actually convicted — not allegations that were never proven — establish that the defendant is a violent danger to others or to society. Critically, the Fifth Circuit disregards uncharged conduct and sentencing-phase allegations that were never adjudicated. If the elements of the predicate offense do not establish a connection to violence or dangerousness, the historical-tradition prong of **Bruen** cannot be satisfied.

## The Court's Historical Analysis

The Fifth Circuit in **Hembry** examined whether founding-era American law contained analogous regulations that would support a lifetime firearms disqualification predicated on simple drug possession. The court concluded it did not. As the panel noted, narcotics regulation in the United States is a creature of the early twentieth century — federal criminalization of nonmedical drug use did not begin until the Harrison Narcotics Tax Act of 1914, and the Pure Food and Drug Act's labeling requirements only date to 1906. There is no founding-era analog because the conduct in question was not even criminal at the founding.

The government argued that drug purchasers enable drug traffickers, and that the violence endemic to drug trafficking should therefore attach to buyers. The Fifth Circuit rejected this theory, drawing a distinction that I find analytically correct and important: **drug trafficking** is categorically different from **drug possession** for purposes of the dangerousness inquiry. A drug trafficker must, by the nature of the enterprise, resort to actual or threatened violence to enforce contracts that courts will not enforce — there is no legal remedy for a drug deal gone wrong. That structural coerciveness is analogous, in my view, to the kind of dangerous conduct that historically supported disarmament. A simple drug possessor, by contrast, has demonstrated no such propensity for violence.

## Strategic Concerns for the Trump DOJ

The result in **Hembry** is correct as a matter of doctrine. I have no disagreement with the Fifth Circuit's analysis. But I want to be direct about a strategic point that is equally important.

The Supreme Court's Second Amendment jurisprudence is most durable when it is established in cases involving sympathetic — or at least neutral — fact patterns. **Heller** involved a law-abiding retired special police officer who wanted a handgun for home defense. **Bruen** involved applicants for unrestricted carry permits who had no disqualifying history. These are ideal vehicles for building precedent.

**Hembry** is not such a vehicle. The Court's current majority is simultaneously committed to the Second Amendment and deeply skeptical of extending constitutional protection to individuals with criminal histories — a tension that became visible in **Rahimi**, where even a majority that reaffirmed **Bruen**'s methodology upheld disarmament of a domestic-violence restraining-order subject. Pushing a meth-possession case to the Supreme Court risks producing a decision that narrows the **Bruen** framework in ways that disadvantage law-abiding Americans for years to come.

My recommendation to the Trump Department of Justice is clear: do not petition for certiorari in **Hembry**. Let this decision stand in the Fifth Circuit. Preserve the circuit split for a future case — one arising from a sympathetic, unambiguously nonviolent predicate — where the facts will present the constitutional question in its strongest light. The right to keep and bear arms belongs to law-abiding Americans, and the precedents that protect it should be built on cases that reflect that reality.

## Implications

**Hembry** reinforces the Fifth Circuit's position as the most analytically rigorous circuit on § 922(g)(1) challenges in the **Bruen** era. The decision's elemental analysis — which refuses to consider uncharged, unproven conduct and demands a genuine historical analog for the specific disqualifying offense — provides a model for how courts should approach as-applied challenges to the felon-in-possession statute.

The broader trajectory of this jurisprudence points toward a Supreme Court eventually addressing whether § 922(g)(1) is constitutionally applied to nonviolent felons as a class. When that moment arrives, the Second Amendment community's interests will be best served by presenting the cleanest possible factual record — not one featuring methamphetamine, not one featuring domestic violence, but one involving the kind of technical, malum prohibitum offense that makes the lifetime disarmament of an otherwise law-abiding citizen most obviously disproportionate to any legitimate governmental interest. That case is out there. We should be patient enough to wait for it.
