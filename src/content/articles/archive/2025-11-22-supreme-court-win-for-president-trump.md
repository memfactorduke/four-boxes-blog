## Summary

United States Supreme Court Justice Samuel Alito has entered an administrative stay halting the enforcement of a three-judge district court panel's order that would have required the State of Texas to use its 2021 congressional redistricting maps — which favored the Democratic Party — rather than the newly enacted 2025 maps favorable to Republicans, for the 2026 midterm election cycle. Justice Alito's order, entered late on a Friday evening, preserves the status quo and signals that the full Supreme Court is likely to review the lower court's action. This stay is a significant victory for the Trump administration and for the constitutional principle that politically motivated redistricting is a permissible legislative exercise so long as it is not driven by racial animus.

## Background

The State of Texas undertook a redistricting exercise in 2025, redrawing its congressional district maps for the state's more than forty United States House seats. The new maps, enacted by the Republican-controlled Texas legislature at the request of President Donald J. Trump, were drawn to favor Republican candidates in the 2026 midterm elections, with estimates suggesting the new maps could yield as many as five additional Republican House seats from Texas.

This redistricting effort was not without controversy. A subset of Texas Democrats vacated the state legislature in an effort to deny the Republican majority the quorum necessary to pass the redistricting legislation — a tactical maneuver that delayed but ultimately did not prevent the enactment of the new maps. After a prolonged standoff, Democrats returned to Austin, quorum was achieved, and the new maps were enacted into law.

Following enactment, a lawsuit was filed in the United States District Court for the Western District of Texas challenging the new maps under the Voting Rights Act. A three-judge panel, by a vote of two to one, held that the 2025 redistricting was motivated by racial discrimination rather than purely political considerations — i.e., that it was not legitimate political gerrymandering but instead an impermissible racial gerrymander in violation of the Voting Rights Act. The panel ordered Texas to revert to its 2021 congressional maps for the upcoming election cycle. Judge Jerry Smith dissented.

## Justice Alito's Stay Order

Justice Alito entered the following order:

> "Upon consideration of the application of counsel for applicants, it is ordered that the [November 18, 2025 order] of the United States District Court for the Western District of Texas is hereby administratively stayed pending further order of the undersigned or the Supreme Court."

The legal standard for an administrative stay at the Supreme Court requires the applicant to demonstrate, among other things, a likelihood of success on the merits and irreparable harm if the stay is denied. Justice Alito's willingness to enter the stay is a strong signal that he believes the lower court's order is likely to be reversed on the merits.

## The Racial Motivation Finding and Its Weaknesses

The two-judge majority's finding that Texas's 2025 redistricting was motivated by racial discrimination rather than political calculation is, in my view, difficult to sustain. The evidentiary record in this case reflects a standard political redistricting exercise — a majority party drawing maps designed to maximize its electoral advantage — that the courts have historically recognized as constitutionally permissible.

The Supreme Court addressed the interaction between racial gerrymandering and political gerrymandering in **Rucho v. Common Cause**, 588 U.S. 684 (2019), where the Court held that federal courts have no authority to review partisan gerrymandering claims under the Constitution. While **Rucho** does not directly govern claims brought under the Voting Rights Act, its reasoning is instructive. The Court recognized that drawing districts for partisan advantage is a practice as old as the Republic and one that the Constitution does not prohibit.

The majority's attempt to characterize President Trump's public statements to Governor Greg Abbott — urging Texas to redistrict in order to protect Republican House majorities — as evidence of racial motivation rather than political motivation stretches credulity. Political motivation and racial motivation are legally distinct. The fact that minority communities in Texas may vote disproportionately for one party does not transform partisan map-drawing into racial discrimination. Courts have consistently distinguished between these two categories.

## The Stakes for the 2026 Midterms

The practical stakes of this litigation are substantial. As of the date of Justice Alito's order, candidates had already declared their intention to run for House seats drawn under the 2025 maps. A reversion to the 2021 maps would require an entirely new set of candidacy decisions, potentially disenfranchising voters who have already organized around specific district configurations.

More broadly, the five additional Republican House seats that the 2025 maps are projected to yield could prove decisive to the Trump administration's legislative agenda. With narrow House margins, every seat matters. The lower court's order — directing the use of maps drawn four years ago under a prior legislative session and a prior political calculus — was an extraordinary intervention in the democratic process that threatens to substitute the judgment of three unelected federal judges for that of the elected representatives of the Texas people.

## The Pattern of Judicial Overreach

This case fits a broader pattern that I have discussed extensively on this channel: the weaponization of the federal judiciary by progressive plaintiffs and their allies to impede the implementation of democratically enacted laws that the left finds politically objectionable. The Texas redistricting litigation is not fundamentally different in kind from the injunctions that progressive judges have issued against immigration enforcement, against executive orders on multiple subjects, and against state legislation on a range of issues.

In each of these cases, the pattern is the same: a district court judge — often appointed by a Democratic president, often sitting in a liberal jurisdiction — issues a sweeping preliminary injunction that freezes the implementation of a duly enacted law or a properly exercised executive power, on the theory that the plaintiff is likely to succeed on the merits of a constitutional or statutory claim. The Supreme Court is then asked to stay those injunctions while the merits are litigated.

Justice Alito's order in this case is one more example of the Supreme Court functioning as the necessary corrective to lower court overreach. That corrective role is important and should be defended.

## Implications

Justice Alito's stay order is a significant and welcome development. It preserves the 2025 redistricting maps for the immediate election cycle, prevents the irreparable harm of forcing candidates to campaign under invalidated district lines, and signals the Supreme Court's likely disposition of the merits.

The full Court will presumably take up the application in short order. Given the composition of the current Court and the weakness of the majority's racial motivation finding, I expect the stay to be continued and, ultimately, the lower court's order to be reversed on the merits. The State of Texas exercised its constitutional prerogative to redraw its congressional districts following a new census and in response to changed political circumstances. That is precisely what the Constitution contemplates, and no district court has the authority to substitute its electoral preferences for the judgments of the Texas legislature.
