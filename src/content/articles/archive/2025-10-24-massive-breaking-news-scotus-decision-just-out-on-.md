## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has denied certiorari in **Lee v. Poudre School District**, a case in which Colorado parents sued a public school district alleging that school personnel facilitated their minor children's social gender transition without parental knowledge or consent. While the denial of certiorari is technically without precedential effect on the merits, the case is far from over as a constitutional matter — because Justice Samuel Alito, joined by Justices Neil Gorsuch and Clarence Thomas, filed a concurrence in the denial of certiorari that amounts to a pointed warning that the Supreme Court regards this issue as one of major constitutional importance requiring future resolution. The implications of that concurrence extend well beyond the facts of this particular case and directly implicate the right to keep and bear arms.

## Background

**Lee v. Poudre School District** arose in the United States District Court in Colorado after parents filed suit against a Colorado public school system. The parents' allegations were that the school district, through its policies and the conduct of its personnel, had encouraged or facilitated students' social transition to a new gender identity — and had done so deliberately behind the backs of parents, without parental knowledge or consent.

The district court dismissed the lawsuit on procedural grounds, including what the court characterized as issues of mootness and related technical arguments that made the case unsuitable for adjudication in its current procedural posture. The United States Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit affirmed the dismissal, concluding that the case presented procedural obstacles that prevented the court from reaching the merits of the constitutional questions. The parents then petitioned the Supreme Court for certiorari.

The Supreme Court, by a majority, declined to take the case. But it did not do so without comment. Justice Alito, with the express support of Justices Gorsuch and Thomas, concurred in the denial of certiorari while making unmistakably clear that the underlying constitutional issue — whether public school districts violate parents' fundamental rights when they, without parental knowledge or consent, encourage or facilitate a student's gender identity transition — is one that the Court regards as important and unresolved.

## Justice Alito's Concurrence: What He Said

Justice Alito's statement in concurrence with the denial of certiorari deserves close attention. He wrote, in substance, that while he agreed with the procedural basis for declining to hear the case in this particular vehicle, he remained deeply concerned that federal courts were systematically avoiding this constitutional question, and that the factual allegations in **Lee** — if accurate — were both troubling and representative of a broad national pattern.

Specifically, Justice Alito flagged the petitioners' allegation that nearly 6,000 public schools across the country maintain policies that purposely prevent parents from accessing information about their children's gender identity and about school personnel's involvement in influencing those choices. He characterized those allegations as underscoring the "great and growing national importance" of the constitutional question presented.

In other words, three of the most consequential Justices on the current Supreme Court — Alito, Gorsuch, and Thomas — have put the legal community on notice: this issue is coming to the Court, and when the right case arrives, the Court will address it on the merits.

## Why This Matters for Second Amendment Advocates

The connection between the parental-rights issue in **Lee** and the Second Amendment may not be immediately obvious, but it is real and important. The public school system in America has, in many jurisdictions, become an instrument for propagating anti-gun ideology. Children are taught from an early age that firearms are inherently dangerous and socially unacceptable, that the Founders intended only for militias to have guns, and that the Second Amendment should be interpreted narrowly to permit maximum gun control. These lessons are frequently taught without parental knowledge or approval, and in many cases actively contrary to the values that parents seek to instill in their children.

Parents who raise their children with a healthy respect for the Second Amendment — who teach their children how to handle firearms safely, who take their children hunting or to the shooting range, who instill in them an understanding of why the Founders regarded an armed citizenry as essential to a free republic — find themselves in conflict with a public school system that regularly undermines those lessons.

The same structural question is at stake in both contexts: Who has the authority to shape the values and beliefs of American children — parents or the government? The Supreme Court has long recognized, in cases dating back to **Pierce v. Society of Sisters** (1925) and **Meyer v. Nebraska** (1923), that parents have a fundamental constitutional right to direct the upbringing and education of their children. That right has been repeatedly recognized but not always vigorously enforced.

If the Supreme Court ultimately holds — as I believe it should and likely will — that public schools violate parents' fundamental constitutional rights when they deliberately withhold from parents information about significant events in their children's lives, that ruling will have implications for the entire range of ideological instruction in public schools, including instruction designed to instill anti-Second-Amendment beliefs.

## The Procedural Lesson

The denial of certiorari in **Lee** also teaches an important lesson about Supreme Court practice that advocates should understand. The Court's refusal to hear a case does not mean that the Court agrees with the lower court's decision. It typically means only that the particular case is not an appropriate vehicle for resolving the legal question presented — either because of procedural defects, as in **Lee**, or because the factual record is insufficiently developed, or because the Court prefers to wait for the issue to percolate further in the lower courts before weighing in.

Justice Alito's concurrence makes this point unmistakably clear. The three Justices who joined that statement were not concurring in the view that the constitutional question lacks merit. They were concurring in the procedural judgment that **Lee** was not the right case in which to address it, while simultaneously flagging the issue as one of major importance that will require Supreme Court attention in the future.

## Implications

The Supreme Court's action in **Lee** — and particularly Justice Alito's pointed concurrence — is a signal to litigants, attorneys, and advocates that the fundamental rights of parents to direct their children's upbringing are alive and before the Court, even if the Court was not yet ready to address them on the merits in this particular case.

For the Second Amendment community, this development reinforces the importance of parent-driven pushback against the anti-gun ideology embedded in many public school curricula. Parents have the constitutional right — rooted in the due process clause of the Fourteenth Amendment — to raise their children according to their own values, including values that embrace responsible firearm ownership and a deep respect for the constitutional right to keep and bear arms. That right deserves the same vigorous defense in the courts that we bring to bear on behalf of the Second Amendment itself.

The battle over who controls the education of America's children is inseparable from the battle over the future of the Second Amendment. A generation of children taught to fear and despise firearms — and taught to regard the right to keep and bear arms as an embarrassing historical relic — will be far less equipped to defend that right as adults. The stakes could not be higher.
