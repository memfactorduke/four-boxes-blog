## Summary

The United States Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi and Solicitor General John Sauer, has filed a significant brief before the United States Supreme Court in **West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF** — a case that will determine whether 18-, 19-, and 20-year-old Americans have a Second Amendment right to purchase handguns from federally licensed firearms dealers. The DOJ's brief takes the position that the Supreme Court should not grant certiorari in this case at the present time, arguing instead that the Court should wait until it has decided two other Second Amendment cases already on the docket — **Wolford v. Lopez** and **Hamani v. United States** — and then remand the young-adult question back to the lower courts for reconsideration in light of those forthcoming decisions. I want to analyze this brief carefully, because while the DOJ's Solicitor General has performed admirably in many areas this term, this particular position raises important concerns for the future of young adults' Second Amendment rights.

## Background

At the heart of **West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF** is 18 U.S.C. § 922(b)(1) and related regulations, which prohibit federally licensed firearms dealers — FFLs — from transferring handguns to persons under 21 years of age. An 18-year-old American can vote, sign contracts, serve in the military, and exercise every other privilege of adult citizenship — but cannot purchase a handgun from a licensed dealer. The West Virginia Citizens Defense League and other plaintiffs challenged this restriction as a violation of the Second Amendment.

The case arose out of the United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit — a court based in Richmond, Virginia, that has demonstrated a consistent and troubling hostility to Second Amendment rights. In a ruling that should surprise no one familiar with that court's track record, the Fourth Circuit upheld the federal age restriction, concluding that it passes constitutional muster. The plaintiffs then petitioned the Supreme Court for certiorari, asking the Court to take the case and definitively resolve whether the Second Amendment protects the right of adult Americans between the ages of 18 and 20 to purchase handguns.

## The DOJ's Position: Wait and GVR

The Solicitor General's office, acting on behalf of the Trump administration's Department of Justice, filed a brief in opposition to the certiorari petition. The substance of that brief, as I understand it, is this: the Supreme Court has already granted certiorari in two Second Amendment cases this term — **Wolford v. Lopez** and **Hamani v. United States**. The DOJ argues that those two cases will generate important Second Amendment precedent applying the *Bruen* framework, and that the better course of action is to wait for those decisions, then remand — through a GVR order, which stands for grant, vacate, and remand — the *West Virginia Citizens Defense League* case and other pending Second Amendment certiorari petitions back to the lower courts for reconsideration in light of whatever new law emerges.

The DOJ is essentially asking the Court to put this case on a shelf. Not to grant cert, not to deny cert, but to hold it in abeyance until *Wolford* and *Hamani* are decided, and then issue a GVR sending the young-adult question back to the Fourth Circuit.

## Why This Position Is Problematic

I want to be careful here, because I hold the Trump administration's Solicitor General John Sauer in high regard for his performance in many high-stakes matters before the Supreme Court this term. He has secured important victories on the emergency docket and has generally advanced a strong, constitutionally grounded position on behalf of the administration. On this particular question, however, I believe the DOJ has made a strategic and jurisprudential error.

The first problem is that the GVR approach does not actually resolve the young-adult question. Under the GVR procedure, the Supreme Court would vacate the Fourth Circuit's decision upholding the age restriction and remand the case for reconsideration in light of the Court's forthcoming decisions in *Wolford* and *Hamani*. But neither *Wolford* nor *Hamani* directly addresses whether 18-to-20-year-olds possess Second Amendment rights. *Wolford* concerns Hawaii's no-carry default rule on private property; *Hamani* concerns the constitutionality of disarming unlawful users of controlled substances. Neither case squarely presents or resolves the young-adult question.

When the Fourth Circuit receives the *West Virginia Citizens Defense League* case back on remand, it will face the same constitutional question it faced before — but with at most indirect guidance from the Supreme Court's decisions in *Wolford* and *Hamani*. Given the Fourth Circuit's demonstrated hostility to Second Amendment rights, there is a very real risk that the court will again uphold the age restriction, potentially with only modest adjustments to its reasoning, and the issue will need to travel all the way back up to the Supreme Court a second time. That is years of additional delay during which millions of young adult Americans are denied a constitutional right.

The second problem is that the federal age restriction on handgun purchases is constitutionally indefensible under the *Bruen* framework, and the DOJ's reluctance to squarely defend its unconstitutionality reflects a degree of institutional caution that does not serve the Second Amendment community well. The constitutional answer here is clear: 18-year-olds are adults. They are members of "the people" within the meaning of the Second Amendment. The historical record at the time of the Founding provides no support for excluding young adults from the right to keep and bear arms — to the contrary, young men of that precise age group were expected to serve in the militia and to bear arms.

## The Fourth Circuit's Track Record

The Fourth Circuit's treatment of this case merits comment. The court upheld the federal handgun purchase restriction as constitutional — a conclusion that is difficult to reconcile with the *Bruen* framework, which requires the government to identify a robust historical tradition supporting the challenged regulation. No such tradition exists for age-based categorical exclusions of adults from the right to keep and bear arms.

The Fourth Circuit has a history of reaching pro-gun-control outcomes in Second Amendment cases, sometimes through reasoning that strains the *Bruen* methodology to the point of distortion. It is precisely because the Fourth Circuit cannot be counted on to faithfully apply *Bruen* on remand that the Supreme Court's direct resolution of the young-adult issue would be far preferable to another trip through that court.

## The Reese Case and Membership Disclosure

An important related development worth noting in this context is the ongoing litigation in the *Reese v. ATF* case, in which the district court judge — on his own initiative, without any party having requested it — imposed a requirement that the Second Amendment organizations involved in the litigation turn over their membership lists to the court. That unprecedented and constitutionally suspect intrusion was promptly and correctly opposed by both the DOJ and the Second Amendment groups, on the grounds that it violated the First Amendment right of association. The district court subsequently vacated the membership disclosure requirement.

The *Reese* episode is relevant to the broader young-adult litigation landscape because it illustrates the kinds of procedural hazards that Second Amendment litigation faces in hostile courts. When a case is remanded to a district court or circuit court that is unsympathetic to Second Amendment rights, the litigation process itself can become a tool for harassment and delay, with courts imposing novel procedural burdens that deter organizations and individuals from exercising their constitutional rights in court.

## What the Second Amendment Foundation Argues

The Second Amendment Foundation, which has its own related litigation in the young-adult space, has taken the position that the Supreme Court should grant certiorari in **West Virginia Citizens Defense League** now rather than deferring to a GVR approach. The Foundation's reply brief outlined five independent reasons why the Court should act, including the depth of the circuit split on the young-adult question and the inadequacy of *Wolford* and *Hamani* to resolve it.

I find the Foundation's argument persuasive. The circuit split is real, the constitutional answer is clear, and the millions of young Americans who are currently denied a Second Amendment right they are constitutionally entitled to exercise are not served by further delay. The Supreme Court should take this case.

## Implications

The Department of Justice's brief in **West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF** is a significant document not only for what it says but for what it reveals about how the Trump administration is navigating the complex terrain of Second Amendment litigation at the Supreme Court level. The DOJ is generally a strong ally of the Second Amendment community this term, and Solicitor General Sauer deserves significant credit for the administration's overall constitutional posture. On this specific question, however, the administration has opted for an institutional caution that risks prolonging the denial of constitutional rights to adult Americans.

The Supreme Court should grant certiorari in **West Virginia Citizens Defense League**, hear argument on the merits, and deliver a clear ruling that the Second Amendment protects the right of 18-, 19-, and 20-year-old adults to purchase handguns from federally licensed dealers. The Constitution demands nothing less.
