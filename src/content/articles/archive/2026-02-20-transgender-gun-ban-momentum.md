## Summary

A recent mass shooting in Rhode Island—in which the perpetrator identified as transgender—has prompted public debate over whether legislation categorically prohibiting transgender individuals from possessing firearms would be constitutionally permissible. In my analysis, the answer under current Second Amendment doctrine is clearly no. The *Bruen* framework requires that any firearms restriction be grounded in a historically analogous tradition of regulation; no such tradition supports identity-based categorical disarmament. The existing legal mechanisms for addressing dangerous individuals—founded on individualized adjudication of dangerousness with due process protections—apply to all persons regardless of identity, and those mechanisms are both constitutionally adequate and constitutionally required.

## Background

The incident in question involved Robert Doran, who also went by Roberta Esposito, who opened fire at an amateur hockey rink in Rhode Island, killing multiple people including a former spouse and an adult child before dying at the scene. In the immediate aftermath, commentators in various quarters proposed that the government should restrict or eliminate firearms access for transgender individuals as a categorical class. The constitutional question this proposal raises is distinct from the question of whether any given individual who has demonstrated violent intent may be disarmed—a question the existing legal framework already answers—and it is the categorical question that requires serious constitutional analysis.

As I have reviewed the relevant legal landscape, the distinction between individualized disarmament based on adjudicated dangerousness and categorical disarmament based on identity is the central structural divide in modern Second Amendment jurisprudence. The former has deep historical roots and robust constitutional support. The latter has neither.

## The Governing Constitutional Framework

The Supreme Court's decisions in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), **McDonald v. City of Chicago**, 561 U.S. 742 (2010), and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), have established a framework for evaluating firearms regulations that centers on text and historical tradition. Under *Bruen*, the government bears the burden of demonstrating that any restriction on the right to keep and bear arms is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. The Court explicitly rejected interest-balancing approaches and directed courts to assess whether modern restrictions find genuine analogues in founding-era and Reconstruction-era law.

The 2024 decision in **United States v. Rahimi** elaborated on how the *Bruen* framework applies to laws disarming specific categories of individuals. The Court held that the government may constitutionally disarm a person who has been adjudicated—through a proceeding with due process protections—to pose a credible threat of physical violence to themselves or others. *Rahimi* grounded this holding in the founding-era surety law tradition, under which individuals could be required to post bond or face disarmament only after an individualized judicial finding of dangerousness. The decision confirmed that status-based disarmament divorced from an individualized finding of danger is constitutionally suspect.

## Founding-Era Disarmament Mechanisms

The founding generation recognized two principal legal mechanisms for restricting the firearms rights of individuals who posed a danger to the community. Both were predicated on individualized adjudication, not categorical classification.

**Affray laws** were criminal statutes addressing the misuse of arms to terrorize or threaten others. Under these laws, individuals who carried firearms in a manner calculated to provoke public fear—by going armed in a tumultuous fashion, threatening specific individuals, or engaging in riot—faced criminal liability and could be disarmed as a consequence of conviction. The operative trigger was conduct, not status: a person was subject to these laws because of what they had done or threatened to do, not because of who they were.

**Surety laws** were civil mechanisms through which a court, after a hearing, could require an individual found likely to endanger others to post a peace bond. Failure to post the required surety could result in disarmament. As with affray laws, the predicate was a formal legal proceeding with notice and an opportunity to be heard, resulting in an individualized finding that the specific person posed a specific danger to specific others. These laws operated on the basis of demonstrated, adjudicated risk—not on demographic classification.

Neither historical tradition supports a law that disarms all members of a defined demographic group without reference to the danger posed by any individual within that group. The historical record is affirmatively inconsistent with identity-based categorical disarmament.

## The Federal Statutory Framework: 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4)

The federal statutory prohibition most directly relevant to this discussion is 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4), which prohibits firearms possession by any person who "has been adjudicated as a mental defective or has been committed to a mental institution." The statute's operative language is carefully calibrated: it requires a formal adjudication or a formal involuntary commitment, both of which presuppose a legal proceeding with procedural safeguards. Neither provision extends to all individuals who carry a particular diagnosis, share a particular identity, or belong to a particular demographic category.

Gender dysphoria, the clinical diagnosis most commonly associated with transgender identity in psychiatric literature, does not by its terms constitute an adjudication of mental defect within the meaning of 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4). The question of whether any individual with gender dysphoria, or any other psychological condition, poses a danger to themselves or others is an individualized clinical and legal determination—one that cannot be resolved at the level of diagnostic category, let alone at the level of demographic identity. A clinician cannot lawfully certify that a person should be reported to NICS based on identity alone; the referral requires a documented clinical finding that the specific individual poses a specific danger.

## The Due Process Constraint

Beyond the Second Amendment, any categorical firearms prohibition targeting a defined demographic group would face independent constitutional scrutiny under the equal protection and due process components of the Fifth Amendment. A law that singled out transgender individuals for a categorical firearms disability—without any individualized finding of dangerousness and without historical grounding in the Nation's tradition of firearms regulation—would impose a severe burden on a constitutionally cognizable group based on identity alone. The constitutional difficulty compounds with each doctrinal layer.

Mental health professionals are not, as some have suggested, available as instruments of categorical policy in this context. Clinicians operate under professional and ethical standards that require individualized assessment. The "duty to warn"—recognized in most jurisdictions following *Tarasoff v. Regents of the University of California*—requires a mental health professional to notify law enforcement when a patient makes a specific, credible threat of harm against an identifiable third party. This duty applies universally, regardless of the patient's identity, and it operates on the basis of specific threatening communications, not on the basis of who the patient is. It is an appropriate mechanism for addressing individualized, proximate danger, and it cannot be converted into a basis for categorical disarmament.

## The Constitutional Ceiling

In my assessment, there is no legally viable pathway to a categorical firearms prohibition targeting transgender individuals that would withstand constitutional scrutiny under current Second Amendment doctrine. Such a law would fail the *Bruen* test because no historical tradition of identity-based categorical disarmament exists. It would fail the *Rahimi* standard because it would not rest on any adjudication of individualized dangerousness. It would face independent Fifth Amendment challenges under equal protection principles. And it would conflict with the settled understanding that the right to keep and bear arms—like other enumerated rights—is not subject to suspension on the basis of demographic characteristics absent a compelling, individually adjudicated justification.

The tools the legal system already provides are adequate for addressing individuals who have demonstrated dangerous propensities. The existing prohibition in 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4) applies to persons actually adjudicated mentally defective or involuntarily committed. Mental health professionals' duty to warn operates when a patient communicates a specific threat. Courts may issue protective orders based on credible evidence of danger. Law enforcement may seek emergency mental health evaluations where appropriate. These mechanisms are not perfect instruments, but they are the instruments the Constitution permits—predicated on individual conduct and adjudicated risk, not on the identity of the person subject to the restriction.

## Implications

The constitutional analysis here does not turn on any evaluative judgment about transgender individuals, their identity, or their fitness to exercise constitutional rights. It turns on the principle—embedded in the Second Amendment's text, in the founding-era surety tradition, in *Heller*, *Bruen*, and *Rahimi*, and in the due process guarantees of the Fifth Amendment—that the government may not categorically strip a class of persons of a fundamental constitutional right based on identity alone without individual adjudication and due process.

As I have argued consistently, the Second Amendment's protections belong to the "people"—a term the Supreme Court has construed to mean law-abiding Americans generally, without demographic carve-outs. The response to any mass casualty event, however tragic, must be assessed against the constitutional framework rather than the emotional urgency of the moment. Proposals for categorical identity-based firearms prohibitions do not survive that assessment.
