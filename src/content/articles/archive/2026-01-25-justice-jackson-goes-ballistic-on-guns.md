## Summary

The oral argument in **Wolford v. Lopez** — the Supreme Court case challenging Hawaii's law that prohibits licensed concealed carry holders from bringing firearms onto any private property open to the public absent the property owner's express prior consent — produced what I submit is one of the most remarkable exchanges in recent Second Amendment jurisprudence: Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson, a Biden appointee to the Court, invoked post-Civil War Black Codes from the former Confederate states as purported historical support for Hawaii's modern gun control law. That argument drew pointed responses from the Court's conservative justices, most notably Chief Justice John Roberts and Justice Samuel Alito, who observed that the Second Amendment was being treated by Hawaii's counsel as a "second-class right" — a characterization that will likely prove central to the majority opinion when the Court decides this case. In my assessment, the Second Amendment will prevail in *Wolford v. Lopez*, and the oral argument confirms that expectation.

## Background: The Hawaii "Vampire Rule"

The law at issue in *Wolford v. Lopez* has been colloquially termed the "vampire rule" — a reference to the folklore notion that a vampire cannot enter a dwelling without the owner's invitation. Under the Hawaii statute, a person with a valid concealed carry license is prohibited from carrying their firearm onto any private property that is open to the public — a Starbucks, a grocery store, a gas station, a barbershop, a restaurant — unless the property owner has affirmatively and expressly given the carry holder permission to bring the firearm onto the premises in advance.

The practical consequence of this requirement is effectively prohibitive. Few carry holders have any means of identifying who owns or manages the dozens of privately owned but publicly accessible spaces they encounter in the course of an ordinary day, let alone any mechanism for obtaining advance permission from those owners before entering. The law thus inverts the traditional common law presumption that a person licensed to carry may do so until told otherwise, and replaces it with a regime in which carry is forbidden everywhere until specifically permitted — a regime that, as Chief Justice Roberts observed during argument, has no parallel in First Amendment jurisprudence or in any other area of constitutional law.

## The *Bruen* Framework and Hawaii's Burden

Under the analytical framework established in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the Second Amendment's plain text protects the right of law-abiding citizens to carry firearms in public for self-defense. A government that seeks to restrict this right bears the burden of demonstrating that its restriction is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation, rooted in the founding era.

Hawaii conceded at oral argument that its law burdens conduct covered by the Second Amendment's plain text — that is, it burdens "arms-bearing conduct" as the Court uses that phrase. The entire contest in *Wolford*, therefore, turned on the historical tradition inquiry: can Hawaii identify founding-era analogues that sufficiently resemble, in both justification and burden, its sweeping prohibition on carry at all publicly accessible private properties?

The answer that emerged from oral argument is that Hawaii cannot. The historical record contains no founding-era tradition of requiring citizens to obtain affirmative permission from property owners before exercising the right to carry in places open to the public. Hawaii's counsel struggled to articulate a coherent historical basis for the law, and the transcript reveals that multiple justices — across ideological lines — found Hawaii's historical argument unpersuasive.

## Justice Jackson's Black Code Argument

The most discussed exchange of the oral argument involved Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson, who raised the question whether post-Civil War laws from Louisiana and other former Confederate states — laws that restricted the public carrying of firearms — could serve as the historical analogues Hawaii needed to sustain its statute.

As I have explained in prior analysis, these laws are Black Codes. They were enacted in 1865 and immediately thereafter by the unreconstructed governments of the former Confederate states for the express purpose of disarming the newly freed African American population. The intent was to deny freed Black Americans the ability to hunt, protect themselves, provide for their families through subsistence activities, and exercise the full rights of citizenship. By stripping them of firearms, the former slave states sought to force freed people back onto plantations as de facto indentured laborers — the legal form of slavery had been abolished, but the functional dependency it created could be replicated through economic coercion backed by the threat of violence that only the white population remained armed to carry out.

That Justice Jackson — herself a Black woman and a Biden appointee to the Court — would invoke these laws as potential constitutional justification for a modern gun control restriction is, I submit, extraordinary. The irony is not merely rhetorical. Under *Bruen*'s framework, the critical question is not merely whether a historical law superficially resembles the modern restriction, but whether that historical law reflects a legitimate constitutional tradition. Laws enacted by white supremacist governments for the purpose of suppressing the constitutional rights of Black Americans represent, if anything, a tradition of *violating* the Second Amendment — not a tradition of permissible regulation.

## The Conservative Justices' Responses

Chief Justice Roberts and Justice Alito both pressed Hawaii's counsel on precisely this point during oral argument. Chief Justice Roberts drew an explicit parallel to First Amendment jurisprudence: a state could not require a candidate for office, exercising the First Amendment right to canvass door-to-door on private property, to obtain the property owner's advance permission before knocking. The First Amendment has always been understood to include a default presumption in favor of the right — you exercise it until you are told to stop, not the reverse. Roberts challenged Hawaii to explain why the Second Amendment should operate on any different default logic.

Justice Alito put the issue even more starkly, noting that Hawaii's framework "relegate[s] the Second Amendment to second-class status" — a phrase that directly echoes the Supreme Court's own language in *Heller* and *Bruen* admonishing courts not to treat the Second Amendment as a lesser constitutional right. Alito pointed out that Hawaii's approach, applied to other constitutional rights, would be instantly recognizable as unconstitutional: a state could not require citizens to obtain advance permission from private property owners before wearing politically expressive attire onto publicly accessible premises, and yet Hawaii insisted it could impose exactly that burden on the exercise of Second Amendment rights.

Justice Amy Coney Barrett elaborated on this analysis in a particularly illuminating exchange. She posited the scenario in which a state, absent public accommodation laws, enacted a statute requiring Black citizens to obtain the property owner's affirmative consent before entering privately owned but publicly accessible spaces. No court would sustain such a law under the Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection guarantee. The logical implication of her inquiry was clear: if you would not apply that consent-default regime to race, or to free speech, there is no principled basis for applying it to the Second Amendment.

## The Two Dimensions of Every Supreme Court Decision

In assessing *Wolford v. Lopez*, I emphasize a point I have advanced consistently in prior analysis: there are two dimensions to every Supreme Court decision, and Second Amendment advocates must attend to both.

The first dimension is the outcome — who wins the case. I predict with confidence that the Second Amendment will prevail in *Wolford*. The oral argument makes clear that the Court's six-justice conservative majority is skeptical of Hawaii's position, and none of the three liberal justices appears poised to provide the fifth vote for Hawaii even if any of the conservatives were to defect — which the transcript does not suggest.

The second dimension — and in many respects the more important one for the long-term trajectory of Second Amendment jurisprudence — is the reasoning and methodology set forth in the majority opinion. That reasoning will be cited by lower courts in hundreds, potentially thousands, of future cases. A broad ruling that firmly establishes the default presumption in favor of carry, limits the sensitive places exception to historically grounded categories, and rejects post-Civil War Black Codes and Reconstruction-era statutes as permissible historical analogues would have enormous downstream consequences for Second Amendment litigation nationwide.

It is therefore not enough to win in *Wolford*. The Second Amendment community must advocate for a majority opinion that adopts the strongest possible reasoning — one that closes off the avenues lower courts have used to uphold restrictive carry laws by stretching or distorting the historical record.

## Implications

The oral argument in *Wolford v. Lopez* reveals a Supreme Court that is engaged with and attentive to the Second Amendment, that is skeptical of Hawaii's inverted default presumption, and that appears prepared to issue a decision vindicating the right of licensed carry holders to bring their firearms into the ordinary spaces of daily life without first conducting a search for property owners from whom they must beg permission.

Justice Jackson's invocation of Black Codes as potential constitutional support for Hawaii's law is, in my assessment, not only legally incorrect under *Bruen*'s demanding historical tradition test, but historically troubling. The Court's conservative majority is unlikely to be persuaded by it, and the dissent those justices will write in response should serve as a durable repudiation of that historical methodology.

I expect the decision to arrive sometime in late spring or early summer of this term, and I remain confident that it will represent a significant and lasting victory for the Second Amendment.
