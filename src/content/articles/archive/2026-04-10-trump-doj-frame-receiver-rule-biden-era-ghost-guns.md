The Second Amendment community erupted this week over a statement by a Department of Justice trial attorney in the Northern District of Texas indicating that, as of April 8, 2026, the Trump administration is maintaining the Biden-era definition of what constitutes a firearm frame or receiver. The reaction online was sharp — accusations of betrayal, suggestions that the Trump DOJ is perpetuating Biden's so-called ghost gun regime indefinitely.

I want to pump the brakes. What actually happened is far more mundane than the social media furor suggests, and understanding it requires grasping both the procedural posture of the pending *VanDerStok* litigation and a basic administrative law principle that governs rulemaking when a federal agency lacks a Senate-confirmed director.

## The VanDerStok Litigation and Its Current Posture

*VanDerStok v. Garland* involves an Administrative Procedure Act challenge to ATF Final Rule 2021R-05F — the Biden-era rule redefining what qualifies as a "frame or receiver" under federal firearms statutes. The Supreme Court, in *Bondi v. VanDerStok*, resolved a narrow slice of the dispute in a 7-2 decision, holding that the rule did not violate the APA in the specific respects before it. What the Court emphatically did not do was bless the rule wholesale or foreclose further challenges. Significant portions of the underlying litigation remain live.

After the Supreme Court's decision, the case was remanded and is now pending before Chief Judge Reed O'Connor in the Northern District of Texas. Because the Trump administration signaled it might revoke the Biden-era rule — which could render the litigation moot — the parties agreed to stay proceedings while the rulemaking process unfolds. There was one critical exception: the Second Amendment Foundation (SAF) and Defense Distributed declined to wait. They want to press forward to summary judgment now. The litigation is not dead. Two significant plaintiffs are actively pushing to resolve it on the merits.

## What the April 8 Statement Actually Said

Because the parties had agreed to file a proposed summary judgment briefing schedule by April 9, a DOJ trial attorney sent the parties an email on April 8 providing a factual update. The substance: as of April 8, 2026, the government has decided to maintain the current definition of firearm frame or receiver and is not making changes at this time.

That statement was factually accurate as of the date it was made. It is a litigation update — the kind of thing lawyers say to a court when it is demanding to know the current posture of a case before setting a briefing schedule. It is not a statement from the ATF Director, the Attorney General, or the White House. It is a low-level trial attorney describing the status quo for the specific purpose of scheduling motion practice. Reading it as a definitive signal that the Trump administration has abandoned any intent to revise the frame-or-receiver rule is a category error.

## The Real Reason: No Confirmed ATF Director

Here is what the social media commentary has almost universally overlooked: ATF does not have a Senate-confirmed director. Robert Cekada was nominated to lead ATF in January 2026 and has had his confirmation hearing, but the Senate has not yet acted. This matters because President Trump's executive order at the outset of his administration directed all agencies not to propose or issue any rule until a department or agency head appointed by the president reviews and approves it. Trump's own Executive Order "Protecting Second Amendment Rights," signed February 7, 2025, reinforces the expectation of regulatory action — but that action requires confirmed leadership to execute.

This is not a Trump invention. Barack Obama and George W. Bush issued identical directives. Without a confirmed director, ATF is functionally prohibited from initiating new rulemaking — including any revision to the frame-or-receiver definition. That is not a failure of political will. It is the standard operating procedure of the administrative state.

## What Comes Next

The logical inference is that ATF is waiting for Cekada's Senate confirmation before it moves on the frame-or-receiver rule. Once he is seated, there is nothing structurally preventing ATF from initiating a proceeding to amend or rescind the Biden-era definition. The delay is in the Senate's hands, not the administration's.

Meanwhile, SAF and Defense Distributed are pressing forward in *VanDerStok* on the merits. Those are the metrics that matter. A single factual statement by a low-level trial lawyer negotiating a scheduling order in Texas is not a permanent policy declaration — and treating it as one does the Second Amendment community no favors.
