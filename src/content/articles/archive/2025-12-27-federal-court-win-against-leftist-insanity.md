## Summary

United States District Judge Roger Benitez of the Southern District of California — the same judge whose Second Amendment rulings have consistently upheld the constitutional rights of California gun owners — has issued a significant ruling in **Mirabelli v. Olsen**, holding in favor of parents and teachers against California's education system. The case presents the question of whether California public school districts may, in the name of student privacy, conceal from parents the fact that their minor child has expressed gender dysphoria at school, adopted an alternative gender identity, and is being addressed by a new name and pronouns by school staff — without parental knowledge or consent. Judge Benitez ruled that this concealment policy violates the rights of parents and teachers. In my assessment, **Mirabelli v. Olsen** is a critically important decision, and it is directly connected to the Second Amendment in this respect: the contest over who raises the next generation — the state or parents — will determine whether the constitutional values that undergird the right to keep and bear arms survive into the future.

## Background: California's Gender Identity Concealment Policy

California's public school system adopted policies and regulations providing that if a student of any age within the public school system expresses gender dysphoria — meaning, for example, that a boy says he identifies as a girl, wishes to be called by a new name, and wishes to be treated as a member of the opposite sex — the school system is prohibited from informing the student's parents of these expressions without the student's consent.

The school system characterized these policies as privacy protections for the student. The stated concern was that some students who express gender dysphoria may face a hostile reaction from their parents, and that notifying parents without the student's consent could endanger the student.

The effect of these policies, however, extended far beyond the limited circumstance of genuinely endangered students. They created a categorical rule: the school system knew information about a child's expressed gender identity; the child's parents did not; and the school system was affirmatively prohibited from telling parents what it knew. Teachers who believed that parents should be informed — or who felt morally and professionally obligated to communicate with the parents of children in their classrooms — were likewise prohibited.

## Judge Benitez's Ruling: The Legal Analysis

Judge Benitez ruled in favor of the plaintiff parents and teachers, finding that California's gender identity concealment policies violate constitutional rights. The decision is grounded in the well-established constitutional principle that parents have a fundamental right — protected by the Due Process Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment — to direct the upbringing and education of their children.

This principle has deep roots in Supreme Court precedent. In *Pierce v. Society of Sisters*, 268 U.S. 510 (1925), the Supreme Court held that the state may not standardize its children by forcing them to attend only public schools; parents retain the right to direct the religious and educational upbringing of their children. In *Troxel v. Granville*, 530 U.S. 57 (2000), the Supreme Court reaffirmed that the interest of parents in the care, custody, and control of their children is perhaps the oldest of the fundamental liberty interests recognized by the Court.

A school system that conceals from parents material information about their minor child's expressed psychological state — information that is directly relevant to the child's mental health, social development, and medical treatment decisions that parents have a legal right to make — interferes with this fundamental parental right. The school does not step into loco parentis for the purpose of overriding parental rights; it stands in loco parentis as a limited agent of the parents, not as a replacement for them.

Furthermore, the policies at issue suppressed the speech of teachers who wished to communicate with parents. Judge Benitez's ruling, in protecting teachers who sought to inform parents, addresses the First Amendment dimension: the state cannot compel teachers to maintain a coerced silence about matters that are fundamental to the educational and parental relationship.

## The Broader Significance: Who Raises the Children?

The deeper question presented by **Mirabelli v. Olsen** — and by the broader conflict between parental rights and the progressive education establishment in California and elsewhere — is fundamental: who is the primary authority over the raising of children, the parents or the government?

Governor Gavin Newsom and the California education bureaucracy have treated the public school system as a vehicle for instilling ideological commitments that many parents affirmatively reject. When the school system simultaneously indoctrinates children in politically contested ideological positions and prohibits parents from knowing what is happening to their children, it is not acting as a neutral educational institution. It is acting as a competitor to the family — and a competitor with the coercive power of the state behind it.

California's policies in this area reflect a view that the state, through its educational apparatus, has a superior claim to children's development over the objections of parents. That view is constitutionally wrong and historically dangerous. It is precisely the kind of expansion of state authority over the individual — specifically over the most fundamental unit of civil society, the family — that the constitutional system was designed to prevent.

## The Connection to Second Amendment Rights

The connection between parental rights and Second Amendment rights is not superficial. At its most basic, the Second Amendment right is a right of individual citizens to be armed. That right, like all constitutional rights, is reproduced across generations through the transmission of constitutional values — through parents who teach their children about the founding, the Bill of Rights, and the importance of individual liberty, including the right to keep and bear arms.

A generation raised by California's public school system under the policies at issue in **Mirabelli v. Olsen** — raised to believe that gender identity is a matter for institutional management rather than parental guidance, that the school's authority supersedes the family's, that individual identity is plastic and subject to progressive redefinition — is a generation being systematically trained away from the constitutional values that sustain the Second Amendment.

Someone must raise the children. The alternative is the state's educational apparatus. Judge Benitez's ruling — like his Second Amendment rulings that found AR-15s and standard-capacity magazines to be constitutionally protected arms — reflects an understanding that the Constitution means what it says, that individuals have rights that the state must respect, and that courts have an obligation to enforce those rights even when the state would prefer to ignore them.

## Implications

**Mirabelli v. Olsen** is a significant victory for parental rights, for teachers' freedom of conscience, and for the constitutional principle that the family, not the state, is the primary institution through which children are raised and values are transmitted. In my assessment, this decision by Judge Benitez continues a record of fidelity to the constitutional text that has made him one of the most consequential federal judges in the country. The connection to Second Amendment rights is real: the constitutional culture that sustains the right to keep and bear arms is the same constitutional culture that protects parental authority, individual identity, and the primacy of the family over the state. Victories in one domain reinforce victories in the other.
