## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit has issued a significant First Amendment ruling in favor of Professor Stuart Reges, a computer science professor at the University of Washington, who was subjected to retaliation after he parodied the university's official land acknowledgment statement in his course syllabus. The Ninth Circuit's 2-1 majority held that the University of Washington — a public institution and therefore a government actor — violated Professor Reges's First Amendment right to free speech when it investigated, reprimanded, and threatened to discipline him for expressing his personal views on a matter of public concern. The district court's ruling against Professor Reges was reversed, and the case was remanded for further proceedings.

This decision is a meaningful vindication of academic freedom and the First Amendment's particular role in protecting the free exchange of ideas at public universities. It also reinforces a principle that extends well beyond the campus: government actors may not penalize individuals for expressing constitutionally protected views on contested matters of public policy simply because those views are unwelcome.

## Background

The University of Washington, a public university and constituent institution of the State of Washington, adopted an official land acknowledgment statement developed through its Office of Minority Affairs and Diversity. The statement — which reads: "The University of Washington acknowledges the Coast Salish peoples of this land, the land which touches the shared waters of all tribes and bands within the Squamish, Tulalip, and Muckleshoot nations" — was presented to faculty members as a model statement to be incorporated into course syllabi and read at the opening of classes.

Professor Reges, a computer science professor, declined to deliver the university's preferred formulation. Instead, he authored his own statement, which he included in his course syllabus at the start of the academic term. The statement, dated January 3, read: "I acknowledge that by the labor theory of property, the Coast Salish people can claim historical ownership of almost none of the land currently occupied by the University of Washington."

The statement was plainly framed as Professor Reges's personal view and grounded in a recognized philosophical tradition — the labor theory of property associated with John Locke, whose natural rights philosophy directly informed the Declaration of Independence and the constitutional framework of the United States. Far from being gratuitous provocation, it constituted a substantive engagement with the ideological premises underlying land acknowledgment practice, offering a reasoned counterpoint to the institutional message the university was promoting.

## The University's Retaliatory Response

The university's reaction was swift and punitive. Students and faculty complained. The dean intervened. University administrators launched disciplinary investigations. Professor Reges was subjected to adverse employment actions including delays in pay. He was pressured to withdraw the statement, issue a public apology, and conform his syllabus to the university's preferred language. The university created a separate course section — effectively quarantining students who did not wish to be exposed to his views.

Professor Reges refused to capitulate. He maintained his position and eventually brought suit against the University of Washington, arguing that the institution's retaliatory conduct violated his First Amendment rights. The district court ruled against him. He appealed to the Ninth Circuit.

## The Ninth Circuit's Analysis

The Ninth Circuit reversed, ruling 2-1 that the university's actions constituted unconstitutional First Amendment retaliation. The court's analysis turned on several well-established principles.

**The public university is a government actor.** Unlike private universities, which are not bound by constitutional constraints in their internal governance decisions, public universities are arms of the state. The constitutional guarantees that restrict government action — including the First Amendment's prohibition on viewpoint-based censorship and retaliation — apply fully to public universities and their administrations. This threshold principle was undisputed: the University of Washington is a state institution, and its treatment of Professor Reges was state action subject to First Amendment scrutiny.

**The speech addressed a matter of public concern.** First Amendment retaliation claims by public employees require that the speech at issue address a matter of public concern — a standard that measures whether the speech relates to issues of community interest rather than purely private employment grievances. The Ninth Circuit had no difficulty concluding that Professor Reges's statement satisfied this standard. Land acknowledgment practices, the history of property rights, and the appropriate relationship between universities and contested ideological narratives are matters of genuine public significance and continuing public debate.

**The university retaliated for the content of the speech.** The adverse employment actions taken against Professor Reges followed directly from the content of his statement and the fact that it contradicted the university's preferred ideological position. The Ninth Circuit summarized its holding with unusual clarity in its majority opinion: "A public university investigated, reprimanded, and threatened to discipline a professor for contentious statements he made in a class syllabus. The statements, which mocked the university's model syllabus statement on the issue of public concern, caused offense in the university community. Yet, debate and disagreement are the hallmarks of higher education. Student discomfort with a professor's views can prompt discussion and disapproval, but this discomfort is not grounds for the university retaliating against the professor. We hold that the university's actions toward the professor violated his First Amendment rights."

## The Heckler's Veto Problem

A particularly important principle underlying the Ninth Circuit's ruling is the doctrine prohibiting what constitutional law calls the "heckler's veto." The government may not suppress speech because some members of the audience object to it. The fact that students complained about Professor Reges's syllabus statement did not authorize the university to punish him for that statement. Indeed, if student or faculty disapproval could itself justify silencing a professor, the First Amendment's protection for academic expression would be entirely hollow — because controversial viewpoints are, by definition, the viewpoints most likely to generate complaints.

The university's conduct in this case followed precisely the heckler's veto pattern. Complaints were lodged; the institution responded by targeting the speaker rather than defending his right to speak. The Ninth Circuit correctly refused to endorse that approach.

## The Special Role of the University in First Amendment Law

The Ninth Circuit's majority opinion emphasized the distinctive constitutional status of the public university as a forum for intellectual inquiry. In the court's words: "The public university occupies a central place in the law of the First Amendment. The First Amendment protects the free exchange of ideas. The university is a primary generator and repository of ideas, a place in which unfettered academic debate and open discourse promotes the search for truth and prepares students for a discordant world lacking in orthodoxy. When we place limits on what professors may say or impose punishment for the views they express, we detock the marketplace of ideas and imperil future generations who must be exposed to a range of ideas and readied for the disharmony of a democratic society."

These are not novel sentiments; they echo decades of Supreme Court precedent affirming that academic freedom is a special concern of the First Amendment and that the classroom is "peculiarly the marketplace of ideas." What the Ninth Circuit's decision adds is a concrete application of those principles to a contemporary form of campus viewpoint discrimination — the enforcement, through institutional retaliation, of conformity with official ideological statements on contested historical and political questions.

## Compelled Speech and Viewpoint Neutrality

Implicit in the Ninth Circuit's analysis, and worth making explicit, is the compelled speech dimension of this case. The University of Washington was not merely declining to endorse Professor Reges's views. It was affirmatively pressuring him to deliver the university's preferred ideological statement in his own voice to his own students, and punishing him for refusing. The Supreme Court's compelled speech doctrine, most prominently articulated in *Wooley v. Maynard* (1977) and *Barnette v. West Virginia Board of Education* (1943), holds that the government may not compel individuals to affirm beliefs they do not hold. A university that demands faculty members personally voice institutional ideology under threat of adverse employment action has crossed into compelled speech territory — a constitutional violation independent of any retaliation theory.

## Implications

The Ninth Circuit's ruling in *Reges v. University of Washington* is significant for at least three reasons.

First, it establishes that a public university's promotion of official ideological statements does not license adverse action against faculty members who decline to adopt those statements as their own. The line between institutional advocacy and unconstitutional coercion runs precisely at the point where the government's preferred message becomes compulsory.

Second, the decision is a meaningful precedent in the Ninth Circuit — a circuit that covers a large portion of the western United States, including California, Oregon, Washington, and other states where land acknowledgment practices and similar ideological programs have become prevalent in public higher education. Administrators at public universities within this circuit should understand that retaliatory actions against faculty who express dissenting views on contested institutional policies now carry substantial legal risk.

Third, the case illustrates the interdependence of constitutional liberties. The First and Second Amendments operate in the same constitutional order, protecting different dimensions of individual freedom against government overreach. A legal culture that takes government censorship of dissenting academic speech seriously is the same legal culture that takes government restrictions on the exercise of constitutional rights seriously. The vitality of constitutional rights depends on the willingness of courts to apply them rigorously, whatever the political valence of the speech or conduct at issue.

Professor Reges's refusal to be silenced — and the Ninth Circuit's vindication of that refusal — is a reminder that constitutional rights are not self-executing. They require individuals willing to assert them and courts willing to enforce them.
