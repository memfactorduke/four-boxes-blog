## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit has issued a writ of mandamus directing a federal district court judge in Chicago to cease her unauthorized daily supervision of immigration enforcement operations conducted by the Department of Homeland Security. The order represents a significant and correct application of constitutional separation of powers principles. A district court judge's attempt to require a senior executive branch official to appear in her courtroom each evening and report on the day's immigration enforcement activities constituted a profound violation of the division of authority among the three branches of government — precisely the kind of overreach the mandamus power exists to correct.

## Background

ICE and the Department of Homeland Security, under Secretary Kristi Noem, have been conducting immigration enforcement operations throughout the City of Chicago, targeting individuals who have entered the country illegally and refused to comply with removal orders. Chicago has long functioned as a sanctuary jurisdiction, actively seeking to impede federal immigration enforcement.

In this context, a federal district court judge in Chicago — Judge Sarah Ellis, an Obama appointee — entered a temporary restraining order affecting certain aspects of immigration law enforcement in the Chicago area. So far, this was within the ordinary judicial power to issue emergency injunctive relief pending litigation.

What followed, however, was an extraordinary exercise of self-granted power that violated the Constitution's structural separation of the three branches of government.

## The District Court's Unauthorized Monitoring Order

After issuing the TRO, Judge Ellis, acting on her own motion and without any request from the plaintiffs' counsel, entered an additional order requiring Greg Bovino — a Chief Patrol Agent at United States Customs and Border Protection, and a senior Article II executive officer responsible for ground-level immigration enforcement in Chicago — to appear in her courtroom at 5:45 p.m. every weekday. The purpose of these nightly appearances was for Agent Bovino to report to the judge on all use-of-force activities that had occurred each day.

The judge's stated justification was that she had seen social media videos that led her to question whether her TRO was being complied with. Critically, those videos did not concern conduct involving any of the plaintiffs in the litigation before her.

## The Constitutional Problem

The separation of powers violation here is not subtle. The United States Constitution creates three distinct branches of government. Article I vests legislative power in Congress. Article II vests executive power in the President. Article III vests judicial power in the Supreme Court and such inferior courts as Congress may establish.

An Article III judge is vested with judicial power — the authority to resolve disputes between parties, to apply the law to facts presented in cases and controversies, and to issue orders necessary to the resolution of those disputes. An Article III judge is not vested with supervisory authority over Article II executive officers in the performance of their executive functions.

What Judge Ellis attempted was the conversion of a judicial forum into an ongoing supervisory apparatus for the executive branch. By requiring a senior immigration enforcement officer to appear nightly and report on the day's activities, she effectively appointed herself as an overseer of executive operations that were not subject to any specific judicial order she had authority to enforce. This is precisely what the separation of powers prohibits.

As the Seventh Circuit correctly noted in granting the mandamus petition:

> "After the district trial court issued a temporary restraining order affecting some aspects of immigration law enforcement in the Chicago area, the judge on her own motion entered a further order requiring Greg Bovino, a chief patrol agent at U.S. Customs and Border Protection, to appear in court at 5:45 p.m. every weekday to report on the use of force activities for each day. The district court judge then justified this order by stating that she had seen videos that led her to question whether the TRO she had entered was being obeyed. The order was not, however, a response to any motion by counsel for the plaintiffs, and the videos to which the judge referred apparently do not deal with behavior involving any of the plaintiffs."

## The Practical Impact of the Monitoring Order

Beyond the constitutional deficiency, the monitoring order created a serious practical burden on immigration enforcement operations. Agent Bovino's daily responsibilities involved directing enforcement activities across a large metropolitan area. Requiring him to appear in court each evening at 5:45 p.m. would have effectively consumed the latter portion of each workday in preparation for and attendance at the nightly sessions. His attention would have been diverted from enforcement operations to preparing testimony. His time in transit to and from the courthouse was time not spent supervising operations. The effect, whether intended or not, was to substantially degrade the operational capacity of immigration enforcement in Chicago.

## The Mandamus Grant

The Bondi Department of Justice and Noem's Department of Homeland Security immediately sought a writ of mandamus from the Seventh Circuit. The mandamus issued without delay. The Seventh Circuit's order directed Judge Ellis to cease the nightly monitoring requirement, finding that it violated the structural separation of powers.

Mandamus is an extraordinary remedy, available only when a lower court has clearly exceeded its jurisdiction or abused its power in a manner that cannot be corrected through ordinary appellate review. The Seventh Circuit's willingness to grant mandamus here reflects the court's recognition that Judge Ellis's monitoring order was not a close constitutional question — it was a clear overreach that required immediate correction.

## Significance for the Rule of Law

This episode illustrates a pattern that has become disturbingly common in federal courts: district court judges in jurisdictions with particular ideological alignments entering orders that extend well beyond the resolution of specific disputes and that seek to subject executive branch operations to ongoing judicial oversight. The Constitution does not permit this.

The executive branch's authority to enforce the nation's immigration laws derives from Article II and from statutes enacted by Congress. A district court judge does not possess supervisory authority over the exercise of that executive power simply because a lawsuit has been filed in her court. Courts may adjudicate specific claims, grant or deny injunctive relief in specific cases, and review specific government actions for constitutional and statutory compliance. Courts may not appoint themselves as continuing monitors and supervisors of executive branch operations.

The Seventh Circuit's mandamus order correctly restores the proper constitutional boundary.

## Implications

The Seventh Circuit's decision to grant mandamus and vacate Judge Ellis's monitoring order is a significant and correct application of the separation of powers doctrine. District court judges — trial-level Article III judicial officers — do not possess authority to supervise the daily operations of Article II executive officers. The Constitution's structural division of powers between the three branches exists precisely to prevent any single branch from accumulating the functions of another. When a district court judge attempts to convert her courtroom into a nightly briefing room for federal immigration enforcement operations, the mandamus power exists to restore constitutional order. This decision should be understood as an important precedent for the proposition that the judicial power does not encompass ongoing monitoring and supervision of executive branch law enforcement activities.
