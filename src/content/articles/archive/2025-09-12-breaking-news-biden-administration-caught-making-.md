## Summary

The Bureau of Labor Statistics, operating under the Biden administration, has acknowledged that it substantially overstated United States job creation by 911,000 positions during the period from April 2024 through the spring of 2025 — a period that encompassed the 2024 presidential election. This revelation raises profound questions about the integrity of federal statistical reporting and about whether politically appointed or influenced federal officials were manipulating economic data for electoral advantage. Simultaneously, related litigation concerning the removal of Federal Reserve Governor Lisa Cook implicates the foundational constitutional question of whether the President of the United States has plenary authority to remove executive branch officials appointed by his predecessor.

## Background

The Bureau of Labor Statistics is a component of the United States Department of Labor. It is charged with producing objective, accurate data on American employment, inflation, and economic productivity. The BLS's monthly employment reports are among the most consequential economic publications in the world: financial markets move on them, Federal Reserve policy responds to them, and political campaigns cite them as evidence of the governing party's economic stewardship or failure.

During the 2024 presidential election cycle, the Biden administration repeatedly invoked favorable employment figures as evidence of its economic success. The official BLS reports stated that the American economy had added hundreds of thousands of jobs per month, painting a picture of robust growth under President Biden and Vice President Harris.

The subsequent revision disclosed that the BLS had overstated employment by 911,000 positions during the covered period. Six digits. Nearly a million jobs that were reported to exist, and were cited in political and policy debates as evidence of economic strength, simply did not exist.

## The Significance of the Revision

The magnitude of this revision demands careful examination. A discrepancy of 911,000 jobs represents approximately 0.6 percent of total United States employment — a figure that is not trivial. More important than the absolute number is the timing: this overstatement was embedded in official government data throughout the most consequential political moment in a four-year cycle, namely a presidential election in which the incumbent administration was seeking to persuade voters of its economic competence.

The question that cannot be avoided is whether this overstatement was the product of institutional incompetence, methodological error, or deliberate manipulation. I am not in a position to determine which of these explanations is correct, and I make no legal allegation of fraud against any specific individual. What I can observe, as a lawyer and a citizen, is that the pattern — a substantial overstatement of positive economic data, during an election year, in favor of the incumbent administration, followed by a significant downward revision after the election — is precisely the pattern one would expect from a politically compromised statistical agency, whatever the cause.

## The Lisa Cook Connection and Removal Power

Running parallel to this story is the ongoing legal controversy surrounding the removal of Federal Reserve Governor Lisa Cook. President Trump terminated Cook from her position on the Federal Reserve Board of Governors. Cook, who was appointed by President Biden and had been a close associate of prominent Democratic political figures, was also the subject of serious allegations involving mortgage fraud. She had, according to those allegations, claimed multiple residences as her primary residence in order to obtain more favorable financing terms — conduct that raises obvious questions about the fitness of someone entrusted with setting interest rates that affect mortgage rates for every American homeowner.

President Trump's termination of Cook was immediately challenged in federal court. A district court in Washington issued a preliminary injunction ordering her reinstatement. The administration appealed.

The legal question presented is significant: may Congress, by statute, insulate a Federal Reserve Governor from removal by the President except for cause? The answer, under a correct reading of Article II of the Constitution, is no.

## The Constitutional Framework: Article II and Removal Power

Article II of the United States Constitution vests the executive power of the United States in the President. This is not a shared power, a delegated power, or a conditional power — it is an unconditional grant of executive authority to a single individual elected by the American people to exercise it. The President's power to appoint and remove principal officers of the executive branch is a core incident of that constitutional authority.

The Supreme Court's 1935 decision in **Humphrey's Executor v. United States** held that Congress could constitutionally restrict the President's removal of commissioners on so-called independent agencies, on the theory that such agencies performed quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial functions that were not purely executive in character. That decision has been widely criticized by constitutional scholars and has been progressively narrowed by the Supreme Court in subsequent decades.

In **Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau** (2020), the Supreme Court held that Congress could not constitutionally insulate the Director of the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau from at-will removal by the President. The Court's majority opinion, authored by Chief Justice John Roberts, reaffirmed that the President must have the ability to remove executive officers who exercise substantial executive power. In **Collins v. Yellen** (2021), the Court applied the same principle to the Director of the Federal Housing Finance Agency.

**Humphrey's Executor** survived those decisions only in a narrow and carefully circumscribed form. The current Supreme Court has made clear that it is skeptical of the continuing vitality of **Humphrey's Executor** as applied to powerful executive officers who exercise substantial governmental authority.

## The Federal Reserve and Presidential Control

The Federal Reserve occupies a unique and complex institutional position. Its governors are appointed by the President, confirmed by the Senate, and serve fixed terms. Federal statute purports to limit the President's ability to remove them except for cause. Whether that statutory protection is constitutional, after **Seila Law** and **Collins**, is a question of significant moment.

A Federal Reserve Governor participates directly in the most consequential monetary policy decisions in the American economy, including setting the federal funds rate, which in turn drives mortgage rates, consumer credit rates, and business borrowing costs. This is not a quasi-legislative or quasi-judicial function of the kind the **Humphrey's Executor** Court had in mind — it is the exercise of substantial executive-branch power over a domain of profound national economic significance.

The argument for presidential removal authority is correspondingly strong: if the President cannot remove a Federal Reserve Governor who is alleged to have committed mortgage fraud, who is actively hostile to the administration's economic policies, and whose decisions directly affect the financial conditions of every American homeowner, then the accountability mechanism that Article II envisions — a President who answers to the people for the performance of the executive branch — is fundamentally compromised.

## Implications

The BLS employment revision and the Lisa Cook controversy are, at bottom, manifestations of the same underlying constitutional and institutional problem: the existence of a large category of federal officials whose actions directly shape public life, who were appointed by one political administration, and who are insulated from accountability to a subsequently elected President and, through him, to the American people.

The Second Amendment community has a particular stake in the proper resolution of these separation-of-powers questions. The same constitutional principle that protects the President's ability to remove a rogue Federal Reserve Governor also protects his ability to direct the Department of Justice to take pro-Second Amendment positions, to decline to enforce unconstitutional firearms laws, and to appoint executive branch officials who will faithfully implement the Second Amendment agenda for which the American people voted. When unelected bureaucrats are insulated from presidential control, the result is not neutral governance — it is governance by a permanent class of officials who may be, and frequently are, hostile to the constitutional rights that the electorate has endorsed.

The rule of law demands accountability. Statistical agencies must report accurately. Executive branch officials must answer to the President who is accountable to the people. And constitutional rights, including the Second Amendment, must be enforced by an executive branch that is genuinely responsible to those who elected it.
