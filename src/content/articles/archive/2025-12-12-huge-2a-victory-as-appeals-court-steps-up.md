## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit has granted en banc review in two consolidated Second Amendment cases challenging New Jersey's comprehensive post-**Bruen** gun-free zone legislation — **Koons v. Platkin** and **Siegel v. Platkin**. En banc review means that all active judges on the Third Circuit will reconvene to reconsider the 2-to-1 decision issued by a three-judge panel in September 2025, which upheld the vast majority of New Jersey's sensitive-places restrictions over a powerful dissent. This grant of en banc review is, in my assessment, excellent news for the Second Amendment — both because the full court's composition offers a better chance of a pro-Second Amendment result, and because a well-reasoned en banc decision would carry substantially greater precedential weight than the panel decision it replaces.

*Analysis by Mark W. Smith, constitutional attorney and member of the United States Supreme Court Bar.*

---

## Background

In the wake of the Supreme Court's landmark decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen** (2022), New Jersey enacted one of the most expansive packages of "sensitive places" legislation in the country. The legislation designated an enormous range of locations as gun-free zones where licensed carry permit holders were prohibited from bringing their firearms, including parks, beaches, restaurants serving alcohol, entertainment venues, sports arenas, zoos, museums, shopping malls, hospitals, and virtually every form of public gathering space. The effect of the legislation was to create a landscape in which the exercise of the right to carry in public — a right that **Bruen** explicitly recognized and protected — became nearly impossible in practice.

## The Three-Judge Panel Decision

In September 2025, a three-judge panel of the Third Circuit issued a 2-to-1 decision in the consolidated **Koons** and **Siegel** cases that upheld the overwhelming majority of New Jersey's sensitive-places restrictions. The majority opinion was authored by two judges appointed by Democratic presidents. A powerful dissent was authored by one of the finest Second Amendment jurists on the Third Circuit, Judge Bibas.

The panel majority's decision was, in my view, incorrectly decided. It did not faithfully apply the **Bruen** text-and-history methodology. Instead, it engaged in the kind of reasoning that **Bruen** was specifically designed to foreclose — drawing broad historical analogies from loosely related historical sources, aggregating historical evidence at a level of generality that the Supreme Court has never endorsed, and ultimately upholding restrictions that have no genuine historical analog in the Founding or Reconstruction eras.

The three specific areas in which the panel found Second Amendment violations — the vampire rule (prohibiting carry on private property open to the public without express owner permission), the requirement that firearms be unloaded in vehicles, and a mandatory insurance requirement — are the right answers to the wrong questions. The far more expansive restrictions on carry in designated sensitive places were upheld, effectively restoring much of what New Jersey had lost under the district court's preliminary injunction.

## The Procedural Background: The Administrative Stay

To understand where we are now, it is necessary to understand what has already occurred procedurally. District Court Judge Renée Marie Bumb issued a preliminary injunction several years ago that enjoined virtually all of New Jersey's sensitive-places laws, finding that they were likely unconstitutional under **Bruen**. New Jersey immediately appealed and sought a stay — a pause — of Judge Bumb's injunction pending appeal. The same three-judge panel that later issued the September 2025 decision on the merits granted an administrative stay, with one partial exception: it agreed that the vampire rule was unconstitutional in all likelihood and declined to stay the injunction as to that particular restriction.

The practical effect was that for a period, New Jersey gun owners had the benefit of Judge Bumb's injunction with respect to most sensitive-places restrictions, then lost that protection when the administrative stay was entered, and now await the en banc court's reconsideration.

## Why En Banc Review Is Good News

The grant of en banc review is significant for several reasons.

First, the full Third Circuit's composition is different from a randomly selected three-judge panel. A panel can be unlucky in its composition — drawing judges who are skeptical of Second Amendment rights and willing to use broad historical analogies to uphold restrictions. The full court, with all active judges participating, presents a better distribution of judicial perspectives and is more likely to produce a decision that faithfully applies **Bruen**.

Second, an en banc decision carries substantially greater precedential weight than a panel decision. A panel decision can be distinguished or limited by subsequent panels; an en banc decision binds all future panels in the circuit. If the full Third Circuit issues a well-reasoned decision striking down New Jersey's sensitive-places restrictions, that decision will bind the entire circuit — covering New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware — for years.

Third, the en banc grant signals that a meaningful number of active Third Circuit judges believe the panel got it wrong. En banc review is not automatically granted; it requires a vote of the active judges. The fact that a majority voted to rehear the case suggests genuine concern about the panel's analysis.

## The Core Legal Questions

The central issue in **Koons** and **Siegel** is the extent to which government authorities may designate public spaces as "sensitive places" in which licensed carry is prohibited, consistent with the Second Amendment. **Bruen** acknowledged that some historically recognized sensitive places — government buildings, legislative chambers, courthouses, polling places, schools — may legitimately be designated as firearms-free. But the Court was explicit that the government cannot simply declare vast swaths of public life to be sensitive places without historical justification.

New Jersey's post-**Bruen** legislation attempts to do exactly what the Court cautioned against: use the sensitive-places doctrine as a mechanism to recreate, through designation, the same practical prohibition on public carry that the Court struck down in **Bruen** itself. If the Third Circuit applies **Bruen** faithfully, New Jersey's legislation cannot survive.

The question is whether the en banc court will be willing to apply **Bruen** faithfully — or whether it will, like the panel majority, reach for expansive historical analogies and levels of generality that the Supreme Court has not endorsed in order to uphold the restrictions.

## The Role of the Trump DOJ

The Trump Department of Justice has filed an amicus brief in the **Koons/Siegel** litigation arguing that New Jersey's sensitive-places restrictions are unconstitutional. The federal government's participation as amicus in a state court challenge — and its alignment with the Second Amendment plaintiffs — is a meaningful development. It reflects the DOJ's stated commitment to vindicating Second Amendment rights through litigation, including in cases it did not initiate.

## Implications

The en banc grant in **Koons** and **Siegel** represents one of the most consequential Second Amendment proceedings currently pending in the federal courts. A favorable en banc decision could substantially limit the ability of New Jersey — and, through precedent, all Third Circuit states — to use sensitive-places designation as a mechanism to eliminate public carry in practice.

The outcome will depend on the en banc court's willingness to apply **Bruen** as written and intended, rather than as anti-gun jurisdictions have argued it should be cabined and confined. Given the composition of the full Third Circuit and the strength of the arguments on the Second Amendment side, I am cautiously optimistic about the result.

For Second Amendment advocates, the message is clear: en banc review is not the end of the road, but it is an important and potentially decisive step. The full Third Circuit now has the opportunity to correct the panel's errors and issue a decision that properly protects the right of law-abiding citizens to carry firearms for self-defense in the public spaces where they live, work, and travel every day.
