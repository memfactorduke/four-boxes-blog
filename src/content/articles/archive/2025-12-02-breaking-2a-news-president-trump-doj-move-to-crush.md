## Summary

A careful examination of the legal positions being advanced by the Trump administration's Department of Justice in the **Barnett v. Raoul** case before the Seventh Circuit and in the **Cheeseman** litigation in the Third Circuit reveals a profound and underappreciated implication: if the Supreme Court accepts the constitutional framework the Trump DOJ is articulating in support of Second Amendment rights, state laws that prohibit or restrict collective firearms training — the so-called anti-militia training laws enacted by several blue states — would almost certainly be rendered unconstitutional. The Trump administration is relying on Supreme Court precedent that, taken to its logical conclusion, establishes that the right to keep and bear arms is not merely an individual right exercised in solitude but encompasses the right to collectively train with others for the purposes of becoming proficient with arms. This development has not received the attention it deserves.

## Background

The **Barnett v. Raoul** case pending in the Seventh Circuit Court of Appeals challenges the State of Illinois's comprehensive ban on so-called "assault weapons" — including AR-15 style semiautomatic rifles — as well as the state's ban on standard-capacity magazines and restrictions on suppressors. The Trump administration's Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi, has filed briefs in that case taking strong pro-Second Amendment positions grounded in the text-history-tradition methodology established by the Supreme Court in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022).

The **Cheeseman** litigation in the Third Circuit presents a similar set of challenges to New Jersey's analogous restrictions. In both cases, the Trump DOJ has come in as an advocate for the Second Amendment in a manner that previous administrations — including Republican ones — declined to do.

What has not been widely recognized is that the constitutional arguments the Trump DOJ is advancing in these cases, if accepted by the Supreme Court, would sweep considerably broader than just AR-15 bans and magazine restrictions. The underlying logic points directly at the constitutionality of state laws that restrict collective firearms training.

## The Individual Right and Its Collective Dimension

The right to keep and bear arms has been understood since **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008) as an individual right — one that each American possesses as a matter of fundamental constitutional entitlement without regard to membership in any organized militia. This principle was confirmed and extended to the states through **McDonald v. City of Chicago** (2010) and given methodological teeth through **Bruen**.

But the individual character of the right does not exhaust its scope. Just as other constitutional rights that are individually held may also be exercised collectively, so too may the right to keep and bear arms. Consider the most basic examples:

The First Amendment right to petition the government belongs to each individual citizen. But it also encompasses the right to petition the government collectively — to sign a joint letter, to organize a march, to pool resources for advocacy. The individual right is not diminished or nullified by exercising it alongside others. To the contrary, the right's value is in many cases only fully realized in its collective dimension.

The same logic applies to the Second Amendment. The individual has the right to keep and bear arms. That individual right necessarily encompasses the right to practice with those arms — to become proficient in their use. And the right to practice individually encompasses, by the same logic, the right to practice collectively — to train alongside others, to develop shared proficiency and coordination in the use of arms.

## The Anti-Militia Training Laws

Several states — including New York, California, and others — have enacted statutes that, in various forms, prohibit or restrict what might be called collective firearms training. These laws are often drafted broadly enough to encompass perfectly ordinary and benign activities: groups of gun owners going to the range together to practice, firearms training classes, shooting competitions, and other organized exercises that involve multiple people using firearms in a coordinated setting.

The nominal justification offered for these laws is the prevention of so-called "private militias" — the concern that groups of heavily armed individuals might organize for insurrectionary or criminal purposes. That concern is not entirely without foundation; there are legal limits on private paramilitary organizations, and I am not arguing that any and all organized collective action involving firearms is constitutionally protected.

However, the scope of existing anti-militia training laws frequently extends well beyond the prevention of genuinely threatening paramilitary activity. They sweep in ordinary firearms training and practice activities that have no plausible connection to any threat of violence or insurrection. And because they prohibit collective firearms training without reference to any threatening purpose, they functionally impede the exercise of the Second Amendment right in one of its most important dimensions: the development of proficiency in the lawful use of arms.

## The Trump DOJ's Constitutional Framework

The Trump administration's arguments in **Barnett** and **Cheeseman** rest on the proposition that the Second Amendment protects arms that are in common use for lawful purposes and that restrictions on such arms cannot be sustained without a historical tradition of comparable regulation. This framework is drawn directly from **Heller** and **Bruen**.

Applied to collective firearms training, the constitutional analysis runs as follows. First, the right to keep and bear arms necessarily includes the right to become and remain proficient in the use of those arms — a right that is largely hollow if it does not include the right to actually practice. Second, the right to practice necessarily includes the right to practice collectively, for the same reasons that other individual constitutional rights encompass their collective dimensions. Third, there is no founding-era tradition of prohibiting law-abiding citizens from collectively training with arms. To the contrary, the founding generation affirmatively valued collective firearms proficiency as an essential attribute of a free citizenry capable of defending itself and resisting tyranny.

If the Supreme Court accepts the constitutional framework the Trump DOJ is articulating, it will have established a doctrinal foundation that is difficult to confine to the narrow question of AR-15 bans. The same principles that doom an AR-15 ban — no historical tradition of prohibiting commonly used arms — equally doom a law prohibiting law-abiding citizens from training together with those same arms.

## Practical Implications for the Second Amendment Community

The implications of this developing legal framework for the Second Amendment community are significant and extend beyond the specific cases at issue:

**Firearms training organizations would gain constitutional protection.** If the right to collectively train with firearms is recognized as an aspect of the Second Amendment right, organizations that offer group firearms instruction, competitive shooting events, and coordinated training programs would have a strong constitutional basis to challenge state laws that attempt to restrict their activities.

**The Second Amendment right becomes more complete.** A constitutional right that protects the acquisition of arms but not the development of proficiency in their use is a stunted right. The recognition of a right to train — individually and collectively — gives the Second Amendment the practical content that makes it meaningful.

**Anti-gun states lose a regulatory tool.** Blue states that have enacted anti-militia training laws as a back-door mechanism for discouraging gun ownership and use would lose that tool if the constitutional framework the Trump DOJ is advancing is accepted. This is a significant development because these states have been creative in finding indirect ways to burden firearms ownership and use without directly banning guns.

**The AR-15 ban cases are the vehicle.** The most likely path for these principles to reach the Supreme Court is through the assault weapons ban cases, particularly **Barnett** in the Seventh Circuit. Second Amendment advocates should monitor the progress of that litigation closely, as the briefing and argument in that case will shape the constitutional framework that the Supreme Court ultimately adopts.

## Conclusion

The Trump administration's legal advocacy in the Second Amendment context represents a significant departure from the posture of prior administrations and reflects a genuine commitment to vindicating the right to keep and bear arms as a matter of constitutional law. The positions being advanced in **Barnett** and **Cheeseman** have implications that extend well beyond the specific restrictions at issue in those cases. If accepted by the Supreme Court, they would lay the constitutional groundwork for striking down state anti-militia training laws that have never been tested against the demanding standards of **Bruen** analysis. Second Amendment advocates who have focused exclusively on the narrow question of assault weapons bans should appreciate the broader constitutional architecture that is being built in these cases.
