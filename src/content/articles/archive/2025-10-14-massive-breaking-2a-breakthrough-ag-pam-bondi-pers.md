## Summary

A federal district court in Louisiana has vacated the portion of its own October 7, 2025 judgment in **Ree v. Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives** that required the Second Amendment Foundation, the Firearms Policy Coalition, and other plaintiff gun rights organizations to disclose their private membership lists to the federal government. Acting on a joint motion filed by both the Department of Justice and the organizational plaintiffs, United States District Judge Robert Summerhays entered an order vacating the membership disclosure provision, confirming that no Second Amendment organization is required to turn over its membership roster to the DOJ, to Attorney General Pam Bondi, or to any other federal officer. This outcome represents a complete vindication of the position I publicly articulated when the contested provision first came to light.

## Background

**Ree v. ATF** is a Second Amendment challenge to the federal statutory prohibition on federally licensed firearms dealers selling handguns to individuals between the ages of 18 and 20. The case arose in the Western District of Louisiana within the Fifth Circuit. The organizational plaintiffs — which include the Second Amendment Foundation and the Firearms Policy Coalition — prevailed on their motion for a preliminary injunction, obtaining a court order blocking enforcement of the age restriction within the applicable jurisdiction.

In the course of entering a final judgment in the case, Judge Summerhays, apparently in a good-faith effort to define the scope of relief, included a provision directing the plaintiff organizations to disclose their membership lists to the federal government. The apparent rationale was to identify which specific individuals would be covered by the injunction. This provision was not requested by the Department of Justice; indeed, the DOJ had specifically disclaimed any interest in obtaining membership lists and had not sought such disclosure at any point in the litigation.

When news of the membership list provision became public, it generated significant controversy within the Second Amendment community. Many commentators characterized it as an attempt by the government to create a de facto registry of gun owners or Second Amendment supporters. As I explained at the time, those characterizations were factually inaccurate. The DOJ under Attorney General Bondi had not sought this information, did not want it, and regarded compelled disclosure of private organizational membership as inconsistent with both DOJ policy and controlling constitutional precedent.

## The Joint Motion to Vacate

Following the public controversy, the Department of Justice and the plaintiff organizations acted in concert. On Friday, October 10, 2025, they filed a joint motion asking Judge Summerhays to alter or amend his judgment by vacating the membership disclosure provision. The DOJ's submission made two principal points: first, the Department had never requested membership information and had no interest in obtaining it; second, compelling disclosure of private membership lists would be inconsistent with the First Amendment's protection of freedom of association.

The constitutional basis for the DOJ's position draws directly from the Supreme Court's landmark civil rights era decision in **NAACP v. Alabama** (1958), in which the Court held that compelled disclosure of the NAACP's membership list by state authorities violated the First Amendment's guarantee of freedom of association. The Court recognized that the forced revelation of membership in advocacy organizations could expose members to harassment and intimidation and thereby chill the exercise of associational rights. That precedent, established to protect a civil rights organization, applies with equal force to protect Second Amendment organizations today.

## The Court's Ruling

Judge Summerhays acted promptly on the joint motion. On October 14, 2025, he entered an order stating: "Upon consideration of the joint motion to alter or amend the judgment, it is hereby ordered that the judgment of this court issued on October 7, 2025 is vacated." The court further indicated it would schedule a telephone conference to set a hearing and briefing schedule on a revised proposed judgment — the process that should have occurred before the original membership disclosure provision was entered, had the court flagged its intention to the parties in advance.

The outcome is unambiguous. No membership list belonging to any Second Amendment organization is required to be turned over to the federal government. None will be provided to Attorney General Bondi, to Assistant Attorney General Harlan Dillon, or to any other officer of the Department of Justice. The membership records of the Second Amendment Foundation, the Firearms Policy Coalition, and every other gun rights organization that was a party to this litigation remain private.

## The Broader Injunction

Separate from the membership list issue, the substantive injunctive relief obtained by the plaintiff organizations in **Ree v. ATF** remains in place. The district court's injunction blocking the enforcement of the under-21 handgun sales prohibition within the Fifth Circuit has been preserved, and the DOJ worked cooperatively with the plaintiffs to secure the broadest possible scope of relief consistent with the Supreme Court's recent decision in **Trump v. CASA**, which addressed limitations on nationwide injunctions. The result is an injunction that applies throughout the Fifth Circuit and to the members of the plaintiff organizations — a significant Second Amendment victory standing independently of the membership list controversy.

## Implications

This episode carries several important lessons for the Second Amendment community. First, the reflexive online criticism of Attorney General Bondi that erupted when the membership list provision became public was based on a misunderstanding of what actually occurred. The DOJ did not seek, request, or want this information. The membership disclosure provision was a sua sponte action by the district court — not a government initiative. The DOJ's rapid joint motion to vacate the provision confirmed this reality.

Second, the episode illustrates the enduring constitutional significance of **NAACP v. Alabama**. The First Amendment's protection of associational privacy, established to shield civil rights organizations from hostile state governments in the 1950s, operates today as a robust barrier against compelled disclosure of the membership of any advocacy organization, including those devoted to the Second Amendment. The civil rights victories of one era provide constitutional shelter for rights advocates of subsequent eras.

Third, and most importantly, the underlying Second Amendment victory in **Ree v. ATF** — invalidating the federal prohibition on handgun sales to adults between the ages of 18 and 20 — represents a meaningful expansion of Second Amendment rights for young adults across the Fifth Circuit. That relief stands on solid ground and reflects the correct application of **Heller** and **Bruen** to the question of whether law-abiding adults who are old enough to vote, serve in the military, and bear all other responsibilities of citizenship are entitled to the full protection of the Second Amendment.
