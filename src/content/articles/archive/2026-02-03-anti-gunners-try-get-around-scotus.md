## Summary

Hawaii, anticipating a significant defeat at the United States Supreme Court in **Wolford v. Lopez**, has proposed Senate Bill 3041 — a measure that would compel every business open to the public to post a color-coded placard declaring its policy on firearms. In my analysis, this legislation is not merely a creative regulatory pivot; it is a compelled-speech mandate that raises serious First Amendment problems entirely separate from the Second Amendment litigation it is designed to mitigate. The proposal illustrates a recurring pattern among anti-gun legislatures: when courts foreclose one avenue of restriction, they immediately seek another.

## Background: The Vampire Rule and Wolford v. Lopez

At issue in **Wolford v. Lopez** is Hawaii's so-called "vampire rule" — a provision that prohibits concealed-carry permit holders from bringing a firearm onto private property open to the public unless they first obtain express permission from the property owner or manager. The moniker derives from vampire mythology: just as the creature of legend cannot enter a home without an invitation, Hawaii's licensee cannot enter a gas station, grocery store, or laundromat with a firearm absent affirmative consent from someone the license holder may have no realistic means of identifying.

The challenge was brought by attorney Alan Beck and his clients, with the Trump administration's Department of Justice appearing alongside him as amicus before the Supreme Court. The solicitor general's participation signals the administration's view that the vampire rule cannot survive scrutiny under **New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). The Ninth Circuit had previously upheld Hawaii's scheme, making it an outlier relative to the emerging consensus that broad default-deny carriage restrictions are constitutionally untenable.

Oral argument has concluded, and in my assessment the trajectory of the Court's questioning strongly suggests Hawaii will lose — by a margin of at least five justices, possibly more. Hawaii appears to have reached the same conclusion.

## The Legislative Response: SB 3041

Rather than await the inevitable and then comply, Hawaii's legislature has proposed SB 3041. Under the bill, all businesses and restaurants open to the public would be required to display a color-coded placard indicating whether they permit firearms — and separately, large knives — on the premises. The stated rationale tracks the argument Hawaii advanced at the Supreme Court: that the state is merely protecting property owners' right to choose.

In my analysis, that framing collapses immediately upon contact with Hawaii's own existing law. The state has designated approximately twenty-five categories of "sensitive places" — government-mandated gun-free zones applied to properties regardless of the owner's preference. A bar owner who would welcome armed patrons has no choice in the matter under Hawaii law. The state's claim to be vindicating property-owner autonomy is therefore difficult to accept in good faith when it simultaneously overrides that autonomy wherever the legislature has decided guns should be absent.

## First Amendment Compelled-Speech Problems

The more fundamental objection to SB 3041 is constitutional, and it arises under the First Amendment rather than the Second. The government generally may not compel private parties to express a position on a contested public policy question. The Supreme Court's compelled-speech doctrine, rooted in cases like **Wooley v. Maynard** and **Hurley v. Irish-American Gay Group of Boston**, distinguishes between permissible requirements to disclose purely factual commercial information — caloric content on a menu, for instance — and impermissible mandates to adopt or publicize a viewpoint.

Requiring every business in Hawaii to publicly declare whether it welcomes or excludes firearms falls squarely on the impermissible side of that line. The question of whether private establishments should permit firearms is among the most politically charged debates in American public life. Compelling a hot-dog vendor or a laundry operator to take and publish a position on that debate — on pain of legal sanction — raises the same concerns the Court identified in **NIFLA v. Becerra** and its predecessor decisions: that the government cannot conscript private speakers as instruments of its preferred message.

There is also a long-standing American tradition, reaching back to the founding era, of protecting anonymous political speech. The Federalist Papers were published pseudonymously. Pre-constitutional pamphleteers used noms de plume precisely because compelled attribution of political views invites retaliation. SB 3041 would force business owners into a public political stance on a highly divisive issue, under their own names and storefronts, whether or not they have ever formed a view on the subject. That inverts the tradition of anonymous speech that the First Amendment has long sheltered.

## Practical Consequences for Property Owners

Beyond the constitutional dimension, I have reviewed the practical implications of SB 3041 for business owners, and they are considerable. A business that posts a "no firearms" sign is, in effect, publicly declaring itself a gun-free zone — which is to say, publicly advertising to criminals that its patrons are unarmed. The deterrent value of concealed carry derives substantially from uncertainty: a would-be robber who cannot know whether any given patron is armed faces a meaningfully different calculus than one who has been affirmatively told the premises are firearm-free. SB 3041 eliminates that uncertainty and directs criminal opportunity toward disclosed gun-free establishments.

Conversely, a business that posts a "firearms permitted" sign risks alienating customers who hold contrary views. Either declaration forces business owners into an unwanted political identity that has no relationship to their commercial purpose. The measure would also likely prompt new questions in commercial liability underwriting, as insurers begin factoring disclosed firearms policies into risk assessments.

## New York's Playbook, Reprised

I have observed this legislative pattern before. Following **Bruen**, New York convened an emergency session and enacted the Concealed Carry Improvement Act — a sweeping expansion of sensitive places and onerous licensing conditions designed to render **Bruen** a dead letter. That statute has been substantially dismantled by subsequent litigation. Hawaii's SB 3041 appears to be a variation on that strategy: absorb the Supreme Court loss and immediately pivot to a parallel scheme that achieves functionally similar results through different means.

## Implications

If SB 3041 is enacted, it will almost certainly face immediate First Amendment challenge. The compelled-speech doctrine provides a distinct and potentially powerful basis for invalidation that does not require relitigating **Wolford** or **Bruen**. Courts applying strict or even intermediate scrutiny to government compulsion of speech on a contested political question have rarely upheld such measures.

More broadly, the proposal illustrates why Second Amendment advocates cannot treat any Supreme Court victory as self-executing. Anti-gun legislatures will continue to seek workarounds, and those workarounds will come in forms — First Amendment violations, consumer-safety pretexts, insurance mandates — that may not be immediately recognizable as Second Amendment problems. Vigilance across constitutional doctrines remains essential.

The history of the future is yet to be written, as I often note. But the pattern of evasion following defeat at the Supreme Court is now well established, and Hawaii's response to **Wolford** confirms it.
