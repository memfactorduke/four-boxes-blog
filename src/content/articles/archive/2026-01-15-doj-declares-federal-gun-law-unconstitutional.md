## Summary

In what may be the first instance in American history of the United States Department of Justice declaring a federal gun control statute unconstitutional, the DOJ's Office of Legal Counsel (OLC) has issued a formal memorandum concluding that 18 U.S.C. § 1715 — the federal law prohibiting the mailing of concealable handguns through the United States Postal Service — violates the Second Amendment and cannot constitutionally be enforced against law-abiding citizens. The memorandum directs that the executive branch may not enforce the statute as applied to constitutionally protected firearms, and instructs the Postal Service to amend its regulations accordingly.

## Background

### The Statute: 18 U.S.C. § 1715

Enacted in 1927, 18 U.S.C. § 1715 prohibits the mailing through the United States Postal Service of pistols, revolvers, and other firearms capable of being concealed on the person — in short, handguns. The statute has been on the books for nearly a century and has never, until now, been formally challenged from within the executive branch on Second Amendment grounds.

The law predates the modern Second Amendment jurisprudential framework established by **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), **McDonald v. City of Chicago**, 561 U.S. 742 (2010), and most critically **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). Because the statute was enacted in 1927 — nearly a century after the founding era — it enjoys no presumption of constitutionality grounded in founding-era history and tradition.

### The Role of the Office of Legal Counsel

The Office of Legal Counsel is the DOJ component charged with providing authoritative legal advice to the President of the United States and the Attorney General on questions of constitutional and statutory law. OLC opinions carry significant weight within the executive branch; they are, in effect, the internal law of the executive. When the OLC concludes that a statute is unconstitutional, the executive branch is guided — indeed, arguably compelled — not to enforce it.

Under Attorney General Pam Bondi and with the support of the Trump administration, the OLC has now issued such a memorandum with respect to § 1715.

## The OLC Memorandum: Key Conclusions

### The Core Holding

The memorandum reaches a sweeping conclusion, expressly stated as follows: Section 1715 "regulates the ability to transport, receive, and maintain constitutionally protected firearms, which burdens the right protected by the Second Amendment." The OLC found, further, that § 1715's "purpose and burden find no analog in this nation's history and tradition of firearms regulation," citing **Bruen** and the text-history-tradition test the Supreme Court established therein.

The ultimate conclusion is unambiguous: "We therefore conclude that the statute violates the Second Amendment insofar as it burdens the rights of law-abiding citizens to ship and receive arms in common use for lawful purposes." Because the executive branch cannot enforce an unconstitutional statute consistent with its constitutional obligations, the memorandum directs that § 1715 shall not be prosecuted against law-abiding citizens exercising Second Amendment-protected rights, and that the Postal Service must modify its regulations to conform.

### The Right to Transport Arms for Self-Defense

A particularly significant passage in the memorandum addresses the relationship between § 1715's prohibition and the core Second Amendment right of self-defense. The OLC explains: "Section 1715 substantially burdens the right to bear arms protected by the Second Amendment. An individual cannot mail himself a handgun for core constitutionally protected activity such as self-defense, target shooting, or hunting."

The memorandum further observes that traveling with a firearm can be "difficult, if not impossible," rendering mail "the most effective way to transport an individual's firearm to his destination." This passage is analytically important: it links the mailing prohibition directly to the exercise of the right to keep and bear arms in a practical, real-world sense — exactly the kind of functional burden that **Bruen**'s text-history-tradition framework requires courts and the executive to consider.

### No Historical Analog Under the Bruen Framework

Under **Bruen**, for a gun regulation to survive Second Amendment scrutiny, the government must affirmatively demonstrate that the regulation is "consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation." The OLC concluded that § 1715, enacted in 1927, fails this test entirely. No founding-era analog exists for a federal prohibition on the postal transport of handguns — unsurprisingly, given that the federal postal service did not remotely regulate the movement of commonly owned firearms in any manner that approximates § 1715's sweeping prohibition.

## Constitutional Significance

### An Unprecedented Executive Action

In my assessment, what the OLC has done here is unprecedented in the modern era of Second Amendment jurisprudence. It is, to my knowledge, the first time in American history that the Department of Justice has formally declared a specific federal gun control statute unconstitutional and directed that it not be enforced. Prior administrations — including those purportedly sympathetic to the Second Amendment — targeted state gun laws through litigation but did not turn the constitutional searchlight on federal statutes.

This memorandum signals a qualitative shift in the Trump-Bondi DOJ's approach. By acknowledging that federal law, not merely state law, can infringe the Second Amendment, the OLC opens a new front in Second Amendment enforcement.

### The Bruen Ripple Effect

The memorandum demonstrates with clarity the sweeping reach of **Bruen**'s text-history-tradition standard. Where courts once applied balancing tests and means-ends scrutiny to firearms regulations — standards that frequently permitted legislatures to regulate away core rights — **Bruen** commands a purely historical inquiry. Under that inquiry, a law passed in 1927 with no founding-era analog cannot survive.

I submit that this memorandum is what I have long described as a "brick by brick" construction of Second Amendment rights: each executive action, each favorable court ruling, each administrative determination that a given regulation lacks historical grounding adds another layer of protection. The OLC's § 1715 memorandum is a significant brick.

### The Postal Service's Regulatory Obligations

The memorandum's direction to the United States Postal Service to amend its regulations is itself notable. The USPS has long maintained regulations prohibiting the mailing of handguns, implementing § 1715. Those regulations must now be revised to reflect the constitutional scope of the Second Amendment as the OLC has described it. This is executive-branch constitutional enforcement in real time.

## Implications

### Immediate Effect on Prosecutions

The most immediate practical effect of this memorandum is prosecutorial. Federal prosecutors, guided by the OLC's conclusion, may not bring § 1715 charges against law-abiding citizens who seek to mail handguns for constitutionally protected purposes — self-defense, hunting, target shooting, and related activities. While the statute remains on the books as a matter of positive law, the executive branch's refusal to enforce it renders it effectively inoperative during this administration.

### Broader Precedent for Federal Statutory Review

The memorandum also sets a significant precedent: the executive branch can and will review federal gun statutes for constitutional compliance under **Bruen**. This raises important questions about other federal firearms restrictions — including provisions of the Gun Control Act of 1968 — that may similarly lack founding-era analogs. I submit that this memorandum invites, if not demands, a systematic review of the federal statutory framework for firearms regulation through the lens of the **Bruen** standard.

### Interaction with the Courts

While an OLC memorandum does not bind the federal judiciary, it is not without judicial relevance. Courts have at times deferred to executive branch constitutional interpretations, and the OLC's reasoning in this memorandum may prove persuasive when § 1715 is eventually challenged in litigation. The Trump-Bondi DOJ's position, now formally stated, could also shape the government's posture in any future § 1715-related case — including civil challenges to the statute's continued existence.

### The Significance of the Timing

This development arrives at a moment when the Second Amendment landscape is rapidly shifting. The federal courts, emboldened by **Bruen** and **United States v. Rahimi**, 602 U.S. ___ (2024), are striking down gun regulations at an accelerating pace. The OLC's memorandum is therefore not an isolated act but part of a broader constitutional realignment — one in which the Second Amendment is increasingly treated as the co-equal of other enumerated rights.

In my view, this memorandum represents the executive branch finally arriving at the same conclusion that the Supreme Court reached in **Bruen**: that gun laws must be measured against historical tradition, and that laws lacking such tradition cannot stand, regardless of their age, their apparent practical justifications, or their prior acceptance by enforcement agencies. The right of the people to keep and bear arms shall not be infringed — and the executive branch has now stated, formally and clearly, that it takes that command seriously.
