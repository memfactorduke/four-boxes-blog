## Background: The Benson Decision

In my analysis of a significant development, a three-judge panel of the D.C. Court of Appeals handed down a major Second Amendment ruling in *Benson v. United States* in late February 2025. The court held, unequivocally, that the District of Columbia's ban on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds violates the Second Amendment.

As I've examined it, the decision rested on the analytical framework established by the Supreme Court in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen* (2022). Under *Bruen*, the threshold question is whether the regulated item falls within the Second Amendment's plain text. The Supreme Court defined "arms" for purposes of the Second Amendment as any instrument that facilitates armed self-defense. Magazines plainly qualify: they are physical instruments essential to the effective operation of a firearm and thus to armed self-defense.

Having concluded that magazines are textually protected arms, the court turned to the historical analysis required by *Bruen* and *District of Columbia v. Heller* (2008). Under *Heller*, the only category of arms subject to prohibition is those that are both "dangerous and unusual." In my view, arms in common use by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes are, by definition, not unusual -- and therefore cannot satisfy the "dangerous and unusual" standard. With tens of millions of magazines holding more than ten rounds in civilian hands across the country, the court found no basis for treating them as anything other than commonly owned, constitutionally protected arms.

The case arose from Tyrie Benson's conviction for possessing a magazine holding more than ten rounds in violation of D.C. law -- a malum prohibitum offense. The Trump administration's Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi and U.S. Attorney for the District of Columbia Jeanine Pirro, had filed a brief in September 2025 arguing that the magazine ban was unconstitutional, a position the D.C. Court of Appeals ultimately adopted.

## The District's Emergency Motion

Following the decision, the District of Columbia moved swiftly to limit its impact. The District filed an emergency motion asking the D.C. Court of Appeals to suspend the precedential status of the *Benson* opinion while the District prepared a petition for rehearing en banc. The District argued that the ruling was creating "real chaos on the ground" and could insulate criminals from liability simply by attaching a high-capacity magazine to an otherwise unregistered firearm.

The request was extraordinary by any measure. Under the D.C. Court of Appeals' established rules of appellate procedure, a panel decision retains its precedential force unless and until it is vacated following a successful petition for rehearing en banc. The District sought to reverse that default -- stripping the opinion of its binding authority before the full court had even considered whether to grant rehearing.

## Benson's Opposition: No Legal Authority, No Extraordinary Circumstances

As I've analyzed it, the opposition filed by Benson's attorney mounted a methodical attack on the District's position, challenging it on both procedural and substantive grounds.

### No Statutory or Precedential Support

At the outset, Benson's counsel highlighted a glaring deficiency in the District's filing: the motion cited no statute, rule, or judicial decision authorizing the relief it sought. As the opposition stated:

> "This novel request finds no support in any statute, rule, or judicial decision, and the District of Columbia cites none."

The absence of any legal authority for the District's proposed remedy was, in my view, disqualifying on its own terms. The District was asking the court to create "an entirely new remedy out of whole cloth" -- without established rules, procedures, or legal standards to guide the decision, and without the public notice and comment that ordinarily precedes changes to the court's procedural rules.

### No Extraordinary Circumstances

Beyond the procedural deficiency, Benson's attorney argued that the District had failed to identify any extraordinary circumstances warranting departure from normal appellate procedure. In my analysis, the opposition characterized the District's filing as a repackaging of the same substantive arguments the panel had already considered and rejected:

> "At best, the District of Columbia's stated reasons for suspending the precedential effect of the opinion are mere arguments for rehearing the case en banc."

Even assuming those arguments had merit -- which the opposition expressly disputed -- they presented no special justification for bypassing the court's ordinary process for reviewing panel decisions through en banc petitions.

### The "Chaos" Argument Dismantled

The opposition reserved its sharpest language for the District's claim that the ruling was generating real-world chaos. As I see it, Benson's counsel pointed out that the District had offered no evidence to support this assertion. More pointedly, the opposition observed that any potential disruption could be resolved through a straightforward mechanism: the District's own good-faith compliance with the court's decision, rather than an extraordinary procedural maneuver to suspend it.

The opposition noted that the U.S. Attorney's Office for the District of Columbia, under Jeanine Pirro, had already ceased enforcing the magazine ban since at least September 2025 -- months before the court's ruling. If the non-enforcement of the statute were truly a recipe for chaos, that chaos should have materialized long ago. It had not.

## Broader Implications: The Split of Authority

In my analysis, the *Benson* decision carries significance well beyond the District of Columbia. The D.C. Court of Appeals functions as the equivalent of a state supreme court, making its holdings authoritative within its jurisdiction and persuasive beyond it. By holding that magazines are constitutionally protected arms under the Second Amendment, the court created a split of authority with multiple federal circuits that have upheld magazine capacity restrictions.

That split has already generated activity at the Supreme Court level. Supplemental briefs have been filed in both *Gator Custom Guns v. Bonta* and *Duncan v. Bonta*, alerting the Justices to the newly created conflict among the nation's courts on the magazine question. In my view, the split substantially increases the likelihood that the Supreme Court will grant certiorari to resolve the issue -- potentially extending Second Amendment protections against magazine bans nationwide.

## The Scope of Second Amendment Rights

The *Benson* litigation also implicates a foundational principle of Second Amendment jurisprudence: the Amendment codified a pre-existing right rather than creating a new one. This distinction is legally significant because it requires courts to look to the historical scope of the right to keep and bear arms as it existed at the time of ratification in 1791. Under the *Bruen* framework, a modern firearms regulation is constitutional only if it is "consistent with this Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation."

This historical inquiry defines both the affirmative content of the right and its outer boundaries. The government bears the burden of demonstrating a historical analogue for any challenged restriction. In *Benson*, the D.C. Court of Appeals found no historical tradition supporting the prohibition of commonly owned magazines -- a conclusion the District's emergency motion did nothing to undermine.

## Looking Ahead

As I see it, the D.C. Court of Appeals must now decide whether to grant the District's forthcoming petition for rehearing en banc. En banc review in this court is exceedingly rare, and the Department of Justice is expected to oppose it. If rehearing is denied, the District will face a difficult strategic choice: accept the precedent or seek Supreme Court review, with the attendant risk that the Court could issue a ruling invalidating magazine bans across the country.

For the time being, the *Benson* decision remains binding precedent in the District of Columbia and a powerful signal that the constitutional tide on magazine restrictions may be turning, in my view.
