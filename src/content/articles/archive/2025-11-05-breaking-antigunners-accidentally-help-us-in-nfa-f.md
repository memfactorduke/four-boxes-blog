## Summary

The *New York Times* recently published an investigative piece purporting to expose the physical dangers posed to civilian shooters at indoor firing ranges — but in doing so, inadvertently produced what I expect will become a powerful piece of evidentiary support for Second Amendment advocates seeking to remove suppressors from the burdens of the National Firearms Act. The article's own findings confirm that suppressors dramatically reduce blast pressure exposure, undermining anti-gun narratives and handing the firearms community a significant litigation and legislative asset.

## Background

The *New York Times* conducted and published what it described as an investigation into the physical hazards of shooting firearms at enclosed indoor ranges. The article focused specifically on the concussive blast pressure generated by various calibers of ammunition when fired indoors — the pounds per square inch (PSI) of force transmitted to the shooter's body, particularly to the chest and head.

The investigation tested numerous firearms and calibers, ranging from revolvers to AR-15-style rifles to, remarkably, a .50-caliber rifle. The PSI readings were evaluated against what the article described as a safety threshold of 4.0 PSI — the level above which sustained shooting exposure was argued to create a meaningful risk of traumatic brain injury or other physical harm.

## The .50-Caliber Anomaly

The most striking — and, in my view, most intellectually problematic — aspect of the *Times* investigation was its inclusion of a .50-caliber rifle in the test battery. I am not aware of any conventional civilian indoor shooting range that permits the discharge of a .50-caliber rifle inside an enclosed bay. The round itself generates enormous blast, recoil, and pressure that virtually no standard indoor range structure is designed to accommodate. Yet the *Times* included this outlier in its dataset.

Predictably, the .50-caliber rifle was the sole firearm that exceeded the 4.0 PSI threshold. The AR-15-style rifles, revolvers, .357 Magnum, and other calibers commonly used at civilian indoor ranges all remained below that threshold. By including the .50-caliber result in the same analysis as standard sporting calibers, the article created a misleading impression of widespread danger at ordinary shooting ranges.

## The *Times* Discovers the Benefits of Suppressors

The anti-gun intent of the article is not subtle. The obvious objective was to raise alarm sufficient to discourage participation in the shooting sports — particularly among parents whose children might otherwise develop interest in competitive shooting or marksmanship. That political objective is the *Times'* prerogative.

However, buried at the very bottom of the article — as if hoping readers would not reach it — the *Times* disclosed its own findings about risk mitigation. Among the measures identified to reduce blast pressure exposure, the article specifically stated:

> "Attaching a suppressor or blast regulator to the muzzle to direct the blast forward and away from the shooter can also make a big difference."

The article then provided quantitative support for this conclusion. In the *Times'* own testing, firing an AR-15 indoors produced blast readings as high as 1.7 PSI. When a blast regulator was attached to the muzzle, that measurement fell to less than 0.05 PSI — a reduction of more than ninety-six percent.

The article further noted that suppressors and blast regulators are used routinely by law enforcement and the military, acknowledged that they are expensive, and observed that they are "tightly regulated in some states and illegal in others."

## Legal and Legislative Implications

This is where the *Times* inadvertently handed the Second Amendment community an extraordinary gift.

The legal and political fight to remove suppressors from the coverage of the National Firearms Act has been ongoing for years. Second Amendment advocates, legislators, and organizations like Gun Owners of America and the Silencer Shop Foundation have argued — correctly, in my view — that suppressors are fundamentally safety devices. They protect hearing. They reduce noise pollution for communities near ranges. And, as the *Times* has now confirmed with its own data, they dramatically reduce the blast pressure transmitted to the shooter's body.

I predict that the *Times'* own reporting will find its way into legal briefs, Congressional testimony, and regulatory comments in support of suppressor deregulation. When a publication with the institutional credibility and anti-gun editorial posture of the *New York Times* publishes empirical data confirming that suppressors reduce shooter blast exposure by more than ninety-six percent, that data carries enormous persuasive weight in legislative and judicial proceedings.

The article's findings are directly relevant to pending and future litigation challenging the constitutionality of NFA suppressor regulations, as well as to Congressional debates over proposals to remove suppressors from NFA coverage entirely. The argument that suppressors are dangerous or serve no legitimate purpose becomes extraordinarily difficult to sustain when the *Times* itself acknowledges their protective value.

## The Broader Strategic Picture

What the *Times* intended as a discouragement campaign has produced the opposite effect. By conducting and publishing rigorous empirical testing that confirms suppressors reduce blast pressure by extraordinary margins, the *Times* has provided the Second Amendment movement with exactly the kind of mainstream, credible evidentiary support that is most useful in legal and legislative proceedings.

The lesson here is an old one: when your adversary is making a mistake, the prudent course is not to interrupt them.

## Implications

The *New York Times* investigation into indoor shooting range blast pressure represents an unintended but significant contribution to the case for suppressor deregulation. Its empirical findings — that suppressors reduce muzzle blast PSI from 1.7 to less than 0.05 on an AR-15 fired indoors — will serve as potent evidentiary support in NFA-related litigation and Congressional proceedings. The article's own data confirms what suppressor advocates have argued for years: these devices are safety equipment, not weapons. Second Amendment attorneys, organizations, and advocates should take note of these findings and ensure they are preserved and deployed appropriately in future proceedings.
