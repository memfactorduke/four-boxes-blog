## Summary

In *Ziegenfuss v. Martin*, No. 4:24-cv-01049 (N.D. Tex. 2025), U.S. District Judge Mark T. Pittman upheld three Texas statutes that prohibit carrying firearms at racetracks, at businesses deriving 51 percent or more of their income from on-premises alcohol sales, and at venues hosting high school, collegiate, professional, or interscholastic sporting events. The court granted the State's motion for summary judgment, finding that the challenged provisions of Texas Penal Code sections 46.03(a)(4), (a)(7), and (a)(8) are consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. I contend the decision is wrongly decided on multiple grounds and have identified several analytical errors that the Fifth Circuit should correct on appeal.

## Background

The plaintiffs -- Charles Ziegenfuss, David Montgomery, Brian Robinson, and the Firearms Policy Coalition -- brought a facial challenge under the Second and Fourteenth Amendments to three location-based carry prohibitions under Texas law. The challenged statutes make it a criminal offense to intentionally, knowingly, or recklessly possess or carry a firearm, location-restricted knife, club, or prohibited weapon on the premises of: (1) a racetrack; (2) a business that holds a permit or license and derives 51 percent or more of its income from the sale or service of alcoholic beverages for on-premises consumption; and (3) a venue where a high school, collegiate, professional, or interscholastic sporting event is taking place. These provisions have been part of Texas law in various forms since 1973.

The case was assigned to Judge Mark T. Pittman of the Northern District of Texas, a Trump appointee. The court applied the analytical framework established by the Supreme Court in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association, Inc. v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), which requires the government to demonstrate that a modern firearms regulation is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation by identifying relevantly similar historical analogues.

## The Court's Reasoning

Judge Pittman acknowledged that the conduct at issue -- carrying firearms in public for self-defense -- falls within the plain text of the Second Amendment. That threshold finding shifted the burden to the State to justify the restrictions under *Bruen*'s historical-tradition test. The court then concluded that the State had carried its burden, finding historical support for each of the three location-based restrictions.

On the temporal question, Judge Pittman stated that 1791 -- the year the Second Amendment was ratified -- is the primary reference point for the historical analysis, while also holding that Reconstruction-era laws from around 1868 may play a supplementary role. To support this broader temporal window, the court invoked the doctrine of liquidation, quoting James Madison's observation that "a regular course of a practice can liquidate and settle the meaning of disputed indeterminate terms and phrases."

For the alcohol-related restriction, the court relied in part on a 1656 Virginia statute prohibiting firearms at "drinking parties," then looked to nineteenth-century state and territorial laws as confirmatory evidence. For the sporting-events provision, Judge Pittman reasoned that schools have historically been treated as sensitive places, while acknowledging the historical record shows that only students -- not non-students -- were barred from carrying weapons on school grounds during the founding era. The court also referenced the Statute of Northampton (1328) as background support for its conclusions.

## Analytical Errors Identified

I've identified several significant errors in the opinion that I argue should lead to reversal on appeal.

**Misapplication of the temporal framework.** While Judge Pittman correctly identified 1791 as the critical reference point, he failed to apply that principle consistently. The opinion relies heavily on late-nineteenth-century laws without first establishing a foundation in founding-era regulation. Under the *Bruen* framework, Reconstruction-era laws may serve as "confirmatory analogues" -- evidence that confirms a tradition already established at the founding. They cannot, however, independently establish a historical tradition where none existed in 1791. In my analysis, Judge Pittman effectively used nineteenth-century laws as freestanding justifications rather than as confirmation of an existing founding-era practice, because no such founding-era practice existed with respect to these particular location-based restrictions.

**Misuse of the doctrine of liquidation.** The opinion's invocation of Madisonian liquidation is, in my view, a fundamental category error. The doctrine of liquidation, as Madison articulated it, applies to provisions of the Constitution that were genuinely novel at the time of ratification -- structural provisions such as the scope of the Commerce Clause, the jurisdiction of the federal courts, or the rules governing the Senate. These were inventions of the 1787 Convention that had no historical antecedent, and their meaning was understood to crystallize through early governmental practice over the first several decades of the Republic.

The Second Amendment, by contrast, codified a pre-existing right with deep roots in English common law and the tradition of the rights of Englishmen. Because the right to keep and bear arms was not a new creation of the Constitution but rather a recognition of a pre-existing natural right, the doctrine of liquidation has no proper application. The meaning of the Second Amendment is determined by looking backward to what the right was understood to encompass at the time of ratification, not forward to how subsequent practice may have shaped or narrowed it. I warn that permitting liquidation analysis in Second Amendment cases creates a mechanism by which post-ratification restrictions could be used to contract the scope of the right -- an outcome antithetical to the Amendment's purpose.

**Failure to define "sensitive places."** The opinion does not articulate a principled definition of what makes a location a "sensitive place" under *Bruen*. The Supreme Court identified three categories of sensitive places: courthouses, legislative chambers, and polling places as they existed at the time of the founding. In my analysis, the common denominator among these three locations was comprehensive government-provided security. Each featured bailiffs, sheriffs, sheriff's deputies, sergeants-at-arms, or other armed government personnel who both protected the proceedings and controlled access. This is the "government security principle" -- the argument, supported by historical research including Dr. Angus McClellan's recent scholarship, that the defining characteristic of a sensitive place is not the nature of the activity conducted there but the presence of armed governmental security that renders individual self-defense unnecessary.

Under this framework, a location qualifies as a sensitive place only when the government provides armed security, metal detectors, and limited points of entry -- the kind of comprehensive screening seen at federal courthouses and TSA checkpoints. Bars, racetracks, and sporting venues in Texas lack any such security apparatus. Without it, I contend, these locations cannot constitutionally be designated as sensitive places, and the individual right to bear arms for self-defense remains intact.

**Insufficient founding-era evidence.** For the alcohol-related restriction, the court relied on a single 1656 Virginia statute. I've noted that the Supreme Court held in *Bruen* that even three founding-era laws were insufficient to establish a historical tradition -- characterizing such a small number as outliers rather than evidence of a national consensus. A single colonial-era statute from one colony falls well short of the evidentiary threshold required to demonstrate a broadly accepted tradition of firearms regulation.

**Improper reliance on territorial laws.** The opinion cites nineteenth-century territorial laws as further evidence of a historical tradition. I argue that territorial laws are essentially irrelevant to the *Bruen* analysis. Territories were not yet states, the Constitution did not apply to them in the same manner, and there was no meaningful constitutional litigation challenging firearms restrictions in those jurisdictions. Citing territorial laws as evidence of an accepted national tradition of firearms regulation is, in my assessment, analytically unsound.

**The Statute of Northampton.** While Judge Pittman referenced the Statute of Northampton (1328) as historical support, I contend the court misread its significance. As the Supreme Court explained in *Bruen*, the Statute of Northampton was understood to prohibit carrying arms "to the terror of the people" -- that is, in a threatening or menacing manner -- rather than to prohibit the peaceable carrying of arms generally. To the extent the Statute restricted arms in the presence of the king, that restriction is consistent with the government-security principle: the king never traveled without armed guards, making his presence the functional equivalent of a secured government space.

## The Polling-Places Distinction

I want to highlight an important nuance regarding polling places as sensitive places. At the time of the founding, elections were conducted by open vote -- voters publicly announced their choices, which created a genuine risk of intimidation and violence. Government-provided security was present to protect the integrity of the process. The modern adoption of the Australian (secret) ballot system in the late nineteenth century fundamentally altered the character of polling places, reducing both the risk of voter intimidation and the corresponding need for armed security. This historical evolution, in my view, is precisely the kind of contextual analysis that *Bruen* demands and that the opinion in *Ziegenfuss* neglects.

## Looking Ahead

The Firearms Policy Coalition announced its intention to appeal the decision to the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit. The case presents the appellate court with an opportunity to address several unresolved questions in post-*Bruen* sensitive-places jurisprudence, including the proper definition of a sensitive place, the permissible temporal scope of the historical inquiry, and the applicability of the doctrine of liquidation to Second Amendment analysis. In my analysis, how the Fifth Circuit resolves these issues will have significant implications for similar challenges pending across the country.
