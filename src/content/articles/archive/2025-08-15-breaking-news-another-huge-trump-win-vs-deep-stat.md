## Summary

In my analysis, the United States Court of Appeals for the DC Circuit has issued a major ruling in **National Treasury Employees Union v. Russell Vought**, upholding President Trump's authority to restructure and dramatically reduce the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau -- a federal agency that has long been associated with regulatory overreach and that played a role in Operation Chokepoint, an initiative that used the financial system to pressure lawful businesses including firearms dealers and manufacturers. As I will explain, this decision is significant not only for administrative law but for the protection of Second Amendment rights, which depend in part on maintaining the structural constitutional order that prevents the executive branch from being captured by unaccountable bureaucracies.

## Background: The CFPB and Its History

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau was created by Congress in the aftermath of the 2008 financial crisis as part of the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act. The agency was conceived largely through the efforts of Senator Elizabeth Warren of Massachusetts, who subsequently helped establish the bureau and served in an advisory capacity during its early years. The CFPB was designed to serve as a watchdog over the financial services industry, with authority to investigate and take enforcement action against banks, lending institutions, and other financial entities.

Almost from its inception, the CFPB became embroiled in constitutional controversy. In 2020, the Supreme Court held in *Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau* that the CFPB's original leadership structure -- which insulated its director from presidential removal except for cause -- was unconstitutional, because it violated the President's Article II authority over the executive branch. The Court found that the structure impermissibly concentrated unchecked power in a single official not subject to ordinary presidential supervision.

Beyond its structural constitutional problems, the CFPB became associated, during the Obama administration, with Operation Chokepoint -- a coordinated regulatory effort to use the banking system to cut off financial services to industries disfavored by certain government officials. Among the industries targeted under Operation Chokepoint were payday lenders, coin dealers, tobacco retailers, and, critically, firearms dealers and manufacturers. By pressuring banks to deny accounts and services to these industries, the federal government attempted to achieve regulatory objectives that it could not accomplish through legislation -- including undermining the lawful firearms industry.

## The Trump Administration's Restructuring Effort

Upon taking office, President Trump announced a comprehensive effort to restructure the CFPB, reducing its workforce by approximately eighty percent and limiting the agency's activities to only those statutorily mandated functions that Congress had expressly required it to perform. The administration's position was clear: the CFPB would remain in existence and would fulfill its narrow statutory obligations, but it would not engage in discretionary regulatory overreach beyond what the law required.

Predictably, employee unions representing CFPB workers and allied left-wing organizations brought suit challenging the restructuring. The plaintiffs argued that President Trump lacked the constitutional authority to reorganize the CFPB in the manner he proposed, contending that because Congress had created the agency, only Congress could effectively dismantle or restructure it.

## The DC Circuit's Ruling

The DC Circuit rejected the challenge to the Trump administration's restructuring of the CFPB. In my assessment, the court's ruling correctly applies the foundational principles of Article II and the separation of powers. The Constitution vests all executive power in a single President of the United States. Federal agencies that exercise executive power do so as extensions of the President, not as independent entities accountable only to Congress or to their own internal governance structures.

The court held that President Trump possesses the constitutional authority to direct, manage, and restructure agencies within the executive branch, consistent with the principles established in *Seila Law* and the broader jurisprudential tradition recognizing presidential control over executive branch personnel and operations. An executive agency is not a constitutionally autonomous entity that can insulate itself from presidential direction by invoking the statute that created it. The President has authority to streamline executive operations and to reduce discretionary activities that exceed congressional mandates.

## The Structural Constitution and Second Amendment Rights

In my analysis, this case illustrates a principle that Second Amendment advocates must keep firmly in mind: the protection of individual constitutional rights depends not only on the express language of the Bill of Rights but also on the structural constitutional order that distributes and limits governmental power.

The Second Amendment protects the right to keep and bear arms. But the practical enjoyment of that right depends on the existence of a functioning lawful firearms market, accessible financial services for firearms businesses, and a regulatory environment that does not use administrative power to strangle the firearms industry through collateral pressure. When unelected bureaucrats operating with minimal presidential oversight engage in conduct like Operation Chokepoint -- using the machinery of financial regulation to attack lawful Second Amendment activities -- the structural constitution's mechanisms for democratic accountability break down.

By reaffirming the President's authority to control and direct executive branch agencies, the DC Circuit's decision in **National Treasury Employees Union v. Russell Vought** strengthens the constitutional framework that prevents precisely this kind of bureaucratic overreach. A President who can effectively supervise and redirect the CFPB is a President who can ensure that the CFPB does not again become an instrument of Operation Chokepoint-style regulatory warfare against the firearms industry.

## The Principle at Stake

The Founding Fathers designed the constitutional structure of the federal government with a carefully considered allocation of powers among the three branches. The executive branch was placed under the unified direction of a single President, accountable to the people through democratic elections, precisely because the Framers understood that diffuse, unaccountable executive power was a danger to individual liberty.

Unelected bureaucrats who exercise executive power without effective presidential supervision represent exactly the kind of concentrated, unaccountable authority the Constitution was designed to prevent. When agencies like the CFPB grow beyond their statutory mandates and begin targeting lawful industries -- including the firearms industry -- through regulatory pressure, they undermine not only the structural constitution but the specific rights that the constitutional structure was designed to protect.

The DC Circuit's ruling vindicating President Trump's authority to restructure the CFPB is therefore good news for the Second Amendment, not merely for administrative law. It reaffirms that the executive branch must be directed by a democratically elected President and that bureaucratic fiefdoms operating outside effective presidential control are constitutionally impermissible.
