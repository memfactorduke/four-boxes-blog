## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has granted certiorari in **Hammani v. United States**, agreeing to review whether 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3) — the federal prohibition on firearm possession by unlawful users of or persons addicted to controlled substances — is consistent with the Second Amendment. This marks the second Second Amendment case the Court has accepted for the current term, joining **Wolford v. Bonta** out of the Ninth Circuit, which addresses California's so-called "vampire rule" — a default prohibition on carrying firearms in virtually all public places unless specifically authorized by statute or regulation. In my analysis, the Second Amendment will lose the **Hammani** case on the outcome, but the methodology and reasoning the Court employs will be of extraordinary consequence for the future of firearms rights in America. The only question of practical significance is how the Court reasons to that conclusion.

## Background

The question presented in **Hammani** is whether 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3), which prohibits the possession of firearms by any person who is an unlawful user of or addicted to any controlled substance, is consistent with the Second Amendment as construed in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, **McDonald v. City of Chicago**, and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**. The case reached the Supreme Court after the Fifth Circuit invalidated an indictment brought against Ali Daniel Hammani, a dual Pakistani-American citizen alleged to have possessed a firearm while in possession of marijuana and cocaine and alleged to have connections to the Iranian Revolutionary Guard Corps.

The Supreme Court had already granted certiorari in **Wolford v. Bonta** — the Ninth Circuit case challenging California's regime under which carrying a firearm in any location is presumptively prohibited unless the legislature has affirmatively authorized it. That challenge implicates the core of **Bruen**'s holding that law-abiding citizens have a right to carry firearms in public for self-defense, and that states cannot invert the constitutional default by requiring affirmative legislative permission to exercise a constitutional right.

## The Outcome in Hammani: A Near-Certainty

I want to be direct with my analysis: the Second Amendment will not prevail in **Hammani**. There is, in my judgment, a zero percent probability that five justices of this Court will hold that individuals who are actively and regularly using federally controlled substances — cocaine, heroin, marijuana, or similar drugs — retain constitutional protection for firearm possession during the period of that active use.

This is the reality on the ground, and I offer it not to discourage Second Amendment advocates but to enable accurate strategic analysis. The Trump Department of Justice, acting through Solicitor General John Sauer, sought certiorari in this case because the administration holds a coherent worldview: law-abiding Americans deserve full Second Amendment rights, and criminals and drug users do not. That worldview is not unreasonable, and the argument for seeking cert — resolving a circuit split on a significant federal criminal statute — is a defensible one. I personally would have preferred a different vehicle for the Court's second Second Amendment case this term, as I have said at Second Amendment Foundation and Gun Owners of America events. But the decision was made, certiorari has been granted, and we must now work with what we have.

## The Two-Part Architecture of Every Supreme Court Decision

As I have explained in many contexts, every significant Supreme Court decision has two components, and understanding both is essential for anyone who cares about Second Amendment rights.

The first component is the judgment — who wins and who loses. This matters enormously to the parties in the specific case. The difference between a favorable and unfavorable judgment can mean the difference between a criminal conviction and an acquittal, between freedom and imprisonment.

The second component — which in many ways matters more than the judgment itself for the future of constitutional law — is the rationale. The reasoning the Court employs, the methodology it articulates, the doctrinal framework it constructs or reinforces, and the language it uses will govern every Second Amendment case litigated in the lower courts for years or decades to come. In a case like **Hammani**, where the outcome is predetermined, the rationale is virtually everything.

## The Strategic Concern: How Will the Court Lose This Case?

The question Second Amendment advocates should be asking is not "Will the Second Amendment win this case?" — it will not — but rather "How will the Second Amendment lose this case, and will the manner of losing do damage to Second Amendment doctrine in other contexts?"

There are several ways the Court could uphold § 922(g)(3), and the choice among them carries very different implications.

A narrow, case-specific ruling would limit the holding to the particular facts of **Hammani** — a defendant with alleged ties to a foreign terrorist organization, charged with multiple crimes, found in possession of serious controlled substances. Such a ruling would provide little guidance for future cases involving less extreme factual scenarios, such as a state-legal recreational marijuana user in Colorado who possesses a firearm entirely lawfully under state law.

A broader doctrinal ruling could articulate a general principle — perhaps drawn from the **Rahimi** framework of temporarily disarming individuals who pose a demonstrated danger — that encompasses active, habitual drug users. Depending on how broadly the Court defines the relevant historical tradition, such a ruling could either reaffirm the strength of the **Bruen** methodology or introduce analytical flexibility that hostile lower courts will exploit.

The most problematic outcome for the Second Amendment community would be a ruling that weakens the **Bruen** framework itself — by adopting balancing tests, introducing presumptions of constitutionality for longstanding federal statutes, or otherwise diluting the historical tradition test. In my assessment, that outcome is unlikely given the composition of the Court, but it remains a risk worth monitoring closely.

## The Ordered Liberty Framework

I want to offer one additional observation about the underlying policy dimension of this case. The Trump administration's decision to seek certiorari reflects a broader constitutional vision — one that I would characterize as "ordered liberty." This vision holds that full Second Amendment rights belong to law-abiding, responsible citizens; that criminals and those who actively flout the law have diminished claims to those rights while engaged in unlawful conduct; and that a society committed to the rule of law is not obligated to arm those who actively defy it.

This is not an unreasonable constitutional philosophy. As I have argued, the Second Amendment community's strongest position has always been that we are the law-abiding majority — responsible gun owners who use firearms for lawful purposes including self-defense. We do not benefit, as a matter of politics or law, from being associated with criminals and habitual drug users. Getting those individuals off the streets and out of possession of firearms — through the enforcement of existing criminal law — actually serves Second Amendment interests by reducing the incidents that drive public support for additional gun control legislation.

## Implications

The grant of certiorari in **Hammani** makes this a defining Second Amendment term. With both **Wolford** and **Hammani** on the docket, the Court will address two of the most contested areas of post-**Bruen** litigation: the scope of the right to carry in public and the permissible scope of categorical disarmament for particular classes of persons. The outcome in **Wolford** — where I am considerably more optimistic — may substantially advance the right to carry. The outcome in **Hammani** will almost certainly uphold § 922(g)(3), but the precise reasoning will determine whether the decision strengthens or erodes the **Bruen** framework that protects the Second Amendment rights of the law-abiding majority.
