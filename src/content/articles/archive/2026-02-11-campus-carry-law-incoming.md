## Summary

The New Hampshire House of Representatives has passed legislation that would prohibit state-funded colleges and universities from banning the lawful carry of firearms on campus — a significant legislative development that correctly recognizes that publicly funded educational institutions do not possess the authority to strip students, faculty, and visitors of their fundamental constitutional right to keep and bear arms. In my analysis, this bill is constitutionally sound, practically important, and represents precisely the kind of legislative action that the Second Amendment community should be advancing in every state where the political conditions permit it. The opposition arguments — that college students are too immature to exercise their constitutional rights — are not merely unpersuasive but self-refuting.

## Background

New Hampshire occupies a distinctive position in the northeastern United States. It is a constitutional carry state — one that has not required a permit for the carry of a concealed firearm since 2017. Its state constitution, specifically Article 2-A, ratified by a supermajority of New Hampshire voters in 1982, provides that "all persons have the right to keep and bear arms in defense of themselves, their families, their property and the State." That provision is among the strongest individual right-to-arms guarantees in any state constitution in the nation.

Despite this robust constitutional framework at both the state and federal levels, state-funded colleges and universities in New Hampshire have continued to prohibit the carry of firearms on their campuses. Unelected university administrators — not legislators, not voters, not courts — have arrogated to themselves the authority to create gun-free zones across publicly funded educational institutions, effectively treating a constitutional right as a discretionary administrative matter that universities may suspend at will.

The New Hampshire House bill would end that practice. It would require state-funded colleges and universities to allow campus carry, consistent with the constitutional carry rights that New Hampshire law already recognizes for all other public spaces in the state.

## The Constitutional Case for Campus Carry

The constitutional analysis begins with the text of the Second Amendment as interpreted by the Supreme Court in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and extended to public carry by **New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). Heller established that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms for self-defense. Bruen established that this right extends to public spaces and that the government bears the burden of demonstrating that restrictions on public carry are consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation.

State Representative Sam Farrington, a University of New Hampshire student and one of the bill's principal advocates, articulated the constitutional case clearly before the House. He observed that New Hampshire's Article 2-A expressly protects the right to bear arms for self-defense in the state. He noted that New Hampshire became a constitutional carry state in 2017, confirming that the legislature regards the right to bear arms as fundamental and self-executing — not a privilege that requires a government permission slip. And he pointed out the logical incoherence of treating college students as simultaneously old enough to vote, old enough to serve in the military and fight in wars, but somehow not mature enough to exercise a constitutional right on a state-funded campus.

The specific application to state-funded universities is constitutionally significant in a way that goes beyond the general campus-carry debate. A state-funded institution is not a private actor. It is an arm of the state government, operating with public funds, on behalf of the public, and subject to the constitutional constraints that bind state government. When a state-funded university prohibits the carry of firearms on its campus, it is engaging in exactly the kind of state action that the Second Amendment — incorporated against the states through the Fourteenth Amendment in **McDonald v. City of Chicago**, 561 U.S. 742 (2010) — is designed to prohibit.

A privately funded university may occupy a different constitutional position. But a public university, funded by the state's taxpayers and operating under state authority, has no more power to override a constitutional right than any other state actor. The university's claim that it may designate its campus as a gun-free zone is, at its core, a claim that state institutions may selectively nullify constitutional rights in their own domains. That claim is constitutionally unsustainable.

## The Opposition Arguments and Their Deficiencies

The principal argument offered against campus carry is that college students are too immature, too prone to intoxication, and generally too irresponsible to be trusted with firearms. This argument fails on multiple levels.

First, it proves too much. If the claimed maturity and sobriety deficiencies of college students are sufficient to strip them of a constitutional right, those same deficiencies would justify stripping them of the right to vote, the right to enter contracts, the right to consume alcohol, and the right to serve in the armed forces. The law treats college-age adults as fully enfranchised citizens with the complete range of constitutional rights and legal responsibilities. It cannot coherently carve out the Second Amendment as a special exception on grounds of alleged immaturity that the law everywhere else declines to recognize.

Second, the argument is factually and empirically questionable. Numerous states have permitted campus carry for years without the catastrophic consequences that opponents predict. The empirical record on campus carry does not support the claim that armed students create a more dangerous campus environment.

Third, and most pointedly, the argument was spectacularly undermined by the University of New Hampshire's own chief of police, who testified against the bill on the grounds that college students are "too drunk to carry a firearm." One week after that testimony, he was arrested for drunk driving. The credibility of the opposition's lead witness was destroyed by his own conduct. Whatever one thinks of the substantive arguments against campus carry, the position that college students are uniquely prone to irresponsible behavior does not survive this episode.

## The Safety Argument for Campus Carry

The affirmative safety case for campus carry rests on a simple and well-supported empirical observation: gun-free zones do not stop determined attackers; they disarm potential victims. Over 90 percent of mass shootings in the United States occur in designated gun-free zones. The reason is not complicated. An individual planning to commit a mass killing is not deterred by a sign or a policy declaring a location gun-free. Such an individual selects locations where resistance is least likely — where victims will be unarmed and defenseless.

Campus carry does not convert a university into a combat zone. It means that a small percentage of law-abiding students, faculty, and visitors who would otherwise be legally carrying firearms in every other location in the state retain the ability to do so on a state-funded campus. The potential deterrent and defensive effect of that small number of armed individuals is the relevant safety consideration, not the imagined dangers of a universally armed campus.

## Implications

The New Hampshire campus carry bill represents the correct legislative approach to one of the most common and most constitutionally problematic applications of the sensitive-places doctrine — the use of that doctrine to designate entire university campuses as permanent, comprehensive gun-free zones. The bill has passed the House and now proceeds to the New Hampshire Senate, where the Republican majority and Republican governor provide a favorable political environment for passage.

More broadly, the bill illustrates that legislative action in Second Amendment-friendly states can advance constitutional rights through multiple simultaneous channels — not only through federal litigation but through state law that removes barriers to the exercise of rights that state and federal constitutions already guarantee. Every successful campus carry bill, permitless carry expansion, or gun-control prohibition enacted in a state that respects Second Amendment rights builds the political, practical, and legal record that supports the broader constitutional project.
