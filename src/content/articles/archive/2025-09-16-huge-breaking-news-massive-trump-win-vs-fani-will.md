## Summary

The Georgia Supreme Court has denied Fulton County District Attorney Fani Willis's petition to review the Georgia Court of Appeals' decision disqualifying her and her office from the prosecution of Donald Trump and his co-defendants under Georgia's RICO statute. By denying review, the Georgia Supreme Court has effectively affirmed the disqualification ruling, which rests on findings that District Attorney Willis had a disqualifying conflict of interest arising from a romantic relationship with Nathan Wade, the special prosecutor she hired and paid substantial public funds to prosecute the case. This development ends, as a practical matter, the Fulton County prosecution of President Trump in its current form.

## Background

In 2023, Fulton County District Attorney Fani Willis filed an indictment under Georgia's Racketeer Influenced and Corrupt Organizations statute against former President Donald Trump and approximately fifteen co-defendants, alleging that their efforts to challenge the results of the 2020 presidential election in Georgia constituted a criminal enterprise. The indictment relied on the theory that activities including legal challenges, public statements, and communications with state officials amounted to organized criminal conduct under a statute designed to prosecute enterprises modeled on organized crime.

The RICO statute — originally developed to target the organized crime model in which a criminal organization operates through various members at different levels of the criminal hierarchy — was here applied to individuals whose principal activities consisted of pursuing legal theories regarding the conduct of an election. That application raised substantial legal questions, among them whether the statute's application to political and legal activities that did not constitute established crimes at the time they were taken comported with due process and the First Amendment.

The Georgia prosecution drew additional scrutiny when it emerged that District Attorney Willis had engaged in a romantic relationship with Nathan Wade, the special prosecutor she hired to lead the Trump prosecution, and had paid Wade approximately $700,000 or more in public funds for his work on the case. The allegation was that Willis and Wade then used those funds, in part, to take personal vacations and engage in other personal expenditures — a pattern that defense attorneys argued constituted an improper financial benefit to Willis from the prosecution she was overseeing and a disqualifying conflict of interest under Georgia law and rules of professional conduct.

## The Disqualification Proceedings and the Court of Appeals' Ruling

A Fulton County trial judge held a hearing on the disqualification motion and ultimately declined to remove Willis, finding that while the relationship existed, the conflict of interest it created could be remedied by Wade's withdrawal from the case. The Georgia Court of Appeals reversed, concluding that the conflict of interest created by Willis's relationship with Wade — and the financial arrangements that flowed from it — was sufficiently serious to require Willis's disqualification from the prosecution.

The Georgia Court of Appeals' ruling was based on Georgia law governing prosecutorial conflicts of interest and the professional ethics requirements applicable to district attorneys. Under those standards, a prosecutor who has a financial or personal interest in the outcome of a prosecution, or whose personal relationships with members of the prosecution team create a reasonable appearance of impropriety, may be disqualified. The court found that the evidence presented met this threshold.

## The Georgia Supreme Court's Action

The Georgia Supreme Court's decision to deny review of the Court of Appeals' disqualification ruling is, in legal terms, not a ruling on the merits — the Supreme Court's denial of certiorari does not signal agreement or disagreement with the Court of Appeals' analysis. It means only that the Supreme Court, for whatever reason, chose not to grant review. The practical effect, however, is to leave the disqualification ruling in place: Fani Willis and her office cannot continue to prosecute the *State v. Trump* case.

In my view, the decision to bring this prosecution was, from the outset, a problematic exercise of prosecutorial discretion. The conduct at issue — legal challenges to election results, communications with state officials, and related political activity — occurred in a context in which there were legitimate questions about the 2020 election procedures, questions that Congress itself acknowledged when it subsequently amended the Electoral Count Act to clarify the very ambiguities that Trump and his legal team had argued were relevant. The fact that Congress found it necessary to amend the Electoral Count Act is itself evidence that the legal questions Trump raised were not frivolous.

## The Electoral Count Act and the Validity of the Legal Theories at Issue

One aspect of the *State v. Trump* case that deserves careful attention is the relationship between the conduct alleged and the state of the law at the time it occurred. Trump's legal team argued, in essence, that the Electoral Count Act — the statute governing Congress's role in counting electoral votes — contained ambiguities that permitted the Vice President and Congress to conduct a more searching review of the electoral votes submitted by states in which irregularities were alleged.

Congress's decision, in 2022, to amend the Electoral Count Act to eliminate those ambiguities is direct evidence that the ambiguities existed. One does not amend a statute to clarify provisions that are already perfectly clear. The amended Electoral Count Act is Congress's implicit acknowledgment that the pre-2022 statute was susceptible to the interpretation Trump's legal team advanced — which means the theory was legally colorable, even if ultimately wrong, at the time it was argued.

## The Pattern of Politically Motivated Prosecutions

The *State v. Trump* prosecution in Fulton County was one of several simultaneous prosecutions of Donald Trump and his associates arising from his challenges to the 2020 election and his conduct in office. The simultaneous nature of these prosecutions — all filed by Democratic prosecutors or in jurisdictions with Democratic-appointed prosecutors — is a fact pattern that courts and legal analysts have noted in assessing whether prosecutorial discretion was being exercised appropriately.

The disqualification of Fani Willis does not resolve the broader question of whether the *State v. Trump* case was a legitimate exercise of the prosecutorial function or a politically motivated use of criminal process to harm a political opponent. It does, however, remove the specific prosecutor whose conflicts of interest were found to be disqualifying, and it places a significant obstacle in the path of any continued prosecution of the Fulton County RICO case.

## Implications

The Georgia Supreme Court's effective affirmance of Fani Willis's disqualification is a significant legal development in one of the most politically charged prosecutions in modern American history. The disqualification ruling reflects the application of professional conduct standards that exist precisely to prevent the kind of conflict of interest the Court of Appeals found: a prosecutor who has a personal financial relationship with a member of her prosecution team cannot credibly claim the independence and impartiality that prosecutorial neutrality requires.

For the defendants in this case, including former President Trump, the disqualification ruling means that any continued prosecution under the existing indictment must be handled by a different prosecutor — one who, at a minimum, does not carry the conflicts that led to Willis's removal. Whether such a prosecution proceeds, and on what terms, will depend on the decisions of whoever assumes responsibility for the case going forward. The road to a resumed prosecution, if one is pursued, will be long and complex.
