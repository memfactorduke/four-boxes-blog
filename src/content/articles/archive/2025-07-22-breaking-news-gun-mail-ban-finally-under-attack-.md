## Summary

Gun Owners of America and the Gun Owners Foundation have filed a constitutional challenge in federal court in the Third Circuit on behalf of Pennsylvania resident Bonita Shrev, seeking a declaration that 18 U.S.C. § 1715 — a Prohibition-era federal statute enacted around 1927 that prohibits ordinary law-abiding Americans from using the United States Postal Service to ship or receive handguns — is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The complaint, prepared by attorney Daniel Schmutter and a team of lawyers from Cooper & Kirk, advances a textual and historical argument that the federal government's prohibition on using the mails to transport handguns burdens conduct plainly within the scope of the Second Amendment's text and lacks any founding-era historical analog sufficient to justify it under the framework established in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). Having reviewed this complaint, I assess it as among the most well-constructed Second Amendment pleadings I have encountered on the issue of handgun transportation restrictions.

## Background

Federal law has long imposed restrictions on the use of the United States mail to transport firearms. The specific statute at issue — 18 U.S.C. § 1715 — was enacted in the 1920s, during the Prohibition era, and prohibits the mailing of certain categories of weapons. As relevant to this challenge, the statute makes it unlawful for an ordinary private citizen — that is, someone who is neither a licensed federal firearms dealer (FFL) nor acting on behalf of the government — to ship or receive a handgun through the United States Postal Service.

The prohibition applies specifically to handguns. It does not apply to long guns such as unloaded shotguns and unloaded rifles, which may be shipped through the USPS under applicable rules. The asymmetrical treatment of handguns versus long guns is notable because, since **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), the Supreme Court has recognized the handgun as the quintessential self-defense firearm — the arm that the average law-abiding citizen is most likely to choose for the lawful purpose of self-defense in the home.

The statute also treats different categories of individuals differently. Government employees and federal firearms licensees may use the postal service to mail handguns; ordinary citizens cannot. This disparate treatment reflects precisely the kind of class-based double standard that, in other contexts, the Second Amendment community has correctly identified as constitutionally unjustifiable. As Gun Owners of America senior vice president Eric Pratt stated in connection with this lawsuit: "Gun Owners of America is proud to stand with its members and supporters to ensure that their constitutional rights are protected, and we are committed to ending all anti-gun rules for me, but not for thee, in any form they may take."

The plaintiff, Bonita Shrev, wishes to ship a handgun through the United States Postal Service to her father, who lives approximately three hours away in Pennsylvania. She is legally permitted to own the handgun. The transfer of the firearm from daughter to father would otherwise be entirely lawful. But 18 U.S.C. § 1715 prevents her from using the postal service — a federal government entity — as the means of transportation for that otherwise-legal transfer. She has filed suit, with Gun Owners of America and the Gun Owners Foundation as co-plaintiffs, seeking to have § 1715 declared unconstitutional as applied to ordinary law-abiding citizens.

## The Textual Analysis Under Bruen

The **Bruen** framework's first step asks whether the challenged statute burdens conduct within the plain text of the Second Amendment. The operative clause of the Second Amendment reads: "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed."

The complaint advances two distinct but related textual arguments. First, it argues that the ability to ship and receive handguns — which are unquestionably protected "Arms" under **Heller** and **Bruen** — is conduct within the scope of the textual right to "keep" and "bear" arms. Second, it invokes the doctrine of ancillary or implied Second Amendment rights — the principle that certain activities not explicitly mentioned in the text of the Second Amendment are nonetheless textually protected because they are necessary preconditions to the exercise of the enumerated right itself.

On the first point: the right to "keep" arms means the right to possess them. One cannot possess an arm that one cannot obtain. The right to acquire arms is therefore implicit in the right to keep them — a point that the Ninth Circuit's recent decision in **Rood v. Bonta** independently confirmed in the context of ammunition regulation. A federal law that prevents an ordinary citizen from using a primary channel of interstate commerce — the United States Postal Service — to receive a handgun she has lawfully acquired or to transfer a handgun to a family member burdens the textual right to keep arms by restricting one of the most common means by which Americans obtain and transfer lawfully owned property.

On the second point: courts have recognized that certain activities that are not expressly mentioned in the Second Amendment's text are nonetheless protected as ancillary rights — rights that are necessary incidents of the enumerated right to keep and bear arms. The United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit in the *Ezell* case recognized the right to train with firearms as an ancillary Second Amendment right, reasoning that the right to possess a firearm necessarily implies some right to maintain the ability to use it effectively. By precisely analogous reasoning, the right to obtain, transfer, and ship firearms through ordinary commercial channels — including the United States mail — is an ancillary right implicit in the textual guarantee of the right to keep and bear arms.

## The State Action Question

The complaint specifically challenges 18 U.S.C. § 1715, which is a federal statute. The United States Postal Service is indisputably an arm of the federal government. The constitutional challenge here is therefore to direct federal government action — not to private conduct — and there is no question that the Second Amendment applies to federal restrictions on Second Amendment-protected conduct.

The USPS is funded by Congress, its leadership testifies before Congress regarding its budget, and it exists as a creature of federal statute. When the USPS denies a citizen the ability to use the mail to transport a constitutionally protected arm, it acts as an agent of the federal government in a manner that directly implicates the Second Amendment's guarantee against infringement by the government.

## The Historical Analysis

Having established that § 1715 burdens textually protected Second Amendment conduct, the constitutional analysis shifts to the second step of the **Bruen** framework: whether the federal government can identify a historical tradition of analogous firearms regulation dating to the founding era.

It cannot. The federal prohibition on mailing handguns was enacted in 1927 — more than 130 years after the Second Amendment was ratified. There is no founding-era tradition of prohibiting law-abiding citizens from transporting firearms through government-operated communication and transportation networks, because no such networks existed at the founding. The United States mail has operated as a means of transporting property since the founding era, and there is no evidence of any founding-era prohibition on using the mail to transport firearms.

The statute's Prohibition-era vintage is not a virtue. Under **Bruen**, the relevant historical tradition must date to the founding, not to the 1920s. Twentieth-century gun control legislation — however long-standing it may seem from today's perspective — does not constitute a founding-era tradition. A 1927 statute is not old enough to establish the kind of deeply rooted historical tradition that could constitutionally justify restricting the exercise of a constitutional right. The complaint is correct that the handgun mail ban is "not longstanding enough to survive constitutional scrutiny."

## The Asymmetry Problem: FFLs and Government Employees

The complaint's challenge to the statute is strengthened by the statutory asymmetry noted above: FFLs and government employees may use the USPS to ship handguns; ordinary law-abiding citizens cannot. This disparity is constitutionally troubling on its face. If the government's asserted justification for restricting handgun mailing is preventing firearms from falling into the wrong hands, it is difficult to understand why FFLs and government employees are exempt from that concern. The Second Amendment does not create a hierarchy of rights under which some citizens are entitled to exercise the right to keep and bear arms more fully than others based on their occupational status.

The exemption for FFLs and government employees actually undermines the government's likely historical and interest-based arguments. If there were a genuine historical tradition of prohibiting civilian transport of handguns by mail, that tradition would presumably not have created exemptions for licensed dealers and government personnel — distinctions that are creatures of the modern regulatory state rather than the founding era.

## Implications

**Shrev v. USPS** — and the broader constitutional challenge to 18 U.S.C. § 1715 — is an important and long-overdue Second Amendment case. The handgun mail ban has existed for nearly a century without serious constitutional challenge, largely because the pre-**Bruen** framework made Second Amendment challenges difficult to mount. Under **Bruen**'s textual and historical methodology, however, the statute is constitutionally vulnerable.

The case will initially be litigated before a federal district court within the Third Circuit. The Third Circuit covers Pennsylvania, Delaware, and New Jersey — a jurisdiction that has recently demonstrated some receptivity to Second Amendment claims, as evidenced by the **Core Civic v. New Jersey** decision discussed elsewhere on this site. If the district court rules against the challengers, the case will proceed to the Third Circuit on appeal.

Regardless of the outcome in the Third Circuit, this case presents a Second Amendment question of national significance. If § 1715's handgun mail ban is ultimately struck down, it will open the mails to ordinary law-abiding citizens seeking to transport their lawfully owned handguns through a channel of commerce that has been arbitrarily closed to them for nearly a century. That outcome would be both constitutionally required and practically beneficial for millions of law-abiding gun owners across the country.
