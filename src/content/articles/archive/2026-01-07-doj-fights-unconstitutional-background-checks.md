## Summary

The Trump-Bondi Department of Justice has filed a formal brief in the Ninth Circuit's en banc proceeding in **Kim Rhode v. Rob Bonta**, making the detailed constitutional argument that California's point-of-sale ammunition background check system is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The DOJ's brief advances two foundational propositions: first, that the acquisition of ammunition falls squarely within the Second Amendment's plain text — because the right to bear arms necessarily protects the right to possess the means necessary to make arms functional — and second, that California's regime, being novel and without historical analog, cannot survive the burden-shifting framework that **Bruen** imposes once the plain-text threshold is crossed. In my assessment, this brief is exceptionally well-constructed and its core arguments are correct. The constitutional significance of establishing that ammunition acquisition is covered by the Second Amendment's plain text extends far beyond this case alone.

## Background

### California's Ammunition Background Check System

California enacted the nation's first — and only — point-of-sale ammunition background check system, codified in California Penal Code §§ 30352 and 30370. The law requires a California resident to submit to a fresh background check every time she purchases ammunition, regardless of how recently she completed a prior check and regardless of whether her eligibility to possess firearms has changed in any way. Each check is one-time use only; a resident who purchases ammunition on Monday and seeks to purchase ammunition on Tuesday must submit to an entirely new check.

The law offers four procedural pathways for completing the required check. Each involves fees ranging from $1 to $31 per transaction. Each involves delays ranging from minutes to days, with no guarantee of timely completion. The system cross-references purchaser information against multiple databases and generates denials in a non-trivial percentage of transactions even for entirely law-abiding citizens — denials attributable to database errors, name-matching failures, and administrative deficiencies entirely unrelated to any individual's legal eligibility to purchase ammunition.

Courts that have examined this regime have characterized it in notably harsh terms: "unnecessarily complicated," "onerous and convoluted," "cumbersome," "extensive," "unwieldy," and "first of its kind." The DOJ brief, with apt precision, states that California's system has "earned every one of those labels."

### The Litigation: Kim Rhode v. Rob Bonta

**Kim Rhode**, an Olympic gold medalist in competitive shooting, brought this Second Amendment challenge as lead plaintiff. The district court ruled in favor of the plaintiffs, finding the ammunition background check regime unconstitutional. A three-judge panel of the Ninth Circuit affirmed. In what has become a characteristic pattern at the Ninth Circuit, the full court voted to rehear the case en banc following the panel's pro-Second Amendment ruling — a procedural mechanism that, in practice, serves to reverse panel decisions with which the circuit's liberal majority disagrees.

It is into this en banc proceeding that the Trump-Bondi DOJ has entered as a formal participant, filing a brief that endorses the constitutional arguments of the Second Amendment plaintiffs and directly challenges the validity of California's ammunition background check regime.

## The DOJ's Plain-Text Argument

### Ammunition Is Covered by the Second Amendment's Text

The analytical foundation of the DOJ's brief — and the argument of greatest long-term constitutional significance — is the proposition that acquiring ammunition constitutes "conduct covered by the Second Amendment's plain text" under **Bruen**'s step-one inquiry.

Under **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the threshold question in any Second Amendment challenge is whether the conduct regulated by the challenged law falls within the plain-text protection of the Second Amendment. If it does, the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that the regulation is "consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation." The plain-text inquiry is thus the gateway through which every constitutional challenge must pass — and where the DOJ's brief makes its most important contribution.

The DOJ's argument proceeds from the text of **Bruen** itself. As the Court explained, the right to "bear arms" refers to the right to wield a firearm "for the purpose of being armed and ready for offensive or defensive action." The Second Amendment does not protect the right to carry a firearm merely as an ornament. It protects the right to an operable firearm for the purpose of immediate self-defense — and that necessarily requires ammunition.

The argument, stated in the DOJ's own language, is this: "Acquiring ammunition is conduct covered by the Second Amendment's plain text. The right to acquire ammunition is part and parcel of the right to bear arms." The right to bear arms, understood as the right to wield a functional firearm in self-defense, implies a corresponding right to obtain the bullets necessary to make the firearm effective. A firearm without ammunition is not a weapon capable of offensive or defensive action; it is an inert piece of metal. Both firearms and ammunition are necessary to the exercise of the right that the Second Amendment protects, as is the ability to acquire either.

### The Logic of Implied Constitutional Protection

The DOJ brief invokes an additional constitutional principle that I regard as analytically sound and strategically important: constitutional rights implicitly protect those closely related acts necessary to their exercise. The general power to do a thing includes every particular power necessary for doing it. No axiom is more clearly established in law or reason.

Applying this principle to the Second Amendment, the right to keep and bear arms for self-defense necessarily implies the right to acquire the ammunition that makes self-defense possible. As the Ninth Circuit itself has previously recognized in this litigation: "without bullets, the right to bear arms would be meaningless." The right to possess firearms for protection implies a corresponding right to obtain the bullets necessary to use them. This is not a novel or exotic proposition; it is a straightforward application of constitutional logic.

The practical significance of establishing this principle at the appellate and ultimately at the Supreme Court level cannot be overstated. If the right to keep and bear arms includes the right to acquire arms — and it does — that principle becomes the constitutional foundation for challenging not only ammunition background check systems, but per-round taxes, restrictions on the types of ammunition that may be purchased, and other regulatory schemes designed to render legally possessed firearms practically unusable.

### The Burden Shifts to California

Once it is established that California's ammunition background check requirement implicates the Second Amendment's plain text — that is, once step one of the **Bruen** inquiry is resolved in favor of the Second Amendment challengers — the burden shifts to California to justify the challenged regulation by reference to the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation.

This is where California's ammunition background check system faces its fatal constitutional defect: there is no historical tradition to invoke. As the DOJ brief states, California's law's purpose — the hindrance of law-abiding citizens' exercise of Second Amendment rights — "finds no analog among valid regulatory schemes of the past." That conclusion is unsurprising. The history and tradition surrounding the Second Amendment establish that firearms regulations must serve legitimate objectives and may not be designed simply to inhibit the ability to possess or carry operable, protected firearms.

No founding-era, antebellum, or Reconstruction-era jurisdiction required a background check before every ammunition purchase. No other state in the contemporary United States has imposed such a requirement. California's regime is novel in the most complete sense: unprecedented historically and an outlier among the fifty states today. A regulation with no historical analog cannot survive a **Bruen** challenge.

## California's Flawed Counter-Argument

### The Ninth Circuit's Prior Misapplication

The argument that California and the en banc court have advanced — that ammunition purchases do not implicate the Second Amendment's plain text at all — is, in the DOJ's accurate assessment, wrong. The proposition that the Second Amendment's protection ends at the firearm itself, and that the government may freely regulate acquisition of the means necessary to operate that firearm, is inconsistent with **Bruen**'s textual analysis and with the logic of **Heller**.

**District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), held that the Second Amendment protects the right to keep and bear arms for self-defense and that this right extends to the home as the place where self-defense is most acutely needed. The Court's reasoning rested on the understanding that the protected right is the right to an operable, functional means of self-defense. A gun without ammunition is not operable. Restricting access to ammunition burdens the Second Amendment right as surely as restricting access to the firearm itself.

## Strategic Significance

### The Importance of the Plain-Text Victory

I have emphasized in prior analyses that the most strategically significant aspect of the DOJ's brief is the plain-text argument — the argument that acquiring ammunition is conduct covered by the Second Amendment's text. This matters not just for this case but for the entire trajectory of Second Amendment litigation.

The reason is this: once the Supreme Court establishes that the right to keep and bear arms includes the right to acquire arms — meaning the right to acquire ammunition, components, and the means necessary to render arms functional — that principle becomes available to defeat a broad range of regulatory schemes designed to render legally owned firearms practically unusable. Ghost gun regulations, restrictions on home manufacture of firearms, per-round ammunition taxes, and other de facto disarmament schemes all become vulnerable to constitutional challenge once the plain-text acquisition right is settled.

We cannot prevail in those future battles until we first establish, clearly and authoritatively, that the right to keep and bear arms extends to acquiring the means necessary to exercise that right. The DOJ's brief in **Rhode v. Bonta** advances that foundational argument in precisely the right venue: a case involving a novel, historically unmoored regulation in a circuit that has historically been hostile to Second Amendment claims, with the full institutional weight of the United States government behind the constitutional argument.

### The En Banc Posture

The en banc Ninth Circuit presents a challenging venue for Second Amendment plaintiffs. The full court voted to rehear this case specifically because a three-judge panel ruled in favor of the Second Amendment. The en banc bench reflects the overall composition of the Ninth Circuit, which has historically been the most resistant to Second Amendment arguments of any federal circuit.

The DOJ's intervention changes the calculus in important ways. When the United States government formally endorses a constitutional position in a federal appellate proceeding, the court must engage with that position as the considered judgment of a co-equal branch of government, not merely the advocacy of private litigants. The institutional weight of a DOJ brief is categorically different from a brief filed by even the most capable private appellate counsel.

Moreover, if the en banc court reverses the panel and upholds California's ammunition background check regime despite the district court, panel, and DOJ all concluding it is unconstitutional, the case presents an exceptionally strong vehicle for Supreme Court review. The split between the en banc majority and all prior reviewing tribunals, combined with the DOJ's formal position, makes certiorari virtually certain in that scenario.

## Implications

### Ammunition as a Protected Constitutional Category

The DOJ's brief formally commits the United States government to the position that ammunition acquisition is protected by the Second Amendment's plain text. That commitment has implications beyond California. It signals to courts across the country that regulations targeting ammunition as a mechanism for indirectly burdening Second Amendment rights will face the full weight of the federal government's constitutional opposition.

State and local regulations that impose per-round taxes on ammunition, that restrict the types of ammunition available for purchase, or that impose waiting periods and background check requirements on ammunition transactions are all vulnerable under the principle the DOJ is advancing. The constitutional argument does not depend on California's particular implementation; it depends on the threshold proposition that ammunition acquisition is covered by the Second Amendment's text.

### The Bruen Framework in Action

**Rhode v. Bonta** demonstrates **Bruen**'s two-step methodology operating precisely as designed. Step one: does the challenged regulation implicate the Second Amendment's plain text? For California's ammunition background check system, the answer is yes, because acquiring ammunition is conduct covered by the Second Amendment's protection of the right to bear operable arms for self-defense. Step two: can the government demonstrate a historical tradition supporting the challenged regulation? For California's regime, the answer is no — it is novel, unprecedented, and without historical analog.

The result that **Bruen** compels is the invalidation of California's ammunition background check system. The DOJ's brief makes that case rigorously and correctly. The question is whether the en banc Ninth Circuit will apply **Bruen** faithfully or will find some basis to evade its application — and, if it does the latter, whether the Supreme Court will accept the invitation to correct the error.

### The Right to Acquire Arms

At bottom, **Rhode v. Bonta** presents the foundational question of whether the Second Amendment's protection of the right to keep and bear arms extends to acquiring the means necessary to keep and bear those arms effectively. The answer, as the DOJ correctly argues, is yes. A constitutional right that protects the exercise of self-defense but permits the government to freely restrict acquisition of the ammunition necessary for that exercise is not a right at all — it is a license that the government can revoke through indirect restriction. The Second Amendment secures more than that. It secures the complete right: the right to possess, the right to carry, and the right to acquire the means to make the exercise of those rights meaningful.
