## Summary

Professor Jennifer Mascot, previously confirmed by the United States Senate to the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit, has now received her presidential judicial commission — the final administrative prerequisite before she may be sworn in and take the bench. With the commission in hand, Judge-designate Mascot requires only the oath of office before she may participate as a fully seated circuit judge. This development carries immediate and profound significance for the Second Amendment because the Third Circuit is scheduled to hear an en banc oral argument on October 15, 2025, in **Cheeseman v. Platkin** and the consolidated **Association of New Jersey Rifle & Pistol Clubs v. Platkin**, which challenge New Jersey's ban on AR-15-type semi-automatic rifles, its ban on ammunition magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds, and related firearms restrictions.

## Background

The United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit has jurisdiction over federal appeals arising from New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware. Among its pending matters is a consequential en banc challenge to several of New Jersey's most restrictive firearms laws, including a categorical prohibition on so-called "assault weapons" — specifically semi-automatic rifles such as the AR-15 — and a ban on standard-capacity detachable magazines holding more than ten rounds of ammunition.

**Cheeseman v. Platkin** and the associated **Association of New Jersey Rifle & Pistol Clubs v. Platkin** cases arose after the Supreme Court's landmark decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022) recast the analytical framework governing Second Amendment challenges. Under **Bruen**, firearms regulations must be evaluated against the historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States, rather than through a means-ends balancing test. New Jersey's bans face serious constitutional scrutiny under this standard given that semi-automatic rifles like the AR-15 are among the most commonly owned firearms in the nation and are therefore presumptively protected arms under the "in common use" doctrine articulated in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008).

President Trump nominated Professor Jennifer Mascot, a respected constitutional law scholar and Second Amendment expert, to fill a vacancy on the Third Circuit. The Senate confirmed her nomination by a vote of 50 to 47. As I explained at the time of her Senate confirmation, confirmation alone does not constitute assumption of judicial office. Under the constitutional framework, a newly confirmed federal judge must additionally receive a signed judicial commission from the President of the United States and must then be administered the oath of office. Only upon completion of all three steps — confirmation, commission, and oath — does a nominee become a duly seated Article III judge empowered to exercise the judicial power of the United States.

## The Significance of the Presidential Commission

The presidential commission is the formal instrument through which the executive branch attests that a confirmed individual has been duly nominated and confirmed and is authorized to exercise federal judicial power. It bears the President's signature and the seal of the United States. The commission is a constitutionally significant document: it is the instrument the Supreme Court examined in **Marbury v. Madison** (1803), the foundational case establishing the principle of judicial review. That a commission mattered enough to serve as the focal point of **Marbury** underscores its constitutional weight.

With the presidential commission now confirmed to have been issued to Professor Mascot, the remaining prerequisite is the oath of office. The oath may be administered privately and promptly. The timing is critical: the en banc oral argument in **Cheeseman** is scheduled for the morning of October 15, 2025, in Philadelphia. If Judge Mascot is sworn in prior to the 9:30 a.m. commencement of that argument, she will be eligible to sit on the full Third Circuit panel for that hearing.

## Implications for the Cheeseman Oral Argument

The composition of the panel hearing a case can be outcome-determinative. An en banc proceeding before the full Third Circuit assigns every active judge of the court a vote on the outcome. Judge Mascot's addition to the court changes the composition of that bench in ways that are likely to be favorable to a faithful application of **Bruen** and **Heller**.

As a constitutional law scholar with demonstrated expertise in the Second Amendment, Judge Mascot is well-positioned to assess the arguments advanced on behalf of New Jersey's firearms restrictions and to bring rigorous originalist and textual analysis to the court's deliberations. Her presence on the panel for the October 15 argument represents a meaningful development for Second Amendment advocates and for the rule of law.

## The DOJ's Second Amendment Task Force

Separate from the Mascot confirmation development, the Department of Justice's Civil Rights Division — under the leadership of Assistant Attorney General Harlan Dillon — has continued to develop and publicly articulate the administration's posture with respect to Second Amendment litigation. The DOJ has made clear that it regards itself as a proponent of the constitutional right to keep and bear arms. This posture is relevant to Third Circuit litigation insofar as the federal government may file statements of interest or amicus briefs in cases challenging state firearms restrictions.

## Implications

The issuance of Judge Mascot's presidential commission, immediately preceding the en banc oral argument in **Cheeseman v. Platkin**, is a potentially pivotal development in the ongoing constitutional litigation over New Jersey's firearms restrictions. New Jersey's AR-15 ban and its magazine capacity restrictions represent some of the most aggressive state-level gun control measures in the country. Under the **Bruen** historical tradition test, those restrictions face a demanding standard of constitutional justification.

The timing of this judicial appointment — literally on the eve of the oral argument — reflects the Trump administration's commitment to seating originalist jurists in circuit courts where major Second Amendment cases are pending. For the millions of law-abiding gun owners in New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware who are subject to the Third Circuit's jurisdiction, the seating of Judge Mascot is a development of immediate practical consequence. How the Third Circuit ultimately rules on New Jersey's bans will establish binding precedent for the tri-state region and will likely contribute to the circuit split that the United States Supreme Court will eventually need to resolve.
