## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit, sitting in Chicago, convened a three-judge panel to hear oral argument in **Barnett v. Raoul** and consolidated cases challenging Illinois's Protect Illinois Communities Act (PICA) — the state's comprehensive ban on semi-automatic firearms and standard-capacity magazines. In a development of historic significance for Second Amendment litigation, the Department of Justice's Civil Rights Division, represented by Division head Harmeet Dhillon, appeared before the court and argued affirmatively that PICA violates the Second Amendment and must be struck down. The argument arose in the context of reviewing United States District Judge Steven McGlynn's thorough trial-level opinion holding PICA unconstitutional, a ruling that represents one of the most carefully developed factual records in any Second Amendment case to date.

## Background

Illinois's Protect Illinois Communities Act represents one of the most sweeping firearms restrictions enacted by any state in the post-**Bruen** era. The Act bans the possession of a broad category of semi-automatic firearms described as "assault weapons" — including the AR-15, the most widely owned rifle in the United States — as well as standard-capacity magazines, which the Act labels "large-capacity magazines."

The procedural history of the challenges to PICA is instructive and technically complex. Following the Supreme Court's decision in **Bruen** (2022), which replaced the prior means-ends balancing framework with a text, history, and tradition analysis, the Seventh Circuit confronted the question of how to analyze the constitutional challenges to PICA under the new standard. In its prior consideration of related cases at the preliminary injunction stage, the Seventh Circuit had adopted what became known as the **Bevis** standard — a framework that, rather than directly applying the **Bruen** analysis, asked a series of factual questions about whether the banned weapons were "particularly dangerous" or "like" weapons used in military service, among other inquiries.

That framework was widely criticized as insufficiently consistent with **Heller** and **Bruen**, but the Seventh Circuit declined, at that stage, to revisit it. Instead, the court remanded the cases to United States District Judge Steven McGlynn of the Southern District of Illinois, directing him to develop a comprehensive factual record addressing the questions posed by the **Bevis** framework.

## Judge McGlynn's Trial and Opinion

Judge McGlynn's approach to the remand was thorough and, in my view, exemplary. He conducted what amounted to a full trial on the factual questions posed by the Seventh Circuit — an unusual procedure in Second Amendment cases, which are typically resolved on the basis of legal briefs and do not require evidentiary hearings. The trial produced an extensive record addressing the historical, technical, and empirical questions raised by the **Bevis** framework.

Following the trial, Judge McGlynn issued a comprehensive opinion in which he held that "considering all of the evidence presented, the court holds that the provisions of PICA criminalizing the knowing possession of" the prohibited weapons are unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. His factual findings addressed, among other things, the widespread civilian ownership of AR-15s and similar semi-automatic rifles, the lawful purposes for which they are used, and the absence of any historical tradition of banning such weapons in the United States.

Judge McGlynn's findings rest on a factual record compiled through an actual trial — a record that is entitled to deference at the appellate level under the clear-error standard applicable to findings of fact. This procedural posture makes the government's task on appeal considerably more difficult than it would be in a purely legal challenge, because the Seventh Circuit must accept Judge McGlynn's factual findings unless they are clearly erroneous.

## The DOJ's Oral Argument

The appearance of Harmeet Dhillon of the DOJ Civil Rights Division before the Seventh Circuit three-judge panel represents a watershed moment in Second Amendment litigation. For the first time in the history of the republic, the United States Department of Justice appeared before a federal appellate court and argued that a state law banning semi-automatic rifles such as the AR-15 is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment.

Ms. Dhillon argued that PICA violates the Second Amendment because the AR-15 and similar semi-automatic rifles are in common use by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes — the constitutional standard recognized in **Heller** — and because there is no historical tradition of banning such commonly owned weapons that would satisfy the **Bruen** test. The DOJ's position directly contravenes the government of Illinois, which argued that PICA's prohibitions fall within a historical tradition of regulating dangerous weapons.

The significance of the DOJ's intervention cannot be overstated. The Solicitor General's office and the Civil Rights Division represent the most experienced and influential advocates in the federal court system. Their affirmative support for the Second Amendment position signals to the Seventh Circuit — and to every other federal court considering similar challenges — that the constitutional question is not a close one, and that the weight of federal executive authority falls on the side of protecting the right to keep and bear commonly owned semi-automatic rifles.

## Appellate Analysis and Expected Outcome

The three-judge panel's composition and the questions posed during oral argument will be the subject of careful scrutiny by Second Amendment advocates and scholars. The core legal question before the panel is whether Judge McGlynn's factual findings — that AR-15s and similar rifles are in common use, that there is no historical tradition of banning them, and that PICA's prohibitions are therefore unconstitutional — are clearly erroneous or legally deficient under the **Bevis** framework.

I am of the view that Judge McGlynn's opinion is well-grounded in both the factual record and the applicable law. The **Heller** test for what weapons are protected by the Second Amendment — specifically, the "in common use" test — is straightforwardly satisfied by the AR-15, which is owned by tens of millions of law-abiding Americans. Under **Bruen**, the government bears the burden of demonstrating a historical tradition of analogous regulation, and no such tradition exists for banning semi-automatic firearms that are in widespread civilian ownership.

## Implications

The Seventh Circuit's decision in **Barnett v. Raoul** will have immediate practical consequences for the validity of PICA, but its significance extends far beyond Illinois. The decision will serve as the primary circuit court precedent on assault weapons bans in the Seventh Circuit, will influence the analysis in related cases in the Third Circuit (**Cheeseman v. Platkin**) and elsewhere, and will be a central factor in the Supreme Court's consideration of whether to grant certiorari in pending petitions challenging such bans.

The DOJ's argument in this case, combined with its amicus filings in other circuits, has constructed a powerful and coherent executive branch position in support of AR-15 rights that I expect will be persuasive both in the circuit courts and, ultimately, before the Supreme Court. The federal government's commitment to this position — demonstrated through actual oral argument by the head of the Civil Rights Division — represents a qualitative shift in the trajectory of Second Amendment litigation that augurs well for the protection of this fundamental right.
