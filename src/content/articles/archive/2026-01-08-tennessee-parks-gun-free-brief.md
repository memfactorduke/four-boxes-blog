## Summary

The state of Tennessee has filed an appellate brief in the Tennessee Court of Appeals in **Hughes v. Lee**, seeking to reverse a Chancery Court declaratory judgment that found unconstitutional, among other provisions, Tennessee's prohibition on carrying handguns in public parks by individuals who do not possess a valid concealed carry license. In my assessment, Tennessee's brief contains significant analytical errors in its application of the "sensitive places" doctrine established by the Supreme Court in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). The brief's core historical argument — that no public parks existed until after the Civil War, and that post-Civil War gun bans in parks therefore establish a valid tradition — is both factually inaccurate and legally insufficient under **Bruen**'s demanding historical standard.

## Background

### The Case: Hughes v. Lee

**Hughes v. Lee** is a challenge to multiple aspects of Tennessee's firearms carry regulations, brought by private plaintiffs with the support of Gun Owners of America. The lead plaintiff, Steven Hughes, challenged, among other provisions, Tennessee's law prohibiting the carrying of handguns in public parks by persons who do not hold a valid concealed carry license (or, under Tennessee's permit-less carry law, by persons who otherwise would not be entitled to carry).

The Tennessee Chancery Court — the trial court of first impression — issued a declaratory judgment finding that the public parks ban, as applied, violated both the Second Amendment to the United States Constitution and the analogous provision of the Tennessee Constitution. The court's ruling was a significant Second Amendment victory.

The state of Tennessee, through its Attorney General, appealed. The appeal is now pending before the Tennessee Court of Appeals in Jackson. Tennessee's appellate brief argues that the Chancery Court erred in striking down the parks ban.

### The Tennessee Constitutional Dimension

Tennessee's constitution contains its own right-to-bear-arms provision. Under Tennessee precedent, courts have interpreted the Tennessee provision as coterminous with the federal Second Amendment — that is, the scope of protection afforded by the state constitutional provision is the same as that afforded by the federal Second Amendment. As a consequence, the analysis under both provisions rises and falls together: if the Second Amendment permits the parks ban, the Tennessee constitution does as well, and vice versa.

## Tennessee's "Sensitive Places" Argument

### The Sensitive Places Doctrine Under Bruen

In **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), the Supreme Court noted, in dicta, that "nothing in our opinion should be taken to cast doubt on prohibitions on the carrying of firearms in sensitive places such as schools and government buildings." The Court did not define the outer limits of the sensitive places category.

In **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the sensitive places question arose directly because New York argued that the entire island of Manhattan constituted a "sensitive place" in which concealed carry could be banned. The Court firmly rejected this expansive reading. Justice Thomas's majority opinion made clear that the sensitive places doctrine must be confined to locations with genuine historical analogs — the Court's examples of "sensitive places" were limited to legislatures, polling places, courthouses, and similar governmental venues. The Court was explicit that simply declaring a location sensitive does not make it so; the government must identify a historical tradition of restricting arms in that particular type of place.

### Tennessee's Historical Argument: Parks Did Not Exist Before the Civil War

Tennessee's brief advances the proposition that public parks, as an institution, did not meaningfully exist in America until after the Civil War. On this view, post-Civil War park regulations — including regulations prohibiting firearms in parks — provide the relevant historical tradition supporting the current parks ban.

This argument is legally and historically insufficient for at least two reasons.

**First**, post-Civil War regulations come too late to establish the founding-era tradition that **Bruen** requires. **Bruen** anchors the historical analysis at the founding era — the period of the Constitution's ratification in 1788 and the Bill of Rights in 1791 — with Reconstruction-era evidence serving only as supplementary evidence, not as the primary basis for establishing a tradition. Laws enacted after the Civil War, standing alone, cannot establish the historical tradition that **Bruen** demands when those laws have no analog in the founding era.

**Second**, and more fundamentally, Tennessee's premise that public parks did not exist at the founding is factually incorrect. Public commons, greens, and open public spaces existed throughout the colonial and founding period. Thomas Jefferson wrote about parks as civic spaces. The Boston Common — one of the oldest public open spaces in America, established in 1634 — functioned as a public gathering ground well before the founding. Most significantly, the Battle of Lexington began on Lexington Green — an eighteenth-century public common that was, in every meaningful sense, a park: an open, publicly shared green space used for community gatherings, recreational activities, and civic events.

If public parks, commons, and greens existed at the founding — and they did — then the question **Bruen** demands we ask is whether founding-era governments restricted firearms in those spaces. The historical evidence does not support any such tradition. Founding-era Americans routinely carried firearms in public, including in public open spaces, as a matter of ordinary life. There is no founding-era body of law restricting the carry of firearms in parks or public commons.

### The Erroneous Conflation of Parks with Schools and Government Buildings

Tennessee's brief attempts to leverage **Heller**'s reference to schools and government buildings as "sensitive places" to support the parks ban. But this conflation is analytically unwarranted.

Schools and government buildings occupy a distinct place in the sensitive places doctrine because they were specifically identified — if only in dicta — in **Heller**, and because they share common characteristics that distinguish them from public parks: they are enclosed, government-controlled environments with defined entry and exit points, formal administrative control, and specific governmental or educational functions. The Supreme Court's acknowledgment in **Heller** that "sensitive places" may include schools and government buildings was not an open-ended license to expand the category to any space where the government chooses to prohibit firearms.

Public parks are fundamentally different. They are open spaces. They are typically not enclosed. Entry is generally unrestricted. They are places where the public gathers for recreation, exercise, family activities, and civic events — activities that are entirely compatible with the peaceful exercise of Second Amendment rights. There is no basis in **Bruen**'s historical methodology for treating a neighborhood park as equivalent to a courthouse or legislative chamber.

As **Bruen** made clear, the government cannot simply invoke the label "sensitive place" and escape constitutional scrutiny. The label is not self-executing. The government must identify an actual historical tradition supporting the restriction in that particular type of location.

## The Broader Pattern: Tennessee's Analytical Missteps

### The Timing Problem

Perhaps the most fundamental error in Tennessee's brief is its reliance on post-Civil War analogies as if they were founding-era evidence. **Bruen** places the historical inquiry primarily at the founding, with Reconstruction era evidence playing a secondary role. Post-Civil War park regulations — even assuming they exist in the numbers Tennessee's brief suggests — cannot substitute for founding-era evidence of firearms restrictions in public open spaces.

The legal standard is clear: a regulation's constitutionality under the Second Amendment turns on whether a sufficiently similar regulation existed at the founding. Post-Civil War regulations are not founding-era evidence. If no founding-era analog exists — and none does, for restrictions on firearms in public parks — the modern regulation cannot stand.

### The Constitutional Significance of Allowing Unlicensed Carry in Parks

The specific provision at issue — the prohibition on park carry by individuals without a concealed carry license — also raises a distinct Second Amendment concern. **Bruen** struck down New York's "proper cause" requirement precisely because it conditioned the exercise of a fundamental constitutional right on a showing of particularized need. Requiring a government-issued license as a prerequisite to exercising a constitutional right in a public space is presumptively suspect, and the state must justify the conditioning of the right on licensure by reference to historical tradition.

There is no founding-era tradition of requiring government permits before carrying firearms in public parks. The permit requirement compounds the constitutional infirmity of the parks ban.

## Implications

### A Disappointment, Not a Defeat

It is genuinely disappointing that Tennessee — a state that presents itself as strongly pro-Second Amendment — has filed an appellate brief that misapplies **Bruen** and seeks to overturn a Second Amendment victory. The right result in **Hughes v. Lee** is for the Court of Appeals to affirm the Chancery Court's declaratory judgment. If the Tennessee Court of Appeals applies **Bruen** correctly, it will find no founding-era tradition supporting firearms restrictions in public parks and will affirm.

### The Importance of Getting the Bruen Analysis Right

This case illustrates a broader point that I have emphasized repeatedly: the quality of Second Amendment advocacy — including the quality of Second Amendment-related briefs filed by purportedly sympathetic state governments — matters enormously. An incorrect historical analysis, even one filed by a friendly state, creates bad precedent if courts accept it. The Second Amendment community must be vigilant in identifying and correcting analytical errors, even when those errors come from nominally allied sources.

### What the Courts Should Do

The Tennessee Court of Appeals should carefully apply **Bruen**'s text-history-tradition standard. The relevant question is simple: did founding-era governments restrict the carrying of firearms in public open spaces such as parks, commons, and greens? The historical record does not support an affirmative answer. The Chancery Court reached the correct conclusion, and that conclusion should be affirmed.
