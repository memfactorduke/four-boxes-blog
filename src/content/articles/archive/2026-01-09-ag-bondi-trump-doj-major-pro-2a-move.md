## Summary

The United States Department of Justice under President Donald Trump and Attorney General Pam Bondi has filed a significant amicus brief in the Ninth Circuit's en banc proceeding in **Kim Rhode v. Rob Bonta**, challenging California's novel, first-of-its-kind ammunition background check regime as an unconstitutional infringement of the Second Amendment. The DOJ's intervention — the first time in living memory that the federal government has filed a pro-Second Amendment brief against California's gun control laws — represents a watershed moment in the federal government's approach to Second Amendment litigation and signals a clear-eyed strategic focus on dismantling the most egregious outlier gun regulations in the nation.

## Background

### California's Ammunition Background Check Law

California is the first state in American history to require an in-person background check before every ammunition purchase. The regime, codified in California Penal Code §§ 30352 and 30370, provides four distinct avenues for completing the required background check. Each avenue involves a fee ranging from $1 to $31 per transaction. Each involves its own inherent and unpredictable delays — from minutes to days or more — and each authorization is one-time use only, meaning a California resident must submit to a new background check every time she purchases ammunition, regardless of how recently she completed one.

The most commonly used method — the "standard" check — clears a purchaser to acquire ammunition only within a fraction of transactions, because the system cross-references purchaser information against various databases and generates denials in a meaningful percentage of cases even for law-abiding citizens who are legally entitled to purchase ammunition. The system has been characterized by the lower courts, and now by the DOJ's brief, using terms including "unnecessarily complicated," "onerous and convoluted," "cumbersome," "extensive," "unwieldy," and "first of its kind." As the DOJ brief aptly states: California's regime has "earned every one of those labels."

### The Litigation History: Rhode v. Bonta

**Kim Rhode**, a multiple Olympic gold medalist in competitive shooting, brought this challenge as a lead plaintiff. The district court initially ruled in favor of the Second Amendment plaintiffs, finding the ammunition background check regime unconstitutional. A three-judge Ninth Circuit panel affirmed that ruling. As is characteristic of the Ninth Circuit whenever the Second Amendment prevails at the panel level, the full court voted to rehear the case en banc — effectively seeking to overturn the panel decision through the weight of the full circuit's liberal majority.

It is against this backdrop — a Second Amendment ruling that survived the trial court and a three-judge panel, now facing a hostile en banc court — that the Trump-Bondi DOJ has filed its amicus brief.

## The DOJ's Brief: Key Arguments

### California's Regime Is Novel and Without Historical Analog

The DOJ brief hammers home two strategic points that are carefully calibrated to the Supreme Court's **Bruen** framework. The first is novelty: California's point-of-sale ammunition background check system is entirely unprecedented in American history. No other state has ever imposed such a requirement. No founding-era, antebellum, or Reconstruction-era jurisdiction imposed analogous restrictions on the acquisition of ammunition.

Under **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the government bears the burden of demonstrating that a challenged firearms regulation is "consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation." Where a regulation is novel — where no historical analog exists — that burden cannot be met. The DOJ's brief identifies California's regime as precisely such a novel, historically unmoored restriction.

### The Outlier Problem

The second strategic pillar of the DOJ's brief is the "outlier" characterization. California is the only state in the country to impose this type of ammunition background check. Every other state has declined to enact a comparable regime. The DOJ brief emphasizes this repeatedly: this is an outlier law, an outlier regulation, an outlier jurisdiction.

The significance of this framing for Supreme Court purposes is substantial. The Supreme Court has historically been skeptical of laws that represent radical departures from the national consensus. When a single jurisdiction imposes a restriction that no other jurisdiction has seen fit to impose, the Court is far less likely to conclude that such a restriction reflects a deeply rooted historical tradition. The outlier character of California's ammunition regime thus compounds the already-fatal absence of any founding-era analog.

### Ammunition Is Protected by the Second Amendment

The DOJ's brief makes the affirmative argument that ammunition purchases fall within the scope of the Second Amendment's protection, even though the word "ammunition" does not appear in the constitutional text. The reasoning is straightforward and analytically compelling: a firearm without ammunition is a useless piece of metal. The right to keep and bear arms necessarily implies the right to possess the means necessary to exercise that right effectively. A regulation that makes ammunition practically difficult or impossibly expensive to acquire burdens the Second Amendment right as surely as a regulation on the firearm itself.

The DOJ argues that under **Bruen**'s plain-text analysis, the threshold question — whether the challenged regulation "implicates the Second Amendment's plain text" — must be answered in the affirmative whenever a regulation restricts access to ammunition. Once that threshold is crossed, the burden shifts to California to demonstrate historical tradition, and that tradition does not exist.

### Burdens on Law-Abiding Citizens

The DOJ's brief also addresses the practical burden of California's ammunition background check system on law-abiding citizens. The system generates delays that are inherently unpredictable — a citizen might clear the check in minutes or might wait days. Denials fall on a non-trivial percentage of law-abiding applicants due to database errors, name-matching errors, and other administrative failures that are entirely unrelated to the purchaser's legal eligibility. Each denial or delay imposes a real cost on a citizen's ability to maintain adequate ammunition for self-defense, training, and lawful recreational use.

Under **Bruen**, these practical burdens on law-abiding citizens are not mere inconveniences to be weighed against claimed government interests in a balancing test. **Bruen** expressly rejected the interest-balancing framework. The only question is whether the historical tradition supports such a restriction — and it does not.

## Strategic Significance

### The First Federal Intervention Against California

As a strategic matter, the DOJ's brief in **Rhode v. Bonta** marks the first time — in my living memory, and perhaps in history — that the federal government has filed in support of Second Amendment rights against California's gun control regime. Prior administrations of both parties consistently deferred to California's legislative judgments, either supporting California's regulations or staying silent. The Trump-Bondi DOJ has broken decisively with that tradition.

This matters not only for this case but as a signal of strategic intent. The DOJ has made clear that it is not merely interested in defending existing federal law; it is actively seeking to dismantle the most aggressive and unconstitutional state gun restrictions. California, New Jersey, Illinois — the states that have most aggressively exceeded constitutional limits on firearms regulation — are now facing an adversarial federal government in the courts.

### The En Banc Posture

The Ninth Circuit's decision to rehear this case en banc — a decision made precisely because the Second Amendment won at the panel level — illustrates a pattern that Second Amendment practitioners know well: the Ninth Circuit, dominated by liberal appointees, reliably votes to rehear cases in which the Second Amendment prevails before a three-judge panel, in an effort to reverse favorable rulings. The DOJ's intervention makes this task significantly harder. An en banc panel must contend not only with the arguments of private litigants but with the formal position of the United States government — a party whose views command respect in federal appellate proceedings.

### The Path to the Supreme Court

If the en banc Ninth Circuit reverses the panel decision and upholds California's ammunition background check regime, the case is positioned for Supreme Court review. Given that the Second Amendment plaintiffs prevailed at both the district court and panel levels, and given the DOJ's formal endorsement of their constitutional arguments, a petition for certiorari would present an exceptionally strong vehicle for the Supreme Court to address the constitutionality of ammunition regulations under **Bruen**.

I submit that this is precisely the kind of case the Supreme Court should take: a clear-cut challenge to a novel, historically unmoored, outlier regulation that has already produced pro-Second Amendment rulings at two levels, and in which the federal government now formally agrees that the challenged law is unconstitutional.

## Implications

### The Bruen Standard in Practice

The DOJ's brief in **Rhode v. Bonta** demonstrates, in practice, how **Bruen**'s text-history-tradition framework operates as a powerful tool for challenging novel gun regulations. By framing the inquiry as a historical question — does any analog exist? — **Bruen** forces the government to defend its regulations on historical grounds. Novel regulations, by definition, cannot be defended on such grounds. California's ammunition background check regime is novel in the most complete sense: no other state has tried it, and no historical jurisdiction ever imposed anything like it.

### Ammunition as the Next Frontier

In my assessment, the DOJ's formal position that ammunition purchases are protected by the Second Amendment opens an important new front in Second Amendment litigation. Regulations that target ammunition as a de facto mechanism for rendering firearms useless — whether through background checks, per-round fees, or outright bans on certain types of ammunition — are now squarely within the scope of constitutional challenge. California's regime is the most obvious target, but the principle extends broadly.

### Wars Are Won Battle by Battle

The Trump-Bondi DOJ's intervention in **Rhode v. Bonta** is, as I see it, another skirmish in the long campaign to vindicate the Second Amendment as a co-equal constitutional right. Wars are won by accumulating victories, battle by battle, case by case. The DOJ's willingness to engage as an affirmative advocate for the Second Amendment — filing briefs, arguing for strict application of **Bruen**, and targeting the most egregious state gun control regimes — represents precisely the kind of sustained, strategic effort that is necessary to build enduring constitutional protection for the right to keep and bear arms.
