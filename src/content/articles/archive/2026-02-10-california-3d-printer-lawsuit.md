## Summary

California Attorney General Rob Bonta, together with San Francisco City Attorney David Chiu, has filed a lawsuit in San Francisco County Superior Court against Gatalog Foundation, Inc. and Control Pew LLC, alleging that these Florida-based organizations unlawfully distributed computer code enabling the 3D printing of ghost guns, machine gun conversion devices, and high-capacity magazines. In my analysis, this lawsuit is meritless on multiple constitutional and statutory grounds — it implicates both the Second Amendment right to personally manufacture firearms for lawful purposes and serious First Amendment concerns about the regulation of computer code as protected speech — but it is nonetheless a significant instrument of lawfare that will impose substantial litigation costs on the defendants regardless of its ultimate legal merit.

## Background

The personal manufacture of firearms has been a recognized and lawful practice in the United States since before the founding. An individual who personally manufactures a firearm for his own use — not for sale or distribution — has never historically been required to serialize that firearm, register it with any government entity, or obtain a manufacturing license. This practice predates the National Firearms Act of 1934, predates the Gun Control Act of 1968, and predates the modern federal regulatory framework for commercial firearms dealers and manufacturers. It is, in short, deeply rooted in the American legal and constitutional tradition.

Modern 3D printing technology has made the personal manufacture of certain firearm components more accessible. An individual with access to a 3D printer and the appropriate digital design files can produce certain firearm frames, receivers, or other components. Gatalog Foundation and Control Pew LLC allegedly operated websites making such design files available for download. The organizations and their operators appear to be based in Florida, where their websites were accessible globally via the internet.

California has enacted legislation purporting to prohibit various aspects of ghost-gun manufacture and possession, and Attorney General Bonta has framed the lawsuit as enforcement of those state laws. The San Francisco City Attorney joined the action, and both officials characterized it in their press statements as a "landmark" or "trailblazing" enforcement action against the ghost gun industry.

## The Legal Claims and Their Constitutional Vulnerabilities

Having reviewed the complaint, I find its legal foundations substantially deficient on multiple grounds.

**Second Amendment Dimension.** The right to personally manufacture a firearm for lawful purposes is an aspect of the Second Amendment right recognized in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and extended and refined in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). Heller held that the Second Amendment protects the individual right to keep and bear arms for traditionally lawful purposes, including self-defense. The personal manufacture of a firearm for self-defense is a traditionally lawful purpose with deep historical roots.

Under the Bruen framework, California bears the burden of demonstrating that its prohibition on personally manufactured firearms — and the facilitation of such manufacture through the provision of design information — is consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation in America as of 1791. No such historical tradition exists. The founding era is replete with evidence of individuals personally crafting and manufacturing their own arms; indeed, the very concept of a government-required serial number on a personally manufactured firearm would have been alien to the founding generation.

California's complaint, by targeting the distribution of design information enabling personal manufacture, reaches conduct even further removed from the commercial firearms market that has historically been the subject of regulatory attention. The state is not merely regulating the sale of finished firearms; it is attempting to prohibit the dissemination of information about how to make them. That extension of regulatory authority cannot be reconciled with either the historical tradition of personal manufacture or the constitutional framework Heller and Bruen established.

**First Amendment Dimension.** Computer code is speech. The Ninth Circuit recognized this principle in the context of encryption software, and the principle applies equally to computer-aided design files for 3D printing. The government may regulate the conduct of manufacturing; it has a far more difficult First Amendment case when it attempts to prohibit the dissemination of information about how manufacturing is accomplished.

California's lawsuit effectively asks the court to hold that distributing digital design files — information — is unlawful because of what some recipients might do with that information. That theory is a direct collision with First Amendment principles about the regulation of information based on its potential downstream harmful uses. The Supreme Court has repeatedly and firmly held that the First Amendment protects speech that some recipients might use for harmful purposes. The remedy for harmful uses of information is regulation of the harmful conduct itself, not a prior restraint on the information.

**Jurisdictional and Choice-of-Law Issues.** The defendants appear to be based in Florida. Their websites were accessible globally. California's laws apply within California; they do not extend as a general matter to websites operated from other states that happen to be accessible by California residents. The state's theory would effectively allow California to impose its regulatory preferences on digital content providers located anywhere in the nation — or the world — on the theory that their content can be accessed within California's borders. That theory has profound implications for internet commerce and digital speech that the courts have not endorsed and should not endorse.

**The Lawfare Purpose.** It is worth being candid about what this lawsuit is really designed to accomplish. California's complaint is not a serious attempt to vindicate a clearly established legal right. It is an attempt to impose litigation costs — legal fees, discovery burdens, reputational harm, and business disruption — on individuals and organizations engaged in lawful activity that California's political establishment opposes on ideological grounds. The lawsuit will be filed in San Francisco Superior Court, before California judges with California values, and even if the defendants ultimately prevail, they will bear substantial costs in the process. That is the design.

This is lawfare in its most precise sense: the weaponization of legal process not to vindicate rights but to punish political opponents for exercising them. It is a tactic that the Second Amendment community should recognize, document, and resist — both by defending the specific defendants and by pursuing legislative and litigation strategies that increase the costs to states that engage in frivolous constitutional litigation.

## The "Ghost Gun" Framing

The term "ghost gun" is a rhetorically effective but legally meaningless label. A firearm that lacks a commercial serial number is not inherently illegal, dangerous, or untraceable in any way that distinguishes it from a serialized commercial firearm in terms of the harm it can cause. Criminals who wish to obtain firearms for use in crime can do so through theft, straw purchases, and the black market, all of which predate 3D printing by decades. The "ghost gun" narrative is a means of framing lawful personal manufacture as categorically criminal in the public imagination, creating political pressure for legislation that could not survive careful constitutional analysis if presented on its actual terms.

California's framing of Gatalog's alleged activities as "facilitating the illegal manufacture" of firearms begs the central legal question, which is whether personal manufacture of firearms is in fact illegal under California law and, more fundamentally, whether California's law is itself constitutional. The complaint assumes the constitutional conclusion it should be obligated to prove.

## Implications

**People of California v. Gatalog Foundation** is a legally meritless but practically significant lawsuit. It will impose costs on the defendants, generate attention and resources for California's anti-gun political agenda, and create the appearance of law enforcement activity around a class of conduct — personal firearm manufacture — that is constitutionally protected and historically rooted.

The defendants will have strong Second Amendment and First Amendment defenses available to them. The constitutional arguments against California's ghost-gun regulatory scheme, and against the theory that distributing 3D-printing design files can be prohibited consistent with the First Amendment's protection of speech, are substantial. But these defenses must be asserted and litigated, and that process will impose real costs.

For the broader Second Amendment community, the California lawsuit is a reminder that the battleground for constitutional rights extends beyond federal litigation. State courts, state regulatory proceedings, and state enforcement actions are all fronts on which the right to keep and bear arms must be defended — even when, as here, the state's legal theory is constitutionally infirm and its primary purpose appears to be the infliction of costs on ideological opponents rather than the vindication of law.
