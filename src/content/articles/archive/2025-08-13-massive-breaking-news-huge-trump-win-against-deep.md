## Summary

On August 13, 2025, the United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit issued a significant 2-1 ruling in favor of President Donald J. Trump and the Department of Government Efficiency, reversing a lower federal district court decision that had sought to block DOGE personnel from accessing federal agency databases containing information about government employees. The case arose from a lawsuit filed by the American Federation of Teachers and other plaintiffs, who alleged that federal workers possessed cognizable privacy rights that prevented President Trump's DOGE team — operating under the direction of Treasury Secretary Scott Bessant — from reviewing personnel and financial data held in executive branch agency systems. The Fourth Circuit's decision, grounded in part on controlling Supreme Court precedent involving the Social Security Administration, represents a significant legal victory for the principle of presidential authority over the executive branch and provides further confirmation that courts will apply prior Supreme Court rulings as building blocks in the broader project of reasserting executive control over the federal bureaucracy.

## Background

The Department of Government Efficiency was established by President Trump as an advisory body charged with identifying inefficiencies, redundant expenditures, and administrative waste throughout the federal government. Working under the direction of Secretary Bessant at the Department of the Treasury, DOGE personnel sought access to the information technology systems, personnel databases, and financial records maintained by various federal agencies, including the Department of Education and the Office of Personnel Management. The stated purpose was to examine compensation structures, staffing levels, and agency operations as a predicate to recommending workforce reductions and cost savings.

The American Federation of Teachers, together with other plaintiffs, filed a lawsuit in the United States District Court — based in the circuit that encompasses Virginia and Maryland, directly proximate to the seat of federal government — arguing that federal employees enjoyed privacy rights in the personal and financial data maintained in agency systems. The district court agreed, ruling that the DOGE team's access to this information was unlawful and entering an order blocking such access. That ruling was appealed to the Fourth Circuit.

## The Fourth Circuit's Decision

The Fourth Circuit reversed the district court by a vote of 2-1. The majority opinion rests on a straightforward proposition that, when stated plainly, reveals the implausibility of the plaintiffs' position: the President of the United States, as the constitutional head of the executive branch and the ultimate employer of all federal executive employees, possesses the authority to direct other executive branch personnel to review the employment data of his own workforce for purposes of evaluating the efficiency and performance of federal operations.

A critical component of the Fourth Circuit's reasoning was its application of prior Supreme Court precedent. Earlier in 2025, the Supreme Court had already addressed a related DOGE-access dispute involving the Social Security Administration, ruling that DOGE personnel affiliated with the executive branch could access the Social Security Administration's databases as part of their government efficiency mandate. The Fourth Circuit's majority extended that controlling precedent to the Department of Education, the Office of Personnel Management, and other agencies, concluding that the legal reasoning the Supreme Court had already provided left no room for a contrary result when applied to substantially similar fact patterns across different agencies.

The practical holding is this: the President of the United States — the constitutional Chief Executive who bears ultimate responsibility for the faithful execution of all federal laws and the management of all executive branch operations — is legally entitled to direct his own team of government employees to examine the records of other government employees working within the very branch he leads. That this proposition required litigation at all reflects the extent to which an entrenched federal bureaucracy has sought, through sympathetic lower court judges, to insulate itself from ordinary managerial oversight.

## The Role of Legal Precedent as a Building Block

One of the most instructive aspects of this decision is the manner in which the Fourth Circuit used the Supreme Court's prior Social Security Administration ruling as a legal foundation for its holding. This is precisely the mechanism by which constitutional and legal progress is built — each favorable precedent becomes a brick upon which subsequent favorable decisions are constructed, case by case, circuit by circuit, until the ultimate governing rule is settled at the level of the Supreme Court.

The plaintiffs' argument — that federal employees who work for the president of the United States possess privacy rights that prevent the president from reviewing their employment data — was always a legally tenuous position. The federal government is not a private employer with a closed personnel file. It is a constitutional entity whose entire workforce operates under the direction of a President accountable to the American electorate. The notion that career civil servants could insulate themselves from managerial scrutiny by invoking personal privacy rights against their own constitutional employer contradicts the structure of Article II and the basic logic of hierarchical executive organization.

## The Second Amendment Connection

This ruling, while not directly implicating the Second Amendment, is critically important to the Second Amendment community for reasons that extend beyond its immediate subject matter. The fight to vindicate constitutional rights — including the right to keep and bear arms — takes place within a broader institutional and political environment. A federal administrative state that is bloated, inefficient, and resistant to constitutional accountability is also one that is capable of directing enormous regulatory resources toward the suppression of Second Amendment rights. Agencies like the ATF exercise delegated legislative authority that affects the lives of tens of millions of law-abiding gun owners, and the question of whether those agencies are subject to genuine presidential direction and accountability is not a matter of abstract administrative law — it is a matter of practical consequence for every person who keeps a firearm for lawful purposes.

When courts confirm that the President possesses genuine authority to manage and restructure the executive branch, they are simultaneously confirming that a President committed to the Second Amendment has the tools necessary to rein in agencies that have operated as autonomous regulatory fiefdoms hostile to the constitutional rights of ordinary Americans.

## Implications

The Fourth Circuit's decision in favor of DOGE data access carries several important implications.

First, it establishes a clear circuit court precedent — built on Supreme Court authority — that affirms the president's right to direct executive branch employees to access and review the records of other executive branch employees in furtherance of legitimate management objectives. This will be a significant obstacle to future litigation attempting to use privacy claims as a shield against executive oversight.

Second, the decision reinforces the principle that lower court judges cannot permanently enjoin the President's exercise of clearly established executive authority simply because a sympathetic set of plaintiffs appears before them. The circuit court's willingness to reverse the district court sends a signal that such injunctions will face serious appellate scrutiny.

Third, the ruling contributes to a body of legal precedent that supports the Trump administration's broader project of reducing the size and scope of the federal administrative apparatus. Every legal victory that confirms presidential management authority over the bureaucracy is a precedent that makes the next such victory easier to achieve.
