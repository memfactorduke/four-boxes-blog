## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has issued a six-to-three order in **Trump v. Bole** staying a lower court decision that would have required three commissioners of the Consumer Product Safety Commission to remain in their positions notwithstanding President Trump's decision to remove them from office. The Court's majority held that the stay application is squarely controlled by the Court's earlier decision in **Trump v. Wilcox**, in which the Supreme Court stayed a similar lower court order reinstating members of the National Labor Relations Board and the Merit Systems Protection Board whom President Trump had dismissed. The three dissenting justices — Justice Kagan, joined by Justice Sotomayor and Justice Brown Jackson — would have denied the application. This decision represents a significant and continuing extension of executive authority over so-called independent agencies and reflects the current Court's evident skepticism toward the proposition that Congress may effectively insulate agency officers from presidential removal.

## Background

The Consumer Product Safety Commission is an independent federal regulatory agency created by Congress in the 1970s. Like many independent agencies, the CPSC's enabling statute provides that its commissioners may be removed by the President only "for cause" — that is, only upon a finding of neglect of duty, malfeasance, or similar grounds. This "for cause" removal protection was designed to insulate agency commissioners from removal at the President's will, thereby preserving the agency's independence from direct political control by the executive.

The constitutional justification for for-cause removal protections in independent agencies traces historically to the Supreme Court's 1935 decision in **Humphrey's Executor v. United States**, in which the Court held that Congress could constitutionally restrict the President's power to remove commissioners of multi-member independent agencies exercising quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial functions. For much of the twentieth century, **Humphrey's Executor** served as the principal constitutional basis for the entire architecture of the modern administrative state's independent agencies.

The current Supreme Court has been systematically reconsidering the scope and continued validity of **Humphrey's Executor** in a series of decisions over the past several years. In **Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau** (2020) and **Collins v. Yellen** (2021), the Court significantly curtailed the reach of **Humphrey's Executor** by holding that for-cause removal protections for single-director agencies are constitutionally impermissible. More recently, in **Trump v. Wilcox**, the Court appeared to signal that even multi-member independent agencies may not be fully insulated from presidential removal authority.

## The Underlying Dispute

President Trump removed three commissioners of the Consumer Product Safety Commission who had been appointed by Democratic administrations. The commissioners sought reinstatement in federal court, arguing that the CPSC's for-cause removal statute prohibited their removal absent a showing of cause — and that no such cause had been established. A federal district court in Maryland agreed, ordering the commissioners reinstated. The United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit affirmed that order. The Trump administration sought a stay from the Supreme Court, presenting the question whether the Fourth Circuit's order should be paused pending further proceedings.

## The Supreme Court's Order

The Court's majority granted the stay in a brief order that drew directly on its reasoning in **Trump v. Wilcox**. The key passage from the majority's order reads:

> "The application for stay presented to the Chief Justice and by him referred to the court is granted. The application is squarely controlled by **Trump v. Wilcox**. Although our interim orders are not conclusive as to the merits, they inform how a court should exercise its equitable discretion in cases like this. The stay we issued in **Wilcox** earlier this year reflected our judgment that the government faces greater risk of harm from an order allowing a removed officer to continue exercising the executive power than a wrongly removed officer faces from being unable to perform her statutory duty. The same is true on the facts presented here, where the Consumer Product Safety Commission exercises executive power in a similar manner as the National Labor Relations Board and the case does not otherwise differ from **Wilcox** in any pertinent respect."

The majority's framing is significant. The Court did not merely conclude that the equities favored a stay as a matter of case-specific discretion. Rather, it stated that the application is "squarely controlled" by **Wilcox** — language that suggests the majority views the underlying legal question as essentially settled, at least for purposes of the preliminary stay analysis. The Court further stated that the CPSC "exercises executive power" in a manner similar to the NLRB — a characterization that directly implicates the Article II Vesting Clause's command that the "executive Power" shall be vested in the President.

## The Constitutional Foundation: Article II and the Vesting Clause

Article II, Section 1 of the United States Constitution provides that "the executive Power shall be vested in a President of the United States of America." The Supreme Court in **Seila Law** and its progeny has interpreted this Vesting Clause as conferring on the President a plenary executive power that includes the authority to supervise, direct, and — critically — remove officers exercising executive power on his behalf.

The constitutional problem with independent agencies is structural. If an agency officer exercises executive power — that is, if the agency implements and enforces federal law — then by the terms of the Article II Vesting Clause, that power is vested in the President. Congress cannot, consistent with that constitutional structure, insulate the officer from presidential supervision and removal by creating a statutory "for cause" removal protection. To do so would effectively create a fourth branch of government — independent from the Congress that created it, independent from the courts that review it, and independent from the President in whom the Constitution vests executive authority.

As I have argued for years, this structural problem is not merely a technical constitutional concern. It has profound practical consequences for democratic accountability. When an independent agency officer makes a decision that affects millions of Americans — whether it concerns consumer product safety, labor relations, or financial regulation — the elected President cannot be held accountable by voters for that decision if the President had no power to remove the officer making it. Democratic accountability requires that the officers exercising executive power serve at the will of the President, who in turn is accountable to the people through the electoral process.

## Justice Kagan's Dissent

Justice Kagan, joined by Justice Sotomayor and Justice Brown Jackson, dissented from the stay order. The dissent would have denied the application and allowed the Fourth Circuit's order — which required the reinstatement of the removed CPSC commissioners — to remain in effect pending further proceedings.

The dissent's position reflects a view that **Humphrey's Executor** and the established constitutional framework for independent agencies remain valid and should not be overridden through the mechanism of a preliminary stay application. The dissenters appear to believe that the removal of for-cause protections from independent agency commissioners represents a dramatic and unjustified departure from settled constitutional law that should require full briefing and argument before the merits question is effectively resolved.

That view is understandable as a matter of judicial process, but it is incorrect as a matter of constitutional text. Article II's Vesting Clause is unambiguous. The executive power shall be vested in the President. An officer who exercises executive power and who cannot be removed by the President is an officer whose existence the Constitution does not permit — regardless of how long Congress has been attempting to create such officers, and regardless of how much of the modern administrative state has been built on the assumption that such officers may constitutionally exist.

## Implications

**Trump v. Bole** extends the Supreme Court's emerging doctrine regarding presidential removal authority to yet another independent agency — the Consumer Product Safety Commission. Together with **Trump v. Wilcox** and the Court's earlier decisions in **Seila Law** and **Collins**, this stay order is part of a consistent and accelerating judicial reconsideration of the constitutional basis for the independent agency model.

The practical implications are substantial. Dozens of federal independent agencies are structured with for-cause removal protections similar to those at issue in **Bole**. If the Supreme Court ultimately holds — on the merits rather than in the context of a preliminary stay — that such protections are unconstitutional across the board, the President will acquire the power to remove the commissioners and directors of those agencies at will. That would represent a fundamental realignment of executive branch authority and a significant restructuring of the modern administrative state.

The case will return to the lower courts for merits proceedings while the stay remains in force. President Trump's legal position continues to prevail at every preliminary stage. As the merits of the removal power question are more fully litigated and eventually reach the Supreme Court for a definitive resolution, the trajectory strongly favors a constitutional ruling that for-cause removal protections for officers exercising executive power are incompatible with Article II's Vesting Clause.
