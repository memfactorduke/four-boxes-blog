## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the DC Circuit has issued a landmark ruling in the consolidated cases of **Wilcox v. Trump** and **Harris v. Trump**, affirming President Donald Trump's authority under Article II of the Constitution to remove members of independent multi-member federal boards and agencies without the restrictive "for-cause" limitations that Congress had previously sought to impose. This decision represents a significant victory for the principle of democratic accountability in the executive branch and has direct implications for the long-term protection of Second Amendment rights by ensuring that a duly elected president can staff the federal government with personnel aligned with the mandate given to him by the American people.

## Background

The cases of **Wilcox v. Trump** and **Harris v. Trump** arose from President Trump's decision, upon returning to office, to remove Gwynne Wilcox and Kathy Harris from their positions on two prominent independent multi-member federal boards — the National Labor Relations Board (NLRB) and the Merit Systems Protection Board, respectively. Both individuals had been appointed by Democratic presidents Barack Obama and Joe Biden to fixed multi-year terms. The statutes creating these boards contained provisions specifying that board members could only be removed "for cause" — meaning documented fraud, criminality, or comparable serious misconduct — rather than at the simple discretion of the sitting president.

President Trump's legal position, advanced by his administration through Attorney General Pam Bondi and Solicitor General John Sauer, was straightforward: Article II of the Constitution vests the executive power in the President of the United States, and that constitutional grant of authority necessarily includes the unfettered power to remove executive branch officers who exercise executive power on behalf of the government. Allowing a prior Democratic administration to install officials who could not be displaced by a subsequent Republican president created, in effect, a shadow executive branch operating contrary to the agenda of the elected chief executive.

## The Legal Framework: Humphrey's Executor and Its Legacy

The central legal tension in these cases stems from the Supreme Court's 1935 decision in **Humphrey's Executor v. United States**, which upheld congressional limitations on the president's ability to remove members of the Federal Trade Commission. For nearly nine decades, that precedent was invoked to insulate a growing archipelago of independent federal agencies from presidential control, creating what critics have long characterized as an entrenched administrative apparatus — career officials appointed by Democratic administrations who could not be removed by a Republican successor regardless of how directly their actions obstructed that successor's lawful agenda.

The logic underlying **Humphrey's Executor** rested on a distinction between "purely executive" officers, whom the president could remove at will, and officers exercising "quasi-legislative" or "quasi-judicial" functions, who could be insulated from removal. Over the intervening decades, this distinction proved increasingly difficult to maintain as federal agencies expanded dramatically in size and power. The result was a federal bureaucracy of unelected officials — appointed substantially by Democratic presidents — who could effectively frustrate the policy directives of elected Republican presidents without legal consequence.

## The DC Circuit's Ruling

In a significant decision that the legal and political commentariat had not yet fully absorbed at the time of this writing, the United States Court of Appeals for the DC Circuit ruled in favor of President Trump, holding that he possessed constitutional authority under Article II to remove Wilcox and Harris notwithstanding the for-cause removal protections contained in their enabling statutes. The court's ruling acknowledged the foundational constitutional principle that the president cannot be compelled to retain within the executive branch officers whose policy preferences and actions are directly antithetical to his constitutionally mandated responsibilities.

This ruling is particularly noteworthy given the institutional identity of the court rendering it. The DC Circuit has historically been regarded as the premier venue for administrative law litigation and has, over many decades, developed a jurisprudential tradition broadly sympathetic to the expansion of the administrative state. A ruling from this court affirming presidential removal power therefore carries considerable weight and represents a meaningful departure from the court's historical posture.

## The Connection to Second Amendment Rights

The relationship between presidential removal power and Second Amendment rights may not be immediately apparent, but it is direct and significant. The deep state — the constellation of unelected federal bureaucrats who exercise enormous power over American life — has been one of the primary engines driving anti-gun policy at the federal level. Agencies including the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives (ATF), components of the Department of Justice, and various regulatory bodies have, under Democratic appointees, pursued regulatory agendas that pushed the boundaries of statutory authority and Second Amendment jurisprudence.

When a president committed to the Second Amendment takes office but lacks the practical ability to replace entrenched anti-gun bureaucrats, those bureaucrats can continue implementing and defending anti-gun policies with the coercive resources of the federal government. The ability to staff the executive branch with officers who share the president's constitutional views on the right to keep and bear arms is therefore an indispensable component of any serious effort to protect and vindicate Second Amendment rights at the federal level.

## The Supreme Court Horizon: Seila Law and Beyond

The DC Circuit's ruling in **Wilcox** and **Harris** does not exist in isolation. The Supreme Court's 2020 decision in **Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau** had already begun the process of constraining **Humphrey's Executor** by striking down for-cause removal protections for the CFPB's single director. The logical extension of **Seila Law**'s reasoning — that the president must be able to exercise meaningful control over officers exercising substantial executive power — points toward a broader reconsideration of whether multi-member independent agency heads can constitutionally be shielded from presidential removal.

The current Supreme Court term may well present the opportunity for the Court to substantially limit or overrule **Humphrey's Executor** altogether. The trajectory of the Court's Article II jurisprudence, from **Morrison v. Olson** to **Seila Law** to the present cases, reflects a growing judicial skepticism of congressional attempts to insulate executive officers from presidential control. A definitive ruling in this area would have profound consequences for the balance of power between elected presidents and the unelected administrative state.

## Implications

The DC Circuit's ruling in **Wilcox** and **Harris** is a significant but not final chapter in the broader struggle to restore constitutional accountability to the executive branch. Several implications deserve attention:

**Electoral accountability is restored, at least in part.** When voters elect a president, they are electing someone to implement a particular set of policies across the executive branch. A president who cannot remove officials hostile to his agenda is a president stripped of a core constitutional prerogative. This ruling affirms that democratic elections have consequences within the executive branch.

**Independent agencies face heightened constitutional scrutiny.** The decision signals to multi-member independent boards and commissions across the federal government that their protected-removal status may be legally fragile. This will create appropriate incentives for these agencies to moderate any tendency toward political activism that might otherwise be shielded from accountability.

**The path for Second Amendment advocates is broader than courts.** Winning Second Amendment cases in federal courts is essential, but it is insufficient standing alone. Second Amendment advocates must also win the battles for control of the executive branch — the agencies, the enforcement personnel, and the regulatory posture of the federal government. The legal tools to accomplish this are now somewhat more robust as a result of this ruling.

**The Supreme Court may go further.** The cases of **Wilcox** and **Harris** may ultimately reach the Supreme Court, where the Justices will have an opportunity to clarify — and potentially limit — the continuing vitality of **Humphrey's Executor**. Given the current composition of the Court and its recent Article II jurisprudence, a further expansion of presidential removal power is a plausible outcome.

The trend, as I have often noted, remains our friend. Each incremental victory in restoring constitutional governance strengthens the foundation upon which durable Second Amendment protections must ultimately rest.
