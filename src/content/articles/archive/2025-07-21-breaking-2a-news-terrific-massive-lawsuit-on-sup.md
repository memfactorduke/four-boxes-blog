## Summary

The National Rifle Association, the Second Amendment Foundation, the Association of New Jersey Rifle and Pistol Clubs, the American Suppressor Association, and individual plaintiffs have filed a landmark constitutional challenge in the United States District Court for the District of New Jersey, seeking to have New Jersey's state ban on suppressors and silencers declared unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The complaint was prepared by attorney Daniel Schmutter and a team of lawyers from Cooper & Kirk — the same law firm that has been at the forefront of post-**Bruen** Second Amendment litigation nationally. Having reviewed this complaint in detail, I assess it as one of the finest Second Amendment pleadings I have encountered. It brings together the textual, historical, empirical, and doctrinal arguments for suppressor protection under the Second Amendment with a comprehensiveness and analytical precision that, in my view, presents the government with an essentially insurmountable constitutional challenge. The arguments advanced in this complaint track closely those that I and others in the Second Amendment legal community have been making for years.

## Background

New Jersey's criminal code prohibits the possession of suppressors — devices that, when attached to a firearm's muzzle, reduce the acoustic report of discharge — within the state. The prohibition applies regardless of whether the suppressor is lawfully registered under the National Firearms Act, regardless of the purpose for which it is possessed, and regardless of the identity or background of the possessor. A New Jersey resident who lawfully acquires a suppressor under federal NFA procedures — paying the $200 tax, submitting to a background check, and registering the device with the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives — nonetheless commits a crime under New Jersey law simply by possessing the device within the state's borders.

More than 4.5 million suppressors are currently registered with the federal government under the NFA. These devices are possessed by law-abiding Americans across the country for legitimate purposes: hearing protection while hunting and at shooting ranges, tactical use by law enforcement and military personnel, and home defense where the acoustic trauma of a full-volume discharge in an enclosed space can cause permanent hearing damage. The United States Marine Corps and numerous other military and law enforcement agencies use suppressors as a matter of course. They are, beyond any reasonable dispute, ordinary firearms accessories in common use by law-abiding Americans.

Federal data compiled by the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives indicates that, of the millions of suppressors registered under the NFA, only approximately 30 to 40 have been involved in criminal offenses in any given year. The crime rate involving registered NFA suppressors approaches zero. Suppressors are among the most rarely misused items subject to federal firearms regulation.

New Jersey's ban on these devices cannot withstand constitutional scrutiny under the framework established by the Supreme Court in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). The complaint filed by the NRA, SAF, and their co-plaintiffs makes exactly that argument with exceptional analytical rigor.

## The Textual Argument: Suppressors Are "Arms"

The threshold question in any **Bruen** analysis is whether the challenged restriction burdens conduct within the plain text of the Second Amendment. The operative clause of the Second Amendment — "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed" — uses the word "Arms" to define the scope of the protected right. The first and most fundamental question in the suppressor context is therefore whether a suppressor is an "arm" within the meaning of that text.

The complaint answers that question emphatically in the affirmative, drawing on multiple independent lines of authority.

First, the Supreme Court in **Bruen** stated that a "modern arm" is any instrument — including modern instruments that did not exist at the founding — that facilitates armed self-defense. A suppressor is a physical object — an instrument — that self-evidently facilitates armed self-defense. By reducing the acoustic report of a firearm and protecting the shooter's hearing during defensive use, a suppressor makes the firearm more practically effective as a self-defense tool. That is the definition of an instrument that facilitates armed self-defense.

Second, Congress itself has treated suppressors as firearms under the National Firearms Act since 1934. The NFA expressly defines "firearm" to include "any silencer." If Congress, in the exercise of its legislative judgment, classified suppressors as firearms subject to the most restrictive category of federal firearms regulation, that classification is powerful evidence that suppressors are properly understood as arms within the meaning of the Second Amendment.

Third, the complaint advances a particularly compelling structural argument: banning suppressors is not merely a ban on a single accessory — it is a ban on an entire category of firearms. If you ban suppressors in New Jersey, you have necessarily banned all firearms within the state that are capable of firing suppressed rounds. Because essentially any firearm can accept a suppressor, a suppressor ban effectively functions as a ban on the discharge of suppressed firearms — an entire functional category of arms defined by their capacity to operate with reduced acoustic output. Under **Heller**, the government may not ban an entire category of arms that are in common use for lawful purposes. The complaint's framing of a suppressor ban as a category-of-arms ban — rather than merely an accessory restriction — is analytically powerful and, in my view, correct.

## The Common Use Doctrine and the Dangerous-and-Unusual Test

**Heller** established that arms that are "in common use at the time for lawful purposes like self-defense" cannot constitutionally be banned. The Court drew a distinction between arms in common use — which are categorically protected — and arms that are "dangerous and unusual" — which may be regulated or prohibited. The test for whether an arm falls into the latter category is conjunctive: the arm must be both dangerous and unusual to fall outside **Heller**'s protection.

Suppressors fail — or rather, succeed from the Second Amendment challenger's perspective — on both prongs of the dangerous-and-unusual test, separately and together.

On the "unusual" prong: with over 4.5 million suppressors currently registered under the NFA and in the possession of law-abiding Americans across the country, suppressors are unquestionably in common use. They are not unusual in any meaningful sense of that word. The sheer volume of lawfully owned suppressors in the United States — a number that has approximately doubled in the past decade — demonstrates that these devices enjoy widespread civilian ownership. A device owned by more than four and a half million Americans cannot credibly be characterized as unusual.

On the "dangerous" prong: suppressors are not, in any meaningful sense, uniquely or specially dangerous. As the complaint notes in detail, suppressors actually enhance safety by providing hearing protection to shooters and bystanders, reducing the risk of tinnitus and permanent hearing loss associated with unsuppressed gunfire. The Marine Corps, law enforcement agencies, and hunters across the country use suppressors precisely because they are safety devices. A device that makes firearms safer to use in training and defensive scenarios is, if anything, less dangerous than its unsuppressed counterpart — not more so.

Moreover, the empirical data on criminal misuse confirms the safety analysis. Approximately 30 to 40 criminal incidents involving NFA-registered suppressors per year, out of a national inventory of more than 4.5 million devices, represents a criminal misuse rate that is statistically negligible. The government cannot credibly characterize suppressors as dangerous in any constitutional sense given that they are overwhelmingly used by law-abiding Americans for entirely lawful purposes and are vanishingly rarely involved in criminal conduct.

## The Historical Argument

Even if a court were to find that the textual analysis requires independent corroboration through historical evidence — which is unnecessary given the clarity of the common-use and dangerous-and-unusual analysis — the historical record supports the challengers.

There is no founding-era tradition of prohibiting the possession of accessories or attachments that reduce the acoustic output of firearms. Suppressors as a practical technology were not commercially developed until the early twentieth century, with the first commercial suppressor patented by Hiram Percy Maxim in 1909. The government accordingly cannot point to any founding-era analog for a suppressor ban — because the founding era had no knowledge of suppressor technology.

Under **Bruen**, the government bears the burden of demonstrating that a challenged regulation is "consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation." A ban on a technology that did not exist at the founding obviously cannot be supported by a historical tradition of regulating that specific technology. The government must instead identify a historical tradition of analogous regulations — regulations that imposed a comparably burdensome restriction on a comparably situated category of arms, for a comparably sufficient justification. No such analog exists for a blanket ban on a commonly owned firearm accessory used for hearing protection and enhanced defensive utility.

## Safety Benefits: The Positive Case

Beyond refuting the state's anticipated constitutional arguments, the complaint affirmatively documents the safety benefits of suppressors in extensive empirical detail. This affirmative case is worth noting because it reinforces the anti-"dangerous" prong of the constitutional analysis.

Unsuppressed gunfire typically generates acoustic reports in excess of 140 to 160 decibels — well above the threshold at which permanent hearing damage begins (approximately 85 decibels for prolonged exposure, and as low as 120 decibels for instantaneous exposure). Suppressors typically reduce the acoustic report of a firearm by 20 to 35 decibels — bringing many firearms, particularly pistols used with subsonic ammunition, to levels that are still loud but meaningfully less damaging to hearing. In a home defense scenario, a homeowner who fires an unsuppressed pistol indoors risks permanent hearing injury even after a single defensive discharge. A suppressor significantly mitigates that risk without impairing the firearm's defensive effectiveness.

The United States Marine Corps, which issues suppressors to many of its combat units, has publicly documented the hearing protection benefits of suppressors and their utility in tactical operations, where reduced acoustic signatures improve communication and reduce auditory fatigue. Numerous European countries — including Finland, Norway, and New Zealand — treat suppressors as ordinary safety equipment and make them freely available with minimal regulatory restriction, reflecting an international recognition of their safety benefits that even gun-rights skeptics in other nations have acknowledged.

## Implications

The suppressor ban challenge filed in **NRA v. New Jersey** is a landmark case that will test the outer boundaries of the **Bruen** framework's protection of firearms accessories and attachments. Under any principled application of the **Heller**/**Bruen** analytical methodology, New Jersey's suppressor ban is indefensible. Suppressors are arms under the Second Amendment's text. They are in common use by law-abiding Americans for lawful purposes. They are not dangerous-and-unusual within the meaning of **Heller**. And there is no founding-era historical tradition of banning them.

The case will be litigated initially before the District Court of New Jersey, within the Third Circuit. Given that court's recent receptivity to Second Amendment arguments — as demonstrated by the **Core Civic v. New Jersey** supremacy clause ruling — and the strength of the complaint, there is reason for cautious optimism about the outcome at the district court level. Any adverse ruling would then proceed to the Third Circuit on appeal.

The ultimate resolution of this case — and related NFA suppressor litigation across the country — will define whether the **Bruen** framework's promise of meaningful Second Amendment protection extends to the full range of commonly owned arms and accessories, or whether courts will carve out exceptions for items the government characterizes as uniquely threatening. Based on the legal arguments set forth in this complaint and the constitutional framework established by the Supreme Court, the answer should be clear: suppressors are protected arms under the Second Amendment, and New Jersey's ban on them cannot stand.
