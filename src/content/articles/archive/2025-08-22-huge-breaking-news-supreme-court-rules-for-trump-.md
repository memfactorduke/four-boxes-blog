## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has ruled five-to-four in **National Institutes of Health v. American Public Health Association**, staying the injunctions issued by federal district courts in Massachusetts and affirmed by the First Circuit that would have compelled the Trump administration to continue disbursing approximately $783 million in National Institutes of Health grants that President Trump terminated on grounds that the grants were linked to diversity, equity, and inclusion initiatives and other ideological priorities of the prior administration. The Supreme Court's ruling is a significant victory for the constitutional principle of presidential control over executive spending decisions and simultaneously a firm rejection of the forum-shopping strategy that opponents of the Trump administration have persistently employed — a strategy that has direct implications for Second Amendment litigation across the country.

## Background

President Trump, exercising his administration's authority to review and terminate federal grants inconsistent with the administration's policy priorities, terminated approximately $783 million in NIH grants that had been awarded to various research institutions, non-profit organizations, and affiliated entities. The administration's stated grounds for termination included the grants' connection to DEI initiatives, affirmative action programs, and other ideological priorities that the administration concluded were inconsistent with its policy direction.

The grantees, rather than pursuing their claims in the legally appropriate forum — the United States Court of Federal Claims, which is the specialized Article I court established precisely for claims against the federal government for money — filed suit in the United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts. The Boston federal district court, acting in what the Supreme Court ultimately determined was an improper exercise of jurisdiction, issued injunctive relief ordering the Trump administration to continue funding these grants. The First Circuit Court of Appeals affirmed the district court's injunctions.

The Supreme Court, in a five-to-four ruling authored by the Court's conservative majority — with Justices Kagan, Sotomayor, and Brown Jackson dissenting, along with Chief Justice Roberts — stayed the lower court injunctions pending further proceedings.

## The Supreme Court's Jurisdictional Holding

The core of the Supreme Court's ruling is a jurisdictional point of substantial practical importance: claims against the federal government for money — claims seeking to compel the government to pay money that it has declined to pay — must be brought in the United States Court of Federal Claims, not in federal district courts exercising general federal question jurisdiction.

The Tucker Act, 28 U.S.C. § 1491, grants the Court of Federal Claims exclusive jurisdiction over monetary claims against the United States exceeding $10,000. The Court has consistently held that Congress's creation of this specialized forum reflects a deliberate policy choice: claims for money from the federal government are to be adjudicated in a court with specialized expertise in government contracts and federal fiscal matters, not in generalist district courts.

When the NIH grantees filed in the District of Massachusetts rather than in the Court of Federal Claims, they were engaging in a form of forum shopping — the practice of selecting the court most likely to rule in your favor rather than the court with proper jurisdiction over your claim. The Boston federal courts have developed a well-deserved reputation as hostile to the Trump administration's policy agenda, and the decision to file there rather than in the Court of Federal Claims reflected a calculation that the grantees would receive a more sympathetic hearing in Boston.

The Supreme Court's stay sends an unambiguous message: this strategy will not be tolerated. If the grantees believe they have a legitimate contractual claim for breach of the grant agreements — a point the Court expressly reserved for decision on the merits — they must pursue that claim in the Court of Federal Claims, where the government's fiscal obligations and the applicable statutory and regulatory framework will be assessed by judges with specialized expertise in precisely these kinds of disputes.

## The Vote and Its Significance

The five-to-four division of the Court in this case merits attention. The five justices in the majority — the Court's conservative wing, including Justice Amy Coney Barrett — voted to stay the injunctions. Chief Justice Roberts joined the three liberal justices in dissent, suggesting that Roberts would have resolved the jurisdictional question differently or on different procedural grounds.

The significance of this alignment is that it confirms the current Court's conservative majority's willingness to police lower court overreach — particularly in cases where district courts have issued sweeping injunctive relief against executive action without a sound jurisdictional basis. The majority's willingness to grant a stay, which requires a showing of likelihood of success on the merits, irreparable harm, balance of equities, and the public interest, reflects a strong view that the lower courts acted without proper jurisdiction.

## The Forum Shopping Problem and Second Amendment Litigation

I want to be direct about why this ruling matters to the Second Amendment community beyond its immediate subject matter. The forum shopping strategy that the NIH grantees employed — filing in courts known to be ideologically hostile to the Trump administration — is the same strategy that gun control advocates have long deployed against Second Amendment rights.

Gun control advocacy organizations routinely file challenges to pro-Second Amendment executive and legislative actions in the Ninth Circuit, the First Circuit, the Second Circuit, and the D.C. Circuit, where they can reliably expect sympathetic treatment from courts that have demonstrated a persistent unwillingness to faithfully apply **Heller**, **McDonald**, and **Bruen**. The inverse strategy — Second Amendment advocates filing in circuits more likely to apply the law faithfully — is itself a response to this dynamic.

The Supreme Court's message in **NIH v. APHA** — that courts must exercise their jurisdiction within proper bounds and that parties cannot use forum shopping to obtain injunctions against the executive that a properly constituted court would decline to issue — has resonance beyond the grant termination context. Every time the Supreme Court reinforces the principle that courts must operate within their proper jurisdictional boundaries and apply the law as written rather than as activist judges might prefer it, the foundation for honest Second Amendment adjudication is strengthened.

## Implications

The Supreme Court's stay in **NIH v. APHA** will, in the immediate term, require the grantees to pursue their claims in the Court of Federal Claims if they wish to litigate on the merits. The Court of Federal Claims, which is generally less susceptible to the political pressures that have made the Boston federal courts a preferred venue for anti-Trump litigation, will assess the merits of any breach of contract claims under the governing statutory and regulatory framework.

More broadly, this ruling is part of a pattern of Supreme Court decisions reinforcing presidential authority over the executive branch — a pattern that has direct implications for the Second Amendment in an administration that has consistently advocated for Second Amendment rights. When the executive branch operates with full constitutional authority to implement its policy agenda, including with respect to ATF regulation, DOJ litigation strategy, and the appointment of pro-Second Amendment judges, the result is better outcomes for the right to keep and bear arms.

The wall being built, brick by brick, in favor of the Second Amendment extends to the structural constitutional law that determines who makes firearms policy and how. This ruling is another brick in that wall.
