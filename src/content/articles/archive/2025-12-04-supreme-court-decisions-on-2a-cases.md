## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has scheduled conference dates — this Friday and the following Friday — to determine whether it will grant certiorari in several significant Second Amendment cases that have been accumulating on the docket. The Court has already agreed to hear two Second Amendment cases on the merits this term: **Wolford v. Hawaii**, addressing Hawaii's so-called "vampire rule" restricting concealed carry on private property open to the public, and **United States v. Hmurovic**, challenging the constitutionality of the federal prohibition on drug users possessing firearms under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3). Beyond these already-granted cases, the Court's conference agenda includes high-stakes petitions involving assault weapons bans, standard-capacity magazine restrictions, and the continuing vitality of the **Bruen** framework. I will analyze each of the major pending cases, assess the likelihood that the Court will grant certiorari, and explain what is at stake for the right to keep and bear arms.

## Background: The Current Supreme Court Term

This Supreme Court term has the potential to be a blockbuster for Second Amendment jurisprudence. Following the Court's transformative 2022 decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, which established the text-history-tradition methodology as the governing standard for Second Amendment challenges, the lower courts have been working through a substantial backlog of Second Amendment cases. Some of those lower courts have applied **Bruen** faithfully; others have resisted it. The Supreme Court granted certiorari in **Rahimi** in the 2023-24 term to address one such lower-court conflict and will now have the opportunity to address others.

The two cases already granted certiorari deserve particular attention as a predicate to understanding what the Court may do with the additional petitions before it.

## The Already-Granted Cases

### Wolford v. Hawaii — The "Vampire Rule" Case

**Wolford v. Hawaii** arises from the Ninth Circuit and presents a challenge to Hawaii's so-called "vampire rule" — a default rule under Hawaiian law providing that concealed carry permit holders may not enter private property that is open to the public without obtaining express permission from the property owner or manager. The name derives from the metaphor of vampires, who must receive an invitation before entering a dwelling.

The practical effect of the vampire rule is severe. A licensed concealed carry permit holder in Hawaii who wishes to enter a convenience store, grocery store, gas station, rest stop, or any other commercial establishment open to the public must first somehow ascertain who owns the property and obtain that owner's affirmative permission. For the vast majority of ordinary commercial transactions, this is a practical impossibility. The owner of the Shell station on the highway is not known to the passerby, and there is no mechanism by which a traveler can obtain permission in real time before pulling in for fuel.

I am confident that the Supreme Court will rule in favor of the Second Amendment in **Wolford**. The Hawaii rule cannot withstand scrutiny under the **Bruen** text-history-tradition methodology. There is no founding-era analog for a rule requiring armed citizens to obtain affirmative permission before entering commercial establishments that are otherwise open to the general public. The rule effectively nullifies the right to bear arms in public for the vast majority of everyday activities. The Ninth Circuit's decision upholding it represents exactly the kind of lower-court resistance to **Bruen** that the Supreme Court will need to correct.

### United States v. Hmurovic — The Drug User Prohibition Case

**United States v. Hmurovic** presents a challenge to 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3), which prohibits persons who are unlawful users of or addicted to controlled substances from possessing firearms. Mr. Hmurovic was a regular user of marijuana — a federally controlled substance — who simultaneously possessed a firearm, leading to his prosecution under § 922(g)(3).

I believe that Mr. Hmurovic will lose this case. The Supreme Court will almost certainly hold that § 922(g)(3) is constitutional as applied to a regular user of a federally illegal substance who simultaneously possesses firearms. The founding era contains ample historical support for the proposition that individuals who posed a danger to the community — including those whose judgment was impaired by intoxicants — could be disarmed. A regular marijuana user who simultaneously possesses firearms presents precisely the kind of case where the historical record supports the application of a disarmament statute.

This case should not be read as endangering the rights of responsible gun owners who happen to hold a state-issued medical marijuana card. The Court's decision will be driven by the specific facts of regular illegal drug use combined with firearm possession, and the historical analogs available to justify disarmament in that narrow context. The Second Amendment community should focus its energy on the cases where the constitutional protection is clear and strong, and **Hmurovic** is not such a case.

## The Pending Certiorari Petitions

### The Assault Weapons Cases: Viramontes v. Cook County and Barnett v. Raoul

The most consequential Second Amendment question pending before the Court is whether the Constitution protects the right to keep and bear AR-15s and other semiautomatic rifles that government officials have labeled "assault weapons." **Viramontes v. Cook County** presents this question in the context of Cook County, Illinois's ban on these rifles. The case arises from the Seventh Circuit.

My assessment is that the Court will likely not grant certiorari in **Viramontes** this term, though not because the Justices are unwilling to protect the right to keep AR-15s. Rather, I suspect the Court is awaiting the development of the **Barnett v. Raoul** case in the Seventh Circuit — a case that has the benefit of the Trump administration's Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi, standing up as an amicus in support of the Second Amendment. The Barnett case may present the constitutional question in a more fully developed record that will make for a cleaner vehicle for a definitive ruling.

If the Court ultimately grants certiorari in an assault weapons ban case — whether this term or next — the ruling could and should hold that bans on AR-15s and similar semiautomatic rifles are unconstitutional. Under the **Bruen** methodology, the relevant inquiry is whether semiautomatic rifles are arms "in common use" for lawful purposes by law-abiding Americans. They manifestly are — there are over twenty million AR-15 pattern rifles in civilian hands in the United States. No amount of historical manipulation can manufacture a founding-era tradition of banning the most commonly owned rifle in America.

### The Magazine Restriction Cases: Duncan v. Bonta and Gator Custom Guns

The Court will also conference petitions challenging so-called "large capacity" magazine restrictions. **Duncan v. Bonta** arises from the Ninth Circuit and challenges California's ban on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds. **Gator Custom Guns** presents a similar challenge arising from a Washington State Supreme Court decision upholding that state's magazine restriction.

Both cases turn on the same fundamental question: Are magazines that hold more than ten rounds — which the gun control lobby calls "large capacity" but which are more accurately described as "standard capacity," since they are the most common type sold with modern pistols and rifles — protected by the Second Amendment? The answer, under **Bruen**, should be yes. Standard-capacity magazines are commonly used by tens of millions of law-abiding Americans for self-defense, sport shooting, and other lawful purposes. There is no founding-era tradition of limiting the number of rounds an individual could keep in a firearm or carry into battle.

The Court should win these cases as well, but the question of timing — whether it takes up these issues this term or defers to a subsequent term — remains open.

## The Broader Picture: Is This a Blockbuster Term?

Whether this term qualifies as a "blockbuster" for Second Amendment jurisprudence depends on which cases the Court decides to hear. At minimum, the Court will issue significant rulings in **Wolford** and **Hmurovic**. If the Court also grants certiorari in an assault weapons ban case or a magazine restriction case, this term will have transformative potential for the scope of Second Amendment protection.

The trajectory of the Court's Second Amendment jurisprudence has been consistently favorable since **Heller** in 2008 and **McDonald** in 2010, and that trajectory continued through **Bruen** in 2022. The current Court has a six-justice majority that takes the Second Amendment seriously as a constitutionally protected right, not merely as a preference to be balanced against government interests in gun control. The question is not whether the Court will ultimately vindicate the right to keep semiautomatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines — I am confident it will — but rather the speed and directness with which it does so.

## Implications

Second Amendment advocates should monitor the results of these conference sessions carefully. A grant of certiorari in an assault weapons ban case would represent a historic opportunity to resolve definitively whether the most commonly owned rifle in America falls within the scope of constitutional protection. A ruling in favor of **Wolford** would strike a significant blow against the creative restrictions that anti-gun states like Hawaii have erected to nullify the practical exercise of concealed carry rights won in **Bruen**.

The Court's docket is becoming the most important arena in the ongoing battle for the Second Amendment, and the cases now before it are among the most consequential in the history of that amendment's development. I will continue to monitor and analyze each development as it occurs.
