## Summary

In the aftermath of the Minneapolis shooting incident involving a firearms-carrying protester, several Trump administration officials made public statements that, read in isolation, raised questions among some Second Amendment advocates about the administration's commitment to armed protest rights. In my analysis, those concerns — while understandable in context — do not accurately reflect the administration's institutional record on the Second Amendment, and they confuse imprecise statement by officials operating outside their areas of expertise with deliberate policy. Press Secretary Caroline Levitt's formal reaffirmation of the President's support for Second Amendment rights, the ongoing record of the DOJ's Second Amendment unit, and the administration's appearance before the Supreme Court in **Wolford v. Lopez** collectively demonstrate a pro-Second Amendment posture that should be evaluated by its actions rather than by stray comments.

## Background: The Minneapolis Incident and Administration Statements

The Minneapolis shooting involved a confrontation between a protester — Alex Prey — who was carrying a firearm at a demonstration and law enforcement. Prey's firearm, by all available video evidence, did not leave his waistband. Nevertheless, senior administration officials including FBI Director Kash Patel and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent made statements suggesting that bringing a loaded firearm with extra magazines to a protest was problematic, inadvisable, or potentially indicative of bad intent.

I have reviewed these statements carefully, and my assessment is that they reflect subject-matter unfamiliarity rather than deliberate anti-Second Amendment policy. Scott Bessent is an accomplished finance professional — a former hedge fund manager and now Treasury Secretary. His expertise lies in monetary policy, international trade, currency markets, and fiscal policy. Expecting him to navigate the nuances of Second Amendment doctrine — the right to carry at public demonstrations, the distinction between lawful possession and criminal misuse, the historical tradition of armed protest extending back to the founding era — is not realistic. I would not expect a Second Amendment attorney to accurately explicate the mechanics of the repo market on short notice under media pressure. The inverse holds equally.

## The Constitutional Framework for Armed Protest

The right to carry firearms at public protests is grounded in the same historical tradition that underlies the Second Amendment as a whole. In 1770, John Adams — defending British soldiers charged in the Boston Massacre — explicitly argued in court that Boston citizens had every right to be armed in public and to carry arms for defensive purposes. The First Amendment right to assemble, to petition, to speak, and to march is co-equal with the Second Amendment right to keep and bear arms. Neither subordinates the other. There is no historical tradition of banning firearms from public demonstrations. Accordingly, under **Bruen**'s methodology, a law prohibiting armed attendance at a protest would bear a heavy burden of historical justification that I do not believe the government could satisfy.

The governing principle — articulated clearly by Press Secretary Caroline Levitt in her formal statement on behalf of the administration — is the distinction between lawful possession and criminal misuse. As she stated, "The President supports the Second Amendment rights of law-abiding American citizens. Absolutely. There has been no greater supporter or defender of the right to bear arms than President Donald J. Trump." She correctly identified that while Americans have a constitutional right to bear arms, they do not have a constitutional right to impede lawful law enforcement operations, and that individuals who carry weapons while confronting law enforcement raise the risk of force being used against them.

That is the correct legal framework. Possession is protected; misuse is not. Armed presence at a protest is constitutional; deployment of a weapon to obstruct law enforcement is not. These are not in tension. They are complementary principles that together define the scope of the Second Amendment right.

## Evaluating the Administration by Its Actions

I have consistently applied a simple analytical framework to evaluate the Trump administration's Second Amendment record: examine what they do, not isolated statements by individual officials responding to breaking news. By that standard, the record is strongly favorable.

The administration established a dedicated Second Amendment unit within the Department of Justice — a first in American history. Associate Attorney General Harmy Dhillon has publicly rebuked state magazine bans as unconstitutional. U.S. Attorney Jeanine Pirro has declined to enforce the DC magazine ban. The DOJ filed an amicus brief in **Wolford v. Lopez** before the Supreme Court, arguing in favor of the Second Amendment right to carry in places open to the public. The DOJ has filed pro-Second Amendment briefs in the Seventh Circuit in **Barnett v. Raoul** and in the Third Circuit in **Cheeseman v. Platkin** on magazine bans, and has challenged the DC gun ban in the District of Columbia. The administration is on record supporting the Second Amendment's protection of semi-automatic rifles, standard-capacity magazines, and suppressors.

Eric Pratt of Gun Owners of America, appearing on the BBC, articulated the community's nuanced view accurately: the Trump administration has taken "some of the most pro-Second Amendment actions in modern history," while also noting areas where the community hopes to see improvement — particularly regarding the DOJ's handling of certain National Firearms Act litigation and efforts to moot pending Second Amendment challenges to Biden-era ATF regulations. These are legitimate strategic disagreements, and I share some of them. But they are disagreements about approach and sequencing, not about whether the administration is fundamentally committed to the right to keep and bear arms.

## The Priority Agenda: Where the Administration Must Win

I want to be direct about the issues that matter most. The administration's Second Amendment credibility, and the durability of the Second Amendment framework we are building, depends on winning the following propositions in court — and the administration is on the right side of all of them:

**Semi-automatic rifles cannot be banned.** The administration has made clear it will not defend AR-15 bans and will file against them. Justice Kavanaugh has signaled that the Supreme Court intends to take an AR-15 case within the next term or two. The case law being developed now will feed directly into that ruling.

**Standard-capacity magazines cannot be banned.** As analyzed above, a magazine ban is a categorical gun ban, and the DOJ's briefs in the Seventh and Third Circuits are establishing the correct doctrine.

**Suppressors cannot be categorically banned.** The same arms-definition analysis that protects magazines — a suppressor is an instrument that facilitates armed self-defense — applies to suppressors. The administration's position here is correct, though I have noted reservations about certain tactical decisions on NFA-related litigation.

**The vampire rule cannot stand.** The DOJ's appearance in **Wolford v. Lopez** before the Supreme Court is among the most significant pro-Second Amendment executive actions of the modern era.

On all four of these threshold issues, the administration is aligned with the Second Amendment community. The appropriate response to areas of disagreement — whether about litigation strategy on particular cases or about the precision of individual officials' public statements — is engagement, not alarm.

## Implications

The Minneapolis incident and its aftermath illustrate a dynamic that will recur throughout this administration and any that follows: Second Amendment questions will arise unexpectedly, in the context of breaking news events, and will be answered in real time by officials who may not be expert in this area of law. The appropriate analytical response is to distinguish between the institutional record and the momentary statement, and to evaluate the administration's posture by the former rather than the latter.

The Second Amendment community's most productive role is to remain engaged — providing accurate legal framing, documenting the administration's pro-Second Amendment actions, pushing back precisely and specifically where the administration's litigation strategy warrants it, and building the public and legal record that will sustain favorable Supreme Court outcomes for years to come. The four boxes of liberty — the soap box, the ballot box, the jury box, and the cartridge box — are all in play. Tending carefully to all of them, in their proper order, is the work of this moment.
