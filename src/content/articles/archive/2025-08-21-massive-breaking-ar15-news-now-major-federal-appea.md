## Summary

In my analysis, the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit has just issued a sua sponte order -- that is, an order issued on the court's own initiative without any request from the parties -- directing that the consolidated cases challenging New Jersey's bans on AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles and so-called large-capacity magazines be reheard en banc. This means the full complement of active judges on the Third Circuit, rather than the three-judge panel that originally heard argument on July 1, 2025, will now rehear the cases. As I will explain, this development is, in my view, extraordinary and almost certainly a strong signal that a majority of the Third Circuit intends to rule in favor of the Second Amendment.

## Background

The Third Circuit, which exercises appellate jurisdiction over New Jersey, Delaware, and Pennsylvania, has been confronted with a series of consolidated cases challenging New Jersey's prohibition on so-called large-capacity magazines and its ban on AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles. The lead cases are **Cheeseman v. Polillo** and **Association of New Jersey Rifle & Pistol Clubs v. Attorney General of New Jersey**. These cases were consolidated and argued before a three-judge panel on July 1, 2025.

A lower federal district court judge had previously found that only so-called "Colt-branded" AR-15s constituted protected arms under the Second Amendment, while leaving New Jersey's broader restrictions intact. That cramped interpretation of the Second Amendment's text was appealed to the Third Circuit, setting the stage for appellate resolution of whether semi-automatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines are constitutionally protected arms under the framework established in *District of Columbia v. Heller* (2008) and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen* (2022).

## The Three-Judge Panel and Its Composition

The three-judge panel that heard argument on July 1, 2025 was, in my assessment, distinctly unfavorable to the Second Amendment. Based on my analysis of the panel's composition, I had concluded that a ruling finding that AR-15s, semi-automatic rifles, and standard-capacity magazines were not protected arms under the Second Amendment was the near-certain outcome from that panel, likely by a 2-to-1 vote.

Under the Third Circuit's internal rules, after oral argument a panel drafts its decision and circulates that draft to every active judge on the court before the opinion is published. This circulation mechanism exists precisely to allow the full court to decide whether to convene en banc before an adverse precedent becomes binding law within the circuit.

## What the Sua Sponte En Banc Order Signals

On August 21, 2025, before the three-judge panel had issued any published opinion, the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit entered a sua sponte order directing that the cases be reheard en banc in Philadelphia, Pennsylvania, on October 15, 2025. No party had requested en banc rehearing. The court acted entirely on its own motion.

In my analysis, this sequence of events is highly revealing. What almost certainly occurred is the following: the three-judge panel circulated a draft opinion concluding that AR-15s, semi-automatic rifles, and standard-capacity magazines are not protected arms under the Second Amendment. Upon review of that draft, a majority of the Third Circuit's active judges concluded that the panel's proposed ruling was inconsistent with controlling Supreme Court precedent and with the text of the Second Amendment. The full court then voted to hear the cases en banc -- vacating the panel proceeding and superseding any anticipated panel ruling -- before that ruling could be issued.

En banc review is a genuinely extraordinary remedy in the Third Circuit. It consumes significant court resources and is reserved for cases involving questions of exceptional importance or where a panel threatens to depart from established precedent. The fact that the court invoked en banc review sua sponte, before a panel decision even issued, makes this development even more remarkable.

## Composition of the En Banc Court

With the recent confirmation of Judge Emil Bou to the Third Circuit, the court's active judges now include a slight majority of Republican-appointed judges over Democratic-appointed judges -- approximately seven to six, with the precise count depending on timing and senior judge participation. Under applicable Third Circuit rules, the one senior judge who sat on the original three-judge panel is also eligible to participate in the en banc rehearing.

In my view, if one attempts to assess the likely outcome based on the composition of the en banc court, the calculus is considerably more favorable to the Second Amendment than the three-judge panel would have been.

## The Pending Judicial Nomination of Jennifer Mascot

I must note one development that is, in my assessment, of critical importance. Nominee Jennifer Mascot has been nominated by President Trump to fill a vacancy on the Third Circuit. If the United States Senate confirms her nomination before October 15, 2025, she would be eligible to participate in the en banc argument and decision in these cases.

Her confirmation would give the Third Circuit another Trump-appointed judge, potentially providing a wider margin in favor of the Second Amendment. I believe the Second Amendment community should urge the Senate to expedite her confirmation hearing so that she is in a position to participate in the October 15 oral argument.

## The Legal Standard for AR-15s and Magazines

In my view, the controlling legal framework for evaluating whether a type of arm may be prohibited comes from the Supreme Court's 2008 decision in *District of Columbia v. Heller*. That decision identified only one historically grounded tradition that could justify a categorical ban on a class of arms: the longstanding prohibition on weapons that are both "dangerous and unusual." Both conditions must be satisfied conjunctively.

Applying that standard to AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles and magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds, in my analysis, yields a straightforward result. There are, by conservative estimates, tens of millions of AR-15-pattern rifles in civilian hands across the United States, and hundreds of millions of standard-capacity magazines in lawful circulation. By any measure, these arms are in common use. Because they are not unusual, the "dangerous and unusual" standard cannot be satisfied, and a categorical ban cannot be sustained under *Heller*'s framework.

## Implications

The Third Circuit's sua sponte en banc order is, in my assessment, one of the most significant developments in Second Amendment litigation in recent years. The court has effectively intervened before a hostile panel opinion could become binding law, substituting instead the judgment of the full circuit. The likely outcome of en banc review, given the court's current composition, is a ruling affirming that AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines are constitutionally protected arms under the Second Amendment.

Such a ruling would represent a major victory for Second Amendment rights in New Jersey, Delaware, and Pennsylvania. It would also place significant pressure on other circuits that have upheld similar restrictions, and could ultimately accelerate the Supreme Court's consideration of this fundamental question.
