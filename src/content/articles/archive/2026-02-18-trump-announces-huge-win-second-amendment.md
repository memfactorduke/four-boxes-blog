## Summary

Attorney General Pam Bondi and Secretary of Veterans Affairs Doug Collins have jointly announced that the Department of Veterans Affairs will cease referring veterans appointed civilian fiduciaries to the National Instant Criminal Background Check System (NICS), ending a practice that had stripped Second Amendment rights from an estimated 250,000 to 300,000 veterans without any individualized adjudication of dangerousness. The administration has determined that this decades-old practice violated both 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4) and veterans' constitutional rights by treating a purely administrative financial management decision as the equivalent of a judicial finding of mental defect. The announcement has significant downstream implications for the constitutional framework governing firearms disability more broadly, including legal challenges to state and federal red flag statutes.

## Background

Federal law prohibits firearm possession by any person who "has been adjudicated as a mental defective or has been committed to a mental institution." 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4). The statute's operative language is clear: it requires an *adjudication*—a formal legal determination made through a process with due process protections—before an individual's Second Amendment rights may be suspended on mental health grounds. Congress chose the word "adjudicated" deliberately, and its plain meaning encompasses a proceeding with notice, an opportunity to be heard, and a finding of fact.

Beginning during the Clinton administration, the Department of Veterans Affairs adopted an interpretation that effectively eviscerated this statutory requirement. When a VA physician determined that a veteran needed assistance managing his or her financial affairs—paying bills, preparing tax filings, managing a mortgage—and appointed a third-party civilian fiduciary for that purpose, the VA reported the veteran's name to the Department of Justice for entry into the NICS database. The theory was that the physician's decision to appoint a fiduciary constituted an "adjudication" of mental defect sufficient to trigger the firearms disability under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4). By 2009, this practice had resulted in the submission of approximately 90,000 veterans' names to the NICS system. The number of affected veterans has grown substantially since.

## The Constitutional Defect

In my analysis, the VA's interpretation rested on a fundamental misreading of both the statute and the Constitution. The appointment of a financial fiduciary is a purely administrative act. It reflects a clinician's judgment that a veteran would benefit from assistance with financial management—a determination that carries no necessary implication about that individual's capacity for violence, their danger to themselves or others, or their fitness to exercise constitutional rights. No hearing is conducted. No evidence of dangerousness is assessed. No judicial officer makes a finding. The veteran receives no formal notice that the referral will result in a firearms disability, no opportunity to contest the characterization, and no mechanism for relief short of initiating affirmative legal proceedings. The gap between the statutory requirement of "adjudication" and the administrative reality of a physician's fiduciary referral is not a matter of reasonable statutory construction—it is a constitutional violation.

The Fifth Amendment's Due Process Clause requires, at minimum, that before a fundamental constitutional right is suspended, the government afford the affected individual meaningful notice and an opportunity to be heard before a neutral decision-maker. The right to keep and bear arms, as recognized in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), is a fundamental individual right. Its deprivation—even on a temporary basis—triggers procedural protections that the VA's referral process entirely bypassed. Inquiries conducted by Senators Tom Coburn and Richard Burr confirmed that the VA submitted veterans' names to NICS not on the basis of any finding of dangerousness or criminal record, but solely because a physician determined that financial management assistance was appropriate.

## The *United States v. Rahimi* Framework

The Supreme Court's 2024 decision in **United States v. Rahimi** articulated the governing constitutional framework for assessing Second Amendment disabilities. The Court held that the government may disarm an individual consistent with the Second Amendment only where that individual has been adjudicated—through a proceeding that provides due process protections—to pose a credible threat of physical violence to themselves or others. This requirement of individualized adjudication of danger is the constitutional floor. Regulatory schemes that disarm individuals based on status alone, without a finding of dangerousness through a process that affords the individual an opportunity to be heard, fail to satisfy this standard.

The VA's fiduciary referral practice is difficult to reconcile with *Rahimi* on any fair reading. A veteran who needs help managing a mortgage is not, by virtue of that administrative classification, an individual who has been found to pose a credible threat of physical violence. The appointment of a fiduciary says nothing about danger; it reflects only a physician's assessment of financial management capacity. Under *Rahimi*, that is insufficient to justify a permanent Second Amendment disability. As I have argued, the *Rahimi* framework is consistent with the founding-era distinction between those who posed an individualized, adjudicated danger and those who were peacefully exercising their rights: the former could be restricted; the latter could not.

## The Administration's Action

The Department of Veterans Affairs announced that, after a thorough internal review, it had concluded that the fiduciary referral practice violated both the Gun Control Act and veterans' Second Amendment rights. The Department stated that federal law requires a decision by a judicial or quasi-judicial body before an individual may be reported to NICS, and that a physician's administrative determination to appoint a fiduciary does not satisfy that requirement. The VA will cease making such referrals going forward.

Attorney General Bondi's statement acknowledged that it was "both unlawful and unacceptable for veterans who serve our country to have their constitutional rights threatened" through this mechanism. She directed the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives to review its regulations and propose changes to prevent future violations of veterans' Second Amendment rights. In my view, the administration's position correctly applies *Rahimi*: the constitutional criterion for a firearms disability is a proper adjudication of danger, not an administrative judgment about financial capacity.

## Broader Implications for Red Flag Statutes

The significance of this development extends well beyond the population of veterans directly affected. The legal framework the administration has invoked—that Second Amendment rights may be suspended only upon individualized adjudication of dangerousness, with due process protections—applies with equal force to the class of state and federal statutes commonly known as red flag or extreme risk protection order laws. These statutes typically permit a court to temporarily disarm an individual based on ex parte petition, without prior notice to the subject, and often without any evidentiary hearing before the order takes effect. The constitutional tension between red flag procedures and the *Rahimi* adjudication requirement is substantial, and the administration's stated position here provides a clear articulation of the constitutional standard against which those statutes will be measured.

The DOJ's affirmation that a proper judicial or quasi-judicial determination of danger is the constitutional predicate for a firearms disability is consistent with the originalist framework I have described at length: the founding-era surety tradition required a hearing, a finding, and an individualized assessment of risk before any restriction on the right to bear arms could be imposed. Administrative convenience has never constituted an adequate substitute for that process. As I have observed, the Second Amendment community's objection to red flag laws is not to the underlying goal of keeping firearms from genuinely dangerous individuals—a goal that serves everyone's interests—but to the procedural failures that allow those laws to be invoked without the safeguards the Constitution demands.

## Implications

The Trump administration's decision to terminate the VA fiduciary referral practice represents a meaningful application of the *Rahimi* framework to an administrative context that had evaded serious constitutional scrutiny for decades. It restores Second Amendment rights to a substantial population of veterans who were disarmed without any individualized finding of dangerousness. It also establishes an important institutional precedent: the executive branch has formally concluded, on constitutional grounds, that administrative determinations divorced from any adjudication of danger cannot support a firearms disability under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4).

The long-term significance of this precedent will depend on how courts and legislatures apply the *Rahimi* framework going forward. If the principle that individualized adjudication of danger is constitutionally required is consistently enforced, it will constrain not only the VA's referral practices but a broader range of regulatory schemes that have used status-based classifications as proxies for dangerousness. In my view, that result is not only constitutionally correct but represents a faithful application of both the Second Amendment's text and the founding-era tradition from which it derives.
