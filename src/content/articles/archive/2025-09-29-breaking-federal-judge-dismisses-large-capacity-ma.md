## Summary

The United States District Court for the District of Columbia recently dismissed a Second Amendment challenge to the District's prohibition on so-called large-capacity magazines — specifically, magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds — following an unusual and tactically sophisticated concession by the Firearms Policy Coalition (FPC) and its co-plaintiffs. Rather than representing a defeat for Second Amendment advocates, this dismissal reflects a deliberate strategic decision to avoid the entrenchment of unfavorable standing precedent in the D.C. Circuit, and to preserve the issue for resolution at the appellate level or, potentially, before the Supreme Court of the United States. The case illustrates with particular clarity the intersection of Article III standing doctrine and Second Amendment litigation strategy.

## Background

The District of Columbia's gun laws occupy a unique and contested place in American constitutional law. The District was, of course, the defendant in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008), in which the Supreme Court for the first time held that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms. Notwithstanding that historic defeat, the District has continued to maintain an array of restrictive firearms regulations, including a prohibition on the possession of magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds.

The D.C. Circuit — the federal court of appeals that has jurisdiction over the District — has over time accumulated a body of precedent that creates distinctive and, in my view, constitutionally dubious obstacles to Second Amendment challenges in federal court. In particular, the D.C. Circuit's standing doctrine in the gun rights context has been interpreted in a manner that makes it substantially more difficult for plaintiffs to establish Article III standing than in other circuits.

## Standing Under Article III: The Threshold Requirement

To invoke the jurisdiction of a federal court, a plaintiff must satisfy the constitutional requirement of standing under Article III of the United States Constitution, which limits the federal judicial power to "Cases" and "Controversies." The Supreme Court has distilled this requirement into a three-part test: the plaintiff must demonstrate (1) an injury in fact — a concrete, particularized, and actual or imminent harm; (2) a causal connection between the injury and the challenged conduct; and (3) redressability — a likelihood that a favorable judicial decision will remedy the harm.

In Second Amendment cases in the D.C. Circuit, plaintiffs challenging firearms restrictions have faced heightened scrutiny of the injury-in-fact element. The circuit's precedent has, in practice, required a showing of a specific, credible threat of enforcement against the particular plaintiff — a standard that, while not facially unreasonable, has been applied in ways that effectively preclude facial challenges to restrictive gun laws by law-abiding citizens who possess or wish to possess the prohibited items but have not been threatened with prosecution.

## The FPC's Strategic Concession

In the present litigation, the Firearms Policy Coalition and co-plaintiff Mr. Huitema brought a challenge to the District's large-capacity magazine ban. In a move that is both procedurally unusual and strategically sophisticated, the FPC effectively acknowledged to the district court that, under the prevailing D.C. Circuit standing precedent, its clients could not satisfy the standing requirements as currently construed by that circuit. Accordingly, the FPC urged the court to dismiss the case without prejudice — a dismissal that preserves the right to re-file — on the grounds that the deficient standing precedent needs to be corrected either by the D.C. Circuit sitting en banc or by the Supreme Court.

This is a calculated litigation strategy that merits careful analysis. By securing a dismissal on standing grounds rather than litigating to an adverse ruling on the merits, the FPC has avoided the creation of a circuit precedent that would be far more damaging and difficult to overcome — namely, a ruling holding that the D.C. magazine ban is constitutional under the Second Amendment. A standing dismissal is without prejudice to the merits and leaves the constitutional question open; a merits ruling against the plaintiffs would constitute binding circuit precedent that would govern future challenges.

## Article III and the D.C. Circuit's Standing Problem

The broader issue flagged by this litigation is of considerable constitutional significance. The D.C. Circuit's standing doctrine in Second Amendment cases has operated, in practice, to insulate the District's gun laws from meaningful constitutional scrutiny. If law-abiding citizens cannot establish standing to challenge a law that they are subject to and that they believe violates their constitutional rights — merely because they have not yet been prosecuted under it — then the constitutional right effectively becomes unenforceable in the courts where the law applies.

This is precisely the kind of situation in which the Supreme Court's intervention is warranted. The Court has repeatedly emphasized that the Second Amendment is not a second-class right, and that courts may not apply heightened procedural obstacles to Second Amendment claims that would not be tolerated in the context of other constitutional rights. A standing doctrine that effectively forecloses Second Amendment challenges in the federal courts of the District of Columbia is, in my view, inconsistent with **Bruen**'s mandate and with the Court's broader equal-treatment principle for Second Amendment rights.

## Implications

The dismissal of this case, properly understood, is not a loss for the Second Amendment. It is a deliberate and well-reasoned decision to litigate on ground of the plaintiffs' choosing rather than on ground that favors the government. The FPC and its allies have signaled, through this maneuver, their intention to press the standing issue at the appellate level — and potentially to seek Supreme Court review of the D.C. Circuit's standing precedent if the circuit court declines to correct its own errors.

The broader implications for Second Amendment litigation strategy are instructive. The most effective litigants do not simply file cases; they construct multi-stage strategies that account for adverse precedent, anticipate the need for appellate intervention, and make tactical decisions about which battles to fight and on which terrain. The FPC's approach in this case exemplifies that kind of strategic sophistication, and I expect it will serve the long-term interests of Second Amendment rights — particularly as the District of Columbia's firearms regulations face mounting constitutional pressure in the post-**Bruen** legal landscape.
