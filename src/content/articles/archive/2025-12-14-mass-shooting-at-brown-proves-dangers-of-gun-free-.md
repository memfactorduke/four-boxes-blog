## Summary

A mass shooting at Brown University in Providence, Rhode Island has left two students dead and seven others injured, six of whom are in critical condition. Brown University is one of America's most prestigious Ivy League institutions, and it operates under a comprehensive gun-free zone policy applicable to all persons on campus regardless of whether they hold a valid permit to carry a firearm. This tragedy is a devastating illustration of what I — and a growing body of empirical research — have long maintained: gun-free zones do not protect the people within them. They create defenseless target environments for those who intend to commit violence.

## Background

The shooting occurred on the evening of December 13, 2025. As of the time of this writing, university and law enforcement officials had not identified the perpetrator or released any surveillance footage showing the shooter within the building where the attack took place. At least nine individuals were shot. Two have died. Six remain in critical condition at local hospitals. It was a heinous, unconscionable act of violence.

Rhode Island is one of the most aggressively gun-restrictive states in the country. Brown University goes further still, maintaining a Weapons and Firearms Policy — with an effective date of June 27, 2025 — that expressly prohibits all weapons and firearms at all university locations and events. The policy applies to all individuals on university property, including employees, students, contractors, volunteers, guests, and visitors, and does so explicitly "regardless of whether they possess a valid permit to carry a firearm or other weapon."

Let that language sit for a moment. Brown University has made a deliberate and conscious choice to disarm every law-abiding, permit-holding individual on its campus. Every single one.

## The Failure of the Gun-Free Zone

The Brown University shooting is not an anomaly. It is the predictable result of a policy architecture that disarms the law-abiding while doing nothing to deter or prevent violence by those who intend harm.

The 18th-century Italian Enlightenment jurist Cesare Beccaria — whom the founders read and cited extensively — recognized this dynamic with crystalline clarity. He observed that laws restricting the possession of arms punish only those who are not disposed to commit crime; those who intend to commit crime will obtain arms through whatever means are necessary. The law disarms the innocent and arms the malicious by comparison.

More than two centuries later, the pattern Beccaria identified continues to repeat itself with tragic regularity. A person planning a mass shooting does not read a "no firearms allowed" sign and reconsider. He reads it as a guarantee that his intended victims will be unable to protect themselves. The gun-free zone sign does not deter; it attracts.

## What Brown's Policy Actually Achieves

Consider what Brown University's firearms policy actually accomplishes in practice. It ensures that on a campus of thousands of students, faculty members, administrators, contractors, and visitors, not a single law-abiding person with a valid carry permit is allowed to carry a firearm in defense of herself or others. If a faculty member has spent years legally obtaining and maintaining a carry permit in a state that issues such permits, she must disarm the moment she steps onto Brown's property. If a student from a permitless carry state has been trained in safe firearms handling and understands the responsibility of armed self-defense, he too must leave his means of protection at the campus boundary.

The shooter, by contrast, reads no such constraint into Brown's policy. He brings his firearm because he intends to use it, and the presence of a campus prohibition does not factor into his planning at all — except, perhaps, as a favorable consideration.

The result is a campus on which the ratio of armed potential victims to armed criminals is precisely zero. This is not a policy that reduces violence. It is a policy that ensures that when violence arrives, it encounters no resistance.

## The Caesarean Argument in Contemporary Terms

I want to draw a connection that Brown University's faculty, despite their academic credentials, apparently failed to absorb from their own history curriculum. The lesson of Beccaria, confirmed by centuries of criminological observation, is that restricting the arms of the law-abiding does not make society safer — it makes the law-abiding more vulnerable to those who will not be restricted by any declaration.

This insight is not partisan. It is not ideological. It is a straightforward observation about human behavior and incentive structures. Bad actors do not comply with gun-free zone signs. Good actors do. The net effect is a population that is less capable of defending itself than it would be in the absence of the restriction.

## The Contrast with Protected Leadership

Here is the question I always ask in situations like this: why do the most powerful, most sophisticated, and most influential people in the world protect themselves with armed security? The President of the United States is surrounded at all times by agents carrying firearms. The Vice President travels with armed protection. Corporate executives hire armed security details. Religious leaders of global stature are surrounded by men with weapons.

These people have the resources to study the question of physical security in depth. They have access to the best risk management advice available. And they have universally concluded that armed protection — the actual presence of people carrying actual firearms — is the most effective means of deterring and responding to violent attack.

If that is the conclusion of the world's most sophisticated security analysts, why would we impose a different standard on the students and faculty members of Brown University? Why would we tell them that they must rely on declarations and policies while their university president is protected by individuals whose protection depends on firearms?

The answer, I believe, is not that gun-free zones are sincerely believed to be effective. The evidence against their effectiveness is overwhelming. The answer is that gun-free zones serve a symbolic and ideological function — they signal alignment with the gun control movement's values — regardless of whether they actually protect anyone.

## Rhode Island's Legal Framework

Brown University's policy exists within the broader context of Rhode Island's gun laws, which are among the most restrictive in the nation. Rhode Island imposes substantial barriers on the exercise of Second Amendment rights, and its legal framework effectively discourages the kind of widespread lawful carry that might, in practice, provide some deterrent effect in public spaces.

The combination of state-level restrictions and Brown's total ban on campus carry creates a perfect storm of defenselessness. The students who were shot at Brown University on December 13, 2025, had no realistic option for self-defense available to them. The state had restricted their rights, and their university had eliminated whatever remained.

## Implications

The Brown University shooting should prompt a serious reckoning with the empirical record on gun-free zones. The evidence does not support the proposition that they reduce violence. The weight of the evidence suggests the opposite: that they concentrate violence by creating environments where those who intend harm can be confident they will encounter no armed resistance.

My expectation, unfortunately, is that the reaction from Brown University's administration — and from the broader gun control movement — will be to call for more restrictions, not fewer. The lesson they will draw is not that their policy failed, but that the restrictions did not extend far enough. This response, if history is any guide, will ultimately produce more of the same.

The students and faculty of Brown University, like all law-abiding Americans, deserve the right to defend themselves. A policy that strips them of that right in the name of safety, while delivering neither safety nor security, is not a compassionate policy. It is a negligent one.
