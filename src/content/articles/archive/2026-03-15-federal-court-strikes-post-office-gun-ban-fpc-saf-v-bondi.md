## Summary

In my analysis, a federal district court in Texas has entered a permanent injunction barring the federal government from enforcing its ban on firearms in United States Post Offices against members of the Firearms Policy Coalition (FPC) and the Second Amendment Foundation (SAF). Judge Reed O'Connor held that the prohibition -- codified in federal regulation -- violates the Second Amendment because post offices lack comprehensive security measures and no historical tradition supports banning firearms in analogous locations. As I've examined, the injunction applies nationwide to all current and future members of both plaintiff organizations, with limited exceptions for post offices located within separately restricted facilities such as military installations. The Department of Justice has filed an appeal to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit.

## Background

As I've discussed on The Four Boxes Diner, federal regulations have long prohibited the carrying of firearms on postal property, including post office buildings and their surrounding grounds. The Firearms Policy Coalition and the Second Amendment Foundation, along with individual plaintiffs, filed suit challenging the ban as a violation of the Second Amendment under the framework established by the Supreme Court in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). The case was originally styled against then-Attorney General Merrick Garland and has since been re-captioned to reflect the current Attorney General, Pam Bondi.

In my analysis, the challenge centered on whether post offices qualify as "sensitive places" where the government may constitutionally prohibit firearms. Under *Bruen*, the government bears the burden of demonstrating that a firearms regulation is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. The plaintiffs argued that no such tradition supports a blanket prohibition on carrying firearms in post offices -- commercial facilities open to the general public and lacking any form of security screening comparable to courthouses or airports.

## The District Court's Ruling

In my analysis, Judge Reed O'Connor of the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Texas ruled in favor of the plaintiffs. The court's analysis proceeded along two principal lines.

First, as I see it, the court found that carrying a firearm into a post office is conduct squarely protected by the Second Amendment's plain text. Under *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and *Bruen*, the right to "bear" arms includes the right to carry firearms in public for self-defense. Post offices are public-facing commercial establishments, and the act of carrying a firearm into one falls within the textual scope of the amendment.

Second, having established that the plain text is implicated, the court turned to the government's burden of identifying a historical tradition of analogous regulation. In my view, Judge O'Connor found that the government failed to carry this burden. Post offices bear no resemblance to the narrow category of historically recognized "sensitive places" -- such as legislative assemblies, polling places, and courthouses -- where firearms restrictions have deep roots. Critically, the court emphasized the absence of comprehensive security measures at post offices. Unlike airport terminals screened by the Transportation Security Administration, post offices provide no alternative security apparatus to protect individuals who are disarmed by the regulation. The absence of such measures fatally undermined the government's position, as I see it.

On these grounds, the court entered a final judgment declaring the post office firearms ban unconstitutional as applied to the plaintiffs and their organizational members.

## Scope of the Permanent Injunction

As I see it, the most significant practical dimension of the ruling concerns the scope of the remedy. The Department of Justice had urged the court to limit relief to the named individual plaintiffs, arguing that extending the injunction to all organizational members would effectively create a nationwide order based on a single district court decision. Judge O'Connor rejected this argument.

In my analysis, applying what the court described as "basic black-letter" Supreme Court precedent on associational standing and injunctive relief, the court held that the permanent injunction extends to all present and future members of both the Firearms Policy Coalition and the Second Amendment Foundation. The court's order stated:

> The court grants in part defendants' motion and the permanent injunction as set forth in the court's order number 35 and the final judgment number 36 applies to plaintiffs and to all present and future members of Firearms Policy Coalition Inc. and Second Amendment Foundation.

The distinction between a preliminary and a permanent injunction is significant here. A preliminary injunction is a temporary measure issued during the pendency of litigation. A permanent injunction, by contrast, is entered as part of a final judgment on the merits -- it represents the court's definitive resolution of the legal questions at issue. The permanence of this order means it will remain in effect unless and until it is reversed or stayed on appeal.

There do not appear to be geographical limitations on the injunction's reach. Because the challenged regulation is a federal rule of nationwide application, and because the organizational plaintiffs have members throughout the country, the relief extends to post offices across the United States. The only exceptions are post offices located within facilities where firearms are independently prohibited for separate reasons -- for example, a post office situated on a military base, where distinct federal regulations governing military installations would still apply.

## Practical Implications for Organizational Members

In a public exchange on X (formerly Twitter) following the ruling, the Second Amendment Foundation responded to a series of questions I posed regarding the practical implications of the decision for organizational members.

On the threshold question of membership status, SAF directed individuals who are uncertain whether they hold current memberships to contact the organization directly. Those wishing to join or renew may do so through the SAF website for an annual fee of twenty-five dollars. The injunction's protection attaches only to members of the plaintiff organizations; individuals who are not members of either FPC or SAF are not covered by the court's order.

On geographic scope, SAF confirmed that the injunction applies to all post offices nationwide, subject only to the exception for post offices located within separately restricted facilities.

On the question of enforcement posture -- specifically, whether the Department of Justice would treat the injunction as a bar to arrest (preemption) or merely as a defense to be raised after prosecution is initiated (an affirmative defense) -- SAF stated that this determination rests with the Department of Justice. The organization urged DOJ to cease enforcing the unconstitutional regulation entirely, and not merely against SAF members. This distinction carries real-world consequences. If the government treats the injunction as preemptive, federal agents would be instructed not to arrest covered individuals for carrying firearms in post offices. If the government treats it as an affirmative defense, members could potentially face arrest and would need to demonstrate their membership status in subsequent legal proceedings.

On the possibility of appellate reversal, SAF advised members to monitor the organization's communications for updates, noting that it would promptly disseminate information if the injunction were stayed or reversed by the Fifth Circuit. Members who carry in reliance on the injunction should be aware that the legal landscape could shift if the appellate court intervenes.

## The Appeal and the DOJ's Position

In my analysis, the Department of Justice has appealed the ruling to the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit. The government's concerns about the scope of injunctive relief appear to extend beyond the Second Amendment context. The DOJ has expressed broader institutional worries that expansive organizational injunctions issued by single district courts could be exploited in other areas of litigation -- potentially allowing large classes of individuals to benefit from favorable rulings by joining plaintiff organizations after judgment is entered.

While this concern has some institutional logic, it does not alter the applicable legal standards. The Supreme Court has long recognized that organizations may sue on behalf of their members and that injunctive relief in such cases appropriately extends to the membership. Judge O'Connor's order is consistent with that precedent, as I see it.

The Fifth Circuit, which has been broadly receptive to Second Amendment claims in the post-*Bruen* era, will now review the district court's analysis. The appellate court will assess both the merits of the constitutional holding and the propriety of the injunctive relief.

## Broader Significance

As I've examined it, the ruling in *Firearms Policy Coalition v. Bondi* represents a significant expansion of Second Amendment protections in the post-*Bruen* landscape. It reinforces the principle that the government cannot designate public-facing commercial facilities as "sensitive places" absent a genuine historical pedigree for such restrictions and absent comprehensive security measures that would justify disarming law-abiding citizens.

The decision also highlights the practical importance of organizational membership in the current litigation environment. Much as membership in the NAACP provided African Americans with access to the legal victories secured by that organization during the civil rights era, membership in Second Amendment organizations now provides a mechanism for individuals to benefit directly from successful constitutional litigation. The court's extension of relief to future members further strengthens this dynamic, creating an ongoing incentive for individuals to affiliate with organizations engaged in Second Amendment advocacy.

As I frequently emphasize on The Four Boxes Diner, the case stands as a reminder that there is no right without a remedy. The breadth of the permanent injunction entered by Judge O'Connor ensures that the constitutional right recognized in this case is accompanied by a meaningful and enforceable remedy -- one that extends to hundreds of thousands of organizational members across the country.
