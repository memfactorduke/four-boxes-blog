## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit has issued a major decision in **United States v. Escobar-Teel**, holding that illegal aliens present in the United States are not part of "the people" as that phrase is used in the Second Amendment's text — and therefore have no Second Amendment rights. Critically, Judge Amul Thapar, one of the most influential jurists in the country and a frequent feeder judge to the Supreme Court, authored a concurring opinion in the case that goes substantially further than the majority in articulating the textual and historical basis for this conclusion. The reasoning in Judge Thapar's concurrence reflects precisely the analysis I have advanced on this channel, and it will almost certainly serve as the template for how the broader federal judiciary addresses this question going forward.

## Background

I have addressed the question of whether illegal aliens possess Second Amendment rights on multiple prior occasions, and my answer has always been straightforward: they do not. The text of the Second Amendment protects the right of "the people" to keep and bear arms, and illegal aliens — persons who have entered or remained in the United States in violation of federal law — are not part of "the people" in any constitutionally cognizable sense.

Some commentators and lower court decisions have argued otherwise, suggesting that the phrase "the people" should be read to extend to all persons physically present within the United States. I have consistently characterized that argument as incorrect, and the Sixth Circuit's decision in **United States v. Escobar-Teel** vindicates that position in comprehensive fashion.

## The Facts of the Case

The defendant, Mr. Escobar-Teel, was an illegal alien who had been present in the United States for approximately ten years when he was apprehended in possession of a firearm. He was charged under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(5), the federal statute prohibiting illegal aliens from possessing firearms. He challenged the statute on Second Amendment grounds, arguing that illegal aliens fall within the textual scope of "the people" and that the government could not satisfy its burden to identify a historical tradition sufficient to justify the prohibition.

## The Majority Opinion

The Sixth Circuit majority — consisting of an Obama-appointed judge and a Biden-appointed judge — reached the conclusion that Escobar-Teel had no Second Amendment rights. However, the majority's analytical path is critically important. Notwithstanding that both judges were appointees of Democratic presidents, the majority held that illegal aliens are not protected by the Second Amendment. This bipartisan convergence on the correct result is significant, but the reasoning the majority employed differed from the approach Judge Thapar advocated.

The majority reasoned that even if illegal aliens could be considered part of "the people" as a textual matter, the government had satisfied its burden of identifying a historical tradition of firearms regulation sufficient to justify disarming them. This approach — which first assumes illegal alien membership in "the people" and then asks whether the government can justify the specific restriction through historical analog — is the wrong framework, as Judge Thapar correctly explains.

## Judge Thapar's Concurrence

Judge Amul Thapar, who has appeared on this channel and whose scholarship and judicial opinions reflect some of the finest constitutional analysis on the federal bench today, filed a concurring opinion that addresses the question from the correct starting point: the text of the Second Amendment itself.

Judge Thapar's key insight is that the threshold question — whether a particular class of persons falls within the textual scope of "the people" — must be resolved *before* any historical analysis begins. The two-step Bruen framework asks, first, whether the regulated conduct implicates the plain text of the Second Amendment; and second, if it does, whether the government can justify the regulation through historical tradition. If illegal aliens are not part of "the people" in the first place, the inquiry ends at step one. There is no need to assess whether a historical tradition exists, because the Second Amendment simply does not apply.

This textual approach, which I have long argued is the correct one, rests on the understanding that "the people" as used in the Constitution is a term of art referring to members of the political community — citizens and lawful permanent residents who share a full and mutual relationship with the constitutional order. Persons who have entered or remained in the country in violation of its laws have no claim to membership in that political community for purposes of constitutional rights that are specifically tied to that membership.

## Why Methodology Matters

I want to emphasize a point that I return to frequently: in appellate jurisprudence, the *reasoning* of a decision is often more consequential than the outcome. Both the majority and Judge Thapar reached the same result — Escobar-Teel loses. But their analytical paths are fundamentally different, and those differences will determine how future cases are decided.

The majority's approach — treating illegal aliens as potentially within "the people" and then requiring the government to justify the prohibition historically — is dangerous because it opens the door to future arguments that the government's historical showing was insufficient in different circumstances, with different defendants, under different statutes. Judge Thapar's approach closes that door at the threshold. If illegal aliens are not "the people," the government never needs to justify the regulation, and no future defendant can win by attacking the historical record.

I fully anticipate that Judge Thapar's concurrence will be cited by courts across the country and will almost certainly reflect the view the Supreme Court would take if it were ever required to address this question directly — though as I have previously noted, I doubt the Supreme Court will ever feel the need to do so, given how clearly the lower courts are converging on the correct answer.

## Implications

The decision in **United States v. Escobar-Teel** reinforces the constitutional framework that illegal aliens, by virtue of their status as persons who have violated federal immigration law and have no lawful relationship with the American constitutional order, are outside the textual scope of the Second Amendment's protections. No federal circuit court has held otherwise, and the convergence of both Democratic and Republican appointees on this conclusion reflects the strength and clarity of the textual argument.

Judge Thapar's concurrence is the more important document for purposes of future jurisprudential development. It provides a clean, textually grounded basis for concluding that the Second Amendment does not extend to illegal aliens — a conclusion that neither requires nor depends on contested historical analysis, and that cannot be circumvented by creative historical arguments in future cases.

For practitioners, scholars, and Second Amendment advocates, **Escobar-Teel** and Judge Thapar's concurrence represent a significant and welcome contribution to the body of law defining who is — and who is not — among "the people" protected by the Constitution's most fundamental individual right.
