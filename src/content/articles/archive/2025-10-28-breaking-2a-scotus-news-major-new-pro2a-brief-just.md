## Summary

The Second Amendment Foundation has filed a reply brief before the United States Supreme Court in **West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF**, arguing forcefully that the Supreme Court should grant certiorari to resolve whether the Second Amendment protects the right of 18-, 19-, and 20-year-old adults to purchase handguns from federally licensed firearms dealers. The brief responds to the position taken by the Trump administration's Department of Justice — through Solicitor General John Sauer — which asked the Court to defer consideration of the young-adult handgun issue until after it decides two other Second Amendment cases already on the docket. The Second Amendment Foundation disagrees, and in my view, the Foundation has the better of the argument.

## Background

Federal law, specifically 18 U.S.C. § 922(b)(1) and related regulations, prohibits federally licensed firearms dealers — FFLs — from selling handguns to individuals under the age of 21. Under this statute, an 18-year-old American who is fully eligible to vote, enter binding contracts, serve in the military, and exercise every other privilege of adult citizenship is nonetheless barred from purchasing a handgun from a licensed dealer. This prohibition has long been contested as inconsistent with the Second Amendment's text and history.

**West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF** arose out of the United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit, which sits in Richmond, Virginia. The Fourth Circuit — a court that has displayed a persistent and troubling hostility to Second Amendment rights — upheld the federal age restriction, concluding that the law is constitutional. The West Virginia Citizens Defense League and the other plaintiffs in that case then petitioned the United States Supreme Court for certiorari, seeking a definitive ruling on whether the Second Amendment extends to 18-to-20-year-old adults.

## The Two Cases Already Granted

The Supreme Court has already agreed to hear two Second Amendment cases this term. The first is **Wolford v. Lopez**, which asks whether Hawaii's so-called "vampire rule" — the no-carry default law that presumes permit holders may not bring firearms onto private property open to the public unless the owner expressly consents — violates the Second Amendment. The second is **Hamani v. United States**, which asks whether 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3), the federal prohibition on firearm possession by unlawful users of or persons addicted to controlled substances, is constitutional as applied.

Because those two cases are already on the docket, Solicitor General John Sauer's office filed a brief in *West Virginia Citizens Defense League* arguing that the Supreme Court should not grant certiorari in the young-adult handgun case now. The Government's position is essentially that the Court already has its hands full with *Wolford* and *Hamani*, and that after deciding those cases the Court should then send the young-adult question back to the lower courts via a GVR — grant, vacate, and remand — for reconsideration in light of whatever new precedent emerges.

## The Second Amendment Foundation's Reply

The Second Amendment Foundation, represented through the attorneys handling the *West Virginia Citizens Defense League* petition, filed a reply brief contesting the Government's wait-and-see approach. That brief makes five independent arguments for why the Supreme Court should grant certiorari now, rather than deferring the question.

The core of the Foundation's argument is that the young-adult handgun issue is sufficiently mature and well-developed in the lower courts to warrant immediate Supreme Court review, and that the cases already granted — *Wolford* and *Hamani* — will not definitively resolve the age-restriction question. The Federal statute restricting 18-to-20-year-olds from purchasing handguns from FFLs is a distinct question from the public-carry issues in *Wolford* and from the controlled-substance prohibition in *Hamani*. A GVR approach would simply delay resolution of an important constitutional question while subjecting thousands of young Americans to an ongoing deprivation of their Second Amendment rights.

The reply brief also correctly highlights the depth of the circuit split on the young-adult question. Different federal circuit courts have reached inconsistent conclusions about whether 18-to-20-year-olds possess full Second Amendment rights, creating precisely the kind of national inconsistency that the Supreme Court's certiorari jurisdiction exists to resolve.

## Why the Second Amendment Foundation Has the Better Argument

I agree with the Second Amendment Foundation that the Supreme Court should grant certiorari in *West Virginia Citizens Defense League* now. The constitutional answer is clear: 18-year-olds are adults. They are members of "the people" as that phrase is used in the Second Amendment. The right to keep and bear arms, as the Supreme Court recognized in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008) and reaffirmed in **Bruen**, belongs to the people — not merely to those the government has decided are old enough to exercise it.

The historical record at the time of the Founding provides no support for the proposition that young adults between the ages of 18 and 21 were categorically excluded from the right to keep and bear arms. To the contrary, young men of precisely that age group were expected to serve in the militia and to possess arms for that purpose. The notion that Congress may, by statute, carve 18-to-20-year-olds out of a fundamental constitutional right as a class has no principled historical basis under the *Bruen* framework.

The Trump administration's Solicitor General has performed admirably in many respects this term, securing important victories on the emergency docket and advancing a generally pro-constitutional agenda. On this particular question, however, the decision to urge the Court to defer the young-adult issue reflects a misplaced caution. Telling the Supreme Court that it need not address the Second Amendment rights of millions of adult Americans right now is not the kind of bold constitutional advocacy that the moment demands.

## Implications

The Supreme Court's decision on whether to grant certiorari in *West Virginia Citizens Defense League* will itself be significant. If the Court takes the case, it will have the opportunity to deliver a landmark ruling confirming that the Second Amendment's protections extend fully to all adults — including those between the ages of 18 and 20 — and that the federal age restriction on handgun purchases from FFLs is unconstitutional.

If the Court declines and instead issues a GVR after deciding *Wolford* and *Hamani*, the young-adult question will return to the Fourth Circuit for reconsideration. Given that circuit's track record on Second Amendment issues, there is no guarantee of a favorable result on remand, and the issue may require yet another round of litigation before it ultimately reaches the Supreme Court.

The Second Amendment Foundation's reply brief is an important document in that ongoing fight. It sets out clearly, precisely, and persuasively why the Court should act now. I commend the Foundation for pressing the issue vigorously.
