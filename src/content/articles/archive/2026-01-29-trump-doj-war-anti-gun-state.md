## Summary

The Trump administration's Department of Justice has filed a significant brief in the United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit in **Firearms Policy Coalition v. Campbell** (also styled in part as **Granada v. Campbell**), challenging Massachusetts's handgun roster scheme under the Second Amendment. The brief advances two doctrinal contributions of lasting importance: a precise account of why the word "infringed" in the Second Amendment is not a dictionary term but the output of the **Bruen** methodology, and a sustained argument that the right to keep and bear arms necessarily includes the right to acquire arms through commercial channels. In my analysis, this is an excellent brief — a model of how the Second Amendment unit of the Department of Justice should be engaging rogue anti-gun state laws.

## Background: The Massachusetts Handgun Roster

Massachusetts maintains an "approved firearms roster" — a state-curated list of handgun models that licensed dealers may lawfully transfer to customers within the Commonwealth. Any handgun not on the roster may not be sold by a federally licensed firearms dealer (FFL) in Massachusetts. The practical effect is a de facto ban on many commonly owned and widely marketed handgun models, including multiple variants of the Glock — among the most manufactured and commonly owned pistols in America according to ATF data. Massachusetts characterizes this scheme as a consumer-protection measure, ensuring that only firearms meeting state-defined safety standards reach consumers.

I have reviewed this characterization and find it unconvincing. The roster functions as a ban on the commercial sale of constitutionally protected arms. Its operational parallel is instructive: in **Minneapolis Star & Tribune Co. v. Minnesota Commissioner of Revenue**, 460 U.S. 575 (1983), the Supreme Court struck down a targeted tax on newsprint and ink as an indirect burden on First Amendment-protected speech. The Court recognized that an indirect restriction on the instrumentalities of a constitutional right can be just as impermissible as a direct restriction on the right itself. So too here: a roster that prevents commercial acquisition of commonly owned arms is no less a burden on the Second Amendment because it operates through dealers rather than directly against individual possession.

## The DOJ Brief: Defining "Infringed"

One of the brief's most significant contributions is its treatment of the word "infringed" in the Second Amendment's text: "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed." The district court below had attempted to define "infringed" at the textual stage of the **Bruen** analysis — essentially asking whether the Massachusetts roster left individuals with "alternate means" to exercise their rights. The DOJ correctly identified this as moving "the cart before the horse."

I have emphasized this point in my own analysis, and I am gratified to see the Department of Justice articulate it with precision. The word "infringed" is not a term susceptible to dictionary definition at the outset of a Second Amendment inquiry. It is the output — the answer — to the **Bruen** methodology. The equation runs as follows: (1) Does the plaintiff's desired conduct implicate the plain text of the Second Amendment? (2) If yes, the burden shifts to the government to establish a historical tradition of firearms regulation sufficiently analogous to the challenged law. (3) If the government cannot meet that burden, the law is an infringement. If it can, the law is not.

The word "infringed" is thus the product of completing the methodological analysis, not a predicate for it. As the DOJ writes: "Whether a law infringes the right to bear arms is a legal conclusion based on text and history under Bruen, not a factual inquiry into whether individuals have alternate means to exercise their rights." This is exactly correct, and courts that attempt to define "infringement" in isolation — asking whether workarounds exist — are skipping the historical-tradition analysis that the Supreme Court mandated.

## The Right to Acquire Arms

The brief also confronts directly the district court's holding that there is no constitutionally protected right to acquire firearms. This conclusion is, in my view, untenable — indeed, it is self-defeating. The right to "keep" arms — to possess them — is meaningless without a right to obtain them in the first place. No one is born with a firearm in hand. Commercial acquisition is the primary means by which law-abiding Americans exercise their Second Amendment rights. If the government may freely prohibit the commercial sale of commonly owned arms, the right to keep and bear arms is reduced to a theoretical entitlement unmoored from practical exercise.

The DOJ makes exactly this argument, and it is the correct one. The roster's restriction on commercial transfers implicates the plain text of the Second Amendment at its most basic level: it prevents people from acquiring the arms they seek to keep and bear. That is sufficient to shift the burden to Massachusetts to justify the restriction through historical analogy — a burden the Commonwealth cannot meet, because there is no founding-era tradition of state government maintaining approved lists of constitutionally protected firearms.

I also want to highlight a forward-looking dimension of the "right to acquire" argument. Establishing this principle in the courts is the necessary doctrinal predicate for future challenges to restrictions on the means of acquisition — including, eventually, restrictions on home manufacture. The doctrinal sequence must be built step by step: first, establish the right to acquire; then, develop the case law on the various channels of acquisition. The Trump DOJ is correctly laying that groundwork in this brief.

## The In-Common-Use Standard and Burden Allocation

The brief's treatment of the in-common-use test is also worth noting. **Heller** established that arms in common use by law-abiding Americans for lawful purposes may not be categorically banned. The DOJ correctly identifies Glock pistols and similar roster-excluded handguns as squarely within this protection — the ATF's own manufacturing data confirms their widespread prevalence.

On burden allocation, the brief takes the correct position that it is the government's burden to demonstrate that an arm is *not* in common use — not the rights claimant's burden to demonstrate that it is. This mirrors the structure of criminal procedure, in which the prosecution bears the burden of proof and the defendant need not affirmatively demonstrate innocence. Courts that shift the burden to Second Amendment claimants to prove common use are deviating from **Bruen**'s framework and from basic constitutional adjudication principles.

## The First Circuit: An Uphill Venue

I should be candid about the procedural environment. The First Circuit, based in Boston, is the most hostile circuit in America to Second Amendment claims — more adverse, in my assessment, than even the Ninth Circuit. The likelihood of a favorable panel decision in the First Circuit is low. But that is not the point. The point is establishing the record, developing the arguments, and setting up the case for eventual Supreme Court review. A strong government brief in the First Circuit, like the one the DOJ has filed here, advances that objective regardless of how the First Circuit ultimately rules.

## Implications

This brief represents the Trump Department of Justice at its best on Second Amendment issues — technically precise, doctrinally sophisticated, and strategically positioned to build durable precedent. The "infringed as output" argument and the "right to acquire" argument are both correct and important. They deserve to become fixed features of Second Amendment jurisprudence.

The Massachusetts handgun roster is an unconstitutional restriction on the commercial sale of protected arms. The DOJ has made that case compellingly. The First Circuit will likely disagree; the Supreme Court, when this case or a successor reaches it, should not.
