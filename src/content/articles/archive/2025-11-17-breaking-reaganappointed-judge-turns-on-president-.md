## Summary

The media has been aggressively promoting a narrative that "Reagan-appointed judges" are now ruling against President Trump, presenting these rulings as evidence of bipartisan judicial rebuke of the current administration. This narrative is, in a word, misleading. Understanding why requires a basic understanding of how federal district court judges actually get appointed — specifically, the century-old Senate custom known as the "blue slip" process. Once you understand the blue slip, the "Reagan judge" story collapses as a political talking point and reveals itself to be precisely the kind of game the media plays to obscure what is actually happening in the federal courts.

## Background

For several weeks, a steady stream of media coverage has emphasized that "Trump-appointed judges" — and now "Reagan-appointed judges" — are ruling against President Trump and his administration. The framing is deliberate: it is designed to suggest that even judges appointed by Republican presidents cannot stomach what the Trump administration is doing. The implication is that judicial resistance to the administration is therefore nonpartisan, principled, and broadly legitimate.

I addressed the "Trump-appointed judge" version of this narrative approximately ten days prior. Now the story has morphed to highlight a specific judge in Massachusetts, Ronald Reagan appointee Senior District Judge Mark Wolf, age 78, who resigned his seat in a public splash specifically to speak out against President Trump.

## What the Blue Slip Process Actually Is

To understand why the "Reagan judge" framing is misleading, you must first understand the blue slip process — a Senate custom that has been in place for approximately one hundred years.

When a president nominates an individual to serve as a federal **district court** judge (the trial level of the federal judiciary), a blue piece of paper — a "blue slip" — is sent to each of the two United States senators from the state in which that district court sits. Both home-state senators must return the blue slip to the Senate Judiciary Committee with an affirmative indication of approval before the Judiciary Committee will even hold a hearing on the nominee. If either senator withholds the blue slip, the nomination effectively dies — no hearing, no committee vote, no Senate floor vote.

This is not a constitutional requirement. It is a Senate rule, a custom of comity that has persisted for a century. And it has an enormous practical consequence: a Republican president who wants to fill a federal district court vacancy in a state with two Democratic senators must negotiate with those senators. The Democratic senators will not return the blue slips for a genuine conservative originalist — someone who takes the Constitution's text seriously and would, for example, faithfully apply *Heller* and *Bruen* to gun cases. They simply will not.

## The Practical Reality of Blue Slip Appointments

What happens in practice is a deal. The Republican president's White House and the Democratic senators from a given state negotiate and agree on a candidate — typically someone palatable to both sides, which in blue states almost invariably means someone who is not a committed constitutional conservative. Democratic senators from New York, New Jersey, Massachusetts, California, Hawaii, Maryland, Illinois, and similar states have, for decades, used the blue slip to ensure that even Republican presidents cannot appoint genuine originalists to district court seats in their states.

This is precisely why, when you see headlines about a "Trump-appointed judge" blocking a Trump administration policy, the nuance that the media omits is that such a judge was very likely appointed through a blue slip process in which Democratic senators from that state had meaningful input — and effectively veto power — over the selection.

## The Case of Judge Mark Wolf

Senior District Judge Mark Wolf of Massachusetts is the specific individual now being presented as a "Reagan-appointed judge" who has turned against Trump. The relevant facts are as follows: President Ronald Reagan did, technically, submit the paperwork nominating Mark Wolf to be a federal district court judge in Massachusetts in 1985, and the Senate confirmed him. Those are the facts that the media reports.

What the media does not report is who the United States senators from Massachusetts were in 1985: Senator Ted Kennedy and Senator John Kerry. Those two senators had to return their blue slips approving the Wolf nomination before the Senate Judiciary Committee would even schedule a hearing. The rhetorical question I would pose is this: do you genuinely believe that Ted Kennedy and John Kerry were going to approve a committed originalist conservative — someone who deeply respected the Second Amendment, took the Bill of Rights seriously, and would faithfully apply the Constitution's text — for a lifetime appointment to the federal bench in Massachusetts?

Of course not. The blue slip process meant that Mark Wolf was, in all practical terms, an acceptable candidate to two of the most liberal Democratic senators in the country. The fact that Reagan's name appears on the nomination paperwork is technically accurate and simultaneously almost entirely meaningless as a measure of that judge's constitutional philosophy.

## The Media's Game

The media's use of the "Reagan-appointed judge" framing is a form of misdirection. It wants the audience to conclude that Reaganite conservatism — ideologically aligned with constitutional originalism — has now turned against Trump. But the judge being held up as evidence of this trend was never a Reaganite conservative in any meaningful judicial sense; he was a compromise candidate acceptable to two liberal Democratic senators in 1985.

The game the media plays here is the same game it plays with "Trump-appointed judges who rule against Trump": in blue states, virtually every district court judge — regardless of which president technically submitted the nomination — was shaped by a blue slip process that gave Democratic senators substantial power over the selection. Those judges reflect that process.

## Implications

Understanding the blue slip dynamic is essential to evaluating nearly every story about federal district court judges ruling against the Trump administration. When a federal district court judge in New York, California, Massachusetts, Maryland, or Hawaii issues a ruling adverse to the administration, the question the media never asks — but that every informed observer should ask — is: who were the senators who returned the blue slips for that judge?

The answer, almost invariably in blue states, is Democratic senators who would not have approved a genuine constitutional conservative. That fact does not make the judge's ruling right or wrong on the merits. But it does demolish the narrative that such rulings represent principled conservative or bipartisan pushback against the administration.

The blue slip process is one of the most important, least understood features of the federal judiciary. Now you understand it. Do not let the media play you with it.
