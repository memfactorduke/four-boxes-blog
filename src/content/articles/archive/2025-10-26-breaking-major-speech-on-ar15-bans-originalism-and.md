## Background

The Second Amendment is, at this moment in American history, the cutting edge of constitutional law. That is true for two distinct but related reasons. The first is the broader political and jurisprudential battle over how courts should interpret the Constitution — the conflict between originalism and the living constitution. The second, of course, is the specific and urgent question of what the Second Amendment means for the tens of millions of law-abiding Americans who own and wish to use firearms.

These two reasons are not independent of each other. They are deeply intertwined. The Second Amendment is uniquely positioned at the intersection of both because it presents the conflict between originalism and the living constitution in its starkest and most consequential form.

## Originalism Versus the Living Constitution

The battle over constitutional methodology is not abstract. It has real-world consequences for every right enumerated in the Bill of Rights. Those who subscribe to a living-constitution theory believe that unelected federal judges may reinterpret the Constitution's provisions to fit their own conceptions of what modern society requires. Under that approach, constitutional rights are what five Supreme Court Justices say they are on a given day — not what the text, history, and tradition of the document actually provide.

The Second Amendment community has a profound interest in the originalist approach because originalism defers to the Founding generation's understanding of liberty, self-defense, and the relationship between an armed citizenry and a free government. The Founders understood that a people who could not be disarmed could not be tyrannized. The Second Amendment was not, as some revisionist historians have claimed, merely a compromise about state militias. It was a codification of the preexisting, fundamental, individual human right to keep and bear arms for self-defense.

If you want to understand where the living-constitution approach leads, look no further than contemporary Western Europe. There, citizens have no meaningful right to keep and bear arms. Free speech is circumscribed to exclude any expression that offends the ruling political consensus. Citizens are governed not by themselves through elected representatives, but by expert bureaucracies and unelected officials who claim authority to manage every aspect of life. That is the destination toward which the American progressive movement seeks to lead this country.

To reach that destination, however, two things must happen. First, the American people must be disarmed — which is why the Second Amendment is ground zero in the progressive campaign to transform constitutional jurisprudence. Second, the principle of originalism must be defeated in the courts, so that constitutional text no longer operates as a genuine constraint on governmental power. The Second Amendment movement stands as the most significant obstacle to both objectives.

## Why the Second Amendment Is the Tip of the Spear

The Second Amendment occupies a unique strategic position in the originalism debate for an underappreciated reason: unlike virtually every other area of constitutional law, the Second Amendment has not accumulated decades of liberal activist precedent that originalist jurists must laboriously dismantle before they can vindicate the Constitution's original meaning. In areas like administrative law, for example, courts must first overturn entrenched precedents dating back to the New Deal era before they can implement originalist principles.

The Second Amendment was largely ignored by the federal courts for most of the twentieth century. Liberal judicial activists had no interest in developing a robust body of Second Amendment jurisprudence — precisely because doing so would have required them to acknowledge that the Amendment confers an individual right to keep and bear arms. By leaving the Second Amendment alone for decades, those jurists inadvertently left the field clear for the current Supreme Court to interpret the Amendment on a blank slate, guided entirely by text, history, and tradition.

That blank slate is an extraordinary opportunity. The current Supreme Court — with its majority of originalist Justices — can write Second Amendment law from scratch, in accordance with what the Founding generation actually believed and practiced, without having to overcome a mountain of contrary precedent. No prior Supreme Court decision needs to be overruled to vindicate the Second Amendment's full scope. The Court simply needs to apply the methodology it announced in **Heller**, refined and elaborated in **Bruen**.

## The Bruen Framework and How to Apply It

The *Bruen* framework requires courts to ask whether a challenged firearm regulation is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation. If the government cannot identify a historical analog — a regulation from the Founding era or the period surrounding ratification of the Fourteenth Amendment — that is sufficiently similar in both justification and burden to the challenged modern law, the modern law fails constitutional scrutiny.

This framework has dramatic implications for the vast landscape of modern gun control. Most of what passes for contemporary firearms regulation has no meaningful historical analog. Background check requirements, assault weapons bans, high-capacity magazine restrictions, waiting periods, red flag laws — none of these were part of the Nation's Founding-era tradition of firearm regulation. Under *Bruen*, the government must defend each of these modern enactments by pointing to a specific historical practice that is analogous in both what it regulated and how it regulated it. In most cases, no such analog exists.

The battle over so-called "assault weapons" bans is illustrative. The AR-15 is the most popular rifle in America, owned by approximately 24 million law-abiding citizens. Attempts by various states to prohibit its possession are constitutionally indefensible under *Bruen* because there is no Founding-era tradition of banning weapons that are in common use by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes. The Supreme Court in *Heller* made this point explicitly, noting that the Second Amendment protects weapons "in common use at the time." The AR-15 is unquestionably in common use.

## The Historical Origins of the Second Amendment

One of the great misconceptions that the progressive movement has worked hard to propagate — including through the public school system, which has in many jurisdictions become an instrument for advancing anti-gun ideology — is that the American Revolution was primarily about taxation. The slogan "no taxation without representation" has been elevated above the actual precipitating cause of the Revolutionary War: gun control.

The shot heard around the world at Lexington and Concord in April 1775 was not fired over marginal tax rates or trade regulations. General Thomas Gage marched his forces out of Boston toward Lexington and Concord for a single purpose: to seize the arms and black powder stored by American patriots in that region of Massachusetts, and to arrest patriot leaders including John Hancock. The Colonists resisted not an oppressive tax policy but an attempt by the British Crown to disarm them — to convert them from free citizens into subjects incapable of resisting tyranny.

That historical reality is what gives the Second Amendment its unique significance in the constitutional order. The Founders understood from direct personal experience that an armed people is a free people, and that the first step toward despotism is always the disarmament of the citizenry. They wrote that understanding into the Constitution, and it remains as valid today as it was in 1791.

## Understanding Second Amendment Litigation

For those seeking to advocate effectively for Second Amendment rights, it is essential to understand the structure of constitutional challenges and the methodology courts use to evaluate them. Too much misinformation circulates on this subject — some of it deliberately spread by anti-gun activists who have a financial and ideological interest in muddying the constitutional waters.

Under *Bruen*, the threshold question in any Second Amendment challenge is whether the challenged law burdens conduct covered by the Amendment's plain text. If it does, the law is presumptively unconstitutional, and the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that the law is consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation. The government bears this burden; the challenger does not need to disprove historical support for the law.

This framework matters enormously for how litigation is structured and argued. When advocates, litigants, and journalists understand it correctly, they can identify the strongest arguments and avoid the common error of treating Second Amendment cases as if they were governed by the balancing tests that the *Bruen* majority explicitly rejected.

## Implications

The current moment in Second Amendment law is genuinely historic. For the first time in American history, the Supreme Court has established a clear, text-and-history-based framework for evaluating gun regulations. The lower courts are now working through an enormous docket of challenges to laws ranging from magazine capacity restrictions to assault weapons bans to prohibitions on possession by various categories of persons.

The outcome of this litigation will not only determine the scope of the Second Amendment for a generation — it will also determine whether originalism succeeds as a constitutional methodology more broadly. The Second Amendment community is fighting not just for the right to keep and bear arms. It is fighting for the principle that the Constitution means what it says, that its text constrains government power, and that the meaning of a constitutional provision is determined by the people who ratified it — not by the ideological preferences of unelected judges.

That is a fight worth having. And it is a fight we are winning.
