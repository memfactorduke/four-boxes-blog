## Summary

Cook County, Illinois has filed its opposition brief urging the United States Supreme Court to deny certiorari in **Veramontes v. Cook County**, the major pending challenge to Illinois's semi-automatic rifle and AR-15 ban. The brief is, by any fair assessment, a weak document — and its weakness is instructive. Cook County's lawyers spent the opening pages of their brief reciting examples of mass shootings committed with semi-automatic firearms, a rhetorical approach that the Supreme Court has repeatedly and explicitly rejected as a basis for limiting constitutional rights. The brief's principal legal argument — that there is no circuit split on the semi-automatic ban question — reflects a genuine procedural reality while simultaneously obscuring the profound constitutional wrong that the absence of a split represents.

## Background

**Veramontes v. Cook County** presents a direct challenge to Illinois's categorical ban on semi-automatic rifles, including AR-15-style firearms, under the Second Amendment. The case has been working its way through the federal courts and now stands before the Supreme Court on a petition for certiorari. The Supreme Court is expected to conference the case within the coming weeks.

Cook County has filed its opposition brief, urging the Court to deny cert and allow the Illinois ban to remain in force. I have reviewed that brief, and I want to walk through its principal arguments — because understanding those arguments, and their weaknesses, is essential to understanding where this litigation stands.

## Cook County's Emotional Opening

The first and most prominent feature of Cook County's opposition brief is its reliance on an extended recitation of mass shooting incidents committed with semi-automatic rifles. Multiple opening pages are devoted to this catalogue of tragedies.

This is not a legal argument. It is an appeal to emotion, and it is an appeal that the Supreme Court has considered and explicitly rejected. When the Court decided **District of Columbia v. Heller** in 2008, it was operating in full awareness of the Virginia Tech massacre, which had occurred just the year before in 2007. That terrible shooting — in which dozens of people were shot and many killed using semi-automatic handguns with standard-capacity magazines — was actively litigated in the *Heller* proceedings through amicus briefs. The Supreme Court was not unaware that deranged individuals misuse firearms to commit atrocities.

And yet the Court held in **Heller** that we do not calibrate the scope of constitutional rights by reference to the worst misuse of those rights by the worst actors. As the Court stated, constitutional rights are not measured by their potential for abuse. The same principle applies to the First Amendment: we do not narrow freedom of speech because some people use speech to spread dangerous ideas, and we do not narrow the Second Amendment because some people misuse firearms to commit violence.

A brief that opens with pages of mass shooting examples, in the aftermath of **Heller**, **McDonald**, and **Bruen**, is a brief that has run out of legal arguments. When your best available material is an appeal to the emotions of the Justices rather than an engagement with the text of the Constitution and the historical record, you have conceded the legal merits.

## The Circuit Split Question

Cook County's legal argument — as opposed to its rhetorical appeal — centers on the absence of a circuit split. The argument is this: one of the traditional criteria for Supreme Court certiorari is the existence of a genuine disagreement among the circuits on an important question of federal law. Since no circuit has ruled in favor of an AR-15 ban challenge, there is no circuit split, and therefore no traditional basis for the Court to intervene.

This argument is technically accurate in a narrow sense, but it obscures more than it reveals.

The absence of a circuit split on semi-automatic rifle bans is not the result of any genuine consensus among the federal courts of appeals. It is the structural consequence of the geographic concentration of semi-automatic rifle bans in a small number of heavily Democratic states — principally Illinois, California, New York, Connecticut, Maryland, and Massachusetts. These states sit in the Second, Seventh, and Ninth Circuits — all of which are dominated by Democratic-appointed judges selected with the blessing of anti-gun Democratic senators.

Texas does not have an AR-15 ban. Florida does not have an AR-15 ban. Georgia does not have an AR-15 ban. The Fifth, Eleventh, and other circuits covering these states have never had occasion to consider whether such a ban would be constitutional — because no such ban exists in those jurisdictions to challenge. The only federal appellate courts that have had to confront these bans are those sitting in the jurisdictions that passed them, and those jurisdictions have produced judges who have been systematically selected for their willingness to sustain anti-gun legislation.

The Supreme Court understands this dynamic. The Justices are sophisticated institutional actors who are well aware that the absence of a circuit split on AR-15 bans reflects jurisdictional self-selection rather than any genuine judicial consensus. Deep-blue states pass the bans; deep-blue circuits uphold them; no red state creates the opposing precedent that would generate a split. If the Court were to defer to this structural absence of a split, it would be allowing the geographic accident of where progressive legislatures are located to determine the scope of a constitutional right that applies to all Americans equally.

## The Merits of the Semi-Automatic Ban Challenge

On the merits, the challenge to semi-automatic rifle bans is, in my assessment, an extremely strong one — what litigators sometimes describe as a "layup" for the Second Amendment.

**Heller** established that arms in common use for lawful purposes are categorically protected by the Second Amendment. Semi-automatic rifles, including AR-15-style firearms, are among the most commonly owned firearms in the United States. There are estimated to be tens of millions of them in private hands. They are used extensively for hunting, target shooting, home defense, and competitive marksmanship. They are unquestionably "in common use for lawful purposes" within the meaning of **Heller**.

The government's burden under **Bruen** is to identify a historical tradition of firearms regulation that would justify a categorical ban on such weapons. No such tradition exists. There is no founding-era analog to a categorical ban on the most commonly used military-pattern rifles of the day. To the contrary, the founding-era tradition strongly supported private ownership of military-grade weapons — indeed, the Second Amendment was specifically directed at preserving the capacity of the citizenry to serve as an effective counterweight to government tyranny.

## The Strategic Calculus for the Court

The real question is not whether the Supreme Court would rule in favor of the Second Amendment if it takes **Veramontes** — it would, and the ruling would likely be decisive. The question is whether the Court will choose this term to take on the political and institutional controversy that an AR-15 ban case will inevitably generate.

The Court's decision to grant or deny cert in **Veramontes** will be driven primarily by its own assessment of how to sequence the development of Second Amendment doctrine, not by Cook County's arguments. If the Court denies cert at this stage, it will almost certainly have nothing to do with the legal merits of Cook County's brief. It will reflect the Court's institutional judgment about timing, sequencing, and the pace at which it wishes to build out the Second Amendment framework it established in **Heller** and **Bruen**.

## Implications

Cook County's opposition brief in **Veramontes** is a legally weak document that relies primarily on emotional appeals the Supreme Court has previously and explicitly rejected. The brief's technical circuit-split argument, while accurate in its narrow characterization, reflects a structural reality that the Court understands perfectly well — that the absence of a split results from geographic self-selection rather than judicial consensus. On the merits, the challenge to Illinois's semi-automatic rifle ban is compelling under **Heller** and **Bruen**: AR-15-style rifles are among the most commonly owned firearms in America, they are used for a wide variety of lawful purposes, and there is no founding-era historical tradition supporting a categorical ban on arms in common use. Whether the Court grants cert in **Veramontes** this term will depend primarily on its own institutional sequencing decisions — but when it does take an AR-15 ban case, the result should be a significant victory for the Second Amendment.
