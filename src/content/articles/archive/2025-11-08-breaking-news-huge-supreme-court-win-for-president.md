## Summary

In a remarkable development that crossed ideological lines, Supreme Court Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson — the Court's most reliably progressive member — granted an administrative stay of a federal district court order commanding President Donald J. Trump to disburse approximately four billion dollars in Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) benefits during the government shutdown, when no congressional appropriation for those payments existed. The stay, issued late on a Friday night, paused Judge John McConnell's order from the U.S. District Court for the District of Rhode Island and directed the U.S. Court of Appeals for the First Circuit to consider the matter on an expedited basis. The fact that Justice Jackson — not a justice known for ruling in the executive's favor — felt compelled to grant this stay is itself an extraordinary measure of just how constitutionally deficient Judge McConnell's underlying order was.

## Background

During the federal government shutdown, U.S. District Judge John McConnell — a Barack Obama appointee in the District of Rhode Island — entered an extraordinary order commanding the Trump administration to pay out approximately four billion dollars in SNAP welfare benefits by a date certain. The problem was direct and constitutionally fundamental: Congress had not appropriated funds for SNAP during the current fiscal year because the government was shut down, and the executive branch had already exhausted its entire SNAP contingency reserve, which had amounted to over five billion dollars.

In other words, Judge McConnell ordered the President to spend money the federal government simply did not possess. The governing SNAP statute itself conditions payments on the existence of a congressional appropriation and available funds — neither of which existed at the time of the order.

Plaintiffs had strategically filed in Rhode Island, knowing the case would be reviewed by the U.S. Court of Appeals for the First Circuit — a court I have consistently identified as the most constitutionally unreliable appellate court in the country. The First Circuit sits in Boston, Massachusetts, and has demonstrated a persistent institutional willingness to push the boundaries of judicial authority in ways that undermine the constitutional structure.

## The Reserve Fund Complication

There was an additional constitutional outrage embedded in Judge McConnell's order that deserves separate attention. The four billion dollars that the judge ordered disbursed was not simply money the government did not have in the abstract — it was to be drawn from a children's food program reserve fund. Judge McConnell was, in effect, ordering the executive branch to divert money set aside for children's nutrition programs and redirect it to SNAP welfare recipients. The executive branch explained this to the Court explicitly, noting that complying with the order would require raiding a child welfare fund to pay adult SNAP recipients.

## The Solicitor General's Emergency Application

Solicitor General D. John Sauer filed an emergency application with the Supreme Court on behalf of the Trump administration, seeking an immediate administrative stay of Judge McConnell's order. The application noted that the orders required the federal government to transfer an estimated four billion dollars by that Friday — money that Congress had not appropriated, that the executive branch did not have, and that the SNAP statute itself did not require to be paid absent a congressional appropriation.

The Solicitor General's application made the constitutional point precisely: the SNAP funding lapse was a crisis occasioned by congressional inaction — a shutdown — and it was a crisis that only Congress could resolve through legislative action. The executive branch could not manufacture money that the legislature had declined to appropriate.

## Justice Jackson's Stay

The application was presented to Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson in her capacity as the Circuit Justice assigned to oversee the First Circuit. Justice Jackson granted an administrative stay, pausing Judge McConnell's order from going into effect while directing the First Circuit to consider the matter expeditiously.

The significance of this action can hardly be overstated. Justice Jackson is the justice least likely, based on her record and jurisprudential commitments, to rule in favor of the Trump administration on a contested constitutional question. Her decision to grant the stay — acting unilaterally, without convening the full Court — reflects a judgment on her part that the district court's order was sufficiently improper that even the party aligned with the plaintiffs' policy goals could not in good conscience allow it to stand unchecked.

This is precisely what I have said repeatedly: you know an order is constitutionally untenable when even Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson, acting alone, shuts it down.

## The Constitutional Framework

The three-branch structure of the federal government is not merely an organizational diagram. It is a system of enumerated powers designed to prevent any single branch from acquiring the functions of the others.

Congress, under Article I, holds the power of the purse. It appropriates funds. No other branch can compel the government to spend money that Congress has not authorized.

The President, under Article II, administers those appropriations and executes federal law. He has no authority to create money out of nothing on a district court's order.

The federal courts, under Article III, adjudicate disputes arising under law. They do not appropriate funds, and they may not order the executive to spend money the legislature has not provided.

Judge McConnell's order violated all three of these structural principles simultaneously. An Article III court told the Article II executive to spend money Article I had never appropriated. The Supreme Court, through Justice Jackson's stay, said: no.

## The First Circuit's Role

After the initial stay was granted, the First Circuit took up the matter — and, as one might expect, affirmed Judge McConnell's order with Obama and Biden appointees on the panel. The Trump administration immediately returned to the Supreme Court, which then extended the stay over Justice Jackson's own noted dissent from the extension (though she had granted the initial stay). The full Court's willingness to extend the stay — extending it through the likely reopening of the government and effective mootness of the case — confirms that the constitutional problems with the underlying orders were serious and widely recognized.

## Implications

Several lessons emerge from this episode.

The First Circuit's institutional unreliability continues to manifest in predictable ways. Cases arising from states within the First Circuit — Rhode Island, Massachusetts, Maine, New Hampshire, Puerto Rico — will face a more challenging appellate environment than cases in other circuits. This is not a rhetorical point; it has practical consequences for how cases are planned, where suits are filed, and what litigation strategies are employed.

More fundamentally, this episode illustrates why structural constitutional principles matter independent of the political valence of the immediate dispute. The separation of powers is not merely a conservative or liberal value — it is a foundational commitment of constitutional government. When even the most ideologically committed progressive justice on the Supreme Court feels obligated to stay a ruling because it so plainly exceeds judicial authority, the principle has demonstrated its vitality.

The case ultimately became moot upon the government's reopening. But the constitutional record it created — particularly Justice Jackson's stay — will stand as a data point the next time a district court within the First Circuit attempts to commandeer the executive's spending authority.
