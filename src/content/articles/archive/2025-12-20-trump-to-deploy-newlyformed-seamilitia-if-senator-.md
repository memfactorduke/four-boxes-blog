## Summary

Senator Mike Lee of Utah has introduced the Cartel Mark and Reprisal Authorization Act of 2025, a bill that would authorize the President of the United States to issue letters of marque and reprisal — a constitutionally enumerated congressional power, explicitly provided in Article I, Section 8 — to privately armed American citizens and entities to conduct hostile operations against drug cartel members and cartel-linked organizations outside the territorial boundaries of the United States. A companion measure has been introduced in the House of Representatives by Representative Tim Burchett of Tennessee. The legislation would revive a constitutional mechanism last exercised during the War of 1812 and deploy it against a twenty-first century threat: the maritime and cross-border operations of Mexican drug cartels and Venezuelan narco-trafficking organizations that have caused incalculable harm to American communities.

The proposal represents a constitutionally grounded, historically precedented, and strategically significant use of America's privately armed citizenry in the service of national defense — precisely the kind of innovative constitutional thinking that the Founders anticipated when they included the letters of marque and reprisal clause in the original text of the Constitution.

## Background

Article I, Section 8 of the United States Constitution grants Congress the power "to grant Letters of Marque and Reprisal." At the time of the founding, letters of marque and reprisal were a well-established instrument of statecraft: government-issued licenses authorizing private citizens — known as privateers — to engage in hostile maritime operations against designated enemies of the state. Privateers operated under a formal contractual framework. The letter itself specified the scope of authorized conduct, the identity of permissible targets, the geographic limits of operations, and the temporal duration of the authorization. A privateer operating within the scope of his letter was acting with the full legal sanction of the issuing sovereign. A privateer who exceeded that scope acted without legal authority and exposed himself to treatment as a pirate or war criminal under the laws of nations.

The letters of marque and reprisal clause reflects the founders' understanding that private citizens could be valuable instruments of national defense — particularly at sea, where government naval resources were limited and where commercial maritime expertise was concentrated in the private sector. As Professor John Yoo of Berkeley has explained, at the time of America's founding, the sovereign of any nation could authorize holders of letters of marque and reprisal to engage in hostile actions against enemies of the state, with private parties known as privateers authorized to engage in reprisals for private commercial gain.

The last time Congress authorized the issuance of letters of marque was during the War of 1812. The historical record of that conflict provides a vivid illustration of the doctrine's utility: General Andrew Jackson's decisive victory at the Battle of New Orleans in January 1815 — the engagement that cemented American control over the Louisiana Territory and effectively ended British military ambitions in North America — was achieved in substantial part through the combined efforts of regular military forces, private militia, and privateers. Among the most consequential of those privateers was Jean Lafitte, a French-born privateer and former pirate whose detailed knowledge of the Louisiana waterways and whose armed maritime forces contributed materially to Jackson's victory. The partnership between official military command and private armed force was not incidental to the American victory at New Orleans — it was central to it.

## The Lee Bill: Statutory Structure and Constitutional Basis

The Cartel Mark and Reprisal Authorization Act of 2025 tracks the constitutional text directly. The bill would authorize the President of the United States to issue letters of marque and reprisal to privately armed and equipped persons and entities, upon the President's determination that the targets are members of a cartel, members of a cartel-linked organization, or conspirators associated with a cartel who are responsible for acts of aggression against the United States. The President would issue the letters with "suitable instructions to the leaders thereof to employ all means reasonably necessary to seize, outside the geographic boundaries of the United States and its territories, the person and property" of designated cartel members and affiliates.

The bill's operative framework preserves the core characteristics of the traditional letters of marque model. Authorized conduct is defined and limited by the letter's terms. Operations are confined to areas outside United States territory — the open seas and foreign jurisdictions — thereby avoiding the legal complications that would arise from domestic law enforcement operations. Targets are designated through formal presidential determinations or prior congressional and executive designations of cartel and cartel-linked organizations. Private parties operating within the scope of their letters act with legal sanction; those who exceed it do not.

Senator Lee's statement accompanying the proposal articulates the constitutional logic directly: "The Constitution provides for letters of marque and reprisal as a tool against the enemies of the United States. Cartels have replaced corsairs in the modern era, but we can still give American private citizens and their businesses a stake in the fight against these murderous foreign criminals. The Cartel Mark and Reprisal Reauthorization Act will revive this historic practice to defend our shores and seize cartel assets."

## Why the Letters of Marque Power Fits the Cartel Problem

The drug cartel threat presents a set of characteristics that make the letters of marque mechanism particularly apt.

The cartels operate as quasi-sovereign entities in the maritime and cross-border spaces between Mexico, Venezuela, and the United States. They control transportation corridors for narcotics, human trafficking, and weapons. They possess armed forces, sophisticated communications, and the capacity to conduct operations across vast geographic expanses. They have been designated as foreign terrorist organizations and as significant transnational criminal organizations by the United States government, placing them squarely within the category of entities against whom the letters of marque power was designed to operate.

At the same time, the logistical demands of effective anti-cartel operations — particularly at sea, along the Gulf of Mexico and Caribbean maritime routes through which cartel supply chains flow — exceed what regular government naval forces alone can address efficiently. The United States has an enormous pool of former military personnel, combat veterans with maritime expertise, and private maritime operators who possess the skills and motivation to conduct such operations effectively. The letters of marque mechanism provides the constitutional and legal architecture to deploy that human capital in a structured, authorized, and legally bounded fashion.

## The Second Amendment Dimension

The letters of marque proposal intersects with Second Amendment principles in a way that is worth noting. The constitutional vision underlying both the Second Amendment and the letters of marque clause is the same: privately armed American citizens are a legitimate and constitutionally recognized instrument of national defense. The Second Amendment's preservation of the individual right to keep and bear arms reflects the founders' understanding that an armed citizenry contributes to national security in ways that complement, rather than displace, official military forces. The letters of marque clause operationalizes that same vision in the context of maritime and hostile external operations.

The proposal thus demonstrates that the constitutional architecture of the founding era was not designed to confine private arms entirely to domestic self-defense. The founders contemplated a nation in which privately owned arms, privately organized forces, and government-issued authorizations could work together to defend American interests — including against external threats that formal military channels might be unable to address alone.

## Implications

Senator Lee's letters of marque proposal is significant for at least three reasons.

First, it demonstrates that constitutional provisions that have fallen into disuse are not dead. Article I's letters of marque and reprisal clause has not been exercised for over two centuries, but its text is unambiguous and its purpose is as relevant today as it was in 1812. The constitutional power to authorize private military action against enemies of the state did not expire with the age of sail.

Second, the proposal offers a creative and constitutionally grounded alternative to the difficult questions surrounding the use of official military force against non-state actors operating in foreign jurisdictions. Letters of marque operations by private actors carry different legal and diplomatic profiles than operations by official U.S. military forces — profiles that may offer operational and political advantages in the complex environment of counter-cartel operations.

Third, and most broadly, the proposal serves as a reminder that the full text of the Constitution contains tools and powers that modern governance has largely forgotten. The letters of marque clause, the Second Amendment, the Militia Clauses, and other constitutional provisions that center on the role of armed private citizens in national defense reflect a coherent founding-era vision of a republic in which government military power and private armed force work together. That vision is worth recovering.

Whether the Cartel Mark and Reprisal Authorization Act advances through Congress or not, Senator Lee's proposal has performed the valuable service of returning a dormant constitutional provision to public attention and demonstrating its continued relevance to twenty-first century national security challenges.
