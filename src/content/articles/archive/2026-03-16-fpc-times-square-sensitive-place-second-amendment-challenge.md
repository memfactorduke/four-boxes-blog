## Summary

The Firearms Policy Coalition (FPC) has filed a major federal lawsuit, *Goldberger v. James*, challenging New York's designation of Times Square as a "sensitive place" where licensed individuals are prohibited from carrying firearms. The complaint, brought on behalf of plaintiff Yehuda Goldberger and the FPC against New York Attorney General Letitia James, Manhattan District Attorney Alvin Bragg, and other state and city officials, argues that the Times Square carry ban violates the Second and Fourteenth Amendments under the framework established by the Supreme Court in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association, Inc. v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). As I've analyzed in depth, this lawsuit contends that Times Square lacks the comprehensive government-provided security that historically characterized the founding-era locations the Supreme Court identified as sensitive places, and that the ban is therefore constitutionally impermissible.

## Background: New York's Post-Bruen "Sensitive Places" Regime

On June 23, 2022, the United States Supreme Court decided *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association, Inc. v. Bruen*, holding that the Second and Fourteenth Amendments protect an individual's right to carry a handgun for self-defense outside the home and striking down New York's discretionary "proper cause" carry-licensing scheme as unconstitutional. As I've studied extensively, this decision, consistent with the Court's earlier rulings in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and *McDonald v. City of Chicago*, 561 U.S. 742 (2010), reaffirmed that the right to "bear" arms means the right to carry firearms in public for self-defense, among other lawful purposes.

Just over a week later, on July 1, 2022, New York Governor Kathy Hochul convened an emergency legislative session and signed into law the Concealed Carry Improvement Act (CCIA). The statute imposed sweeping new restrictions designed to supplant those the Supreme Court had just invalidated. Among its most significant provisions, the CCIA designated an expansive list of public locations as "sensitive places" where carrying firearms is unlawful -- even for individuals who hold valid carry licenses under state law. Times Square, the iconic commercial and entertainment district in Midtown Manhattan, was designated as one such sensitive location.

The practical effect of the Times Square designation is substantial. Times Square occupies a large area in the geographic center of Manhattan. Even individuals who do not live or work in the district must frequently pass through it when traveling across Midtown -- from the west side to the east side, for example. Under the CCIA, merely transiting through the area while lawfully carrying a handgun constitutes a felony offense, effectively severing a central corridor of the city from Second Amendment protection.

## The Complaint's Core Arguments

The complaint in *Goldberger v. James* advances several interrelated constitutional arguments, each grounded in *Bruen*'s text-history-and-tradition analytical framework.

**Times Square is indisputably a public place.** The complaint emphasizes that Times Square is an open public thoroughfare, accessible to anyone -- including criminals -- without restriction. The plaintiffs argue that the carry ban "unlawfully deprives individuals of the right to keep and bear arms in what is indisputably a public place where the need for self-defense may arise." By designating such a space as a gun-free zone, New York has "made a mockery" of the Supreme Court's holding in *Bruen*, which reaffirmed, in the words of Justice Clarence Thomas, that "personal security extends to more than just those who work in marbled halls guarded constantly by a vigilant and dedicated police force."

**The Supreme Court already rejected this reasoning.** In my analysis of this complaint, I've observed that *Bruen* itself addressed and rejected the very justifications New York now relies upon. In its discussion of historical limits on the right to carry, the Court explicitly rejected the argument that general law-enforcement presence in a densely populated area could justify broad carry restrictions. As the complaint notes, the Court found "no historical basis for New York to effectively declare the island of Manhattan a sensitive place simply because it is crowded and protected generally by the New York City Police Department." While New York has not designated all of Manhattan as sensitive, the plaintiffs contend it has "done the next worst thing" by declaring "a large portion and central thoroughfare of New York City a sensitive place based on that same rejected reasoning."

**No historical tradition supports the ban.** Under *Bruen*, the government bears the burden of demonstrating that a modern firearms regulation is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. I've examined the complaint's argument that New York cannot carry this burden because no founding-era analogue supports banning firearms in an open public area that lacks comprehensive security measures.

## The Government-Security Principle

The heart of the complaint's analytical framework is what I call the "government-security principle" -- the argument that the defining characteristic of a constitutionally permissible sensitive place is not a government-applied label, but the actual presence of armed security that renders individual self-defense unnecessary.

In *Bruen*, the Supreme Court identified three categories of locations that it assumed were sensitive places at the time of the founding: courthouses, legislative chambers, and polling places. As I've analyzed in my research, the complaint argues that the common denominator uniting these three locations was comprehensive government-provided security:

- **Courthouses** were protected by bailiffs and, following the Judiciary Act of 1789, by the newly created United States Marshals Service. Congress established the Marshals Service contemporaneously with the federal court system, and its primary function was protecting the courts -- demonstrating that the founders understood security to be an essential component of restricting arms in judicial settings.

- **Legislative chambers** were protected by sergeants-at-arms and other legislative security personnel.

- **Polling places** were protected by sheriffs, deputy sheriffs, and similar local law-enforcement officers during the conduct of elections.

In each case, the government did not merely post a sign declaring the location gun-free. It deployed armed personnel, controlled access through limited points of entry, and took affirmative steps to ensure that neither law-abiding citizens nor criminals could bring weapons into the space. As I've developed in my analysis, this principle maps directly onto modern security infrastructure: federal courthouses, the United States Capitol, and TSA airport checkpoints all feature metal detectors, limited entry points, and armed security -- the hallmarks of a genuine sensitive place.

Times Square, by contrast, has none of these characteristics. There are no metal detectors, no controlled points of entry, no screening of any kind. Anyone -- including armed criminals -- may freely walk into the area at any time. Under the government-security principle I've articulated, a location where the government takes no meaningful steps to prevent armed entry cannot constitutionally be classified as a sensitive place. As the complaint puts it, if the government does not treat a place as genuinely sensitive through its actions, the mere application of a label is constitutionally insufficient.

## The Historical Limits: Carry "To the Terror of the People"

The complaint also identifies a distinct historical principle that I've researched extensively. At the founding, the well-established legal limit on carrying firearms in places of public congregation -- locations like Times Square -- was not a blanket prohibition on carry. Rather, founding-era law prohibited carrying arms only with the "intent to terrify." This distinction is critical: the historical tradition permitted peaceable carry in crowded public spaces and restricted only the menacing or threatening brandishing of weapons. New York's blanket ban on all carry in Times Square, regardless of the carrier's conduct or intent, has no founding-era analogue and is therefore inconsistent with the historical tradition *Bruen* requires.

In my law review article in the Pepperdine Law Review, I've examined the influence of Enlightenment thinker Cesare Beccaria on the American founders' understanding of the right to bear arms, and this provides further support for this distinction.

## The Polling-Places Nuance

I want to highlight an important historical nuance regarding the treatment of polling places that bears on the broader sensitive-places analysis. At the time of the founding, voting was conducted by open declaration -- voters publicly announced their choices, creating a genuine risk of intimidation and violence. Government security was provided accordingly. It was not until the late nineteenth century that the Australian (secret) ballot system was widely adopted in the United States, fundamentally changing the character of polling places by eliminating public disclosure of votes and reducing the corresponding security risks.

This historical evolution, in my view, illustrates why founding-era sensitive-place designations cannot be mechanically transplanted into the modern era without examining the underlying conditions that justified them. The presence of comprehensive security -- not simply the type of activity conducted at a location -- is what made founding-era sensitive places constitutionally distinct.

## Looking Ahead

The lawsuit is filed in federal court in New York and will proceed in the first instance before the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York, within the jurisdiction of the Second Circuit. I view this case as a clean vehicle for testing the constitutional limits of New York's post-*Bruen* sensitive-places regime and, more broadly, for defining what "sensitive place" means under the Second Amendment.

Given that the complaint directly engages the Supreme Court's own language in *Bruen* regarding New York City and sensitive places, I believe the case may ultimately be destined for review by the Supreme Court itself. The resolution of the government-security principle -- whether a location must have comprehensive security to qualify as a sensitive place -- carries implications well beyond Times Square, potentially affecting sensitive-place designations nationwide.

The Firearms Policy Coalition has indicated it will pursue the case aggressively. I'll be reporting on key developments as they occur on The Four Boxes Diner.
