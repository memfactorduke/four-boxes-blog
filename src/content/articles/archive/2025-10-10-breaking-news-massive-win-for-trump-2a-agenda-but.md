## Summary

The United States Senate has confirmed Professor Jennifer Mascot to the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit by a vote of 50 to 47. Senate Majority Leader John Thune and Senate Judiciary Committee Chairman Chuck Grassley shepherded the confirmation through with commendable speed. The confirmation is a significant victory for President Trump's judicial agenda and potentially for the Second Amendment, given the Third Circuit's pending en banc oral argument in **Cheeseman v. Platkin** — a challenge to New Jersey's assault weapons ban, magazine capacity restrictions, and related firearms prohibitions. However, Senate confirmation alone does not make Mascot a judge: she must still receive a presidential commission and be sworn in before she may exercise judicial authority. The race is now on to complete those steps before the scheduled October 15 oral argument.

## Background

On September 17, 2025, I published an article in the National Pulse explaining in detail why it was critically important for the United States Senate to act expeditiously to confirm President Trump's nomination of Professor Jennifer Mascot to the Third Circuit. I followed that article two days later with a video on this channel elaborating on the strategic significance of her confirmation and the urgency created by the pending **Cheeseman** en banc argument.

My argument was straightforward. The Third Circuit is the federal appeals court with jurisdiction over New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware. An en banc argument — a hearing before the full active membership of the court — had been scheduled in **Cheeseman v. Platkin** for October 15, 2025. That case presents the question of whether New Jersey's prohibition on semi-automatic rifles classified as "assault weapons," its ban on standard-capacity magazines holding more than ten rounds, and related firearms restrictions are constitutional under the Second Amendment as interpreted in **Heller** and **Bruen**. Professor Mascot, as a prominent constitutional scholar with deep expertise in the Second Amendment, was precisely the kind of jurist whose addition to the Third Circuit could influence the outcome of that argument and the eventual en banc decision.

The confirmation vote of 50 to 47 reflects the narrow Republican Senate majority and the importance of holding every vote. I want to give credit where it is due: Senate Judiciary Committee Chairman Chuck Grassley has been a consistent and reliable guardian of constitutional judicial appointments. His committee moved Mascot's nomination with commendable alacrity given the stakes and the timing. Senate Majority Leader Thune provided the leadership necessary to secure the floor vote. And it is worth remembering that Grassley was the architect of what became known as the "Garland rule" — the practice of not confirming a Supreme Court justice in an election year — which ultimately led to Justice Neil Gorsuch's confirmation in President Trump's first term.

## The Remaining Steps to Judicial Office

I want to be precise about the procedural posture, because the failure to understand it has led to confusion in some commentary. Senate confirmation is a necessary but not sufficient condition for assuming federal judicial office. After confirmation, two additional steps must occur before a nominee becomes a judge.

First, the President must sign the judicial commission. The commission is the formal executive instrument attesting to the nominee's confirmation and authorizing the exercise of judicial power. It must bear the President's signature. The constitutional significance of the commission was famously established in **Marbury v. Madison** (1803), where Chief Justice Marshall held that the signing of a commission is the act that completes the appointment. Without it, even a Senate-confirmed nominee has no power to act as a judge.

Second, the nominee must take the oath of office. The oath affirms the judge's commitment to uphold the Constitution and faithfully perform the duties of office. The oath may be administered by a judge or other official authorized to administer oaths and may occur anywhere, including at the judge's home or chambers.

With Mascot's Senate confirmation now secured, the critical question is whether the commission can be issued and the oath administered before the October 15 oral argument in **Cheeseman**. This is a logistical challenge involving the White House's administrative process for preparing and delivering judicial commissions — typically a matter of days, but potentially compressible under extraordinary circumstances.

## The Stakes in Cheeseman v. Platkin

**Cheeseman v. Platkin** and the consolidated **Association of New Jersey Rifle & Pistol Clubs v. Platkin** challenge several of New Jersey's most restrictive firearms laws. The core provisions at issue are:

New Jersey's categorical prohibition on the acquisition, transfer, and possession of firearms designated as "assault weapons," which includes semi-automatic rifles like the AR-15 with certain features. New Jersey's ban on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds of ammunition. Additional restrictions on semi-automatic pistols with specified features.

Under **Bruen**, these restrictions must be evaluated against the historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States at and around the time of the Founding and Reconstruction. The burden is on New Jersey to demonstrate that its bans are consistent with that historical tradition. There is no Founding-era or Reconstruction-era precedent for categorical bans on entire classes of commonly owned, constitutionally protected arms. The AR-15 is the most popular rifle in America, and standard-capacity magazines are in near-universal use. Under **Heller's** common use doctrine, they are presumptively protected.

The composition of the en banc panel matters enormously. Third Circuit judges appointed by Democratic administrations have, in prior rulings, demonstrated a willingness to uphold assault weapons bans and magazine restrictions notwithstanding **Bruen's** demands. The addition of Judge Mascot — if she can be seated in time — changes the composition of the panel in ways favorable to a faithful **Bruen** analysis.

## Implications

The confirmation of Jennifer Mascot to the Third Circuit is a major victory for the Second Amendment and for the principle of originalist judicial interpretation. The speed with which the Senate acted reflects an appropriate understanding of the stakes created by the pending **Cheeseman** argument. The remaining question — whether the commission and oath can be completed before October 15 — will determine whether Judge Mascot is able to participate in that pivotal argument.

I will continue to monitor and report on this development. What I can say with certainty at this point is that the Trump administration and the Republican Senate have done their part. The confirmation is complete. The presidential commission is the next step, and the clock is running.
