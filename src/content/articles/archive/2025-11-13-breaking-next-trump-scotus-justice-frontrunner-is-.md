## Summary

Ninth Circuit Judge Lawrence Van Dyke is, in my assessment, definitively on the short list for a future appointment to the United States Supreme Court by President Donald Trump. Judge Van Dyke recently gave a major interview at the 2025 National Lawyers Convention of the Federalist Society — an event at which I also presented — discussing his background as a competitive shooter, his views on the importance of judges having real-world familiarity with firearms, and the responsibility of federal judges to remain connected to the people whose lives their rulings affect. His record of principled Second Amendment dissents on the Ninth Circuit, combined with his judicial philosophy and practical grounding in firearms, makes him a uniquely compelling candidate for the nation's highest court.

## Background

Judge Lawrence Van Dyke serves on the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit — a court that, by its composition and historical jurisprudence, has been among the most hostile venues in the country for Second Amendment litigation. On a circuit characterized by repeated, defiant rejections of the Supreme Court's *Heller* and *Bruen* frameworks, Judge Van Dyke has distinguished himself as a consistent, principled, and intellectually rigorous voice for faithful application of the Constitution's text as written.

He is perhaps best known in the Second Amendment community for a dissent he filed in the **Duncan v. Bonta** case — California's large-capacity magazine ban litigation — in which he took the extraordinary step of recording a video in his judicial chambers, with an AK-47 taken apart and laid out before him, in order to demonstrate to his Ninth Circuit colleagues how firearms actually operate mechanically before the majority proceeded to issue what he viewed as a constitutionally deficient ruling. The **Duncan v. Bonta** case is now pending on a certiorari petition before the United States Supreme Court.

## The Federalist Society Interview

At the 2025 National Lawyers Convention of the Federalist Society in Washington, D.C. — at which I had the honor of presenting on the state of Second Amendment litigation — Judge Van Dyke sat for an extended interview in which he discussed his background in competitive shooting, his views on judicial temperament and engagement with the real world, and the importance of judges having genuine firsthand knowledge of the subjects about which they adjudicate.

### How Judge Van Dyke Came to Competitive Shooting

Judge Van Dyke's path to competitive shooting began, appropriately enough, at a Federalist Society event in Dallas — not a shooting event, but an ordinary lunch. Seated at a table with other attendees, he overheard two individuals discussing a type of shooting competition involving tactical scenarios: moving targets, paper targets set up in practical situations, walls to move around, timed stages. He described himself as too shy to interrupt and ask what sport they were discussing, but sufficiently intrigued to follow up later. He eventually learned that the competitions they were describing are the kind of practical shooting sports — such as USPSA or IPSC competition — that millions of Americans participate in.

The final catalyst was his wife and mother, who together purchased him a firearm — a Sig Sauer 5.56 caliber modern sporting rifle, which he described, with some wit, as "a modern sporting rifle for the conservatives in the audience, and an assault rifle for the liberals." That gift set in motion what became a serious engagement with competitive shooting.

### Why Judicial Familiarity with Firearms Matters

One of the most intellectually important points Judge Van Dyke made in the interview concerned the challenge that federal judges face when adjudicating Second Amendment cases without any personal familiarity with firearms. His argument deserves careful consideration.

Federal judges who have never held a firearm, never fired one, and have no experiential frame of reference for what guns actually are — how they feel, how they operate, what distinguishes a semi-automatic mechanism from an automatic one, what a magazine actually is and how it functions — are being asked to make constitutional judgments about those objects in the abstract. Judge Van Dyke drew the analogy to First Amendment cases: it would be deeply odd for a judge with no understanding of speech or religion to adjudicate free speech or religious freedom disputes. The reason most judges feel comfortable with First Amendment cases is that speech and religion are lived experiences; virtually every judge has spoken words and most have some relationship with religion or its absence.

Firearms are not that. For a substantial number of federal judges — particularly those appointed from blue-state backgrounds, educated at elite law schools with largely anti-gun cultures, and having spent careers in environments where firearms were foreign objects — guns exist only as abstractions: dangerous things that appear in criminal cases and mass shooting news coverage. That abstraction, Judge Van Dyke argued, makes it very difficult for such judges to engage with the concrete, practical questions that Second Amendment cases require: What does a firearm actually do? What distinguishes one type from another? What are the real-world implications of a particular regulatory regime?

His solution — going out and engaging directly with firearms through competitive shooting, and bringing law students out to shoot — reflects a genuine effort to ensure that his own judicial analysis is grounded in factual reality rather than constructed from abstractions and media narratives.

### The Insular Nature of the Appellate Bench

Judge Van Dyke also spoke candidly about a risk that he believes all appellate judges must actively guard against: insularity. At the court of appeals level, a judge spends the overwhelming majority of his or her professional life interacting with other judges, law clerks, and attorneys. The clerks — typically brilliant recent law school graduates — are eager to please and tend to affirm rather than challenge. The attorneys who appear in appellate arguments are specialists who have mastered the conventions of a highly formalized proceeding.

This environment, Judge Van Dyke observed, can cause a judge to become deeply detached from the lives of ordinary Americans — the people whose rights and livelihoods are actually at stake in the cases being decided. Competitive shooting, for him, is in part a remedy: it connects him to real communities of Americans — gun owners, sport shooters, veterans, hunters, people from all walks of life — who do not spend their days in marble-floored courthouses. Their perspective on what the Second Amendment means in daily life is not something that comes through briefs and oral arguments; it comes through genuine human connection.

He noted that the Founders themselves were, by and large, deeply conversant with firearms in a way that modern federal judges are not. A judge who wants to understand what the Founders meant when they wrote the Second Amendment would benefit from having some firsthand understanding of the objects that Amendment protects.

## Judge Van Dyke's Second Amendment Record

Beyond the interview's humanizing and philosophically interesting content, it is important to understand why Judge Van Dyke is so prominent in Second Amendment circles. His track record on the Ninth Circuit demonstrates consistent, principled fidelity to the constitutional text and the Supreme Court's Second Amendment jurisprudence, even when — especially when — the Ninth Circuit majority takes a different path.

His dissent in **Duncan v. Bonta**, with its remarkable video component, exemplified not just the substance of his legal disagreement but his commitment to making his analysis accessible and grounded in practical reality. His dissents in the **Wolford v. Lopez** Hawaii vampire rule case, which is now before the Supreme Court, and in numerous other Ninth Circuit Second Amendment decisions, have established him as perhaps the most consequential Second Amendment voice on any federal circuit court.

## Implications

I have no doubt that Judge Lawrence Van Dyke is on the short list for any future vacancy on the United States Supreme Court that President Trump might fill. His judicial philosophy — originalist, textualist, and grounded in both legal rigor and practical common sense — is precisely what the Second Amendment needs on the nation's highest court.

The Second Amendment's future at the Supreme Court will be determined in large part by the composition of that Court. Justices who understand firearms as lived constitutional objects — not as abstractions to be managed through policy preferences — will produce jurisprudence that faithfully honors the Founders' text. Judge Van Dyke's combination of legal excellence and genuine personal engagement with the right to keep and bear arms makes him, in my assessment, one of the most compelling potential Supreme Court nominees in the country today.

For those in the Second Amendment community watching the Court's composition carefully — and we all should be — Judge Lawrence Van Dyke is a name to know well. He is already shaping the law from the Ninth Circuit. He may one day shape it from a far higher bench.
