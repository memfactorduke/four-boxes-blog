## Summary

The Second Amendment Foundation and the Firearms Policy Coalition have filed a joint petition for certiorari in the United States Supreme Court in **Schoenthal v. Raoul**, challenging Illinois's categorical prohibition on law-abiding citizens carrying firearms on public transportation. The petition asks the Court to define the constitutional limits of the "sensitive places" doctrine — which I regard as a euphemism for government-mandated gun-free zones — and to correct the Seventh Circuit's deeply flawed opinion upholding the ban. The case presents a major opportunity for the Supreme Court to provide lower courts with the principled, historically grounded framework they have been unable to develop on their own.

## Background

**Schoenthal v. Raoul** arises from the State of Illinois's flat statutory prohibition on ordinary, law-abiding citizens carrying firearms on public transportation systems. The United States District Court for the Northern District of Illinois reviewed this prohibition and concluded that it violated the Second Amendment. Applying the text-and-history methodology mandated by the Supreme Court in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, the district court found that Illinois could not demonstrate any historical tradition of firearms regulation that would justify a categorical ban on carrying arms in public transit settings.

Illinois appealed, and the Seventh Circuit reversed. The Seventh Circuit's opinion is remarkable for its analytical confusion. Rather than applying the **Bruen** methodology in a principled manner, the court resorted to a scattershot approach that, among other errors, cited late nineteenth-century private railroad company rules as supposed historical analogues for a twenty-first-century government prohibition.

## The Seventh Circuit's Methodological Errors

The Seventh Circuit's opinion in **Schoenthal** illustrates precisely the kind of lower court resistance to **Bruen** that has characterized post-**Bruen** Second Amendment litigation. The court's errors are multiple and significant.

First, the court relied on historical materials from the wrong time period. The relevant inquiry under **Bruen** and **Heller** is the understanding of the Second Amendment at the time of its ratification in 1791 — with some limited consideration of Reconstruction-era materials given the Fourteenth Amendment's incorporation of the right. The late nineteenth century — the era of industrial railroads and the Gilded Age — reflects conditions and understandings that are decades removed from the founding, and carries essentially no weight in the constitutional analysis.

Second, and perhaps most astonishingly, the Seventh Circuit relied on private railroad company regulations as historical analogues for a government regulation. This is constitutionally untenable. The Second Amendment is a constraint on government conduct — it limits what legislatures, executive agencies, and courts may do. It has no application to private conduct. The fact that a private railroad company in 1880 may have posted a rule prohibiting firearms in its passenger cars tells us precisely nothing about what the government may constitutionally prohibit in 2025. Private rules are not laws, and the **Bruen** methodology explicitly requires the government to identify a historical tradition of *government* firearms regulation — not private corporate policy.

## The Sensitive Places Doctrine: Origins and Abuse

Both **Heller** and **Bruen** acknowledged the existence of so-called "sensitive places" — specific locations where the government may restrict or prohibit carrying of firearms consistent with the Second Amendment. The Supreme Court identified three paradigmatic examples from the founding era: legislative chambers such as Congress, courthouses, and polling places.

That is the full scope of what the Supreme Court actually held on this question. Three categories of locations, all of them places where the historical record reflects specific justifications for restricting the introduction of firearms.

Lower courts — particularly those with anti-Second Amendment orientations — have exploited this narrow holding aggressively. They have invoked the "sensitive places" language to uphold firearms prohibitions in parks, restaurants serving alcohol, public housing developments, transit systems, entertainment venues, and a wide range of other locations that bear no meaningful resemblance to the three founding-era examples. The result has been precisely the "judicial interest balancing" that the Supreme Court condemned in **Heller** and **Bruen**, applied under the label of historical analysis.

## The Comprehensive Security Doctrine

The most principled theoretical framework for analyzing sensitive places claims — and the one I believe the Supreme Court should ultimately adopt — is the comprehensive security doctrine. Under this framework, a location qualifies as a "sensitive place" only when the government provides comprehensive alternative security arrangements that substitute for the individual's right of self-defense. Legislative chambers, courthouses, and polling places qualified under the founding-era understanding precisely because they were guarded by armed government officers who provided security for all persons within.

A public bus or train is not such a location. There is no comprehensive security apparatus on public transportation that substitutes for the individual's right to armed self-defense. Absent such a substitute, the government cannot simply declare a location "sensitive" and strip law-abiding citizens of their constitutional rights.

## The Cert Petition's Key Arguments

The Second Amendment Foundation and Firearms Policy Coalition's petition advances several powerful arguments for certiorari and for reversal on the merits.

The petition demonstrates that the lower courts have generated a chaotic and inconsistent body of doctrine on the sensitive places question. Different circuits have adopted wildly different approaches to identifying what qualifies as a sensitive place, producing results that vary dramatically depending on where in the country a law-abiding citizen chooses to live. This doctrinal disarray itself provides strong grounds for Supreme Court review.

The petition further argues — consistent with the historical record — that at the time of the founding, Americans were frequently *required*, not merely permitted, to carry firearms in places where the population might be vulnerable to attack. Laws mandating that citizens bring arms to church, to public gatherings, and to other assemblies where they could defend the community reflected a fundamentally different understanding of the relationship between armed citizens and public safety than the one reflected in Illinois's prohibition.

## Prospects for Certiorari

The question of whether the Supreme Court will grant certiorari in **Schoenthal** is genuinely uncertain. The merits overwhelmingly favor the Second Amendment — the Seventh Circuit's opinion is analytically indefensible, and a flat prohibition on carrying firearms on public transportation has no support in the founding-era historical record. From a purely legal standpoint, this case is what litigators sometimes call a "layup" for the Second Amendment.

The uncertainty arises from institutional considerations. The Supreme Court must weigh not only the legal merits of individual cases but also the sequencing of its Second Amendment jurisprudence. The Court may have strategic reasons — from its own institutional perspective, not mine — to address other Second Amendment questions before taking up sensitive places comprehensively. The absence of a circuit split on the public transportation question (no court outside the Seventh Circuit has yet ruled on this specific issue) may also affect the Court's calculus, though I would note that the absence of a circuit split in Second Amendment cases often reflects the geographic concentration of anti-gun legislation in jurisdictions dominated by Democratic-appointed judges, not genuine consensus on the legal question.

## Implications

**Schoenthal v. Raoul** presents the Supreme Court with a direct opportunity to rein in lower court abuse of the sensitive places doctrine and to provide the principled, historically grounded framework that courts across the country have been unable to develop on their own. The Seventh Circuit's reliance on private railroad regulations and late nineteenth-century materials to justify a flat ban on armed self-defense in public transportation is precisely the kind of analytical error that the Supreme Court must correct. Second Amendment practitioners and advocates should follow this petition closely, as the Court's response — whether it grants cert, denies cert, or calls for the views of the Solicitor General — will be a significant indicator of the Court's intentions for the current term's Second Amendment docket.
