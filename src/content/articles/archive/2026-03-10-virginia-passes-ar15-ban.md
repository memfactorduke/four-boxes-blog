## Summary

Virginia has enacted legislation prohibiting the sale, purchase, importation, manufacture, and transfer of so-called "assault weapons" — a category defined broadly enough to encompass the AR-15 and the overwhelming majority of modern semi-automatic centerfire rifles — together with a companion prohibition on magazines capable of holding more than fifteen rounds, effective July 1, 2026. In my analysis, both provisions are facially unconstitutional under *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022): the AR-15 is the most widely owned rifle in the United States, magazines in the restricted capacity range exist in the hundreds of millions in civilian hands, and the government cannot identify a founding-era historical tradition analogous to a categorical prohibition on either. Constitutional challenges are anticipated, and the legislation's fate will ultimately be determined by whether federal courts apply the *Bruen* methodology faithfully.

## Background

Virginia's legislative action follows the Democratic Party's consolidation of control over the House of Delegates, the State Senate, and the Governor's office in January 2026. Governor Abigail Spanberger is expected to sign the enacted legislation into law. The statute represents one of the most expansive state-level firearms restrictions enacted since the Supreme Court's *Bruen* decision established the text-history-tradition framework as the governing methodology for Second Amendment challenges.

The legislative context is significant for purposes of constitutional analysis because it illuminates the breadth of the restriction and the absence of any limiting principle that might save a narrowly tailored version of the law. The Virginia statute does not target a discrete category of unusually dangerous weapons based on objectively verifiable criteria; it targets the most common semi-automatic centerfire rifle configuration in civilian use, defined by a combination of features that appear in millions of lawfully owned firearms. As I have examined in comparable statutory schemes in California and Maryland, the feature-based definitional approach captures precisely those firearms that are most widely owned for lawful self-defense and hunting purposes, and does so without any demonstrated connection to a historical tradition of analogous regulation.

## The Legislation: Statutory Scope and Definitions

The Virginia statute prohibits, effective July 1, 2026, the sale, purchase, importation, manufacture, and transfer of firearms meeting its definition of "assault weapon." The prohibition encompasses:

- Any semi-automatic centerfire rifle or pistol with a fixed magazine capacity in excess of fifteen rounds
- Any semi-automatic centerfire rifle capable of accepting a detachable magazine and possessing one or more of the following features: a folding, telescoping, or collapsible stock; a thumbhole stock or pistol grip protruding beneath the action; a second handgrip or protruding grip that can be held by the non-trigger hand; a grenade launcher; or a threaded barrel capable of accepting a muzzle brake, compensator, sound suppressor, or flash suppressor

The companion provision prohibits the sale, transfer, and purchase of magazines capable of holding more than fifteen rounds, effective the same date. Both provisions operate prospectively on transactions; the legislation does not appear to require forfeiture of currently possessed firearms or magazines, though enforcement details remain to be developed through implementing guidance.

As I have documented in my analysis of comparable statutory frameworks, these definitional categories are sufficiently broad to capture the AR-15 platform in its standard commercial configuration, along with a substantial portion of the modern semi-automatic handgun and rifle market. The AR-15 is, by any available measure, the most commonly owned centerfire rifle in the United States — estimates of civilian ownership range from fifteen to twenty million or more. Magazines capable of holding more than fifteen rounds are standard equipment for many of the most widely sold handgun platforms. The practical scope of the Virginia statute therefore extends to the primary tools of civilian self-defense in the current market.

## Constitutional Analysis: The Bruen Framework Applied

The constitutional analysis proceeds from the *Bruen* methodology in two steps. First: does the regulated conduct or item fall within the plain text of the Second Amendment? Second, if so: can the government demonstrate a historical tradition of analogous regulation sufficient to justify the restriction?

On the first question, the answer is straightforward under current doctrine. *Heller* established that the Second Amendment protects arms "in common use at the time for lawful purposes like self-defense." 554 U.S. at 624. The Court's articulation of the "common use" standard was intended to distinguish protected arms from those the government may constitutionally prohibit — specifically, the "dangerous and unusual weapons" category that *Heller* identified as historically outside Second Amendment protection. The AR-15, with an estimated civilian ownership base in the tens of millions, and standard-capacity magazines, with civilian ownership in the hundreds of millions, cannot plausibly be characterized as "unusual." They are among the most widely owned items in civilian firearms ownership. Under *Heller*'s own framework, they are presumptively protected.

On the second question, the government must identify a founding-era historical tradition of prohibiting firearms analogous to those Virginia now bans. As I have examined in the academic and litigation literature on this question, no such tradition exists for semi-automatic rifles or for magazine capacity restrictions. The founding era knew nothing of detachable box magazines; the concept of limiting ammunition capacity as a distinct regulatory category is a twentieth-century development with no historical pedigree traceable to 1791, when the Second Amendment was ratified, or to 1868, when the Fourteenth Amendment incorporated it against the states. The *Bruen* Court was explicit: analogical reasoning must identify historical regulations that are "relevantly similar" to the modern restriction, both in the burden they impose and in the justification they reflect. A regulation for which no historical analog exists fails this test, and neither the AR-15 nor standard-capacity magazines have any analogous historical prohibition.

## The Federal Assault Weapons Ban: An Empirical Baseline

The Virginia statute's proponents have offered public safety justifications for the legislation, including assertions that restrictions on semi-automatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines will reduce the frequency or lethality of mass shooting incidents. In my review of the relevant empirical literature, these claims are not supported by the available evidence. The federal Assault Weapons Ban of 1994 — which imposed a ten-year prohibition on the manufacture and sale of many of the same categories of firearms now addressed by the Virginia statute — was the subject of multiple federally commissioned studies that found no statistically significant impact on the rate or severity of firearm violence generally or mass shooting incidents specifically. The legislative findings underlying the Virginia statute do not engage with this empirical record.

The constitutional significance of this empirical gap is limited but real. Under the *Bruen* framework, the government's burden is historical, not empirical: it must demonstrate a tradition of analogous regulation, not prove a present-day policy rationale. But the absence of empirical support for the stated public safety objectives is relevant to the broader constitutional question of whether the legislation's burdens on Second Amendment rights bear any rational connection to the harms the legislature seeks to address.

## The Supreme Court's Pending Cases and Their Relevance

The timing of Virginia's legislation coincides with a period of substantial activity at the United States Supreme Court on related Second Amendment questions. The Court currently has before it petitions for certiorari in **Duncan v. Bonta** (challenging California's magazine capacity restriction) and **Gator Custom Guns v. Wence** (challenging Washington State's magazine prohibition). The D.C. Court of Appeals has recently issued its ruling in **Benson v. United States** holding that the District's large-capacity magazine ban is facially unconstitutional, creating a direct conflict with prior circuit court rulings and substantially increasing the probability of Supreme Court review.

As I have analyzed in detail, the Court's resolution of the magazine capacity question — which presents closely related legal issues to those raised by Virginia's law — is likely to be dispositive of significant portions of the Virginia statute's constitutionality. If the Supreme Court holds, as *Benson* and the *Bruen* framework compel, that standard-capacity magazines are protected arms that cannot be categorically prohibited, Virginia's companion magazine restriction will require invalidation. The AR-15 question, which is addressed in petitions currently before the Court involving California's assault weapon ban, presents if anything a stronger case for constitutional protection given the firearm's extraordinary prevalence in civilian ownership.

## Implications

Virginia's enactment of a comprehensive semi-automatic rifle ban and magazine capacity restriction will generate litigation. The constitutional challenges that will be filed in the Eastern and Western Districts of Virginia will present courts with a direct application of the *Bruen* framework to legislation that, in my analysis, cannot survive that framework's demands. The state will be required to identify historical analogs for a categorical prohibition on the most widely owned rifle in America — a burden it cannot carry, because no such analogs exist.

The broader significance of the Virginia legislation is structural rather than specific. It illustrates the trajectory of state-level firearms regulation in jurisdictions where legislative majorities are willing to enact restrictions that are facially inconsistent with current Supreme Court doctrine, wagering that the litigation process will be slow enough and the lower courts sufficiently deferential to permit the restrictions to remain in effect for a meaningful period even if they are ultimately invalidated. The constitutional remedy for this dynamic is a Supreme Court willing to grant certiorari promptly in cases presenting clear conflicts with *Heller* and *Bruen*, and to issue rulings with sufficient clarity and breadth to foreclose the continuing legislative evasion that has characterized post-*Heller* firearms regulation in a substantial number of states. The cases currently pending before the Court provide the vehicle for exactly that kind of definitive constitutional clarification.
