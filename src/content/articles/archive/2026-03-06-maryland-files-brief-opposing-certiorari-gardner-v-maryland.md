## Summary

Maryland has filed a brief in opposition to certiorari in *Gardner v. Maryland*, urging the Supreme Court not to hear a case that could establish whether the Second Amendment protects the right to carry a loaded firearm while traveling through a state in which one does not hold a local permit. The petitioner, Eva Marie Gardner -- a Virginia concealed carry licensee who was arrested and convicted in Maryland while driving through the state en route to Pennsylvania -- argues that her prosecution violated the Second Amendment as I've analyzed under *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*. In my analysis of Maryland's brief, I find that the state contends the trial record was insufficiently developed on Second Amendment grounds, that no circuit split warrants review, and that pending federal legislation may render the issue moot. Each of these arguments, in my view, faces significant analytical challenges.

## The Facts of Gardner v. Maryland

Eva Marie Gardner, a longtime resident of Virginia, held a valid Virginia concealed carry permit and was traveling by car through Maryland to visit her mother in Pennsylvania. During the drive, Gardner was involved in a traffic accident on a Maryland highway. According to the record -- undisputed by both parties -- another driver struck Gardner's vehicle and forced her off the road. The other motorist, identified as Khaled Binafi, then approached Gardner's car in what she perceived to be a threatening manner.

Gardner retrieved the handgun she was lawfully licensed to carry in Virginia and displayed it to Binafi, who ceased his approach and returned to his own vehicle. Gardner simultaneously called 911, reported the accident, disclosed that she possessed a firearm, and explained that she had used it to protect herself. When police arrived, they determined that while Gardner's firearm was properly licensed in Virginia, she did not hold a Maryland permit. She was arrested, charged, and ultimately convicted of possessing a firearm in Maryland without a state-issued license. Her conviction was affirmed through the Maryland state appellate courts.

Gardner then filed a pro se petition for certiorari with the United States Supreme Court. In a procedural signal that at least one justice found the petition meritorious, the Court directed Maryland to file a response -- the brief now at issue.

## Maryland's Arguments in Opposition

I've analyzed the three principal arguments Maryland's brief advances in opposition to certiorari, and I find that each confronts meaningful obstacles.

### Undeveloped Trial Record

Maryland first contends that the Second Amendment issues were not adequately developed at trial, rendering the case a poor vehicle for Supreme Court review. As I see it, this argument conflates two distinct categories of factual inquiry well recognized in the law. The historical meaning and scope of the Second Amendment is what courts classify as a "legislative fact" -- a question concerning the content and development of law, constitutional history, and public policy. Legislative facts are not dependent on the evidentiary record developed at trial. They stand in contrast to "adjudicatory facts," which concern the specific conduct of the parties -- for example, whether a driver ran a red light or whether a physician committed malpractice.

In my examination of the Supreme Court's Second Amendment jurisprudence, I see that this distinction is confirmed. In *Heller*, *McDonald*, *Bruen*, *Rahimi*, and *Caetano*, the Court resolved foundational questions about the Second Amendment's historical meaning without requiring expert testimony or trial-level factual development on those issues. As I argue, Maryland's suggestion that Gardner's case is procedurally deficient because the trial court did not conduct an evidentiary hearing on eighteenth-century firearms regulation is inconsistent with the Court's established practice.

### No Circuit Split

Maryland next argues that no split of authority among the federal circuits or state courts of last resort warrants Supreme Court intervention. While the precise contours of a circuit split on this issue remain debatable, as I see it, the underlying legal question -- whether the Second Amendment protects the right to carry a firearm while traveling interstate -- is far from novel. Congress itself recognized the problem when it enacted the Firearms Owners' Protection Act of 1986 (FOPA), which provides a limited safe harbor for individuals transporting firearms through jurisdictions with restrictive gun laws, provided the firearms are unloaded and locked in a container separate from ammunition. In my analysis, the very existence of FOPA demonstrates that the tension between state licensing regimes and interstate travel with firearms has long been a matter of national concern, even if the federal courts have not yet produced a clean doctrinal split on the post-*Bruen* constitutional question.

### Pending Federal Legislation

Finally, Maryland points to pending federal legislation on nationwide concealed carry reciprocity as a reason for the Court to deny review. In my analysis, this argument is speculative on multiple levels. No reciprocity bill has been enacted into law, and the legislative prospects for any such measure remain uncertain. More fundamentally, even if a reciprocity statute were signed into law tomorrow, it would have no retroactive effect on Gardner's conviction, which arose under a prior legal regime. A prospective statutory fix cannot moot a live criminal conviction, as I see it.

## The Bruen Framework and the Travelers' Exception

In my analysis of *Gardner*, the central constitutional question is whether Maryland can demonstrate a historical tradition of firearms regulation sufficient to justify requiring a state-specific license from individuals who are merely traveling through the state and who already hold valid permits from their home jurisdictions.

Under the two-step framework established in *Bruen*, I analyze the case as follows. The analysis begins with the Second Amendment's plain text. The text protects "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms." The right to "bear" arms, as the Court explained in *Heller* and reaffirmed in *Bruen*, encompasses the right to carry firearms in public for purposes of self-defense. Gardner was carrying a firearm in public for precisely that purpose. The plain text is therefore implicated, and the burden shifts to the government to identify a historical tradition of analogous firearms regulation.

As I argue, Maryland faces a formidable challenge at the second step. The historical record contains no established tradition of prohibiting travelers from carrying firearms as they passed through a jurisdiction. To the contrary, the available evidence points in the opposite direction. Colonial and founding-era legal sources recognized exceptions and exemptions for travelers, permitting individuals passing through a jurisdiction to carry arms even where local residents faced certain restrictions. This travelers' exception appears in legal sources predating the founding and persists through the early Republic. Far from supporting Maryland's position, the historical tradition affirmatively favors Gardner's claim, as I've found in my analysis.

## The May-Issue Problem

As I've analyzed it, a separate but closely related argument further undermines Maryland's position. Gardner's arrest occurred in 2021, and her conviction followed in early 2022 -- approximately five months before the Supreme Court decided *Bruen* in June 2022. At the time of Gardner's arrest and conviction, Maryland operated a "may-issue" concealed carry licensing regime, under which state officials exercised broad discretion to deny permits to applicants who could not demonstrate a particularized "good and substantial reason" for carrying a firearm. In *Bruen*, the Court struck down New York's virtually identical regime and specifically identified Maryland as one of six additional states operating under the same unconstitutional framework.

As I see it, this timing creates a significant problem for Maryland. Even if the Court were to conclude that states may constitutionally require travelers to obtain a local permit, Gardner could not have obtained one. Maryland's pre-*Bruen* licensing regime -- by the Supreme Court's own subsequent determination -- was unconstitutional. Maryland convicted Gardner for failing to obtain a permit that the state was constitutionally prohibited from withholding on a discretionary basis. The circularity of this position is difficult to defend, in my view.

## Connection to NYSRPA v. City of New York

As I've examined it, the interstate travel dimension of *Gardner* also echoes the issues presented in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. City of New York*, 590 U.S. 336 (2020). In that case, the Court granted certiorari to consider whether New York City's regulation restricting the transport of licensed firearms outside city limits violated the Second Amendment. Before the case could be decided on the merits, New York City and New York State amended the challenged regulations, and the Court ultimately vacated and remanded on mootness grounds. The core question in that case -- whether the Second Amendment protects the right to travel with firearms -- was never resolved. *Gardner* presents a vehicle to reach that question, this time in a posture that cannot easily be mooted through legislative revision, because the petitioner has already been convicted, as I see it.

## Outlook

As I see it, *Gardner v. Maryland* presents a combination of elements that may prove attractive to the Court: a sympathetic petitioner with a clean record who used a firearm for its intended self-defense purpose, a factual scenario that implicates the right to bear arms in its most straightforward application, and a legal question that *Bruen* framed but did not resolve. The Court's earlier direction to Maryland to file a response -- an action that required the interest of at least one justice -- suggests the petition is receiving serious consideration. If certiorari is granted, the case could become the next significant step toward establishing whether the Second Amendment requires some form of interstate recognition of concealed carry permits, with implications reaching well beyond the facts of Gardner's prosecution, in my view.
