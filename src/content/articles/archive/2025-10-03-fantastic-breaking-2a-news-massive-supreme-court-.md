## Summary

In a landmark development for Second Amendment jurisprudence, the United States Supreme Court has granted certiorari in **Wolford v. Lopez** — the case challenging Hawaii's no-carry default rule, commonly referred to as the "vampire rule." This grant of certiorari is major news for every law-abiding gun owner in America, because the constitutional question at stake directly affects whether states may effectively nullify the right to carry a firearm in public by requiring affirmative consent from private property owners before a licensed permit holder may enter any business or commercial establishment. The question the Supreme Court has agreed to decide will also provide significant guidance on the proper application of the **Bruen** framework's sensitive-places doctrine. I want to analyze exactly what the cert grant means, how the circuit split arose, what the question presented actually asks, and why the Trump administration's Department of Justice played an important and underappreciated role in securing this critical grant of review.

## Background

The vampire rule gets its name from the folklore conceit that a vampire cannot enter a home without first being invited. Hawaii's no-carry default statute applies a comparable logic to concealed-carry permit holders: you cannot enter any private property open to the public — a grocery store, a gas station, a movie theater, a shopping mall — while carrying a lawfully permitted firearm unless the property owner has affirmatively invited you in, so to speak, by granting express permission for firearms on the premises.

This statutory design is not a coincidence or an oversight. It is a deliberate strategy by anti-Second Amendment state legislatures to achieve, through nominally neutral property-rights language, what they could not achieve through outright prohibition: the practical elimination of carry rights in all the places where ordinary Americans actually spend their daily lives. The statute does not formally ban carry. It merely makes it effectively impossible by defaulting to prohibition and requiring affirmative opt-in permission from a universe of property owners who will never think to grant it.

The statute was enacted in Hawaii following the Supreme Court's landmark decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, which struck down New York's requirement that carry-permit applicants demonstrate a particularized need for self-defense. After **Bruen** eliminated the "may issue" regime that had functionally banned carry for ordinary citizens, anti-gun jurisdictions sought new mechanisms to achieve the same outcome. Hawaii's vampire rule is one of those mechanisms.

## The Circuit Split That Compelled Review

The most important procedural predicate for the Supreme Court's cert grant is the genuine and intractable circuit split that exists on the constitutionality of the no-carry default rule.

The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit — sitting in San Francisco and having appellate jurisdiction over Hawaii, California, and several other western states — addressed the no-carry default rule in two separate cases involving the California and Hawaii versions of the rule. In the California cases, a Ninth Circuit panel held that the California no-carry default rule was unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The government had failed to identify a historical tradition, at the time of the Founding or in the Reconstruction era, of requiring armed citizens to obtain affirmative permission from private property owners before entering publicly accessible commercial premises.

Yet in the **Wolford** case — involving Hawaii's version of the identical legal rule — a different Ninth Circuit panel reached the opposite conclusion. The same court of appeals, applying the same constitutional framework to the same legal question, held that Hawaii's no-carry default rule was constitutional. This intra-circuit conflict is highly unusual and creates genuine legal uncertainty even within the Ninth Circuit's own jurisdiction.

The conflict deepened when the United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit addressed a related question in **Antonyuk v. James**, the challenge to New York's post-**Bruen** carry restrictions. The Second Circuit's analysis of the sensitive-places doctrine differed in important respects from the Ninth Circuit's analyses, creating an inter-circuit conflict as well.

The combination of an intra-circuit conflict within the Ninth Circuit and a broader inter-circuit conflict created the precise conditions under which the Supreme Court's intervention is both warranted and necessary. The Court cannot allow the same constitutional rule to mean different things depending on which state a gun owner happens to live in.

## Alan Beck's Cert Petition and the DOJ's Amicus Support

The cert petition in **Wolford v. Lopez** was filed by Alan Beck, a highly skilled Second Amendment attorney based in Hawaii. Beck's petition was well-crafted, clearly presenting the question and the circuit conflict in terms that made a compelling case for Supreme Court review.

Equally important — and deserving of specific recognition — was the decision by the Trump administration's Department of Justice to file an amicus curiae brief in support of the cert petition. The brief was submitted on behalf of Solicitor General John Sauer, the head of the DOJ's Civil Rights Division, and other senior officials. The brief urged the Supreme Court to grant certiorari in **Wolford** and to resolve the constitutional question definitively in favor of the Second Amendment.

The Solicitor General's recommendation to grant certiorari carries significant weight with the Supreme Court. The Court regularly follows the Solicitor General's view on whether to grant or deny cert, particularly in cases of constitutional significance. The Trump DOJ's support for the **Wolford** cert petition was, in my judgment, a meaningful factor in the Court's decision to grant review. Those who are inclined to criticize the Trump administration's handling of Second Amendment issues would do well to note this instance of genuine and effective support.

## The Question Presented

The question the Supreme Court has agreed to decide is as follows: whether the Ninth Circuit erred in holding — in direct conflict with the Second Circuit — that Hawaii may presumptively prohibit the carry of handguns by licensed concealed-carry permit holders on private property open to the public unless the property owner affirmatively provides express permission to the handgun carrier.

Several observations about this question are worth making. First, the question is framed as whether the Ninth Circuit "erred" — a framing that, while not determinative, tends to signal that the Court is skeptical of the lower court's conclusion. Second, the question explicitly references the circuit conflict, confirming that the Court is taking the case to resolve a genuine split of authority among the lower courts, not merely to revisit a legal question already settled. Third, the question focuses specifically on the default-to-prohibition aspect of the rule — the feature that makes carry effectively impossible in practice even for licensed permit holders.

## Why the Interlocutory Grant Signals Broader Trends

As I noted in discussing this grant, the Supreme Court took **Wolford** on an interlocutory basis — the case had not yet proceeded to final judgment in the lower courts. I have previously analyzed the Court's general preference for taking cases on a complete record after final judgment, and I acknowledge that I predicted the Court would not grant cert in **Wolford** for that reason. I was wrong, and I am glad to have been wrong.

The interlocutory grant in **Wolford** is not an isolated event. When examined alongside other procedural choices the Court has made in the Second Amendment context, I believe it represents a developing trend: the Court is signaling a willingness to intervene at earlier stages of litigation when the constitutional questions are sufficiently clear and the lower courts are demonstrably failing to apply the **Bruen** framework faithfully. This is an important trend for the Second Amendment community to understand and to build on in future litigation strategy.

## What to Expect on the Merits

When the Supreme Court reaches the merits of **Wolford v. Lopez**, the constitutional analysis should be straightforward under the **Bruen** framework. The government bears the burden of identifying a historical tradition, at the time of the Founding or in the Reconstruction era, of prohibiting armed citizens from entering private property open to the public without affirmative permission from property owners.

No such historical tradition exists. In colonial and founding-era America, a law-abiding citizen carrying arms for self-defense could enter any tavern, market, trading post, or other place of public business without first obtaining the proprietor's express consent to carry. The concept of requiring affirmative permission from a property owner as a precondition for exercising Second Amendment rights on premises open to the general public was wholly foreign to the legal culture of the Founding era. The government will be unable to meet its historical burden, and the vampire rule should be struck down.

## Implications

The Supreme Court's grant of certiorari in **Wolford v. Lopez** is one of the most consequential Second Amendment developments in the current term. For the millions of Americans who live in Hawaii and in other states that have enacted similar no-carry default rules — including New York, California, Illinois, and others — the outcome of this case will determine whether they can exercise their Second Amendment rights in the everyday places where they live their lives.

The constitutional answer, I am confident, will come down on the side of the Second Amendment. The vampire rule is an anti-constitutional gimmick designed to nullify carry rights through an impractical and historically groundless permission requirement. The Supreme Court, with its current strong majority committed to the **Bruen** framework, should not hesitate to strike it down.
