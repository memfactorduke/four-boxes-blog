## Summary

On September 30, 2025, the United States Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi and the leadership of the Civil Rights Division, filed what I consider one of the most consequential legal actions in the modern history of the Second Amendment: the first-ever affirmative lawsuit by the DOJ filed in support of gun owners and against a recalcitrant local jurisdiction. The suit targets Los Angeles County and the Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department for their systemic and unreasonable delays in processing concealed carry weapon (CCW) permit applications — delays that constitute a pattern and practice of infringing upon the Second Amendment rights of law-abiding citizens.

## Background

The right to keep and bear arms, as affirmed by the United States Supreme Court in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008) and incorporated against the states in **McDonald v. City of Chicago** (2010), is a fundamental constitutional right. In **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022), the Supreme Court further confirmed that this right extends to carrying firearms in public for self-defense, and that permitting regimes must not function as mechanisms to deny this right to ordinary, law-abiding citizens.

Notwithstanding these binding precedents, Los Angeles County has for years operated a concealed carry permitting system characterized by extraordinary and unjustified delays. On March 27, 2025, the DOJ's Civil Rights Division initiated what it described as the first-of-its-kind Second Amendment investigation, prompted by numerous complaints from law-abiding applicants who had been subjected to unreasonable delays in CCW permitting decisions by the Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department.

Following an analysis of data and documents spanning over 8,000 CCW permit applications, the Civil Rights Division concluded that the Sheriff's Department had engaged in a pattern and practice of infringing upon the Second Amendment rights of law-abiding citizens seeking to exercise their constitutional right to carry a firearm for self-defense.

## The DOJ's Formal Complaint

The Civil Rights Division filed its affirmative lawsuit against Los Angeles County and the Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department, seeking relief on behalf of law-abiding applicants who have been denied the timely processing of their permit applications. In announcing the lawsuit, Attorney General Bondi stated: "The Second Amendment protects the fundamental constitutional right of law-abiding citizens to bear arms. Los Angeles County's unreasonable delays in processing CCW applications are a direct infringement of that right, and the Department of Justice will not stand idle while any jurisdiction tramples upon the liberties guaranteed by our Constitution."

The lawsuit invokes the Civil Rights Division's mandate to enforce constitutional rights and seeks declaratory and injunctive relief requiring the Sheriff's Department to process permit applications within a reasonable and constitutionally permissible timeframe.

## Historical Significance

I submit that this lawsuit represents a watershed moment in Second Amendment jurisprudence and enforcement. Not since the Department of Justice intervened against southern states that engaged in massive resistance to court-ordered desegregation following **Brown v. Board of Education** — which is to say, not since the 1950s — has the federal government deployed the full weight of the Civil Rights Division against a state or local jurisdiction for systematic defiance of a fundamental constitutional right established by the Supreme Court.

The parallel is not merely rhetorical. In both instances, a jurisdiction has chosen to construct bureaucratic obstacles designed to frustrate the practical exercise of a constitutional right. In both instances, the federal government has been compelled to intervene because the jurisdiction refuses to comply with controlling Supreme Court precedent. And in both instances, the fundamental message from Washington, D.C. is unambiguous: no local or state authority may nullify a right confirmed by the Supreme Court of the United States.

## Legal Theory and Expected Outcome

The DOJ's legal theory rests on solid constitutional ground. Under **Heller**, **McDonald**, and **Bruen**, the Second Amendment is not a second-class right. A permitting system that operates with unconscionable delays — particularly when those delays serve the functional purpose of denying a permit without formally refusing it — is constitutionally indistinguishable from an outright prohibition on carrying. The data and documents reviewed by the Civil Rights Division, encompassing over 8,000 applications, provide a robust evidentiary record of systemic misconduct.

The relief sought — compelling the Sheriff's Department to process applications within a reasonable timeframe — is well within the court's equitable power, and the factual predicate established by the DOJ's six-month investigation is formidable. I am of the view that this case, if litigated to conclusion, represents a strong candidate for summary judgment in favor of the United States and the permit applicants whose rights have been violated.

## Implications

The significance of this lawsuit transcends the immediate dispute. By deploying the Civil Rights Division in affirmative support of Second Amendment rights, the Trump administration has established several important precedents.

First, it signals that the federal government will treat the right to keep and bear arms with the same seriousness it applies to other civil rights — and that local jurisdictions that defy constitutional mandates will face federal legal consequences, not merely private litigation.

Second, this action places every jurisdiction in the country on notice. Cities and counties that have constructed delay-based permitting regimes, onerous fee structures, or other functional obstacles to the exercise of Second Amendment rights now face the prospect of a federal civil rights investigation and the attendant litigation costs, reputational consequences, and injunctive relief that flows therefrom.

Third, and perhaps most significantly for the long-term development of Second Amendment doctrine, the DOJ's investigation and the data it has assembled — over 8,000 CCW applications analyzed — may prove invaluable in related litigation across the country, providing a documented record of the deliberate and unconstitutional nature of delay-based obstruction.

I have long argued that the Second Amendment requires not merely formal legal recognition but genuine, practical enforcement. Today, the Department of Justice has taken a historic step toward making that enforcement a reality.
