During oral argument in **Louisiana v. Callais** — a major case before the United States Supreme Court addressing whether Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act, as applied to require the creation of majority-minority congressional districts, violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment — Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson drew a comparison between the situation of African-American voters and the situation of disabled persons protected by the Americans with Disabilities Act. The comparison has attracted widespread attention and criticism. In my analysis, the comparison is not merely politically awkward; it is legally erroneous, and examining precisely why it fails provides important insight into the constitutional question at stake in **Callais** — a case that, if decided in favor of Louisiana, could be among the most significant victories for the Second Amendment community in recent history, even though it does not involve firearms directly.

## Background: The Stakes in Louisiana v. Callais

The case before the Supreme Court involves a fundamental question: whether Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act of 1965, as amended in the 1980s, violates the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment by requiring states to draw congressional district lines in a manner that creates majority-minority districts — effectively mandating race-conscious redistricting in order to ensure that African-American voters constitute a majority within certain congressional districts.

The Republican Party in Louisiana argues that courts applying Section 2 have, for decades, weaponized the statute to force red states and Republican-controlled legislatures to carve out intentionally race-conscious congressional districts — districts whose raison d'être is to guarantee, for all practical purposes, that an African-American representative will hold those seats. Louisiana estimates that upward of nineteen such congressional districts have been created nationally under Section 2 requirements, all of which systematically benefit the Democratic Party.

The constitutional argument is this: the Voting Rights Act is a statute, which means it is subordinate in the legal hierarchy to the United States Constitution. The Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause provides that all Americans, regardless of race, are entitled to the equal protection of the laws. Forcing states to draw district lines along racial lines — whether to favor or to disfavor any particular racial group — constitutes race discrimination. Race discrimination by government actors violates the Equal Protection Clause. Therefore, to the extent Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act compels race-conscious redistricting, it may be unconstitutional.

## Why This Case Matters for the Second Amendment

I want to be direct about why I am covering a voting rights case on this platform, as some viewers may wonder what redistricting has to do with the right to keep and bear arms.

The answer is straightforward: a Supreme Court decision invalidating the race-conscious redistricting mandate of Section 2 would almost certainly result in the elimination of those nineteen-plus majority-minority congressional districts. Those districts currently elect Democrats with near-certainty. If they were dissolved and replaced by district configurations drawn without mandatory racial engineering, many of those seats would become competitive or swing Republican. The resulting shift in the composition of the House of Representatives — potentially producing a substantially larger Republican majority for years if not decades — would have profound implications for the fate of every piece of federal firearms legislation, from universal background check proposals to assault weapons bans.

A stronger, more durable Republican majority in the House, built on districts drawn without unconstitutional racial mandates, is among the most reliable structural guarantors of Second Amendment rights in the legislative arena. The connection is direct, significant, and overlooked by most Second Amendment commentary.

## Justice Jackson's ADA Analogy: The Precise Legal Error

During oral argument, Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson drew a comparison between the Voting Rights Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act. Her apparent theory was that just as the ADA requires affirmative accommodations to ensure that disabled Americans can access the same public spaces and services as non-disabled Americans — thereby remedying a structural disadvantage — Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act similarly provides remediation for structural disadvantages experienced by African-American voters. If one accepts the ADA analogy, the argument follows, then there is nothing constitutionally suspect about Section 2's requirements.

In my analysis, the analogy fails on multiple independent grounds.

**The nature of the protected characteristic.** Race and disability are categorically different under constitutional law. Race is what constitutional scholars call a "suspect classification" — an immutable characteristic that has been the basis of historical oppression and that has no legitimate relevance to an individual's capacity, merit, or qualifications for civic participation. The Equal Protection Clause's core historical purpose, enshrined through the sacrifice of approximately 600,000 American lives in the Civil War and through the Reconstruction amendments, was to guarantee a colorblind rule of law in which government treats all persons equally regardless of race.

Disability is not treated as a suspect classification under equal protection doctrine. Disability is, in many contexts, both mutable and relevant to a person's capacity to perform particular functions. Reasonable accommodations for disability do not require the government to pretend that disability does not exist; they require adjustments to ensure that disabled individuals can access the same opportunities as non-disabled individuals. The ADA reflects a fundamentally different constitutional logic than the Equal Protection Clause's mandate of racial neutrality.

**Timing and historical context.** The timing of the legislation is also analytically significant. The ADA was enacted in 1990 — after decades of evolving civil rights jurisprudence in which courts, legislatures, and society had grappled with the unique challenges facing disabled Americans in a society not designed with their needs in mind. The legislative record supporting the ADA was extensive, and the constitutional basis for the accommodations it mandated was rooted in the identified pattern of systematic exclusion.

The Voting Rights Act was originally enacted in 1965 to address a specific, documented pattern of intentional racial disenfranchisement — poll taxes, literacy tests, grandfather clauses, and physical intimidation — that prevented African-American citizens from exercising their constitutional right to vote. The original Act was constitutionally justified because it remedied demonstrably intentional, race-based exclusion from the franchise. The 1982 amendments, however, added the disparate impact prong — the provision that forms the basis for mandatory majority-minority districting requirements — which does not require any showing of intentional discrimination. It requires only a statistical showing that a particular voting rule produces different electoral outcomes for members of different racial groups.

The expansion from intentional discrimination to disparate impact constitutes a doctrinal leap that the ADA analogy does not bridge. The ADA addresses physical and structural barriers to access — barriers that are facially neutral but disadvantage disabled people. Section 2's disparate impact provision addresses facially neutral voting rules by demanding race-conscious remediation even absent any discriminatory intent, thereby imposing the very race-consciousness the Fourteenth Amendment was designed to prohibit.

**The Second Amendment parallel.** The reason I raise Justice Jackson's analogy in this forum is that the doctrinal methodology she employed — using analogical reasoning to justify departures from constitutional text and original meaning — is precisely the methodology that anti-Second Amendment advocates employ when they ask courts to read the Second Amendment as applying only to militias, or to uphold gun control laws through interest balancing rather than historical tradition analysis. In both contexts, the error is the same: using a superficially plausible analogy to circumvent the actual text and historical meaning of the constitutional provision at stake.

## The Equal Protection Principle at Stake

The argument Louisiana is making — and which has, in my assessment, a very substantial chance of prevailing — is that the Equal Protection Clause means what it says: all persons, regardless of race, are entitled to equal protection of the laws. A law that requires race-conscious government action — even for remedial or compensatory purposes — violates that principle. It does not matter whether the discrimination is characterized as favorable or unfavorable to any particular racial group. The Constitution prohibits race discrimination, not merely discrimination against disfavored groups.

This is the same principle that the Supreme Court applied in **Students for Fair Admissions v. Harvard** — the 2023 decision striking down race-conscious college admissions programs. The constitutional logic is consistent: government may not allocate benefits, burdens, or entitlements on the basis of race.

## Implications

If the Supreme Court rules in Louisiana's favor in **Callais** and holds that Section 2's disparate impact provision — or its application to require majority-minority districting — violates the Equal Protection Clause, the political and legal consequences will be enormous. States that have drawn majority-minority districts under Section 2 compulsion will be free to redraw those districts on race-neutral criteria, and the resulting congressional map could shift the balance of power in the House for a generation. For the Second Amendment community, that political shift would be among the most durable and consequential victories achievable through constitutional litigation — one that would protect the right to keep and bear arms in the legislative arena for years to come.
