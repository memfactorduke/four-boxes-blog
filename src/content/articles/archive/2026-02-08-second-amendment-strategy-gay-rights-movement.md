## Summary

The gay rights movement's methodical, decades-long litigation campaign—from the loss in **Bowers v. Hardwick** in 1986 to the triumph in **Obergefell v. Hodges** in 2015—offers the most instructive template available for Second Amendment advocates seeking to expand and consolidate constitutional protections for the right to keep and bear arms. In my analysis, advocates must resist the temptation to rush unsympathetic plaintiffs or politically charged fact patterns to the Supreme Court, a mistake that the transgender rights movement has arguably made. Instead, the path forward is incremental, disciplined, and deliberate.

## Background

In 1986, in a case arising out of Georgia, the Supreme Court decided **Bowers v. Hardwick**, 478 U.S. 186 (1986), holding that the Constitution confers no fundamental right to engage in homosexual sodomy. For the gay rights movement, this was a devastating starting point—a direct constitutional repudiation of a core claim. And yet, from that low point, the movement orchestrated one of the most successful constitutional litigation campaigns in American history, culminating less than thirty years later in a ruling that placed same-sex marriage on equal constitutional footing with opposite-sex marriage.

The lesson is not merely historical. It is strategic, and it applies directly to ongoing Second Amendment litigation involving questions such as the constitutionality of the felon-in-possession prohibition under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) and the broader question of whether certain categories of weapons—most prominently post-1986 machine guns—can be brought before the Supreme Court in a posture that maximizes the likelihood of a favorable ruling.

## The Gay Rights Roadmap: Step by Step, Brick by Brick

The gay rights movement did not attempt to win everything at once. Following the loss in **Bowers**, movement lawyers spent the next decade building intermediate precedent. In 1996, in **Romer v. Evans**, 517 U.S. 620 (1996), the Supreme Court struck down Colorado's Amendment 2, a state constitutional provision that had prohibited localities from enacting anti-discrimination protections for gay and lesbian individuals. The Court held that the amendment violated the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment by placing gay citizens on an unequal legal footing relative to all other Coloradans. This was not yet a ruling on marriage—it was not even a ruling on intimate conduct—but it established the constitutional premise that gays could not be subjected to blanket legal disadvantage.

Seven years later, in **Lawrence v. Texas**, 539 U.S. 558 (2003), the Court took the next step, expressly overruling **Bowers** and holding that the Constitution's due process protections extend to consensual intimate conduct between adults of the same sex. Again, not marriage—but by this point the movement had secured recognition that gay individuals possess constitutionally protected liberty interests in their intimate lives.

In 2013, **United States v. Windsor**, 570 U.S. 744 (2013), presented the question whether Section 3 of the Defense of Marriage Act—which denied federal benefits to legally married same-sex couples—violated the Fifth Amendment's due process guarantee. The Court struck down Section 3, finding that it impermissibly denigrated the relationships of same-sex couples who had legally married under state law. This was the penultimate step.

Finally, in **Obergefell v. Hodges**, 576 U.S. 644 (2015), the Court held that the Fourteenth Amendment requires states to license and recognize same-sex marriages on the same terms as opposite-sex marriages. From 1986 to 2015: loss to total victory, achieved through a deliberate sequence of increasingly strong precedents, each built on the last.

## The Contrast: The Transgender Movement's Strategic Overcorrection

In my analysis, the transgender movement provides a cautionary counterexample. Rather than building an incremental record through sympathetic plaintiffs and carefully chosen legal theories, transgender litigation has frequently presented courts with maximally contested fact patterns—particularly cases involving gender-affirming medical interventions for minors—before the legal groundwork was sufficiently developed. The result has been a series of adverse rulings at the appellate level and a Supreme Court that appears unreceptive to claims that outpace consolidated doctrine.

The difference in outcomes tracks directly to litigation strategy, not to the underlying strength of the constitutional claim. The gay rights movement won by controlling the sequencing of its cases. The transgender movement has struggled, in part, because it has not.

## The Application to Second Amendment Litigation

I have reviewed the current state of Second Amendment litigation with this strategic framework in mind, and the parallels are instructive and cautionary in equal measure.

Consider the ongoing questions surrounding 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1), which prohibits felons from possessing firearms. Following **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), and the subsequent decision in **United States v. Rahimi**, 602 U.S. 680 (2024), lower courts are grappling with which historical analogues are sufficient to sustain categorical disarmament. The cases being pressed through the circuits vary dramatically in plaintiff sympathy. Some defendants have genuinely compelling facts; others are precisely the kind of individuals—convicted of serious violent felonies—whom the Supreme Court will be least inclined to help.

If an unsympathetic case arrives at the Court first and produces a bad precedent, it will constrain the development of more favorable doctrine for a generation. The gay rights movement understood this: in the early years, their lawyers were extraordinarily selective about which cases to push forward and which to let lie.

The parallel concern attaches to machine gun litigation. There are serious structural constitutional arguments—rooted in the Commerce Clause and Article I enumerated powers doctrine—against the Hughes Amendment's post-1986 ban on civilian machine gun transfers. Judge Don Willett of the Fifth Circuit has been raising these arguments in concurrences, and they deserve attention. But the question of when, and in what factual posture, to press such a claim to the Supreme Court is one that demands the same level of strategic discipline the gay rights movement exercised between 1986 and 2015.

## Implications

Second Amendment advocates who care about long-term doctrinal development must internalize the gay rights movement's core strategic insight: the sequence and framing of cases matters as much as the underlying legal theory. Advancing claims through sympathetic plaintiffs with clean facts, building intermediate precedent that constrains future adverse decisions, and avoiding the temptation to rush an adverse-fact case to the Supreme Court because it presents an "interesting" legal question—these disciplines are what separated a movement that achieved full constitutional recognition from one that has struggled despite arguably strong underlying claims.

The right to keep and bear arms is among the most clearly enumerated rights in the Constitution. The strategic infrastructure being built around it in the post-**Bruen** era is genuinely promising. But the history of successful constitutional litigation teaches that patience, sequencing, and plaintiff selection are not secondary concerns. They are the difference between winning and losing.
