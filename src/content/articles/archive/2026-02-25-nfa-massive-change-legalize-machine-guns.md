## Background

The Firearm Owners Protection Act of 1986, Pub. L. 99-308, included the provision commonly known as the Hughes Amendment, codified at 18 U.S.C. § 922(o). The provision states, with limited exceptions, that it is unlawful for any person to transfer or possess a machine gun manufactured after May 19, 1986. The effect of this prohibition, combined with the pre-existing National Firearms Act registration requirements, has been to restrict civilian ownership of fully automatic firearms to the finite corpus of pre-1986 registered weapons — a closed registry that has resulted in market prices for registered machine guns that render such ownership largely inaccessible to ordinary citizens.

From a constitutional standpoint, the Hughes Amendment has been the subject of persistent scholarly and judicial skepticism. The provision prohibits the purely intrastate possession of a firearm without requiring the government to prove any connection to interstate commerce. This absence of a jurisdictional element — the statutory hook that links a federal prohibition to Congress's Article I, Section 8 Commerce Clause authority — has been identified by multiple commentators and at least one current Supreme Court Justice as a potentially fatal defect.

## The Hughes Amendment's Article I Deficiency

As I have examined it, the constitutional vulnerability of 18 U.S.C. § 922(o) is most precisely captured in Justice Samuel Alito's dissent in *United States v. Rybar*, 103 F.3d 273 (3d Cir. 1996), written when he served on the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit. Justice Alito observed that the provision regulating intrastate possession of machine guns is structurally identical to the statute struck down by the Supreme Court in *United States v. Lopez*, 514 U.S. 549 (1995), which invalidated the Gun-Free School Zones Act. Both statutes, as Justice Alito noted, "departing from the mode of prior federal criminal statutes governing firearms possession, lack a jurisdictional element" — that is, neither required federal prosecutors to establish that the prohibited conduct occurred "in or affecting interstate commerce."

Justice Alito further noted that when Congress enacted the Hughes Amendment, it "made no findings regarding the effect of the intrastate activity regulated" on interstate commerce — a deficiency the Supreme Court had identified in *Lopez* as undercutting any rational basis inference that could otherwise supply the necessary constitutional foundation. In my assessment, these observations were analytically correct in 1996 and remain so today. The Supreme Court's subsequent decision in *United States v. Morrison*, 529 U.S. 598 (2000), reinforced the principle that Congress may not regulate purely intrastate, non-economic activity under the Commerce Clause simply by asserting an attenuated connection to interstate markets. The Hughes Amendment, which regulates the intrastate possession of a tangible object without any jurisdictional element, sits uncomfortably within the constitutional limits established by *Lopez* and *Morrison*.

## West Virginia's Legislative Strategy and Its Two-Pronged Legal Posture

West Virginia is considering legislation, developed in collaboration with Gun Owners of America, that would authorize the state to transfer or sell post-1986 machine guns to private citizens. The statutory basis for this approach derives from the explicit exemption within the Hughes Amendment itself. Section 922(o)(2)(A) provides that the general prohibition on post-1986 machine gun transfers "does not apply with respect to — (A) a transfer to or by, or possession by or under the authority of, the United States or any department or agency thereof or a State, or a department, agency, or political subdivision thereof."

In my reading of this provision, West Virginia's proposed program falls squarely within the statutory text. The state qualifies as a "State" within the meaning of the exemption. A transfer from the state to a private citizen pursuant to state-issued authorization constitutes a transfer "under the authority of" a State. The legislative history and plain text of the exemption support this reading.

If the Department of Justice challenges West Virginia's program, the state would have two analytically independent arguments available to it.

### First: Statutory Compliance

West Virginia's primary argument would rest on straightforward statutory interpretation: the program is expressly authorized by the plain language of 18 U.S.C. § 922(o)(2)(A). The exemption does not restrict state transfers to government use — it broadly exempts transfers by or under the authority of a state. To the extent DOJ argues that the exemption is narrower in scope, that argument would require reading limiting language into the statute that does not appear in the text. Under standard rules of statutory construction, and under the Supreme Court's textualist methodology, the plain language governs.

### Second: Constitutional Invalidity

Should a court reject the statutory compliance argument and hold that West Virginia's program violates § 922(o), the state would then be positioned to raise its alternative constitutional argument: the Hughes Amendment is void ab initio because Congress lacked Article I authority to enact it. As I have argued above, the provision lacks a jurisdictional element, Congress made no commerce-nexus findings upon enactment, and the regulated conduct — intrastate possession of a discrete physical object — falls within the category of activity that *Lopez* and *Morrison* placed beyond Congress's commerce power.

A ruling that § 922(o) is unconstitutional would extinguish the prohibition entirely. The practical consequence would be that the only remaining restriction on machine gun transfers would derive from pre-1986 statutory provisions and state law — leaving West Virginia's program without any federal impediment.

## The Department of Justice's Dilemma

As I see it, the DOJ faces a genuine strategic risk if it elects to challenge West Virginia's program. A decision to litigate the statutory compliance question invites the court to reach the constitutional question as an alternative ground for decision. Courts are not required to avoid constitutional questions when a party properly places them at issue; and West Virginia, as a sovereign state asserting the lawfulness of its own legislation, would have every incentive to press the Article I argument vigorously.

The institutional risk for the federal government is substantial. A judicial ruling that 18 U.S.C. § 922(o) lacks a sufficient Commerce Clause nexus would invalidate the Hughes Amendment in its entirety — not merely as applied to West Virginia's program. Such a ruling would represent the most significant rollback of federal firearms regulation since *Lopez* itself. The DOJ may well conclude that the prudent course is to decline enforcement in this context, treating West Virginia's program as within the scope of the § 922(o)(2)(A) exemption and avoiding the constitutional confrontation that litigation would invite.

## Implications

In my assessment, West Virginia's legislative initiative has created a significant constitutional pressure point on the Hughes Amendment that warrants serious attention from Second Amendment scholars, litigators, and policymakers. The constitutional deficiency identified by Justice Alito in *Rybar* — the absence of a jurisdictional element connecting § 922(o) to Article I commerce power — has never been adjudicated by the Supreme Court. West Virginia's approach, if litigated, could bring that question before the federal courts under circumstances that present a direct conflict between state law and an arguably unconstitutional federal statute. The outcome of that confrontation could determine whether the post-1986 machine gun prohibition survives constitutional scrutiny — a question that has remained open for nearly four decades.
