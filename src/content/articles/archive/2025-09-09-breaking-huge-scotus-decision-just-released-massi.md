## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has ruled in **Trump v. Slaughter** in favor of President Donald Trump, issuing an emergency stay of the lower courts' order that would have required the reinstatement of Rebecca Slaughter to her position as a commissioner on the Federal Trade Commission following her termination by the President. Chief Justice John Roberts entered the stay order, and the decision portends the imminent overruling of **Humphrey's Executor v. United States** — the 1935 New Deal-era precedent that has for ninety years insulated commissioners of so-called independent agencies from presidential removal. This ruling is a watershed moment in the restoration of constitutional executive power and the dismantling of the deep-state structure that has accumulated in the administrative apparatus since the Roosevelt era.

## Background

Rebecca Kelly Slaughter had served as a commissioner on the Federal Trade Commission — an executive branch agency charged with enforcing antitrust and consumer protection law. Prior to her appointment to the FTC, she had worked closely with Senate Minority Leader Chuck Schumer, and her appointment was understood to reflect Democratic political priorities. She was one of the FTC commissioners whose votes reflected consistent opposition to the economic regulatory priorities of the Trump administration.

President Trump terminated Slaughter from her position. Slaughter challenged her termination in federal court, relying on a statutory provision enacted by Congress during the New Deal era — the provision that gave rise to the Supreme Court's 1935 decision in **Humphrey's Executor** — which provides that FTC commissioners may not be removed by the President except for cause, specifically defined as inefficiency, neglect of duty, or malfeasance in office.

The district court in the District of Columbia issued a preliminary injunction ordering Trump to reinstate Slaughter, concluding that the statutory for-cause removal protection was constitutional and that the President had not demonstrated adequate cause for her termination. The United States Court of Appeals for the DC Circuit affirmed. The Trump administration then sought an emergency stay from the Supreme Court.

## The Supreme Court's Action

Chief Justice Roberts entered an administrative stay, pausing the lower courts' reinstatement order while the full Court considered the stay application. The Court subsequently issued an order granting the stay. This means that Rebecca Slaughter is not reinstated and the lower courts' order requiring her reinstatement is without effect, pending the resolution of the underlying litigation.

The significance of this outcome extends well beyond the individual case of Rebecca Slaughter. A stay requires the Court to assess, among other factors, the likelihood that the applicant will prevail on the merits of the underlying legal question. By granting the stay, the Court has signaled, in terms as clear as preliminary judicial action can communicate, that the President is likely to prevail on the constitutional question — that is, that **Humphrey's Executor** is likely to be overruled, or at minimum significantly curtailed.

## The Constitutional Framework: Three Branches, Not Four

The United States Constitution creates three branches of government. Article I vests the legislative power in Congress. Article II vests the executive power in the President. Article III vests the judicial power in the federal courts. That is the complete structural design of the federal government as the Founders conceived it.

What the New Deal era created, beginning in the 1930s, was effectively a fourth branch: a sprawling administrative state populated by unelected bureaucrats serving in agencies that were deliberately designed to be insulated from political accountability. The theory was that certain functions of government required technical expertise and political neutrality, and that these functions could best be performed by commissioners, directors, and board members who could not be removed by the President without cause. The Supreme Court's 1935 decision in **Humphrey's Executor** gave constitutional blessing to this arrangement.

The practical consequence, however, was not neutral technocratic governance. It was governance by a class of unelected officials who could frustrate the policy agenda of elected presidents, who served indefinitely regardless of electoral outcomes, and who were accountable to no one in any meaningful democratic sense. This is the deep state, and **Humphrey's Executor** is its constitutional charter.

## The Erosion of Humphrey's Executor

The Supreme Court has been progressively narrowing **Humphrey's Executor** for years. In **Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau** (2020), the Court held — in an opinion by Chief Justice Roberts — that Congress cannot constitutionally insulate the single Director of the CFPB from presidential removal without cause. The Court reasoned that a single individual exercising substantial executive power is categorically different from the multi-member commission that **Humphrey's Executor** addressed, and that the President must have the ability to remove such an officer to maintain constitutional accountability.

In **Collins v. Yellen** (2021), the Court applied the same principle to the Director of the Federal Housing Finance Agency, again finding that the for-cause removal protection was unconstitutional as applied to a single officer exercising executive power.

**Trump v. Slaughter** presents the next logical step: can Congress constitutionally insulate members of a multi-member commission from presidential removal? The Court's grant of the emergency stay strongly suggests that the answer is no — that **Humphrey's Executor** itself, even in its application to multi-member commissions, cannot survive the constitutional analysis that **Seila Law** and **Collins** establish.

## The Text of Article II

The textual case for plenary presidential removal power is straightforward. Article II, Section 1 of the Constitution provides: "The executive Power shall be vested in a President of the United States of America." This Vesting Clause is not a partial grant of executive power — it is a complete grant of all executive power to a single individual. The President's power to appoint and remove executive officers is a necessary and inalienable incident of that grant.

A statutory provision that purports to strip the President of the ability to remove an executive officer without cause is a congressional invasion of the executive power that the Constitution vests in the President alone. It is, as a matter of constitutional text, invalid — not because the President is above the law, but because Congress may not by statute override the constitutional allocation of powers among the branches.

## Implications

The implications of **Trump v. Slaughter** and the anticipated overruling of **Humphrey's Executor** are profound. If the Supreme Court definitively holds that Article II prohibits congressional insulation of executive officers from presidential removal, a substantial portion of the administrative state's structural foundation will be swept away. The President will be able to direct, and if necessary remove, the commissioners, directors, and board members of a wide range of federal agencies that have operated for decades as if they were immune from democratic accountability.

For the Second Amendment community specifically, this development carries significant implications. The Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives is an executive branch agency subject to presidential direction. The Department of Justice is an executive branch agency subject to presidential direction. The ability of the President to hold the officials of these agencies accountable for their actions — to remove ATF directors who impose unconstitutional regulations, to demand that DOJ enforce the Second Amendment rather than attack it — is directly implicated by the constitutional principles at stake in **Trump v. Slaughter**.

A constitutionally restored executive branch, in which the President genuinely controls the apparatus of federal law enforcement and regulatory action, is a prerequisite for the sustained vindication of Second Amendment rights at the federal level. The four boxes of American liberty — the soap box, the ballot box, the jury box, and the cartridge box — are all served by a constitutional order in which elections have consequences and elected presidents exercise real authority over the executive branch they lead.
