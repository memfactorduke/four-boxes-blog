## Summary

The Trump-Bondi Department of Justice has filed a brief in **Silencer Shop Foundation v. Bondi**, pending in the United States District Court for the Southern District of Texas, defending the continued enforceability of the National Firearms Act's registration and fingerprinting requirements following Congress's elimination of the $200 transfer tax through the One Big Beautiful Bill. Having analyzed the brief in detail, I conclude that it is substantively flawed in multiple respects and that the legal arguments advanced by the government are likely to fail — and should fail — under a correct application of constitutional principles. I address each of these deficiencies in turn.

## Background

The National Firearms Act of 1934, codified at 26 U.S.C. § 5801 et seq., imposes a comprehensive regulatory regime on certain categories of firearms and related items, including suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns. The NFA's constitutional architecture has historically rested on Congress's Article I taxing power. The $200 transfer tax, levied on each transfer of a regulated item, was the constitutional hook that permitted Congress to impose the accompanying registration, fingerprinting, and approval requirements as necessary and proper to the administration of a valid revenue measure.

Congress, through the One Big Beautiful Bill, eliminated the $200 transfer tax on suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns. This legislative action created a constitutional discontinuity: the taxing power justification for the NFA's ancillary requirements was severed from those requirements by the very act of Congress.

**Silencer Shop Foundation v. Bondi** was filed in the Southern District of Texas to challenge the continued enforcement of the registration and fingerprinting requirements following this legislative change. The DOJ filed a brief opposing the challenge and defending the NFA's continued enforcement.

## Error One: The Article I Taxing Power Analysis

The DOJ brief's most fundamental flaw is its failure to grapple squarely with the elimination of the $200 tax as the constitutional foundation for the NFA's regulatory requirements.

The Supreme Court has been clear, most recently in **National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius**, 567 U.S. 519 (2012), that Congress's taxing power must be exercised through actual revenue generation. A regulatory mandate that carries a financial penalty may be sustained as a tax, but only if it is genuinely designed to raise revenue rather than simply to compel behavioral compliance. When the revenue-generating component is removed — as it was here — the remaining regulatory apparatus must find an independent constitutional justification.

The DOJ brief does not satisfactorily identify that independent justification. The brief's attempt to characterize the remaining registration and fingerprinting requirements as exercises of the Commerce Clause power is constitutionally inadequate. Since the Supreme Court's decisions in **United States v. Lopez**, 514 U.S. 549 (1995), and **United States v. Morrison**, 529 U.S. 598 (2000), Congress's Commerce Clause authority has been understood to require a genuine nexus to interstate commercial activity. A mandatory federal registry of lawfully owned, privately held firearms — maintained with no attendant tax — is a regulation of possession, not commerce. The government's brief does not overcome this fundamental constitutional hurdle.

## Error Two: The Second Amendment Analysis

The brief's Second Amendment analysis is separately deficient under the framework established in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022).

Under **Bruen**, any governmental regulation of firearms must be consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation, and the government bears the burden of demonstrating that historical consistency. The DOJ brief does not identify adequate historical analogues from the founding era or the Reconstruction era — the two primary reference points under **Bruen** — that would support a standing obligation to register lawfully owned firearms with the federal government in the absence of any associated tax or commercial transaction.

This is not a minor analytical gap. The **Bruen** Court was explicit that the government cannot rely on twentieth-century regulatory precedent as a historical analogue, because laws enacted well after the founding period do not illuminate the original meaning of the Second Amendment. The NFA itself was enacted in 1934 — nearly a century and a half after ratification. That the NFA has existed for ninety years does not make it constitutional.

## Error Three: The "Tell Congress" Response

An additional concern arose from a social media exchange in which Harmeet Dhillon, the head of the DOJ's Civil Rights Division, reposted content suggesting that those who dislike the NFA's registration requirements should "Tell Congress" rather than seek judicial relief.

With respect for Assistant Attorney General Dhillon, who has otherwise been a strong advocate for Second Amendment rights, this response reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of the judicial function. Since **Marbury v. Madison**, 5 U.S. (1 Cranch) 137 (1803), it has been settled constitutional law that federal courts possess the authority to invalidate legislation that conflicts with the Constitution. That authority exists precisely because the political branches — Congress and the President — can and do enact unconstitutional laws.

The Second Amendment community did tell Congress. Congress responded by enacting the One Big Beautiful Bill and eliminating the $200 tax. The constitutional consequence of that legislative action — the removal of the primary justification for the NFA's ancillary requirements — is now properly before the courts. The courts are the correct institution to adjudicate that question. Directing plaintiffs back to Congress after Congress has already acted is not a constitutional argument; it is an attempt to avoid judicial review entirely.

## What the NFA Brief Gets Wrong on Policy

Beyond the constitutional deficiencies, the brief's policy assumptions deserve scrutiny. The government's brief implicitly treats the NFA's registry as an important law enforcement tool that the government cannot function without. This premise is questionable.

The NFA registry, as currently constituted, applies only to lawfully acquired items by law-abiding citizens. Persons who acquire suppressors, short-barrel rifles, or short-barrel shotguns illegally are not registered anywhere. The regulatory burden imposed by the NFA falls almost entirely on citizens who are at the least risk of misusing these items. The law-abiding gun owner who submits to fingerprinting, a background check, a waiting period, and federal registration is not the threat the NFA was supposedly enacted to address.

## What the Brief Gets Right: A Narrow Point

In fairness, the brief's observation that the case presents a novel constitutional question warrants acknowledgment. The elimination of the $200 tax by the One Big Beautiful Bill did create a legal situation without direct precedent. The interaction between Article I taxing power limitations and the Second Amendment, in the specific context of tax elimination by Congress, has not been authoritatively resolved by any court at the circuit or Supreme Court level. The government's brief, whatever its deficiencies, correctly identifies this as a question of first impression.

## Implications

I am disappointed by the DOJ's brief in this case. The legal arguments it advances are, in my assessment, likely to lose and should lose. The challengers in **Silencer Shop Foundation v. Bondi** have the stronger constitutional position, and I encourage them and the broader Second Amendment community to press the litigation vigorously.

At the same time, as I have discussed separately, there is a plausible strategic reading of the DOJ's posture that makes the brief somewhat less alarming than it might appear in isolation. By defending the NFA at the trial court level, the DOJ ensures that there remains a live Article III case and controversy that can travel to the Fifth Circuit and ultimately to the Supreme Court — where, I would hope, the government will either revise its analysis or the Court will supply the constitutional correction the lower courts fail to provide.

The Second Amendment community should not wait passively for that outcome. The arguments are on our side, and they deserve to be pressed at every level.
