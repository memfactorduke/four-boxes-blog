## Summary

The Overton window — the concept in political science describing the range of policy ideas that a given society regards as acceptable subjects for mainstream debate and potential implementation — has been expanding dramatically in favor of Second Amendment rights. Several recent examples from the cultural and political spheres illustrate how the conditions that produce favorable legal outcomes in the courts are being cultivated by shifting attitudes in the broader public square. As a constitutional attorney who has spent decades working to protect and advance the right to keep and bear arms, I want to offer my analysis of why this cultural shift matters for the long-term legal battle, and why the trend, broadly considered, remains our friend.

## Background

The Overton window, named after the political theorist Joseph Overton, describes the range of policy options that the public regards as politically feasible at any given moment. Ideas outside the window are considered radical or unacceptable; ideas inside the window are regarded as legitimate subjects for debate and potential adoption. The window is not fixed — it shifts over time in response to cultural, political, and social forces — and its movement in either direction has profound consequences for what legislatures enact and what courts must evaluate.

For the purposes of Second Amendment law and policy, the Overton window metaphor is particularly apt. The constitutional rights recognized by the Supreme Court in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), **McDonald v. City of Chicago**, 561 U.S. 742 (2010), and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), did not emerge from a legal vacuum. They emerged from a decades-long effort — in courts, in legislatures, in academic scholarship, and in the culture — to reclaim the Second Amendment's individual right character from the "collective rights" revisionism that had dominated elite legal discourse for much of the twentieth century.

## The Relationship Between Culture and Law

Political scientists and legal theorists across the ideological spectrum have long recognized that politics — and, by extension, law — is downstream from culture. The judicial appointments process, legislative priorities, prosecutorial decisions, and the social meaning attached to constitutional rights are all shaped, over time, by the prevailing cultural attitudes of the society that produces them.

This is not merely an abstract observation. The trajectory of Second Amendment jurisprudence over the past three decades illustrates the principle concretely. In the 1980s, the proposition that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms was a fringe view within the legal academy. By the 1990s, it was a serious scholarly position defended by a growing body of careful historical and textual analysis. By 2008, it was the holding of the Supreme Court of the United States.

That intellectual and legal transformation did not occur in isolation from the broader culture. It was driven in part by the growth of responsible gun ownership in America, the expansion of shall-issue concealed carry laws across the country, the demonstrated effectiveness of defensive gun use in the lived experience of millions of Americans, and the ongoing work of advocacy organizations, scholars, and practitioners who refused to accept that the Second Amendment meant less than its text says.

## Current Indicators of Progress

The Overton window in 2025 reflects the accumulated gains of that decades-long effort. Several cultural indicators deserve particular attention.

**Self-defense orientation of American gun culture.** The predominant cultural framing of civilian firearms ownership in America has shifted decisively toward self-defense and constitutional rights. This framing is legally important because **Heller** specifically grounded the Second Amendment's core protection in the individual right of self-defense. A culture that embraces lawful self-defense as a positive good — rather than treating it with suspicion or embarrassment — creates the political conditions for legislatures to expand self-defense rights and for courts to take seriously the practical importance of constitutional firearms protections.

**Expansion of lawful carry.** More Americans are exercising their constitutional right to carry firearms for self-defense than at any point in the Nation's history. More than half the states have now enacted constitutional carry legislation, eliminating permit requirements for the carry of firearms by law-abiding citizens. This expansion of lawful carry reflects and reinforces the Overton window shift: the proposition that ordinary citizens should be able to carry firearms for self-defense has moved from the fringe to the mainstream of American legal and political life.

**The Treasury Department and Second Amendment culture.** I want to highlight a specific cultural moment that struck me as illustrative of how pervasively the self-defense orientation of American gun culture has penetrated even elite institutional settings. Secretary of the Treasury Scott Bessent — a former hedge fund manager from New York City, not the archetypal profile of a Second Amendment advocate — has demonstrated, in various public contexts, a comfort and familiarity with firearms ownership and the right to keep and bear arms that would have been remarkable in a Treasury Secretary even a decade ago. Politics is downstream from culture, and culture is now more favorable to Second Amendment rights than at any point in living memory.

## The Legal Significance of the Overton Shift

The Overton window's expansion in favor of Second Amendment rights matters for the legal battle in several concrete ways.

**Judicial selection.** Presidents who are elected in a political environment where Second Amendment rights are salient and popular issues appoint judges who are likely to take those rights seriously. The transformative impact of the Supreme Court's conservative majority — which produced **Bruen** and which continues to build on the **Heller**/**McDonald** foundation — is inseparable from the cultural and political conditions that produced the presidential elections of 2016 and 2024.

**Legislative advocacy.** As the Overton window expands, legislators who once regarded Second Amendment advocacy as politically risky become more likely to support pro-rights legislation. The recent passage of the "Big Beautiful Bill," which among other things eliminated the NFA's $200 tax on suppressors, is an example of legislative progress that reflects the shifting political landscape.

**Litigation strategy.** Courts do not operate in a cultural vacuum. While judges are bound by the law and by their oaths, the prevailing legal and scholarly consensus shapes how they understand and apply legal principles. The shift in the legal academy's understanding of the Second Amendment — from a collective right of no practical significance to an individual right of constitutional importance — has been essential to the Supreme Court's willingness to enforce it.

## Implications

I want to be precise about what I am not saying. The Overton window's expansion in favor of Second Amendment rights does not guarantee victory in every case or every jurisdiction. There are courts, particularly in the Second, Ninth, and First Circuits, that continue to resist the Supreme Court's **Bruen** framework and to uphold gun control measures that are plainly inconsistent with the text, history, and tradition of the Second Amendment. There is still a great deal of work to be done.

What the Overton window's expansion does guarantee is that the conditions for achieving legal progress — favorable judicial appointments, legislative momentum, a sympathetic public, and a growing body of scholarly and advocacy work — are in place. The Second Amendment community should recognize and take encouragement from the cultural momentum while remaining clear-eyed about the legal battles that remain ahead.

Culture and law are not separate spheres. They interact, each shaping the other over time. The expansion of the Overton window in favor of Second Amendment rights is not merely a political development; it is a precondition for the legal victories that will ultimately secure those rights against the ongoing efforts of those who would eliminate them.
