## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit recently heard en banc oral argument in consolidated cases challenging New Jersey's extensive network of "sensitive places" statutes—laws that prohibit licensed firearm carriers from bearing arms in dozens of designated locations, including libraries, zoos, hospitals, and casinos. The central constitutional question is whether a state may categorically exclude law-abiding, permit-holding individuals from exercising their Second Amendment right to carry in public spaces absent a historical tradition of analogous restrictions. In my analysis, New Jersey's framework lacks the historical foundation the Supreme Court's *Bruen* framework demands, and the absence of comprehensive government-provided security at these locations is constitutionally dispositive.

## Background

New Jersey enacted a sweeping legislative response to the Supreme Court's decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), designating scores of locations as "sensitive places" where even holders of valid carry permits may not bear firearms. Two consolidated cases—**Coons v. Attorney General of New Jersey** and **Siegel v. Attorney General of New Jersey**—mount a constitutional challenge to these restrictions. The Third Circuit initially decided these cases in a divided panel opinion before agreeing to rehear them before the full court, reflecting the depth of the legal disagreement over the proper scope of the sensitive places doctrine. The en banc rehearing before more than a dozen judges signals the court's recognition that this issue carries significant constitutional weight across the circuit.

As I have argued in my article in the *Georgetown Journal of Law and Public Policy*, the historical tradition of firearms regulation provides the exclusive metric by which modern sensitive places designations may be assessed. Under the methodology established in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and refined in *Bruen*, the Second Amendment's plain text presumptively protects the right to carry in public. Where a modern law burdens that right, the government must affirmatively demonstrate that the restriction is "consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation." The burden falls squarely on the state, and the relevant historical period runs primarily from the founding era through Reconstruction.

## The Bruen Analytical Framework

The *Bruen* Court established a two-step framework that courts must apply when evaluating Second Amendment challenges. First, the court must determine whether the plain text of the Second Amendment covers the individual's proposed course of conduct. Second, if the conduct is textually covered, the regulation is presumptively unconstitutional unless the government can demonstrate that it is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States. The Court explicitly rejected any means-ends balancing test of the kind that had proliferated in the lower courts following *Heller*, holding that such balancing is inconsistent with the Constitution's text and the Court's categorical approach to enumerated rights.

Applied to carry restrictions in designated locations, the first step is readily satisfied. A law prohibiting an individual from carrying a firearm into a library or a zoo unquestionably burdens the right to "bear" arms—the right to carry firearms in public for self-defense that *Heller* and *Bruen* together recognize. The government must therefore identify historical analogues that are relevantly similar to the challenged restriction in both "why" and "how"—that is, the historical regulation must impose a comparable burden for a comparable reason. New Jersey's task is to identify founding-era laws that restricted armed carry in locations analogous to the dozens of commercial and civic spaces the legislature has designated.

## The Historical Tradition of Sensitive Places

The *Bruen* Court acknowledged that a narrow category of locations has been recognized as "sensitive places" since the founding, identifying legislative assemblies, polling places, and courthouses as paradigmatic examples. The Court explicitly declined to provide a comprehensive definition, but its enumerated examples share a common characteristic that the Court's own cited authorities illuminate: these were locations in which the government provided comprehensive armed security as a condition of the restriction. As I have examined in detail, the amicus brief submitted by the Independent Institute—which the *Bruen* Court cited directly—articulates this principle with precision, observing that from the Statute of Northampton through the American founding, "the principle that weapons-free zones must be accompanied by increased sovereign security remained consistent."

The historical record bears this out with respect to each of the Court's examples. Legislative chambers were guarded by a sergeant-at-arms—among the first appointments made by the First Congress. Courthouses were protected by bailiffs, marshals, and the United States Marshals Service, which Congress created in 1789 specifically to protect the federal judicial apparatus. Polling places were secured by sheriffs and their deputies. The structural commonality is not incidental: when the government disarms a citizen—depriving that person of the ordinary means of self-defense—the historical tradition required a compensating grant of protection. The government assumed responsibility for the security of the disarmed individual within that space.

## The Comprehensive Security Requirement

In my analysis, the absence of comprehensive government-provided security is the constitutionally dispositive factor distinguishing historically recognized sensitive places from the locations New Jersey seeks to designate. A library, a zoo, a casino, and a hospital share no structural resemblance to a courthouse or a legislative chamber. They are open-access commercial or civic facilities that employ no armed security perimeter, maintain no controlled entry points, conduct no weapons screening, and deploy no law enforcement presence specifically tasked with protecting disarmed individuals within their confines. The government's authority to designate a location as sensitive does not arise from legislative fiat alone; it arises from the historical practice of providing reciprocal security in exchange for the deprivation of the right.

The objection that metal detectors and magnetometers did not exist in 1791 does not defeat this analysis. As the *Heller* Court recognized, constitutional principles must be applied to contemporary circumstances using the means available today. The Founders could readily observe an armed individual in an era of long guns; the concealment of a modern handgun requires technological screening to accomplish what visual observation accomplished at the founding. The functional equivalent of the founding-era requirement of visible armed security is a modern security apparatus: controlled entry, weapons screening, and dedicated armed personnel. Locations that impose none of these measures cannot invoke the historical sensitive places tradition as justification for disarming citizens who enter them.

During en banc argument, Judge Paul Mayer pressed precisely this point, noting that "the sensitive place doctrine has never been defined" and questioning whether the term had become a malleable label that regulatory actors could apply to any location they wished to restrict. Judge Mayer suggested that a historically grounded definition would confine "sensitive places" to locations where the government provides comprehensive security—courthouses, military installations, and analogous facilities. In my view, that framing captures the essential constitutional principle with considerable accuracy.

## Alternative Theories and Their Constitutional Deficiencies

The government has advanced several alternative theories in defense of New Jersey's statutes, none of which survives scrutiny under *Bruen*'s historical methodology.

The "core government functions" theory holds that the government may restrict firearms wherever sovereign functions are performed. This theory fails for want of a limiting principle: under its logic, firearms could be excluded from city council chambers, school board meetings, motor vehicle offices, and post offices—a result that would effectively nullify public carry rights across the governmental sphere. The "vulnerable persons present" theory fares no better. The *Bruen* Court explicitly rejected the proposition that concentrated population density or the presence of vulnerable individuals justifies broad carry restrictions, holding that the government cannot treat a city the size of Manhattan as a sensitive place on the ground that many people congregate there. The historical record is, if anything, contrary to this theory: colonial-era governments sometimes required armed attendance at gatherings precisely because the right to self-defense was understood as the most effective protection available.

The "concurrent exercise of other constitutional rights" theory—the claim that places where First Amendment rights are exercised are categorically off-limits for firearms—fails as a matter of constitutional structure. The Bill of Rights was ratified as a coherent whole, and its provisions are not hierarchically ordered such that the exercise of one right suspends another. The historical record confirms that the right to bear arms and the right to assemble were understood as coexisting. The events of the Boston Massacre in 1770 took place in a heavily urban area during a public gathering, and John Adams—arguing the case in defense of the British soldiers—acknowledged that the assembled colonists possessed a lawful right to be armed in that setting.

## The Peaceful Carry Principle and Founding-Era Philosophy

Running through the historical record is a consistent distinction that *Heller*, *Bruen*, and their antecedents recognize: the constitutional right extends to peaceable carrying, while the government may regulate and punish the misuse of arms to terrorize, threaten, or harm. Founding-era affray laws addressed individuals who carried arms in a threatening or tumultuous manner calculated to provoke public fear. Surety laws addressed individuals who, after due process, were found to pose a particularized danger. Neither category supports a categorical prohibition on armed carry in open public spaces by law-abiding individuals who have already been vetted and licensed by the state.

This framework reflects the political philosophy of the founding era. Cesare Beccaria's *On Crimes and Punishments*—a text Thomas Jefferson copied at length in his commonplace book and which was widely read among the founding generation—advanced the proposition that arms restrictions predictably operate to the advantage of those who disregard law at the expense of those who comply with it. As the amicus brief cited in *Bruen* observes, this Beccarian insight informed the founding generation's understanding that the right to bear arms was a general right of the law-abiding public, subject only to narrow restrictions in locations where the government provided compensating security.

## Implications

The Third Circuit's en banc decision in these consolidated cases will carry significant weight for sensitive places jurisprudence across the federal courts. If the court sustains New Jersey's statutes, it will create a circuit conflict with courts that have read *Bruen*'s sensitive places language more narrowly, potentially accelerating Supreme Court intervention. If the court strikes down New Jersey's framework in whole or in part—applying the comprehensive security requirement as the historical organizing principle—it will provide a replicable analytic template for challenges to similar statutes in California, New York, Maryland, Massachusetts, and other states that have enacted comparable post-*Bruen* carry restrictions.

In my view, the constitutional text and the historical record point clearly toward the conclusion that New Jersey's expansive sensitive places designations cannot be sustained. The government either disarms the citizen and provides comprehensive protection in return, or it permits the citizen to exercise the constitutional right to self-defense. The historical tradition recognizes no intermediate position in which the state may disarm the law-abiding public across dozens of ordinary locations while providing no compensating security. That is not a sensitive places doctrine—it is a functional nullification of the right to bear arms.
