## Summary

In my analysis, the Trump administration has reached settlements in two significant civil rights lawsuits that challenged race-based admissions practices at the United States Military Academy at West Point and the United States Air Force Academy. Attorney General Pam Bondi and the Department of Defense have jointly confirmed that race-based admissions criteria will be permanently ended at both institutions, aligning the academies' practices with the Supreme Court's 2023 ruling in *Students for Fair Admissions v. President and Fellows of Harvard College* and with the requirements of the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment.

## Background

The Supreme Court's 2023 decision in *Students for Fair Admissions v. President and Fellows of Harvard College* held, in a clear and categorical ruling, that race-based admissions programs at institutions of higher education violate the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The Court's majority opinion, authored by Chief Justice Roberts, reaffirmed the foundational constitutional principle that racial discrimination in admissions -- whether designed to benefit or to disadvantage any particular racial group -- is prohibited under the Constitution.

Notably, however, the majority opinion in *Students for Fair Admissions* contained an express footnote declining to address whether the ruling's principles applied to the federal military academies, specifically observing that the case did not present that question and that there might be arguments specific to military education not raised in the proceedings before the Court. That footnote, in my assessment, was an invitation to subsequent litigation -- and that litigation followed promptly.

## The Lawsuits Against the Military Academies

Following the *Students for Fair Admissions* decision, civil rights plaintiffs filed suits challenging the continued use of race as a factor in admissions at the United States Military Academy at West Point, the United States Air Force Academy, and the United States Naval Academy. The Naval Academy had previously settled the litigation against it. The lawsuits against West Point and the Air Force Academy have now also been resolved through settlement, with both institutions committing to end race-based admissions practices.

Attorney General Pam Bondi issued a statement announcing the settlements and expressing the Department of Justice's position that merit-based admissions are not merely constitutionally required but also essential to national security. In her statement, Attorney General Bondi noted: "We are proud to partner with the Department of Defense to permanently end race-based admissions at West Point and the Air Force Academy. Admission to these prestigious military institutions should be based exclusively on merit."

The Department of Defense similarly confirmed its position that race-based admissions practices do not advance national security objectives and are inconsistent with the principle of equal treatment under the Constitution.

## The Constitutional Framework

In my analysis, the constitutional case against race-based admissions at the military academies was strong even before the settlements. The Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment provides that no state shall deny to any person within its jurisdiction the equal protection of the laws. Although the military academies are federal institutions technically governed by the Fifth Amendment's due process clause (which has been interpreted to incorporate an equal protection guarantee for federal actors), the constitutional principle is the same: the federal government may not discriminate on the basis of race.

The *Students for Fair Admissions* decision did not create a new constitutional rule but rather reaffirmed and enforced a principle that the Constitution has always embodied. Racial discrimination in government admissions -- regardless of which racial group is favored or disfavored -- is incompatible with the guarantee of equal protection. The Supreme Court's earlier decisions in *Grutter v. Bollinger* (2003) and *Gratz v. Bollinger* (2003) had temporarily permitted race-conscious admissions under a "diversity" rationale in the context of higher education, but *Students for Fair Admissions* overruled that precedent.

## The Merits of the Military Academy Defense

The arguments specifically advanced in favor of permitting race-based admissions at the military academies centered on the claim that a racially diverse officer corps serves compelling national security interests distinct from the general diversity rationale addressed in *Students for Fair Admissions*. Proponents of this view argued that military units are more effective when they reflect the racial composition of the broader population, and that officer diversity therefore serves operational objectives that go beyond the educational benefits considered in *Grutter*.

In my view, however, the settlements reflect a correct assessment that this argument, while superficially distinct, does not ultimately withstand constitutional scrutiny. The Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection guarantee does not recognize a national security exception to the prohibition on racial discrimination. As the Supreme Court observed in *Students for Fair Admissions*, racial classifications are inherently suspect and subject to the most exacting constitutional review. The government bears the burden of demonstrating that its use of race is narrowly tailored to achieve a compelling interest -- a burden that the Department of Defense, upon reflection, apparently concluded it could not carry.

## The Significance of Merit-Based Admissions

The settlements represent a return to merit-based admissions at two of the nation's most prestigious military educational institutions. In my assessment, this is constitutionally correct and institutionally beneficial. The principle articulated by Dr. Martin Luther King Jr. -- that individuals should be judged by the content of their character rather than the color of their skin -- is not merely an aspirational statement but a constitutional command. Objective academic performance, demonstrated aptitude, physical fitness, and leadership character are the appropriate criteria for admission to institutions whose graduates will be entrusted with the lives of service members and the defense of the nation.

The Trump administration's resolution of these lawsuits through settlement, rather than continued litigation, is a practical and efficient approach to ensuring constitutional compliance. It provides certainty to the academies and to future applicants and eliminates the costs and uncertainties of continued judicial proceedings.
