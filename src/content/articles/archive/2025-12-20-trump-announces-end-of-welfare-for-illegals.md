## Summary

The Trump administration's Office of Legal Counsel has issued a binding opinion reversing a Clinton-era OLC interpretation that, for nearly three decades, had created a significant loophole in the Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act of 1996. The Clinton OLC opinion had concluded that PRWORA's prohibition on federal benefits for aliens applied only to mandatory spending programs such as Medicaid and Social Security, leaving open the continued disbursement of federal discretionary welfare benefits to illegal aliens and other non-citizens. The Trump OLC opinion concludes that this interpretation is no longer tenable in light of the Supreme Court's 2024 decision in **Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo**, which overruled the *Chevron* deference doctrine on which the Clinton-era opinion rested. The Trump OLC's new interpretation holds that PRWORA's bar applies to all federal means-tested programs — mandatory and discretionary alike — effectively closing a loophole that has resulted in the disbursement of hundreds of billions of taxpayer dollars to aliens who were never supposed to receive them.

## Background

The Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act of 1996 — PRWORA — was enacted by a Republican Congress and signed by President Bill Clinton. The statute was presented publicly as a landmark reform of the federal welfare system, including a commitment to end the availability of federal means-tested benefits to aliens present in the United States. The statute's plain language was understood at the time of enactment to accomplish precisely that objective.

Almost immediately after PRWORA was signed into law, however, the Clinton administration's executive branch agencies moved to undermine its reach. The Department of Housing and Urban Development and the Department of Health and Human Services — both arms of the Clinton executive branch — submitted a formal request to the Clinton Office of Legal Counsel asking whether PRWORA's benefit restrictions applied to federal discretionary spending programs in addition to mandatory programs.

The Clinton OLC's response was the foundation of the loophole. The opinion concluded that there was "some ambiguity" in PRWORA's scope and that it was a "reasonable interpretation" to read the statute as applying only to mandatory programs — Social Security, Medicaid, and similar entitlements — while leaving discretionary programs untouched. Mandatory programs are those automatically funded at levels prescribed by statute, such as Social Security and Medicaid. Discretionary programs are the broad range of other federally funded benefits — housing assistance, food assistance, educational subsidies, and a host of other means-tested programs — whose appropriation levels are set annually by Congress.

The Clinton OLC's reasoning relied heavily on the *Chevron* doctrine established by the Supreme Court in *Chevron U.S.A., Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council* (1984). Under *Chevron*, courts (and by extension executive branch legal advisors) were instructed to defer to an agency's interpretation of an ambiguous statute, provided the interpretation was not unreasonable. Because Clinton's HUD and HHS had offered a plausible reading of PRWORA's scope, and because *Chevron* instructed deference to agency interpretations where statutory ambiguity existed, the Clinton OLC found it appropriate to endorse the agencies' preferred interpretation — the one that preserved their authority to continue disbursing benefits.

## The Loophole in Operation

The practical effect of the Clinton OLC opinion was stark. By carving out discretionary programs from PRWORA's reach, the executive branch maintained the authority to extend a wide range of federal benefits to aliens — including illegal aliens — for the nearly thirty years since the statute's enactment. The mandatory/discretionary distinction meant that the most politically prominent programs, such as Medicaid and Social Security, were technically off-limits, but the vast universe of other federal benefits remained accessible.

PRWORA had also included several additional substantive provisions designed to close off federal benefits. The statute barred lawful permanent residents — visa holders — from receiving federal means-tested benefits during their first five years of residence in the United States. It further required that when aliens apply for means-tested benefits, the assets and income of any sponsoring family member must be "deemed" part of the alien's resources for eligibility purposes, making it substantially harder for newly arrived aliens with sponsors to qualify for needs-based assistance. The Clinton OLC opinion's restrictive reading of PRWORA's scope allowed these provisions to be bypassed in practice for programs classified as discretionary.

## The Legal Foundation for the Trump OLC Reversal

The Trump administration's Office of Legal Counsel identified the death of *Chevron* deference as the legal predicate for reconsidering the Clinton-era opinion. In **Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo** (2024), the Supreme Court, in an opinion joined by the Trump-appointed Justices, overruled *Chevron* and held that courts must independently determine the best interpretation of ambiguous statutes, without deferring to the interpretation preferred by administrative agencies.

The significance of *Loper Bright* for the PRWORA loophole is direct and decisive. The Clinton OLC opinion had explicitly premised its endorsement of the HUD and HHS interpretations on *Chevron*: because *Chevron* instructed deference to agency interpretations when statutory language was ambiguous, and because those agencies had advanced a plausible reading of PRWORA, the Clinton OLC concluded that their interpretation was defensible. Remove *Chevron*, and the entire analytical scaffolding of the Clinton opinion collapses.

With *Loper Bright* having overruled *Chevron*, the question becomes: what does PRWORA actually mean, assessed by the independent judgment of lawyers reading the statute according to its best interpretation, without any thumb on the scale favoring an agency's preferred reading? The Trump OLC concluded that on a proper independent reading of PRWORA's text, the statute's bar on federal means-tested benefits for aliens applies to both mandatory and discretionary programs. There is no textual basis for limiting the statute's reach to one spending category while exempting the other.

## The Binding Effect of OLC Opinions

The Office of Legal Counsel, a unit within the Department of Justice, functions as the authoritative legal advisor to the President and to the executive branch departments and agencies. OLC opinions are binding on the executive branch. When the Trump OLC issues an opinion reversing the Clinton OLC's interpretation of PRWORA, that reversal has the force of binding executive branch authority. Federal agencies that had been disbursing discretionary benefits to aliens pursuant to the Clinton-era interpretation are now required to conform their conduct to the new, corrected interpretation.

This is not a legislative change; Congress has not acted. Nor is it a court ruling. It is an exercise of the executive branch's authority to interpret its own legal obligations — authority that the prior administration had used to create the loophole in the first instance, and that the current administration has now used to close it. The symmetry is deliberate and legally sound: if a prior OLC opinion can open a loophole in a duly enacted statute, a subsequent OLC opinion, resting on a superior legal foundation, can close it.

## Implications

The Trump OLC's reversal of the Clinton-era PRWORA interpretation is significant both in its immediate practical effects and in its broader legal significance.

In practical terms, the opinion closes the discretionary spending loophole that had allowed hundreds of billions of dollars in federal benefits to flow to aliens over nearly three decades. Federal departments and agencies are now bound to interpret PRWORA as applying to all means-tested federal programs, regardless of whether those programs are classified as mandatory or discretionary.

In legal terms, the opinion illustrates the downstream consequences of *Loper Bright*. By eliminating *Chevron* deference, the Supreme Court has created a framework in which executive branch lawyers must independently assess statutory meaning — and in which prior agency interpretations that relied on *Chevron* are now vulnerable to reconsideration whenever a new administration concludes that the statute's best reading differs from the prior agencies' preferred reading. The Clinton OLC opinion was always more policy preference than textual analysis. Stripped of *Chevron*'s protection, it cannot survive independent scrutiny.

PRWORA said what Congress meant it to say in 1996. It took three decades, the overruling of *Chevron*, and an administration willing to engage in rigorous statutory interpretation to give effect to that meaning.
