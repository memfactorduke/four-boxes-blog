## Summary

The District of Columbia Court of Appeals' decision in **Benson v. United States** — holding that the District's prohibition on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds of ammunition is facially unconstitutional under the Second Amendment — has immediately generated significant downstream litigation activity at the United States Supreme Court. Within days of the ruling, counsel in multiple pending certiorari petitions filed supplemental briefs citing *Benson* as material new authority warranting the Court's immediate review, including in **Lefay v. Fairfax County**, **Duncan v. Bonta**, and **Gator Custom Guns v. Wence**. In my analysis, the *Benson* decision is significant both for its direct holding on magazine capacity restrictions and for its careful treatment of facial constitutional challenges under the methodology established in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022).

## Background

The *Benson* litigation arose from a criminal prosecution in the District of Columbia for possession of a magazine capable of holding more than ten rounds — a violation of D.C.'s long-standing large-capacity magazine prohibition. The defendant challenged the constitutionality of the prohibition under the *Bruen* framework. The case gained particular significance when the United States Attorney for the District of Columbia, acting under the direction of Attorney General Pam Bondi, filed a brief with the D.C. Court of Appeals arguing that the prohibition violates the Second Amendment. That position — that large-capacity magazine bans are constitutionally indefensible under the *Bruen* methodology — reflected the Department of Justice's considered legal judgment, not merely a prosecutorial discretion decision. The D.C. Court of Appeals accepted the constitutional argument, reversed the conviction, and declared the magazine ban facially invalid.

The ruling was significant for two distinct reasons. First, it resolved the merits question in favor of Second Amendment protection for standard-capacity magazines. Second, it provided a careful and replicable framework for applying facial challenge doctrine to firearms regulations — a framework that counsel in other pending cases have promptly deployed before the Supreme Court.

## The Benson Decision: Magazines as Protected Arms

The D.C. Court of Appeals' analysis proceeded from the text of the Second Amendment through the two-step *Bruen* methodology. The court's threshold holding — that magazines constitute "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment — rests on the definition the Supreme Court articulated in *Bruen*: an "arm" is any instrument that facilitates armed self-defense. As I have examined, a detachable magazine is unambiguously an instrument; it is a physical object designed to feed ammunition into a firearm, and without it, the firearm either cannot function or can discharge only a single round before manual reloading is required. A magazine therefore facilitates armed self-defense as directly as the firearm itself.

Having concluded that the plain text of the Second Amendment is implicated, the court applied the second analytical step: it evaluated whether the government could demonstrate a historical tradition of analogous regulation. The record established that magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds are not merely common — they number in the hundreds of millions in civilian ownership. An arm that is ubiquitous in lawful civilian use cannot logically be characterized as "dangerous and unusual," which is the standard *Heller* established for arms the government may constitutionally prohibit. The *Benson* court concluded that the District could not meet its burden, and the prohibition fell.

## Facial Challenge Doctrine and the Benson Framework

In my analysis, the *Benson* court's treatment of facial challenge doctrine is among the opinion's most consequential contributions to Second Amendment jurisprudence. Lower courts adjudicating Second Amendment claims have frequently declined to strike down firearms regulations on facial grounds, reasoning that a challenger must identify the full universe of statutory applications and demonstrate that none is constitutional. This standard is borrowed from contexts such as First Amendment overbreadth, where the doctrine has distinct doctrinal foundations, and its uncritical importation into Second Amendment cases has produced inconsistent and analytically unsatisfying results.

The *Benson* court corrected this error by returning to *Heller* itself. In *District of Columbia v. Heller*, the Supreme Court facially invalidated the District's handgun ban — not because every conceivable application of a handgun prohibition would be unconstitutional, but because the specific statute before the Court, as written, had no constitutional application in the real world. As *Benson* quoted from its own analysis: "It is not within the judicial function for us to draw the line in a new place. So the statute is unconstitutional in all of its applications and is facially invalid." The court properly recognized that a judicial tribunal's function in evaluating a facial challenge is to assess the statute as written, not to hypothesize about legislative alternatives the court might find more constitutionally defensible. To do otherwise would usurp the legislative function under the guise of constitutional avoidance.

The brief filed in *Lefay v. Fairfax County* — a sensitive-places case in which Fairfax County has imposed a comprehensive ban on firearms across county-owned park land — relied on this precise reasoning from *Benson*. As counsel for the petitioners argued before the Supreme Court:

> "In reaching its conclusion, the DC Court of Appeals in *Benson* warned that there are few areas of jurisprudence more difficult to decipher than when a litigant can successfully raise a facial challenge. To guide it through this difficult area, the court in *Benson* relied specifically on this Court's facial holding in *DC v. Heller*."

This is a methodologically sound application of binding Supreme Court precedent, and it squarely rebuts the doctrinal sleight-of-hand by which lower courts have avoided engaging with the constitutional merits of categorical firearms restrictions.

## The Supplemental Briefs: Cascading Effects at the Supreme Court

As I have reviewed them, the supplemental briefs filed in the wake of *Benson* reflect the rapid mobilization of Second Amendment litigators in response to newly issued favorable authority. The *Lefay* supplemental brief reached the Supreme Court within days of the *Benson* ruling, presenting the Court with a direct opportunity to consider the magazine-as-arm holding in the context of a sensitive-places challenge. Additional supplemental briefs in **Duncan v. Bonta** and **Gator Custom Guns v. Wence** — both Ninth Circuit cases directly implicating large-capacity magazine bans — are expected to follow in short order, presenting the Court with a building record of lower court authority on the question.

This pattern is constitutionally significant for a structural reason. When multiple circuit courts and state appellate courts address an identical constitutional question and reach materially different conclusions — with the D.C. Court of Appeals now aligned against the Ninth Circuit and the Washington State Supreme Court on whether magazines are protected arms — the conditions for Supreme Court review are directly implicated. The Court grants certiorari to resolve conflicts among courts of appeals and to address issues of broad constitutional importance. The post-*Benson* supplemental briefs are designed precisely to demonstrate to the Court that both criteria are now satisfied.

## Implications

In my analysis, the immediate significance of *Benson* lies at two levels. As a direct precedent, it provides authoritative appellate authority — from the highest court in the District of Columbia — that large-capacity magazine prohibitions are facially unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. As a methodological matter, its treatment of facial challenge doctrine corrects a persistent error in lower court Second Amendment adjudication and provides a rigorous, text-anchored framework that counsel in related cases can and should deploy.

The broader significance, as I have examined it, is the cascading nature of well-reasoned constitutional precedent. A single decision that correctly applies the governing legal methodology does not merely resolve the case before it — it furnishes the analytical tools that litigants will deploy in materially identical cases across the country. The supplemental briefs already filed in *Lefay*, *Duncan*, and *Gator Custom Guns* demonstrate precisely this dynamic. The Department of Justice's willingness to take and sustain the position that large-capacity magazine bans are unconstitutional — across both its brief in *Benson* and its ongoing enforcement posture — reinforces that this is not an isolated result but a considered legal judgment with national implications. The Supreme Court, when it grants certiorari in one of these cases, will be deciding not merely the fate of a single state or local regulation, but the constitutional status of magazine capacity restrictions throughout the United States.
