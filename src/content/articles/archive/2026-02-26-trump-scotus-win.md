## Summary

In my analysis, the Supreme Court's decision in *Varial v. Texas*, authored by Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson, carries significance beyond its Sixth Amendment holding — specifically, it provides meaningful information about the authorship of the Court's remaining October-sitting decisions, including *Clay v. Louisiana*. The pending *Clay* decision will resolve whether Section Two of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 can require race-conscious congressional districting in a manner that conflicts with the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause. As I have examined, a ruling that invalidates race-based redistricting mandates would have downstream consequences for the composition of the House of Representatives, and thereby for the legislative prospects of Second Amendment rights at the federal level.

## Background

The Supreme Court's October sitting for the current term included ten argued cases. With the issuance of *Varial v. Texas*, only two of those cases remain undecided: a Fourteenth Amendment substantive due process challenge arising from Colorado, and *Clay v. Louisiana*, which presents the constitutional question of whether Section Two of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 can lawfully mandate race-conscious congressional redistricting in tension with the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause.

In the Supreme Court's assignment practice, Justices rotate authorship of majority opinions within a sitting, and it is standard practice to track which Justices have already issued opinions from a given sitting in order to identify likely authors of remaining decisions. Because Justice Jackson issued *Varial v. Texas* from the October sitting, and because the only other October-sitting case still outstanding implicates constitutional questions that align with Justice Samuel Alito's jurisprudential profile — including his authorship of *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization*, 597 U.S. 215 (2022) — it is my assessment that Justice Alito is the most probable author of the *Clay v. Louisiana* opinion.

## The Constitutional Question in *Clay v. Louisiana*

*Clay v. Louisiana* presents a question of constitutional hierarchy that has divided courts and commentators since the Voting Rights Act's enactment. Section Two, as amended in 1982 and as interpreted by the Supreme Court in *Thornburg v. Gingles*, 478 U.S. 30 (1986), has been read to require that certain states draw majority-minority congressional districts to provide minority communities with an effective opportunity to elect representatives of their choice. This interpretive framework has been applied, in practice, primarily to Southern and Republican-controlled states, compelling the creation of districts drawn predominantly on the basis of racial composition.

The constitutional tension is direct. The Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause, ratified in 1868, prohibits state actors from classifying persons on the basis of race without satisfying strict scrutiny — the most demanding standard of constitutional review. The Supreme Court confirmed in *Shaw v. Reno*, 509 U.S. 630 (1993), and *Miller v. Johnson*, 515 U.S. 900 (1995), that racially motivated redistricting — even when ostensibly remedial in purpose — triggers strict scrutiny under the Equal Protection Clause. The question *Clay* presents is whether Section Two, as a congressional statute, can require the very racial classification that the Constitution subjects to such demanding review, or whether the Constitution's structural hierarchy — under which federal statutes must yield to conflicting constitutional commands — forecloses that result.

## The Hierarchical Principle

As I have argued, the resolution of this question turns on a foundational principle of constitutional structure: the Supremacy Clause establishes that the Constitution is "the supreme Law of the Land," and that statutes enacted by Congress must conform to it. When a congressional statute, properly construed, requires conduct that the Constitution independently prohibits, the statute must give way. There is no exception for statutes enacted under Section Five of the Fourteenth Amendment when those statutes exceed Congress's remedial authority, as the Court established in *City of Boerne v. Flores*, 521 U.S. 507 (1997), and *Shelby County v. Holder*, 570 U.S. 529 (2013).

In my reading of these precedents, Section Two, to the extent it mandates the creation of majority-minority districts through racially conscious line-drawing, cannot be reconciled with the Equal Protection Clause's prohibition on race-based governmental classifications absent the narrow justification of remedying specific, identified constitutional violations. The argument that racial parity in legislative representation justifies race-conscious line-drawing as a general matter — without reference to specific discriminatory acts — goes beyond what *Boerne* and subsequent Section Five precedents permit Congress to accomplish through legislation.

## Implications for Congressional Composition and Second Amendment Legislation

In my assessment, a ruling in *Clay v. Louisiana* that curtails or invalidates Section Two's race-conscious redistricting mandate would provide affected states — including Alabama, Louisiana, and Texas — with the constitutional authority to draw congressional maps on a race-neutral basis, subject only to the generally applicable requirements of the Equal Protection Clause and the one-person, one-vote principle.

The practical consequence for legislative politics is significant. As I have observed, the current partisan composition of the House of Representatives reflects, in part, majority-minority districts created under Section Two mandates in states where Republican majorities would otherwise draw competitive or Republican-leaning maps. Estimates of the number of affected seats range from twelve to nineteen. A *Clay* ruling that restores those states' redistricting discretion would allow the affected states to redraw their congressional maps, potentially altering the partisan composition of the House.

For Second Amendment advocates, the composition of the House is not an abstract institutional question. The House has been the primary legislative forum for firearms legislation at the federal level, and the difference between majority support for gun regulations and majority opposition often turns on the margin of a single-digit number of seats. As I have frequently observed in my analysis of Second Amendment law and politics, constitutional litigation does not occur in a legislative vacuum; the durability of Second Amendment protections depends substantially on the political environment in which those protections must be defended.

## Justice Alito and the Significance of Authorship

The expected authorship of the *Clay* opinion by Justice Alito warrants independent attention. Justice Alito has demonstrated, across a career that spans both appellate and Supreme Court service, a sustained commitment to the principle that constitutional text governs over contrary statutory enactment, and that the Court's role is to enforce the Constitution's commands even when doing so requires overruling or narrowing prior precedent. His opinion in *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization* is the most prominent recent illustration of that approach, but the same methodology is evident in his Second Amendment jurisprudence, his Establishment Clause analysis, and his administrative law opinions.

An opinion in *Clay v. Louisiana* authored by Justice Alito would likely address the constitutional hierarchy question with the directness and doctrinal clarity that characterize his majority writings. In my view, such an opinion would provide a durable constitutional framework for evaluating Section Two claims going forward — one grounded in the text and original understanding of the Fourteenth Amendment rather than in the evolving interpretive gloss that has accumulated under *Gingles* and its progeny.

## Implications

The issuance of *Varial v. Texas* and the resulting inference about *Clay v. Louisiana*'s authorship represent a convergence of constitutional and political developments with direct relevance to the legislative environment in which Second Amendment rights must be protected. In my assessment, a *Clay* decision that limits race-conscious redistricting mandates under Section Two would provide a constitutionally grounded correction to an asymmetrical enforcement pattern that has, for decades, systematically altered congressional representation in ways the Fourteenth Amendment's text does not authorize. The downstream effects on House composition — and on the prospects for Second Amendment legislation — make this among the most consequential pending Supreme Court decisions of the current term.
