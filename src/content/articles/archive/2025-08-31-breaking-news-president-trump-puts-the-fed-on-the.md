## Summary

President Donald J. Trump has terminated the appointment of Federal Reserve Board Governor Lisa Cook, citing as grounds for removal the credible allegations that Governor Cook engaged in mortgage fraud in connection with federally backed loan applications. Governor Cook has responded by filing suit against the Trump administration, asserting that the president lacks authority to remove her from a position that carries a statutory fourteen-year term. The resulting litigation presents the federal judiciary with one of the most consequential separation of powers questions of the modern constitutional era: may Congress insulate a Federal Reserve Board governor from presidential removal, and does the Federal Reserve System's current operational structure — which has functioned for over a century as a de facto fourth branch of government unaccountable to any elected official — survive scrutiny under the Article II vesting clause and the Supreme Court's evolving removal power jurisprudence?

## Background

The Federal Reserve System was established by the Federal Reserve Act of 1913, enacted during the presidency of Woodrow Wilson. The Act created an independent central banking system comprising a Board of Governors and twelve regional Federal Reserve Banks, charged with managing the nation's monetary policy, supervising and regulating banks, maintaining the stability of the financial system, and providing financial services to depository institutions and the federal government. From its inception, the Federal Reserve was designed to operate with a degree of insulation from direct presidential control — a design choice that has been justified by proponents on the ground that monetary policy requires technical expertise and independence from short-term political pressures.

The constitutional validity of this design has never been definitively adjudicated by the Supreme Court in the context of the modern removal power framework. The landmark 1935 decision in **Humphrey's Executor v. United States** upheld "for cause" removal protections for members of multi-member independent agencies exercising quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial functions. For decades, **Humphrey's Executor** was read broadly to insulate a wide range of independent agency officials from presidential removal. However, the Supreme Court's decisions in **Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau** (2020) and **Collins v. Yellen** (2021) substantially narrowed **Humphrey's Executor**, holding that the exception it created is limited to its specific factual context and that the broader principle of presidential removal authority under Article II is robust and constitutionally mandated.

## The Constitutional Framework: Three Branches, Not Four

The United States Constitution creates three and only three branches of the federal government. Article I creates the Congress — the legislative branch. Article II creates the presidency — the executive branch. Article III creates the Supreme Court and such inferior courts as Congress may establish — the judicial branch. The separation of powers among these three branches was designed by the Framers as the primary structural safeguard against tyranny — the mechanism by which the concentrated power of an overbearing government could be checked, balanced, and ultimately held accountable to the governed.

The Federal Reserve System, as it has operated in practice, represents a significant structural anomaly within this constitutional architecture. The Board of Governors exercises authority that is quintessentially executive in character — it implements federal law through monetary policy decisions that directly affect every American's economic life. And yet it operates with a degree of insulation from presidential accountability that no other genuinely executive agency enjoys. In my view, this structure is constitutionally suspect in light of the Supreme Court's post-**Seila Law** jurisprudence, and the Cook litigation presents an opportunity for the courts to address that suspicion directly.

## The "For Cause" Statutory Provision

The Federal Reserve Act provides that Board governors serve fourteen-year terms and may be removed by the president "for cause." This provision is the centerpiece of Governor Cook's legal challenge: she argues that the "for cause" limitation means that the President may only remove a governor upon a showing of specific, judicially reviewable grounds, and that whatever Cook may have done in connection with her personal mortgage transactions does not satisfy that standard.

I find this argument unpersuasive for several reasons.

First, the alleged mortgage fraud — the submission of materially false information on applications for federally backed mortgage financing — if proven, constitutes both a federal crime and a category of conduct that is inherently incompatible with the responsibilities of a Federal Reserve Board governor. A governor who participates in setting interest rates and monetary policy affecting the mortgage market, while herself having allegedly obtained financing through fraudulent misrepresentations on mortgage applications, presents an untenable conflict of interest and an embarrassment to the institution that, standing alone, would constitute "cause" for removal under any reasonable construction of that term. The parallel criminal referrals from the Federal Housing Finance Agency and the grand jury investigation subsequently initiated by the Department of Justice reinforce the substantive basis for the President's determination.

Second, the question of who determines whether "cause" exists is itself legally significant. Governor Cook's lawsuit asks a federal court to substitute its judgment for the President's judgment regarding whether the allegations against her satisfy the "for cause" standard. This request raises substantial questions about the proper scope of judicial review of presidential removal determinations. The Article II vesting clause, which places executive power in the President, argues strongly against a judicial role that amounts to second-guessing the President's assessment of whether a senior executive official's conduct justifies removal.

## The Broader Case for Federal Reserve Accountability

Beyond the specific facts of the Cook matter, the broader constitutional question of Federal Reserve independence from presidential supervision is one that the Trump administration has correctly identified as fundamental.

For over a century, the Federal Reserve has exercised extraordinary power over the American economy with minimal direct democratic accountability. The Board of Governors makes decisions — on interest rates, money supply, bank regulation — that directly affect the economic circumstances of every American household. These decisions are made by officials who are appointed through a process involving both the president and the Senate, but who, under the existing statutory framework, cannot be removed by the elected president even when their policy choices are inconsistent with the democratically expressed preferences of the American people as reflected in the election of the president.

This structure is defensible, if at all, only on the ground that central banking genuinely requires technical independence from political influence to function effectively. But this argument proves too much: every major executive function requires technical expertise, and yet we do not therefore insulate the Secretary of Defense, the Secretary of the Treasury, or the Director of the FBI from presidential removal. The Federal Reserve's claim to unique constitutional status as an unreviewable, unremovable body has never been grounded in constitutional text or established constitutional principle.

The Framers of the Constitution understood that the concentration of power without accountability was the fundamental source of tyranny. An institution that controls the money supply, sets the price of credit for every American, and operates free from presidential removal authority is precisely the kind of unaccountable power center that the Constitution's structural design was intended to prevent.

## Implications for American Law and Constitutional Order

The Cook litigation, whatever its immediate outcome, is forcing a long-overdue confrontation with the constitutional status of the Federal Reserve System.

If the courts uphold President Trump's removal of Governor Cook — either on the specific "for cause" grounds or on the broader Article II removal authority — it will fundamentally alter the institutional landscape of American monetary policy by establishing that the President does possess meaningful authority over Federal Reserve personnel. This result would bring the Federal Reserve into closer conformity with the Constitution's structural design and would represent a restoration of democratic accountability over one of the most powerful institutions in the federal government.

If the courts hold that Governor Cook cannot be removed, they will be forced to articulate a coherent constitutional basis for the Federal Reserve's exceptional status — a basis that, in light of **Seila Law** and **Collins**, will be increasingly difficult to ground in the Supreme Court's current removal power jurisprudence.

In either event, the lawsuit that Lisa Cook filed in an effort to retain her position may prove, as predicted, to have been a significant strategic mistake — not merely for her personally, but for the institutional claim of Federal Reserve independence that she sought to vindicate. By inviting the federal courts to adjudicate the constitutional basis for Federal Reserve governor tenure protection in this particular fact pattern, she has opened a door that may not close in the direction she intended.
