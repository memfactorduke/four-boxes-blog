## Summary

The Supreme Court of the United States has heard oral argument in **Wolford v. Lopez**, the challenge to Hawaii's so-called "vampire rule" — a law that prohibits licensed concealed carry holders from entering any private property open to the public while armed, unless the property owner has given prior express permission. Based on the transcript of that oral argument, I assess that the Second Amendment will prevail in this case, very likely by a six-to-three margin reflecting the Court's existing ideological alignment. More significant than the outcome, however, is the question of how the Court will frame its reasoning — the methodology and rationale set forth in the majority opinion will govern hundreds of Second Amendment cases in the lower courts for years to come, and it is imperative that the opinion be written as broadly and firmly as the facts and doctrinal context allow.

## Background: What Is at Stake in Wolford

The certified question in *Wolford v. Lopez* asks whether the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit erred in holding that the State of Hawaii may presumptively prohibit the carrying of handguns by licensed concealed carry permit holders on private property open to the public, absent express permission from the property owner.

The practical consequences of Hawaii's law are severe. A licensed carrier in Hawaii who drives to a gas station, enters a grocery store, stops at a coffee shop, or visits a barber is in technical violation of the law every time they do so while armed — unless they have somehow obtained advance permission from the owner or manager of each establishment. Because the identities and contact information of property owners are generally not posted or publicly accessible, the law effectively creates a blanket prohibition on carry in the overwhelming majority of daily-life locations. The right to carry, as Hawaii has constructed it, exists largely on paper.

The Ninth Circuit, predictably, sustained the law. The question before the Supreme Court is whether that holding is consistent with *Bruen*'s demand for historical justification rooted in the founding era.

## The Question Presented and the Analytical Framework

Under **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the government bears the burden of demonstrating, when a challenged firearms regulation implicates the Second Amendment's plain text, that the regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in America. This is a demanding standard. The government must identify historical analogues that are sufficiently similar in their justification and their burden on the Second Amendment right to support the modern restriction.

As I have explained previously, the *Bruen* framework asks two questions in sequence. First: does the challenged law burden conduct covered by the Second Amendment's plain text? If yes, the burden shifts to the government. Second: can the government identify a sufficiently analogous historical tradition? If not, the law is unconstitutional.

In *Wolford*, as Hawaii itself conceded, the answer to the first question is yes. A law that prohibits a licensed carry holder from bringing a firearm into a gas station — conduct that falls squarely within the right to carry recognized in *Heller* and *Bruen* — burdens the plain text of the Second Amendment. The entire dispute therefore turns on the historical tradition inquiry.

## The Oral Argument Tea Leaves

The oral argument transcript in *Wolford* provides substantial reason for optimism. Chief Justice John Roberts, who does not always side with the Second Amendment's most expansive readings, engaged Hawaii's counsel with a pointed analogy: a political candidate canvassing door-to-door exercises a First Amendment right on private property without needing the property owner's advance permission. The candidate exercises that right until told to stop — not the reverse. Roberts challenged Hawaii to explain why the Second Amendment should operate differently.

That challenge reflects a foundational constitutional principle: the default presumption for constitutionally protected rights runs in favor of the right-holder, not against. A law that requires citizens to affirmatively obtain permission before exercising a constitutional right inverts this presumption and places the burden where the Constitution does not permit it to rest.

Justice Amy Coney Barrett elaborated on the same theme in a different register, positing scenarios involving racial access to private property and political expression to illustrate the absurdity of Hawaii's consent-default regime when applied to other constitutional guarantees. If a state cannot require Black citizens to obtain advance permission to enter publicly accessible private spaces, and cannot require citizens to get property-owner consent before engaging in political canvassing, it cannot require licensed carry holders to obtain the same consent before exercising the right to bear arms.

Justice Samuel Alito was, characteristically, the most direct. His observation that Hawaii is "relegating the Second Amendment to second-class status" cuts to the core of what *Bruen* and *Heller* both warned against: the treatment of the right to keep and bear arms as a disfavored constitutional guarantee entitled to less judicial protection than other enumerated rights. The Court's prior decisions have been explicit on this point — the Second Amendment is not a "second-class right," and courts that treat it as one are applying the wrong legal framework.

## What the Majority Opinion Must Say

Winning *Wolford* is necessary but not sufficient. As I have emphasized consistently, the most consequential aspect of any Supreme Court decision — particularly in an area of law as contested and actively litigated as the Second Amendment — is the reasoning set forth in the majority opinion. It is that reasoning, rather than the specific outcome, that will govern the hundreds of cases currently working their way through the lower courts.

There are several things the majority opinion in *Wolford* should accomplish.

First, it should firmly establish the default presumption in favor of carry in publicly accessible spaces. The common law and founding-era tradition treated armed citizens as having the liberty to carry unless specifically excluded. Hawaii's inversion of that presumption should be rejected not merely as applied to this specific law, but as a general constitutional matter.

Second, the opinion should clarify the limits of the "sensitive places" exception. *Bruen* acknowledged that governments may prohibit carry in discrete, historically grounded locations — legislative assemblies, courthouses, polling places, and analogous venues. The majority should draw a clear line between those historically supported exclusions and broader categorical prohibitions like Hawaii's, making clear that the exception cannot be stretched to encompass ordinary commercial establishments simply because they are privately owned.

Third, the opinion should address — and reject — the use of post-Civil War Black Codes and Reconstruction-era statutes as founding-era historical analogues. These laws were not enacted by constitutional governments operating within the framework of the Second Amendment; they were enacted by unreconstructed Confederate state governments for the express purpose of disarming the Black population. They represent a tradition of constitutional violation, not constitutional permission.

## The Broader Significance of Wolford

Beyond the specific doctrinal questions it presents, *Wolford v. Lopez* is significant because it comes to the Court from the Ninth Circuit — a court that has historically been the most hostile to Second Amendment claims in the post-*Bruen* environment. A Supreme Court decision reversing the Ninth Circuit and providing a strong methodological framework for evaluating carry restrictions will have its most immediate practical effect in the states within the Ninth Circuit's jurisdiction: California, Oregon, Washington, Arizona, Nevada, Hawaii, Idaho, Montana, and Alaska.

Of those states, California presents the most extensive post-*Bruen* litigation landscape. California has enacted or retained dozens of firearms restrictions that are currently being challenged in federal court under *Bruen*'s framework. A broad and clearly reasoned decision in *Wolford* will accelerate the resolution of those challenges in favor of the Second Amendment.

## Implications

I remain confident, based on the oral argument record, that *Wolford v. Lopez* will be decided in favor of the Second Amendment. The Court's six-justice conservative majority has demonstrated, across *Heller*, *McDonald*, and *Bruen*, a sustained commitment to treating the Second Amendment as a genuine constitutional right entitled to the full protection of the judiciary. The oral argument in *Wolford* reflects that commitment, and Hawaii's failure to identify adequate historical support for its consent-default regime should prove fatal to the state's position.

The decision is expected by the end of the current term, in late spring or early summer. When it arrives, it will represent not merely a win for the named plaintiff, but a significant step forward in the long project of ensuring that all Americans — in Hawaii, in California, in New York, and across the country — can exercise their constitutional right to keep and bear arms in the places where they live, work, and go about their daily lives.
