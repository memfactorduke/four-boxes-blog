## Summary

A federal district court judge in Virginia has issued a significant ruling calling into question Virginia's disenfranchisement law as applied to convicted felons, finding that the interplay between federal law and the Commonwealth's statutes creates constitutional problems with the State's blanket denial of voting rights to those convicted of felonies. That ruling provides an important occasion for a deeper analysis of a question that generates more confusion than it should: How can one coherently argue, on the one hand, that nonviolent felons may retain Second Amendment rights, and on the other, that felons may properly be denied the right to vote and the right to serve on a jury? The answer, which I want to explain carefully and clearly, lies in a fundamental distinction between natural rights and civic rights — a distinction that is essential to understanding both the Second Amendment and the broader structure of American constitutional liberties.

## Background

The case at issue involved multiple plaintiffs who argued before Judge Gibney in the Eastern District of Virginia that the Commonwealth's disenfranchisement law — which strips convicted felons of the right to vote — was unconstitutional as applied, particularly in light of the interplay between Virginia's state law and certain provisions of federal law. Judge Gibney granted summary judgment in favor of the plaintiffs, finding that there were constitutional problems with how Virginia was administering its felon disenfranchisement scheme under the relevant federal statutory framework.

I want to set aside the specific interplay between Virginia state law and the federal statutory overlay for a moment, because there is a more fundamental and far more important point to address. This case provides a timely opportunity to explain a conceptual distinction that is frequently misunderstood, even by people who are generally supportive of Second Amendment rights.

## The Apparent Inconsistency — And Why It Is Not Actually Inconsistent

Here is the question that appears, on the surface, to create a contradiction. Second Amendment advocates — myself included — have argued that nonviolent felons, such as the plaintiff in the Third Circuit's **Range v. Attorney General** decision, may retain their Second Amendment rights even after conviction of a nonviolent felony. The argument is that the categorical prohibition on firearm possession by all persons convicted of any felony, codified at 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1), is constitutionally overbroad under the *Bruen* framework when applied to individuals who were convicted of nonviolent offenses and who pose no demonstrable threat of violence.

Yet those same advocates typically agree that felons may be disenfranchised — denied the right to vote and the right to serve on a jury. How can these two positions be reconciled? If a nonviolent felon retains Second Amendment rights, why does he not also retain voting rights and jury service rights?

The answer lies in the fundamental distinction between two entirely different categories of rights: natural rights and civic rights.

## Natural Rights Versus Civic Rights

The right to keep and bear arms, as the Supreme Court confirmed in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008), codifies a preexisting, fundamental, individual right. The text of the Second Amendment itself signals this: it states that the right "shall not be infringed" — language that presupposes the right already exists. It existed before 1791. It existed before the Constitution. It existed before the founding of the American republic. It is, in the language of natural law theory, a natural right — a right that attaches to every human being by virtue of the human condition, not by virtue of citizenship in any particular political community.

The right of self-defense, which the Second Amendment codifies, flows from the most basic impulse of rational creatures: the instinct to preserve one's own life. In a state of nature — before organized society, before constitutions, before governments — every person possessed the right to defend herself with whatever means were available. That is the foundation of the Second Amendment right. Society did not create it. Society merely recognized and codified it.

The right to vote and the right to serve on a jury are categorically different. These are civic rights — rights that exist only because organized civil society created them. There is no right to vote in a state of nature. There is no jury in a state of nature. These are the creations of the particular constitutional and legal order that the American people established. They are rights granted by the political community to those who participate in it as members in good standing.

## The Legal Consequence of This Distinction

Because natural rights preexist civil society and attach to individuals independent of any grant by the government, the government cannot simply take them away by legislative fiat. The Supreme Court's *Heller* and *Bruen* decisions establish that the government may only restrict the Second Amendment right in ways that are consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation. Categorical exclusions of entire classes of persons from Second Amendment protections must be grounded in a Founding-era tradition of disarming that class.

The historical record shows that the Founders disarmed certain categories of dangerous persons — those who had demonstrated through violent conduct that they could not be trusted with firearms in civil society. But the Founders did not categorically disarm all persons who had ever been convicted of any offense, regardless of whether the offense involved violence or demonstrated dangerousness. The nonviolent felon who fraudulently obtained food stamps — the actual situation in **Range v. Attorney General** — is not the historically dangerous person whose disarmament the Founders would have recognized as consistent with the Second Amendment.

Civic rights, by contrast, can be conditioned on compliance with the obligations of civil society — including the obligation to obey the law — precisely because the political community that created those rights has the authority to define their conditions. The community that grants the privilege of voting and jury service may rationally decide that persons who have violated the community's fundamental laws have forfeited, at least temporarily, the right to participate in its civic functions. This is not the deprivation of a preexisting human right; it is the revocation of a privilege that the community itself created and that it has the sovereign authority to govern.

## The Virginia Decision and Its Implications

The federal court's ruling in the Virginia disenfranchisement case does not actually disturb this fundamental conceptual framework. The court's concern was not with whether felons may ever be disenfranchised — a question that has a clear constitutional answer under existing doctrine — but rather with whether the specific interaction between Virginia's disenfranchisement law and particular provisions of federal law created procedural and substantive problems with how the Commonwealth was applying its policy.

The interplay between federal law and state disenfranchisement statutes is a technically complex area, and courts have reached varying conclusions about how various federal provisions affect state felon voting laws. The Virginia decision reflects that complexity.

For Second Amendment purposes, however, the conceptual clarity is essential. Those who argue that nonviolent felons should retain Second Amendment rights are not arguing that convicted criminals should face no civic consequences for their offenses. They are making the specific and limited point that the Second Amendment — which codifies a natural right that predates civil society — cannot be stripped from a person merely because that person has committed a nonviolent offense, absent a historically grounded basis in the Founding era for treating that category of offense as grounds for categorical disarmament.

That position is entirely consistent with supporting felon disenfranchisement in the voting context, because the right to vote is a civic right that the community may govern — not a natural right that government merely recognizes.

## Implications

Understanding the distinction between natural rights and civic rights is indispensable for anyone who wants to engage seriously with Second Amendment jurisprudence or with the broader question of what rights convicted felons do and do not retain. The confusion that arises when people conflate these two categories is not merely academic — it produces sloppy legal arguments and political rhetoric that does not accurately describe what the law is or what it should be.

The Second Amendment movement's position is principled and consistent: the natural right to self-defense codified in the Second Amendment belongs to all members of "the people," subject only to historically grounded exceptions for those who have demonstrated through violent or dangerous conduct that their disarmament is warranted. That position neither requires nor implies any particular view about whether felons should be allowed to vote — because the two questions involve fundamentally different categories of rights.
