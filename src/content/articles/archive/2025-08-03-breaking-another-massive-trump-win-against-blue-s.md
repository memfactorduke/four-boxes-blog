## Summary

A coalition of sixteen states — led by New York and including California, Hawaii, Colorado, Massachusetts, New Jersey, Oregon, Washington, and Wisconsin — filed suit in the United States District Court for the Southern District of New York seeking a preliminary injunction to compel the National Science Foundation to restore hundreds of millions of dollars in federal research grants that the Trump administration terminated pursuant to its April 18, 2025 priority directive. Judge John Cronin, a Trump-appointed judge sitting in the Southern District of New York, denied the preliminary injunction in its entirety. The court's ruling rested on the bedrock principle that when a plaintiff's claim is fundamentally one for money damages against the federal government, the proper forum is the United States Court of Federal Claims under the Tucker Act — not the district court system. The states were in the wrong court, seeking the wrong remedy, and Judge Cronin correctly sent them away.

## Background

The National Firearms Act grants dispute arises from the Trump administration's broader initiative to realign federal spending with national priorities free of diversity, equity, and inclusion criteria. On April 18, 2025, the National Science Foundation issued a priority directive announcing that the agency's scientific research funding would no longer give preference to certain demographic groups at the expense of others. The directive reaffirmed that NSF's core mission is to fund the best science in the national interest — not to serve as a vehicle for identity-based grant allocation.

Pursuant to that directive, the NSF terminated a significant number of previously awarded research grants to universities and nonprofit institutions in the plaintiff states. Those terminations — totaling hundreds of millions of dollars — prompted the sixteen-state coalition to file suit in the Southern District of New York. The states challenged the directive on multiple administrative law grounds and moved for a preliminary injunction that would have required the NSF to reinstate the terminated grants and halt further grant terminations pending the outcome of the litigation.

The states filed in the Southern District of New York rather than in the Court of Federal Claims, a strategic choice that has become standard practice for plaintiffs seeking rapid injunctive relief against Trump administration executive actions in favorable jurisdictions.

## The Court's Analysis

Judge Cronin's denial of the preliminary injunction turned on a threshold jurisdictional question that goes to the structural architecture of suits against the federal government. The United States and its agencies enjoy sovereign immunity — they cannot be sued without their consent. Congress has selectively waived that immunity in specific statutory contexts, and the scope of any given waiver determines both the proper forum and the available remedies.

When a plaintiff seeks money damages from the federal government arising out of a contract or grant agreement, the applicable waiver is found in the Tucker Act, 28 U.S.C. § 1491, which vests exclusive jurisdiction over such claims in the United States Court of Federal Claims. The district courts lack jurisdiction over money claims against the federal government that exceed $10,000. This principle is well established and has been reaffirmed repeatedly by the Supreme Court.

The states attempted to recast their claim as one for injunctive relief — seeking a court order compelling the NSF to reinstate the grants and refrain from further terminations. But Judge Cronin saw through that framing. At its core, the states' claim was that the federal government had made contractual commitments through the grant awards and that those commitments obligated the government to disburse funds. That is a money claim. Dressing it in the language of injunctive relief does not change its essential character or manufacture district court jurisdiction where none exists.

As the court explained, the Supreme Court has addressed precisely this circumstance in the context of the current administration's wave of funding disputes. Litigants who want money from the federal government must pursue that money through the Tucker Act in the Court of Federal Claims. They cannot circumvent that requirement by filing in sympathetic district courts and framing their damages claims as injunctive actions.

## The NSF Priority Directive

The NSF's April 18, 2025 directive — the policy instrument that triggered the grant terminations — reflects the administration's view that federal science funding should be allocated on the basis of scientific merit and national strategic value, not on the basis of whether proposed research advances particular demographic or ideological priorities. The directive specifically states that the NSF's efforts to advance scientific research will not give preference to certain groups at the expense of others.

This policy reorientation is a legitimate exercise of executive discretion over the allocation of federal spending. Congress appropriates funds; the executive determines how those appropriated funds are distributed within the statutory parameters Congress establishes. The NSF, like all federal agencies, operates subject to the President's direction as the head of the executive branch. Where Congress has delegated broad grant-making authority to the NSF without dictating the specific criteria for individual grant awards, the executive retains substantial discretion over how to implement the legislative mandate.

The states' fundamental objection — that the Trump administration is withdrawing funding that the Biden administration had committed — does not state a constitutional violation. Changes in presidential administrations frequently result in shifts in spending priorities. A new administration is not constitutionally required to honor every discretionary spending commitment made by its predecessor, particularly where those commitments reflect policy preferences rather than statutory mandates.

## Implications

Judge Cronin's ruling is significant for two reasons. First, it demonstrates that Trump-appointed judges in traditionally favorable plaintiff jurisdictions — including the Southern District of New York — are applying settled legal principles rather than reaching for results. The Tucker Act doctrine is not a close question, and the court correctly applied it.

Second, and more broadly, the decision signals that states and other litigants who wish to challenge federal grant terminations and spending reallocations must do so through the proper channels. The Court of Federal Claims provides an appropriate forum for the adjudication of breach-of-contract claims against the federal government, but it cannot provide the rapid injunctive relief that plaintiffs have been seeking in the district courts. Plaintiffs who want money must litigate for money — in the right court, under the right legal standard, with the understanding that they are asserting contract claims rather than constitutional emergencies.

The administration's NSF priority directive remains in effect. Grant terminations pursuant to that directive will continue unless and until a court with proper jurisdiction determines that the terminations violate specific statutory or constitutional requirements. On the current record, no such determination is likely.
