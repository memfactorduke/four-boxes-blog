There is genuinely good news coming out of the Trump administration on Second Amendment enforcement. Harmeet Dhillon's team at the Civil Rights Division got the analytical framework exactly right in *Barnett v. Raoul* at the Seventh Circuit. I want to give credit where it is due.

Which is exactly why the brief filed in *Brown v. ATF* in the Eastern District of Missouri is so frustrating to read.

Someone is not babysitting the career lawyers drafting these briefs. The result is a set of unforced errors that could create harmful precedent — not because the DOJ intends to undermine the Second Amendment, but because the attorneys who wrote this brief do not appear to have internalized what *Bruen* actually requires.

## Error One: Burying Common Use in the Wrong Analytical Step

Under *NYSRPA v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the analysis proceeds in two steps. First, does the conduct fall within the plain text of the Second Amendment — is the weapon an "arm"? Second, if yes, the burden shifts entirely to the *government* to show the regulation is consistent with this Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation. The burden never shifts back.

The common-use test from *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), belongs at the historical-tradition step as part of the government's burden. It evaluates whether a weapon falls within *Heller*'s "dangerous and unusual" exception — which requires a weapon to be both dangerous *and* unusual.

The *Brown v. ATF* brief drags that test up into the plain-text step, effectively telling the court: plaintiffs must first prove suppressors and short-barrel rifles are in common use before they can even establish Second Amendment coverage. That inverts *Bruen*. It converts a burden the government must carry into a threshold the plaintiff must clear. If accepted, the government could deny Second Amendment protection to entire categories of firearms simply by challenging their prevalence — without ever producing a single historical analog.

## Error Two: Misreading Heller on Facial Challenges

The brief argues that because suppressors could theoretically be used for criminal purposes, the NFA cannot be challenged on its face — only as-applied challenges would be appropriate.

This cannot survive contact with *Heller* itself. *Heller* was a facial challenge to D.C.'s handgun ban. Justice Scalia's majority struck it down without requiring proof that every application of the ban was unconstitutional. Couldn't D.C.'s handgun ban be constitutionally applied to an armed robber? Of course. The Court still struck it down facially because the statute's categorical prohibition on lawful possession had no limiting principle.

The NFA's suppressor provisions work the same way — possession without registration is the offense, full stop, regardless of whether the possessor is law-abiding or criminal. The DOJ's argument proves too much: virtually no firearms law could ever be struck down facially, because virtually any firearms law could theoretically apply to a criminal. *Heller* already rejected that logic.

## Error Three: Conflating Registration With Shall-Issue Licensing

The brief attempts to justify the NFA's registration requirements by analogizing them to historical shall-issue licensing regimes. This conflation is not new, but it remains wrong.

Then-Judge Brett Kavanaugh addressed it directly in his dissent in *Heller v. District of Columbia* (Heller II), 670 F.3d 1244 (D.C. Cir. 2011) — using the text, history, and tradition methodology that *Bruen* would later adopt. A shall-issue licensing system that confirms a person is not a prohibited possessor is categorically different from a registration regime that compels citizens to seek government approval before exercising a constitutional right. Registration is historically a predicate step toward confiscation. Shall-issue licensing is minimal vetting. The two are not analogous.

A Justice who wrote at length about this distinction now sits on the Supreme Court. The fact that DOJ lawyers continue to conflate these concepts is inexplicable.

## Who Is Writing These Briefs?

The political leadership at DOJ — including Harmeet Dhillon's team — is genuinely committed to advancing Second Amendment rights. Their work in *Barnett v. Raoul* proves they can apply *Bruen* correctly. The *Brown v. ATF* brief reads like the work of career attorneys who were never on board with this administration's Second Amendment agenda.

The fix is straightforward: political appointees need to personally review briefs in Second Amendment cases before they are filed. The *Heller*/*Bruen* methodology is not esoteric. Every DOJ attorney handling these cases needs explicit guidance on how the two-step framework operates, and explicit prohibitions on the burden-shifting and facial-challenge arguments catalogued above.

This administration's instincts on the Second Amendment are sound. Its brief-writing, in too many cases, is not. Those two things must be brought into alignment — because courts build on whatever foundation the parties give them, and a flawed foundation produces a flawed edifice.
