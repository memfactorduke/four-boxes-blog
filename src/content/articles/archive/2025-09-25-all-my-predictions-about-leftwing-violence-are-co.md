## Summary

Several years ago, in the final chapter of my book *First They Came for the Gun Owners*, I made a prediction that I sincerely wished would prove incorrect: that the political left in America, having repeatedly failed to achieve its objectives through lawful democratic and judicial processes, would increasingly resort to threats of violence and actual political violence as an instrument of political coercion. That prediction has been validated, and its validation carries profound implications for the constitutional right to keep and bear arms. The ability of law-abiding Americans to defend themselves, their families, and their communities against political violence is not an abstract theoretical concern — it is a practical necessity that the Second Amendment was designed, in part, to address.

## Background

The right to keep and bear arms guaranteed by the Second Amendment rests on a foundation of multiple and overlapping justifications — the common defense, resistance to tyranny, and individual self-defense among them. The Supreme Court in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008) recognized self-defense as the "core" of the Second Amendment right, and the Court's subsequent decisions have consistently affirmed that this right is operative in the real world, against real threats, by real people.

The question of who poses those real threats — and in what social and political context — is not merely academic. In *First They Came for the Gun Owners*, I devoted the final chapter to an analysis of the left's emerging willingness to employ intimidation, harassment, and political violence against its opponents, and to the implications of that willingness for the Second Amendment debate. That chapter documented a range of conduct: the harassment of Republican officials in restaurants and public spaces; the near-massacre of Republican House members at a congressional baseball practice by a left-wing gunman who shot Representative Steve Scalise of Louisiana; and the broader pattern of political rhetoric from prominent Democratic figures that, at minimum, normalized the view that civility need not be extended to political opponents.

## The Warning I Issued

The passage I wrote at that time is worth recalling in full context. I wrote: "It is clear that the left's war on traditional Americans and their constitutional liberties will not end with words. If leftists lose at the ballot box and in the courthouse, the marketplace, and the public square, they simply won't accept it. We saw that beginning on election night in 2016, and they've been proving it ever since." I further noted that this was "worse than a tantrum" — it was "a campaign of intimidation, violence, and personal destruction."

I also quoted then-Democratic presidential candidate Hillary Clinton, who told CNN in October 2018: "You cannot be civil with a political party that wants to destroy what you stand for, what you care about. That's why I believe if we are fortunate enough to win back the House and or the Senate, that's when civility can start again." The implication — that civility was conditional on electoral outcomes, and that absent those outcomes, incivility was justified — was a statement that I characterized as an incitement to exactly the kind of conduct that has since materialized.

## Contemporary Manifestations

The events since that book's publication have, unfortunately, confirmed the trajectory I identified. Political violence targeting conservative and Republican figures has not abated. Threats against members of Congress, public officials, and ordinary citizens who publicly support conservative causes have become commonplace. The political assassination attempts against former President Trump represent a qualitative escalation of this phenomenon that demands serious analysis and response.

The critical constitutional dimension of this reality is this: the Second Amendment right to self-defense exists precisely for circumstances in which the state cannot provide adequate protection to individuals against violence — whether that violence emanates from common criminals, foreign adversaries, or politically motivated domestic actors. The founders understood that a free people required the means of self-defense; the contemporary evidence confirms that this understanding was not merely historical but permanently applicable to the conditions of a free society.

## The Constitutional Case for an Armed Citizenry

I submit that the unfolding reality of politically motivated violence in contemporary America strengthens, rather than weakens, the constitutional and practical case for widespread civilian armament. The purpose of the Second Amendment is not decorative or historical — it is functional. It ensures that law-abiding Americans retain the practical capacity to defend themselves and their communities against violence, including organized political violence that the state may be unable or unwilling to prevent.

Those who argue for disarming the citizenry — under whatever regulatory scheme — must grapple with the reality that a disarmed population is a population rendered dependent upon the state for its physical safety. In a political environment characterized by the escalating normalization of political violence against conservatives, Second Amendment advocates, and law-abiding gun owners, that dependency is not a neutral policy preference. It is a strategic and potentially catastrophic vulnerability.

The Second Amendment's framers were not naive about the potential for political violence. They had lived through it. The right they enshrined was designed to ensure that no future generation of Americans would be left defenseless against the kinds of threats — internal and external — that history has repeatedly demonstrated to be real.

## Implications

The validation of my earlier predictions is not a source of satisfaction. I would have preferred to be wrong. But the evidence compels the conclusion that the threats I identified have materialized, and that the constitutional and practical case for the Second Amendment is, if anything, more compelling today than it was when I wrote those words.

For the Second Amendment community, this analysis reinforces a fundamental point that I have made consistently: the right to keep and bear arms is not a hobby right, a sporting right, or a hunting right. It is a civil right — a fundamental constitutional protection against the specific and documented threat of political violence and the broader category of threats to individual liberty that an armed citizenry is uniquely positioned to deter and resist. Any effort to disarm law-abiding Americans must be evaluated in light of this reality, and any administration or legislature that proposes such disarmament must answer for the vulnerability it would create in a political environment that has demonstrated a clear and present danger of organized political violence.
