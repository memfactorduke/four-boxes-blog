## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has denied certiorari in the challenge to Missouri's Second Amendment Preservation Act — the state law that attempted to nullify or limit enforcement of federal firearms regulations within Missouri's borders. A three-judge panel of the Eighth Circuit had previously held the law unconstitutional under the Supremacy Clause of the United States Constitution. I was not surprised by either the Eighth Circuit's ruling or the Supreme Court's refusal to hear the case, and I want to explain precisely why — and, more importantly, what Missouri can and should do going forward to protect its citizens' Second Amendment rights through legally sound means.

## Background

I have received a substantial volume of questions from viewers and readers about what happened with the Missouri Second Amendment Preservation Act. Let me walk through the relevant history.

Missouri enacted what it called its Second Amendment Preservation Act — sometimes referred to colloquially as a "Second Amendment sanctuary law" — as a vehicle for limiting or resisting federal gun control enforcement within the state. The Biden administration sued to enjoin the law. A three-judge panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Eighth Circuit — the federal appellate court covering Missouri, Minnesota, and other Midwestern states — ruled that the Missouri law was unconstitutional under the Supremacy Clause of the United States Constitution.

The Supremacy Clause, found in Article VI of the Constitution, provides that the Constitution, federal laws made pursuant to it, and federal treaties are "the supreme Law of the Land." The Eighth Circuit's holding, in essence, was that Missouri's attempt to void or nullify federal gun control laws through state legislation was not permissible under the constitutional structure.

In 2025, the state of Missouri petitioned the United States Supreme Court for certiorari, asking the Court to hear the case and address questions of Tenth Amendment federalism, states' rights, and the proper interpretation of the Missouri Preservation Act in light of what is known as the anti-commandeering doctrine.

## The Trump DOJ's Remarkable Position

What made this case particularly noteworthy — and what prompted so many of my viewers to contact me — was the position taken by the Department of Justice after the transition from the Biden administration to the Trump administration.

The Trump DOJ, in responding to Missouri's cert petition, took a position that was, to put it plainly, candid and legally interesting. The DOJ told the Supreme Court, in effect: we agree that the Eighth Circuit's ruling striking down Missouri's law in its entirety may have been at least partially incorrect. The panel's reasoning was not fully developed with respect to certain arguments Missouri raised, and the Eighth Circuit may well have gotten portions of its analysis wrong.

Despite this concession, the Trump DOJ recommended that the Supreme Court deny certiorari. The DOJ's position was that the Court should decline to hear the case — but leave open the possibility of a negotiated modification of the district court's judgment that would allow constitutionally sound portions of Missouri's law to remain in effect, while enjoining only those provisions that genuinely conflict with federal law.

That is a remarkable position for any administration to take: telling the Supreme Court that the lower court may have erred, but recommending against review anyway, and instead proposing a bilateral resolution between the executive branch and Missouri. The Supreme Court followed the DOJ's recommendation and denied cert.

## Why I Was Not Surprised

I want to be direct about something: I did not extensively cover this case as it was developing, because I assessed the odds of the Supreme Court granting certiorari at essentially zero. That assessment was not a lack of sympathy for Missouri's goals. I generally support efforts to limit unconstitutional federal gun control enforcement. But whether a case is legally or politically sympathetic is entirely separate from whether it is the kind of case the Supreme Court will take.

The Supreme Court grants certiorari in approximately 70 to 80 cases per term out of thousands of petitions. The criteria the Court applies are well established:

**No Circuit Split.** The primary driver of certiorari grants is a division of authority among the federal circuits — when different courts of appeals have reached conflicting results on the same legal question. There is no circuit split on the constitutionality of state Second Amendment preservation acts. The Missouri law was essentially a one-of-a-kind statute. No other state had enacted a genuinely analogous law that had produced conflicting circuit precedent. If the Supreme Court had ruled in Missouri's favor, the decision would have primarily applied to Missouri. It would not have resolved a genuine national conflict in the law.

**Outlier Law, Limited National Application.** The Missouri SAPA was, in legal terms, an outlier — a novel attempt to use state law as a mechanism for nullifying federal statutes, a theory that has a troubled constitutional history going back to the Civil War era. The Court had no compelling institutional reason to weigh in on a law with no analogues in any other jurisdiction.

**The Anti-Commandeering Doctrine Already Protects What Missouri Legitimately Wants.** This is the critical point that I believe many supporters of the Missouri law may not fully appreciate. The anti-commandeering doctrine — a well-established principle rooted in the Tenth Amendment and confirmed by the Supreme Court in **Printz v. United States** and related cases — already provides Missouri, and every other state, with the core protection Missouri was seeking.

## The Anti-Commandeering Doctrine: What Missouri Already Has

The anti-commandeering doctrine is settled constitutional law. It provides that the federal government may not conscript or commandeer state officials or state resources to administer or enforce federal law. The federal government must use its own officers to enforce its own statutes.

What this means in practical terms is significant. A Missouri state law enforcement officer — a Kansas City police officer, a St. Louis County sheriff's deputy, the Missouri Attorney General — has no constitutional obligation to help the federal government enforce federal gun control laws. A state official who encounters a potential federal firearms violation has no legal duty to report it to the ATF, the FBI, or the United States Attorney's office. The federal government cannot mandate that Missouri officers participate in federal enforcement operations.

This is not a matter of controversy. It is established law. And it is a powerful tool for states that want to decline participation in federal gun control enforcement.

What the anti-commandeering doctrine does not permit is the converse: state officials may not actively interfere with federal law enforcement. There is a clear and important distinction between declining to assist — which is constitutionally permissible — and actively obstructing — which is not. A state officer who deliberately blocks federal agents from making a lawful arrest, who affirmatively thwarts a federal enforcement action, or who otherwise takes affirmative steps to defeat federal law enforcement is not exercising anti-commandeering rights. That is active nullification, and the Supremacy Clause forbids it.

The problem with Missouri's SAPA, as written, was that certain provisions crossed the line from the permissible (declining to assist) into the impermissible (actively obstructing or purporting to invalidate federal law). The Eighth Circuit, in striking down the law, did not appreciate with sufficient care the distinction between these two categories — and the Trump DOJ, to its credit, acknowledged as much. But the statute, as drafted, provided enough fodder for the court to reach the conclusion it did.

## A Path Forward for Missouri

If I were advising Missouri's legislature and attorney general, I would pursue two parallel strategies.

**Strategy One: Negotiate the Judgment.** Missouri should engage directly with the Trump DOJ — which has already signaled openness to a negotiated resolution — to restructure the existing injunction. The DOJ has acknowledged that the Eighth Circuit's ruling was overbroad in certain respects. There is room for a modified judgment that allows the vast majority of Missouri's law to take effect, while enjoining only those specific provisions that constitute active interference with federal enforcement rather than mere non-cooperation. Missouri should press that negotiation aggressively, using the DOJ's own admissions as leverage.

**Strategy Two: Legislative Redrafting.** The Missouri legislature should enact a revised Second Amendment preservation statute that is carefully drafted to achieve what the anti-commandeering doctrine already permits, without the provisions that gave the Eighth Circuit grounds to strike the law down in its entirety. The revised statute should make unambiguously clear that:

- Nothing in the law purports to nullify or void any provision of federal law.
- Nothing in the law purports to make Missouri law superior to the United States Constitution or federal statutes enacted pursuant to it.
- The statute's purpose is solely to exercise Missouri's recognized constitutional right under the anti-commandeering doctrine to decline to direct its state officials to cooperate in federal firearms enforcement activities.
- No Missouri official shall be required to affirmatively assist federal agents in the enforcement of federal gun control statutes.
- Nothing in the statute shall be construed to authorize or permit any Missouri official to actively interfere with, obstruct, or impede any lawful federal law enforcement operation.

A statute drafted along these lines would be on constitutionally sound footing. It would accomplish the core of what Missouri's Second Amendment community is seeking — practical non-cooperation with federal gun enforcement — while removing the provisions that drew the Supremacy Clause challenge.

## A Note on the Eighth Circuit's Analysis

I have reviewed the Eighth Circuit's decision in **United States v. Missouri**, and I will say plainly that the panel's reasoning was not its finest work. The opinion was loose in certain respects, leaned heavily in favor of federal supremacy without fully engaging with Missouri's anti-commandeering arguments, and did not give adequate consideration to the distinction between non-cooperation (permissible) and active nullification (impermissible). The Trump DOJ was correct to acknowledge these analytical gaps.

The Eighth Circuit is not a court known for producing cutting-edge constitutional jurisprudence. It is a solid, institutionalist court that tends toward caution and deference. That is not a criticism of the judges as individuals; it is an observation about the court's overall jurisprudential character. The court saw a state law that used confrontational language about federal supremacy and reacted accordingly — without the kind of careful textual and structural analysis that the anti-commandeering question genuinely requires.

## Implications

The Supreme Court's denial of certiorari in the Missouri SAPA litigation is not the end of the road for states seeking to protect their citizens' Second Amendment rights. The anti-commandeering doctrine remains robust, well-established, and available to every state in the union. States have the constitutional right — indeed, I would argue they have a responsibility to their citizens — to decline to direct their law enforcement resources toward the enforcement of federal gun control statutes they regard as unconstitutional.

What states cannot do is use that doctrine as cover for laws that cross the line into active obstruction. Missouri's SAPA, as written, gave courts enough justification to strike it down. A more carefully drafted statute — one that plainly and expressly limits itself to the recognized boundaries of anti-commandeering — could and should survive constitutional scrutiny.

The work of protecting the Second Amendment at the state level is not finished. It requires both legal precision and strategic patience. Missouri has an opportunity to get this right, and I expect this conversation will continue as the state decides how to proceed.
