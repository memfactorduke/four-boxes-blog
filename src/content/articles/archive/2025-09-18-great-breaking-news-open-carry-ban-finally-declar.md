## Summary

Florida's First District Court of Appeal has issued a decision in *McDaniels v. Florida* holding that Florida's longstanding prohibition on the open carrying of handguns violates the Second Amendment of the United States Constitution as interpreted by the Supreme Court in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). The ruling is jurisprudentially significant for several reasons: it represents a direct departure from the Florida Supreme Court's 2017 decision in *Norman v. State*, which upheld the same open carry ban under a different constitutional standard; it requires courts across Florida to navigate the tension between these conflicting precedents; and Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier has responded by issuing a memorandum to law enforcement agencies and prosecuting attorneys statewide concluding that the *McDaniels* decision is binding across the entire state of Florida.

## Background

Florida has banned the open carrying of handguns as a general matter since 1987. Notwithstanding the subsequent adoption of constitutional carry — permitting concealed carry without a permit — and the existence of a shall-issue concealed carry licensing regime, the open carrying of handguns has remained prohibited under Florida law throughout this period.

In 2017, the Florida Supreme Court — the highest court in the Florida judicial hierarchy, whose decisions are binding on all 67 counties in the state — decided *Norman v. State* and held that the open carry ban was consistent with the Second Amendment. The standard the Florida Supreme Court applied in *Norman*, however, was the "intermediate scrutiny" or "means-ends balancing" framework that lower federal courts had developed in the years following *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008). Under that framework, courts weighed the government's interest in restricting firearms against the burden imposed on Second Amendment rights, rather than consulting the historical tradition of firearms regulation that *Bruen* subsequently mandated.

The Supreme Court's decision in *Bruen* in 2022 fundamentally altered the analytical framework applicable to Second Amendment challenges. The *Bruen* Court explicitly repudiated the means-ends balancing approach that lower courts — including the Florida Supreme Court in *Norman* — had employed. Under *Bruen*, intermediate scrutiny, strict scrutiny, and other interest-balancing tests are inapplicable to Second Amendment claims; the sole question is whether the challenged regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in America.

## The First District Court of Appeal's Decision in *McDaniels v. Florida*

Florida's judicial hierarchy consists of trial courts, six intermediate district courts of appeal, and the Florida Supreme Court. The First District Court of Appeal covers 32 of Florida's 67 counties, making it the largest of the six district courts. In *McDaniels v. Florida*, the First District Court of Appeal held that Florida's open carry ban is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment as analyzed under the *Bruen* framework.

The First District's analysis necessarily confronted the conflict with *Norman v. State*: the Florida Supreme Court had previously upheld this precise statute. The First District's resolution of this tension is grounded in the observation that *Norman* applied a constitutional standard — intermediate scrutiny — that the Supreme Court of the United States subsequently and explicitly held was inapplicable to Second Amendment cases. When the nation's highest court authoritatively establishes that a lower court applied the wrong legal standard in adjudicating a constitutional right, a subsequent court applying the correct standard is not bound by the prior result even where the same statute is at issue. The constitutional question, properly framed under *Bruen*, had not been decided in *Norman* because *Norman* asked and answered a different question under a different test.

## Attorney General Uthmeier's Statewide Memorandum

Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier, who has demonstrated strong commitment to Second Amendment rights, responded to the *McDaniels* decision by issuing a memorandum to law enforcement agencies and prosecuting attorneys throughout the state. The memorandum takes the position that the *McDaniels* decision is binding on all 67 counties in Florida — not merely the 32 counties within the First District's territorial jurisdiction — and that the attorney general, as the state's chief law enforcement officer, agrees with the constitutional determination that Florida's open carry ban violates the Second Amendment.

The legal basis for the attorney general's statewide application of *McDaniels* rests, in my assessment, on the recognition that when a court applies a new constitutional standard — the *Bruen* framework — to a statute that had previously been reviewed under a different and now-repudiated standard, the constitutional question under the correct standard is one that has not previously been resolved by a superior court. The Florida Supreme Court's holding in *Norman* that the open carry ban survived intermediate scrutiny does not constitute a binding holding that the ban survives the *Bruen* historical tradition inquiry, because *Norman* never applied that inquiry.

## The Constitutional Analysis Under *Bruen*

The open carry of firearms is, as a matter of historical record, a practice the Founders recognized and did not categorically prohibit. The *Bruen* decision itself arose from a New York challenge to that state's effective ban on carrying firearms in public — including openly — and the Supreme Court held that the Second Amendment protects the right to carry arms in public for self-defense. *Bruen* further held that New York's requirement of a showing of special need before a license to carry would be issued was unconstitutional.

Florida's ban on open carry is structurally similar to the restrictions the *Bruen* Court examined. The question is whether a founding-era tradition of prohibiting the open carry of handguns exists. It does not: the historical record shows that open carry of firearms was common and largely unregulated in the founding era and throughout the nineteenth century. The earliest significant restrictions on open carry arose in the post-Civil War South, in circumstances that the *Bruen* Court identified as constitutionally suspect because they were frequently enacted to disarm freedmen and political opponents rather than as neutral exercises of the police power.

## The Unresolved Tension with *Norman v. State*

The Florida Supreme Court's 2017 decision in *Norman* presents a complicating factor. Under the normal operation of stare decisis, a lower court decision that conflicts with a higher court's prior ruling on the same statute is not binding — the higher court's holding controls. If the Florida Supreme Court considers that its *Norman* decision resolved the constitutional question and that *McDaniels* therefore improperly conflicts with binding precedent, it could grant review and reinstate the open carry ban under the stare decisis principle.

The response to this argument is that *Norman* did not resolve the constitutional question under *Bruen* because *Norman* applied a different constitutional standard. When the Supreme Court of the United States changes the constitutional framework that governs a class of cases, prior state court decisions applying the old framework are not entitled to binding effect on the new question. The Florida Supreme Court, if it takes up *McDaniels*, will have the opportunity to apply the *Bruen* framework itself — but it cannot simply defer to *Norman*'s holding under intermediate scrutiny as if *Bruen* had never been decided.

## Implications

The *McDaniels v. Florida* decision, and the attorney general's statewide memorandum endorsing its constitutionality, represent a significant development in Florida's application of the Second Amendment. Florida already has among the most permissive concealed carry laws in the nation; if the open carry ban is definitively struck down — either through the appellate process or through action by the Florida legislature — Florida's gun owners will have substantially expanded options for exercising their constitutional right to bear arms.

The case illustrates a broader pattern in Second Amendment jurisprudence following *Bruen*: courts that previously upheld firearms restrictions under now-repudiated means-ends balancing tests are being asked to revisit those decisions under the correct constitutional standard. In many instances, restrictions that survived intermediate scrutiny cannot survive the *Bruen* historical tradition inquiry, because there simply is no historical tradition of the challenged regulation to which the government can point. *McDaniels* is one prominent example of this dynamic, and it will not be the last.
