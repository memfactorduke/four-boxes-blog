## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit has issued a ruling in **National Association for Gun Rights v. Connecticut** upholding Connecticut's prohibition on AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles and its ban on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds, holding that Connecticut's restrictions are consistent with the Second Amendment. The opinion is, in my professional assessment, an embarrassment of jurisprudence — not merely wrong in its conclusions, but methodologically dishonest in its execution. The panel cherry-picked a partial sentence from the Supreme Court's controlling decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), while deliberately omitting the remainder of that sentence, which fatally undermines the panel's entire analytical framework.

## Background

Connecticut enacted legislation prohibiting the possession of semi-automatic rifles that the legislature designated as "assault weapons" — a category that includes the AR-15 and functionally equivalent semi-automatic firearms — as well as banning the possession of detachable magazines with a capacity greater than ten rounds. The Second Amendment Foundation, the National Association for Gun Rights, and other plaintiffs challenged the constitutionality of these restrictions and sought a preliminary injunction preventing the law from taking effect.

The federal district court declined to issue the preliminary injunction and upheld the law. The Second Circuit, on appeal, affirmed the district court's ruling, issuing a lengthy opinion — running approximately 75 pages — that purports to apply the **Bruen** framework but in fact departs from it in fundamental and, I submit, deliberate ways.

The AR-15 and comparable semi-automatic rifles are the most popular long guns in America. They are owned by tens of millions of law-abiding American citizens for a wide variety of lawful purposes: home defense, sport shooting, hunting, and general civilian ownership. Under **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), firearms in "common use" for lawful purposes are categorically protected by the Second Amendment. The AR-15 is, beyond any reasonable argument, in common use. A state law that prohibits its possession is on its face constitutionally suspect under **Heller**'s framework.

## The Second Circuit's Analytical Failure

The panel's threshold error is its organizing premise. Rather than asking, as **Bruen** requires, whether the challenged regulation is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation — which is the only constitutionally permissible inquiry under current Supreme Court precedent — the Second Circuit devoted the greater part of its opinion to discussing the ways in which certain semi-automatic rifles have been misused by individuals who commit mass shootings.

This is the wrong question. Constitutional rights are not defined by the worst-case misuse of the freedoms they protect. We do not narrow the scope of the First Amendment's protection of free speech because some individuals use speech to incite violence. We do not circumscribe the freedom of religion because some individuals have invoked religion to justify atrocities. The Second Amendment's guarantee of the right to keep and bear arms is likewise not measured by the ways in which a small number of criminals and psychopathic actors misuse firearms.

The constitutional inquiry, as the Supreme Court made unambiguously clear in **Bruen**, focuses on how ordinary law-abiding Americans exercise their Second Amendment rights. The lawful uses of the AR-15 by tens of millions of peaceable Americans — home defense, sporting purposes, hunting — are what the Second Amendment protects. The criminal misuse of any firearm by a small number of violent actors is not a legitimate basis for categorically prohibiting a protected arm.

## The Cherry-Picked Bruen Sentence

The Second Circuit's analytical dishonesty is most nakedly visible in its treatment of a specific passage from **Bruen**. The **Bruen** majority wrote the following complete sentence: "While the historical analogies here and in **Heller** are relatively simple to draw, other cases implicating unprecedented societal concerns or dramatic technological changes may require a more nuanced approach."

The Second Circuit quoted and relied upon the clause referring to "unprecedented societal concerns" to justify its conclusion that mass shootings with semi-automatic rifles constitute such an "unprecedented societal concern" — thereby allowing the court to apply a "more nuanced approach" rather than the strict text-and-history methodology that **Bruen** otherwise mandates.

The problem with this reading is fatal and obvious to any careful reader of the full sentence: the opening clause states that the historical analogies "here and in **Heller**" are "relatively simple to draw." That clause means that the Supreme Court, in its own decisions in **Heller** and **Bruen**, already identified the relevant historical principles that govern Second Amendment analysis. The reference to "other cases" implicating unprecedented societal concerns means cases presenting novel questions that neither **Heller** nor **Bruen** had occasion to address — not a general grant of authority to lower courts to depart from the text-and-history test whenever a court decides that the firearms at issue are dangerous.

The Second Circuit did not quote this opening clause in its analysis. In a 75-page opinion, the panel could not find space to include the words that immediately precede the clause on which its entire analytical edifice rests. The omission is not careless; it is the kind of selective quotation that, in my professional view, crosses the line from aggressive advocacy into something more troubling when it appears in a federal appellate opinion purporting to faithfully apply Supreme Court precedent.

## The Common Use Doctrine

Beyond the methodological defect, the Second Circuit's ruling is irreconcilable with **Heller**'s holding that arms in "common use" for lawful purposes are categorically protected. The AR-15 platform accounts for an estimated 20 to 25 million rifles currently in civilian possession in the United States — a figure that has grown substantially in the years since **Heller** was decided. Standard-capacity magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds are, similarly, the most commonly produced and sold magazine format for the most popular semi-automatic handguns and rifles in the country.

**Heller** expressly contemplated a "common use" test as the baseline protection for firearms under the Second Amendment. Under any honest application of that test, the AR-15 and standard-capacity magazines are plainly protected. The Second Circuit's ruling, to the extent it upholds the Connecticut prohibition without seriously engaging with the common use doctrine, cannot be reconciled with **Heller**'s text.

## Implications

The Second Circuit's ruling in **National Association for Gun Rights v. Connecticut** is not the final word. Several paths forward exist for Second Amendment advocates. First, the plaintiffs may seek rehearing en banc before the full Second Circuit. Second, the ruling may be the subject of a petition for certiorari to the United States Supreme Court, which has been actively accepting Second Amendment cases for review in the post-**Bruen** environment.

The Supreme Court will ultimately be required to address directly whether so-called assault weapons bans survive **Bruen**'s text-and-history methodology. Multiple circuit courts have now issued opinions on this question, with results that reflect the ideological composition of the courts more than the faithful application of **Bruen**'s command. That circuit conflict, combined with the practical importance of the question for tens of millions of law-abiding AR-15 owners, creates a compelling vehicle for Supreme Court review.

The Second Circuit's willingness to cherry-pick the **Bruen** opinion while ignoring its most important limiting principles is precisely the kind of lower-court resistance to Supreme Court authority that the Court has repeatedly signaled it will not tolerate. As the Supreme Court continues to build on **Bruen**'s foundation, rulings like this one will become increasingly difficult to sustain.
