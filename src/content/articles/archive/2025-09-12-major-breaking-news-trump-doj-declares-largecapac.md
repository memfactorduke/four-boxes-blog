## Summary

The United States Department of Justice, acting through United States Attorney for the District of Columbia Janine Pirro, has filed a significant motion in the District of Columbia Court of Appeals in **United States v. Benson**, moving to vacate a criminal conviction for possession of a so-called large-capacity magazine. The DOJ's filing takes the unambiguous position that the District of Columbia's ban on magazines holding more than ten rounds is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment, and that, in the interest of justice, the conviction cannot stand. This development represents one of the most consequential pro-Second Amendment actions taken by the Trump administration's Department of Justice to date.

## Background

The case arises from a prosecution in the District of Columbia. On November 17, 2022, Tyrie Benson was charged by indictment with a series of offenses including carrying a pistol without a license outside his home or place of business, possession of a large-capacity ammunition feeding device, possession of an unregistered firearm, and unlawful possession of ammunition. After a bench trial on stipulated facts before the Honorable Lynn Leibovitz, Benson was convicted on all charges on April 11, 2023.

Benson noted a direct appeal from his convictions. A panel of the District of Columbia Court of Appeals — which is the local appellate court for the District and is not to be confused with the United States Court of Appeals for the DC Circuit — heard argument on December 12, 2024. The case has been under advisement since that hearing.

During the pendency of the appeal, the Biden administration's Department of Justice argued in support of sustaining the conviction, taking the position that DC's prohibition on so-called large-capacity magazines did not violate the Second Amendment. Benson, for his part, argued that the conviction should be reversed precisely because DC's ban on magazines holding more than ten rounds infringes upon the Second Amendment right to keep and bear arms.

## The Trump Administration's About-Face

With the transition of administrations, the Department of Justice under President Donald J. Trump and Attorney General Pam Bondi assessed the constitutionality of DC's magazine ban and reached a conclusion diametrically opposed to that of its predecessor. United States Attorney Pirro, acting on behalf of the United States, filed a motion pursuant to DC Code Section 17-306 requesting that Benson's conviction under DC Code Section 7-2506.01 — the large-capacity magazine prohibition — be vacated.

The motion states in terms that admit of no ambiguity: "It is the United States' position that this law is unconstitutional, and as a result, the United States is not prosecuting violations of this statute and believes that, in the interest of justice, Benson's conviction should be vacated."

The filing goes on to explain the legal reasoning in detail. The Trump DOJ acknowledges that, while the Biden administration initially defended the DC magazine prohibition against constitutional attack, the United States government has now changed its position as to the validity of the statute under the Second Amendment. The motion continues: "Given the United States' view that a complete ban on large-capacity ammunition feeding devices as defined by the DC Code cannot survive constitutional scrutiny, we respectfully move this Court to vacate appellant Benson's conviction under that statute."

The motion further states that vacatur is warranted in the interests of fundamental fairness because Benson "has been found criminally liable based upon a statute that, as written, encroaches upon the interests protected by the Second Amendment." Critically, the filing adds that "because the United States government would not charge a defendant similarly situated to Benson today under this law if arrested today, vacatur of Benson's conviction for possession of a large-capacity ammunition feeding device would ensure fundamental fairness."

## The Constitutional Principle at Stake

It is important to understand what this filing represents in the broader landscape of Second Amendment jurisprudence. The term "large-capacity magazine" is a rhetorical construction of the gun-control movement. What governments call large-capacity magazines are, in operational reality, standard-capacity magazines — the magazines that come standard with the firearms they feed when those firearms are lawfully manufactured and sold in the United States. A magazine holding fifteen, seventeen, or thirty rounds is not some exotic or unusual accessory; it is precisely the type of ammunition-feeding device that accompanies the most commonly owned firearms in America.

Under the constitutional framework established by the Supreme Court in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, **McDonald v. City of Chicago**, and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, the government must justify firearm regulations by demonstrating that those regulations are consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in America at the time of the Second Amendment's ratification. No such historical tradition exists for banning possession of commonly used magazines. The Trump DOJ's recognition of this principle, and its willingness to act on it in an active criminal appeal, is both legally correct and constitutionally significant.

## Procedural Posture and Likely Outcome

Because the DC Court of Appeals has not yet decided Benson's appeal, the government's motion to vacate arrives at an opportune moment. The filing is unopposed by Benson's counsel — as one would expect, given that it seeks precisely the relief Benson has sought throughout this litigation. When both parties to a criminal proceeding jointly request that a conviction be vacated, courts generally oblige. There is every reason to expect that the DC Court of Appeals will grant the motion and vacate Benson's conviction for possessing a so-called large-capacity magazine.

## Implications

This filing by United States Attorney Pirro on behalf of the Trump DOJ carries implications that extend well beyond the individual fate of Tyrie Benson. By publicly and formally declaring that DC's magazine ban is unconstitutional, the federal government has signaled its opposition to an entire category of firearms restriction that dozens of states and localities have enacted in recent years.

States including California, Colorado, Connecticut, Hawaii, Illinois, Maryland, Massachusetts, New Jersey, New York, Oregon, and Washington maintain their own bans on magazines holding more than ten rounds. While a motion filed in the DC Court of Appeals does not directly invalidate those state laws, the DOJ's constitutional reasoning applies with equal force to them. The government's unambiguous statement that a complete ban on what DC defines as large-capacity ammunition feeding devices cannot survive constitutional scrutiny is precisely the analysis that Second Amendment litigants in those states have been advancing in federal court.

The Trump administration has described itself as the most pro-Second Amendment administration in a century, and this filing substantiates that claim. When the executive branch of the United States government formally declares a category of firearms restriction to be unconstitutional, files a court document saying so, and moves to vacate a criminal conviction on that basis, it sends a powerful signal to lower courts across the country that are adjudicating Second Amendment challenges to similar laws.

The Second Amendment community owes it to itself to recognize the significance of this development. The right to keep and bear arms includes the right to bear arms effectively, and firearms are far less effective when arbitrarily limited to ten rounds. The Trump DOJ has done exactly what a constitutionally faithful executive branch should do: it identified an unconstitutional law, changed course from its predecessor's defense of that law, and moved affirmatively in court to vindicate the Second Amendment rights of an American citizen.
