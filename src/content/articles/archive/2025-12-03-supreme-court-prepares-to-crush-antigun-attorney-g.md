## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has heard oral argument in **First Choice Women's Resource Centers v. Platkin**, a case involving the New Jersey Attorney General's use of an investigative subpoena to compel a pro-life crisis pregnancy center to disclose the names, addresses, and contact information of all its anonymous donors. While the case does not involve firearms directly, every gun rights organization, every Second Amendment advocacy group, and every firearms manufacturer in America has an existential stake in its outcome. The core legal question — whether a federal court may enjoin a state attorney general's politically motivated subpoena before that subpoena is enforced — will determine whether rogue state attorneys general can continue to use the investigative machinery of state government to harass and financially destroy organizations whose values they oppose. Based on the tenor of oral argument, I predict that the Supreme Court will rule in favor of First Choice and impose meaningful limitations on this abusive practice.

## Background

**First Choice Women's Resource Centers** is a pro-life, Christian organization operating in the state of New Jersey. It functions as what is commonly called a crisis pregnancy center — providing counseling, support, and resources to women facing unplanned pregnancies, with a mission oriented toward encouraging birth rather than abortion. At the time the New Jersey Attorney General initiated his investigation, there had been no complaint from any person alleging that First Choice had engaged in any wrongful, deceptive, or unlawful conduct. Not a single individual had accused the organization of deceiving donors or clients, violating any law, or engaging in any conduct that would ordinarily bring an organization within the investigative purview of a state attorney general.

Despite the complete absence of any complaint, New Jersey Attorney General Matthew Platkin issued an extraordinarily broad subpoena demanding that First Choice produce, among other things, the names, telephone numbers, mailing addresses, and other identifying information of all of its anonymous donors. Attorney General Platkin's stated justification was that his office wished to investigate whether any of those donors had been "deceived" into donating — that is, whether they might have donated to a pro-life organization without fully understanding its pro-life mission.

This justification was pretextual on its face. A donor who voluntarily gives money to an organization named "First Choice Women's Resource Centers" that describes itself as a pro-life pregnancy support center has not been deceived about the nature of the organization. The investigation was not a genuine consumer protection inquiry; it was a transparent attempt by a politically hostile state official to expose the identities of donors to a politically disfavored organization, thereby chilling those donors' First Amendment rights of association and potentially subjecting them to harassment.

## The Legal Framework: The Younger Abstention Doctrine and Federal Court Jurisdiction

The procedural posture of **First Choice** presents a fascinating and important jurisdictional question. When First Choice received the state attorney general's subpoena, it did not wait to have the subpoena enforced against it in state court proceedings. Instead, it immediately filed suit in federal court, seeking an injunction preventing the attorney general from enforcing the subpoena on the grounds that doing so would violate First Choice's First Amendment rights of free speech and free association.

The legal obstacle First Choice encountered was the doctrine of **Younger v. Harris** (1971), which generally prohibits federal courts from enjoining ongoing state court proceedings out of comity and federalism concerns — a principle sometimes called "Our Federalism." The New Jersey courts argued, and the Third Circuit agreed, that the federal court lacked jurisdiction to entertain First Choice's challenge because doing so would improperly interfere with the state's enforcement of its investigative authority.

The Supreme Court granted certiorari to address whether this analysis was correct — that is, whether a federal court may exercise jurisdiction to enjoin a state attorney general's subpoena that violates federal constitutional rights before state court enforcement proceedings have commenced.

## The First Amendment Stakes

The First Amendment right of association — the right to gather with and support individuals who share one's political, religious, or ideological commitments — is among the most fundamental in the constitutional canon. In **NAACP v. Alabama** (1958), the Supreme Court held that compelled disclosure of the NAACP's membership rolls violated the First Amendment because exposure of those names would subject members to harassment and retaliation, thereby chilling the exercise of the associational right. That principle has been reaffirmed and extended in numerous subsequent decisions.

The Supreme Court's more recent decision in **Americans for Prosperity Foundation v. Bonta** (2021) struck down California's mandatory donor disclosure regime for charities, holding that forced disclosure of donor identities substantially burdens the right of association and cannot be justified without a sufficiently important governmental interest and a substantial nexus between the disclosure requirement and that interest.

**First Choice** is the next chapter in this line of cases. The question is whether a state attorney general can circumvent the protections established in **Americans for Prosperity** by issuing targeted investigative subpoenas to specific disfavored organizations rather than implementing a generally applicable disclosure requirement. If the answer is yes — if targeted, politically motivated subpoenas are beyond the reach of federal constitutional review until state enforcement proceedings are fully concluded — then the practical protection afforded by **Americans for Prosperity** is largely illusory. Any state attorney general who disagrees with an organization's political activities can simply subpoena its donor rolls, impose enormous legal costs and compliance burdens, and force the organization to litigate through the state court system before any federal constitutional protection kicks in.

## Why Every Gun Rights Organization Has Skin in This Game

I want to be direct about why **First Choice** matters to the Second Amendment community, because it may not be immediately obvious. Consider the following hypothetical: A state attorney general who is hostile to the Second Amendment — and there are many such attorneys general, from New York to California to New Jersey itself — decides to launch an "investigation" into a gun rights organization operating in his state. Perhaps the organization advocates for constitutional carry or opposes the state's assault weapons ban. The attorney general issues a subpoena demanding the names, addresses, and contact information of all the organization's donors and members, claiming he wants to investigate whether those donors understood they were supporting an organization that opposes gun control.

The mechanism is identical to what happened to First Choice. The legal principle is the same. If the Supreme Court rules that federal courts lack jurisdiction to enjoin such subpoenas before state enforcement proceedings commence, then the gun rights organization must either comply — handing over its membership rolls to a hostile government official — or litigate through the state court system, potentially for years, while incurring crippling legal expenses. Either outcome achieves the attorney general's objective of chilling the organization's activities and exposing its supporters to potential harassment.

This is not a theoretical concern. Anti-gun attorneys general have demonstrated a consistent willingness to use the investigative and enforcement powers of their offices to target the firearms industry and Second Amendment advocacy organizations. The legal tools developed in cases like **First Choice** are the same tools that will be deployed against gun rights groups.

## Reading the Oral Argument Tea Leaves

Based on the tenor of the oral argument and the questions posed by the Justices, I believe the Court will rule in favor of First Choice. The Justices appeared deeply skeptical of the proposition that a federal court must stand aside while a politically motivated state subpoena runs its course through state proceedings, particularly when the subpoena is targeted specifically at constitutionally protected associational activities. Multiple Justices pressed the New Jersey Attorney General's representatives on the chilling effect of forcing organizations to litigate through state enforcement proceedings before obtaining federal constitutional review.

My prediction is that the Court will hold either that the **Younger** abstention doctrine does not apply in circumstances where the state proceeding was initiated in bad faith or for the purpose of chilling constitutionally protected activities, or that the doctrine of **Americans for Prosperity** provides a sufficiently established constitutional right that federal courts may protect through pre-enforcement relief.

## Implications

A ruling for First Choice would have immediate and practical benefits for the gun rights community. It would establish or reinforce the principle that organizations whose First Amendment associational rights are threatened by politically motivated state investigations can seek immediate federal court protection rather than being forced to navigate hostile state courts before federal relief becomes available. This procedural protection is not merely technical — it is the difference between an organization's ability to function during litigation and financial collapse under the weight of prolonged legal combat.

The broader lesson of **First Choice** is that the First and Second Amendments are inextricably linked. An attorney general who can destroy a pro-life organization through politically motivated investigations can destroy a pro-gun organization by the same methods. The constitutional principles protecting political association and anonymous donor privacy are the same whether the organization in question supports the right to life or the right to keep and bear arms. Second Amendment advocates should be as invested in the outcome of **First Choice** as they are in the most direct Second Amendment cases before the Court.
