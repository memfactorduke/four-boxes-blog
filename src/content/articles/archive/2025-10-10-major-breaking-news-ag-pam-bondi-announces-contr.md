## Summary

The United States Department of Justice, through Solicitor General John Sauer, has filed a brief in the United States Supreme Court opposing the petition for certiorari in **McCoy v. Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives**. **McCoy** involves the same question at issue in **Ree v. ATF** — whether the federal prohibition on federally licensed firearms dealers selling handguns to individuals between the ages of 18 and 20 violates the Second Amendment — but arises from the Fourth Circuit, which ruled against Second Amendment rights. The DOJ argues that **McCoy** is not a proper vehicle for resolving this question because the case may become moot before decision and because superior vehicles are already before or approaching the Court. This filing has generated criticism from some in the Second Amendment community, but I believe it reflects sound and strategically defensible reasoning.

## Background

### The Legal Question

Federal law prohibits federally licensed firearms dealers from selling handguns to persons under the age of 21. Persons between the ages of 18 and 20 may legally possess handguns but may not purchase them from licensed dealers. The constitutional question is whether this restriction on a class of adults who are fully capable of voting, serving in the military, entering into contracts, and exercising all other legal rights is consistent with the Second Amendment's guarantee of the right of "the people" to keep and bear arms.

The Fifth Circuit addressed this question in **Ree v. ATF** and concluded that the prohibition is unconstitutional as applied to adults between 18 and 20. The Trump DOJ agreed with this conclusion and, as I have reported previously, worked cooperatively with the plaintiffs in **Ree** to secure the broadest possible injunctive relief.

### The Fourth Circuit's Decision in McCoy

The Fourth Circuit Court of Appeals, covering Virginia, Maryland, West Virginia, North Carolina, and South Carolina, reached the opposite conclusion, upholding the under-21 handgun purchase restriction. This is unsurprising given the Fourth Circuit's well-documented hostility to Second Amendment claims. The court that includes Senior Judge Harvey Wilkinson — who has written that an AR-15 is comparable to a machine gun in its destructive capacity — has been among the most aggressively anti-Second Amendment circuits in the federal appellate system.

The **McCoy** petitioners sought Supreme Court review, asking the Court to grant certiorari, hear the case, and resolve the circuit split created by the conflict between the Fifth Circuit's pro-Second Amendment ruling in **Ree** and the Fourth Circuit's contrary decision in **McCoy**.

### The Mootness Problem

The DOJ's brief raises a threshold jurisdictional concern: mootness. The petitioners in **McCoy** were between 18 and 20 years old when the case was filed. The Supreme Court's review process — from certiorari grant through briefing, argument, and decision — typically takes more than a year. By the time the Court could hear and decide **McCoy**, the petitioners will have turned 21 and the challenged prohibition will no longer apply to them. A case in which no live controversy remains is moot, and the Supreme Court cannot issue advisory opinions in moot cases.

This is a genuine and serious procedural problem that makes **McCoy** a risky vehicle regardless of the merits. A certiorari grant in a case that becomes moot before decision would result in the Court dismissing the case as moot without reaching the constitutional question — wasting a precious Supreme Court opportunity and potentially unsettling the Fifth Circuit's favorable ruling in **Ree**.

### Superior Vehicles Pending Before the Court

The DOJ's brief also identifies several other cases that raise the same or related questions in superior procedural postures. **Wolford v. Lopez** has already been granted certiorari and involves related Second Amendment methodology questions that will guide the resolution of age-restriction issues. **United States v. Hammani** — the marijuana-user disarmament case — is also pending and will provide Second Amendment analytical guidance.

Most significantly, the brief identifies two other petitions — **West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF** and **NRA Inc. v. Glass** — that specifically present the Second Amendment rights of 18-to-20-year-olds but do not suffer from the mootness defects afflicting **McCoy**. The DOJ's brief explicitly tells the Court that these pending petitions present the question in a better procedural posture and that the Court should hold the **McCoy** petition pending resolution of those cases.

## The DOJ's Affirmative Position on the Merits

A critical point that critics of this filing have largely ignored: the DOJ's brief does not argue that the under-21 handgun purchase restriction is constitutional. It does not argue that 18-to-20-year-olds lack Second Amendment rights. To the contrary, the DOJ's position — as reflected in its actions in **Ree v. ATF** — is that law-abiding adults between 18 and 20 are entitled to Second Amendment protection. The DOJ agreed with the Fifth Circuit's ruling in **Ree**, cooperated with the plaintiff organizations to secure broad injunctive relief, and did not appeal the constitutional ruling.

The brief's opposition to certiorari in **McCoy** is a procedural judgment, not a substantive retreat. The DOJ is not saying "don't resolve this question." It is saying "don't resolve this question in this case, at this time, when better vehicles are available and when this case may not even be justiciable."

## Why the Critics Are Mistaken

Some commentators have read the DOJ's brief as evidence that the Trump administration opposes Second Amendment rights for young adults. This reading is incorrect and is not supported by the actual text of the filing or the administration's overall litigation record.

The question the Second Amendment community should ask is not "is the DOJ opposing cert in this case?" but rather "is the DOJ committed to winning this constitutional question for law-abiding young adults?" The answer to the latter question is yes, as demonstrated by the administration's conduct in **Ree v. ATF**. The DOJ is managing the pace and vehicle selection for Supreme Court litigation with care and strategic discipline. That is exactly what a sophisticated litigation team committed to winning should be doing.

## Implications

The DOJ's brief in **McCoy v. ATF** illustrates the complex strategic calculus involved in litigating constitutional questions at the Supreme Court. Unlike private litigants who may have limited control over which cases reach the Court, the Solicitor General's office can influence docket development by identifying better vehicles, flagging procedural obstacles, and guiding the Court toward cases that are more likely to produce favorable, durable precedent.

For Second Amendment advocates, the practical consequence of this filing is that the constitutional question of whether adults between 18 and 20 years old have the right to purchase handguns from licensed dealers will not be finally resolved in **McCoy**. But it will be resolved — in a better case, with better facts, with a petitioner who has not reached the age of 21 before the decision is issued. The Solicitor General's strategic patience here is an asset, not a liability.
