## Summary

In a unanimous 9-0 decision authored by Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson, the Supreme Court held in *Urias v. Bondi* that federal district courts may not conduct de novo review of asylum and refugee status determinations made by administrative law judges within the Department of Homeland Security; rather, district courts are limited to assessing whether the administrative record contains substantial evidence supporting the agency's factual findings. In my analysis, the decision restores a foundational principle of administrative law — that executive branch adjudicative findings on factual questions are entitled to judicial deference when supported by substantial evidence — and forecloses a procedural avenue that had allowed immigration adjudications to be relitigated from scratch in forum-selected federal district courts. The unanimity of the decision reflects the doctrinal soundness of its administrative law underpinnings, transcending the political valence that immigration disputes typically carry.

## Background

Under federal immigration law, asylum seekers whose claims are denied by Department of Homeland Security administrative law judges may appeal within the agency to the Board of Immigration Appeals. If the BIA affirms the denial, the applicant has exhausted the administrative process. Historically, courts of appeals — not district courts — exercised judicial review of final agency orders in immigration cases, applying a deferential substantial evidence standard to the agency's factual determinations.

A circuit split developed, however, regarding whether federal district courts could exercise parallel jurisdiction over asylum claims through collateral statutory avenues, and if so, what standard of review applied. Several circuits, including the Tenth and Second, had permitted what amounted to de novo review of the refugee status determination — effectively authorizing district courts to take a fresh evidentiary look at whether an applicant satisfied the statutory definition of a refugee, independent of the administrative law judge's findings. *Urias v. Bondi* presented the Court with the opportunity to resolve that split and establish a uniform standard.

## The Refugee Status Determination Framework

The statutory definition of "refugee" under the Immigration and Nationality Act has a precise legal meaning that excludes purely economic migration. An applicant must demonstrate a well-founded fear of persecution on account of race, religion, nationality, membership in a particular social group, or political opinion. The persecution feared must be carried out by the government of the country of origin or by entities the government is unable or unwilling to control. General conditions of poverty, violence, or underdevelopment, absent a nexus to one of the enumerated grounds, do not satisfy the statutory standard.

Administrative law judges within DHS are tasked with making individualized factual determinations on these questions after adversarial hearings at which the applicant may present evidence and testimony. The ALJ's decision rests on credibility assessments, documentary evidence, and country condition information — the precise categories of evidence that administrative adjudicators are institutionally positioned to evaluate. The question *Urias* resolved is what role federal courts play after the agency has made those determinations.

## The Court's Holding

Justice Jackson's opinion holds that federal district courts reviewing DHS asylum determinations are bound by the substantial evidence standard: they may set aside an agency finding only if the record as a whole would compel a reasonable factfinder to reach a contrary conclusion. This is a deferential standard that places the burden on the party challenging the agency's determination to establish that the record cannot support it. Under this framework, every reasonable inference is drawn in favor of the agency's finding, and the reviewing court does not conduct an independent weighing of the evidence.

The opinion forecloses the practice of de novo review in this context — the practice under which district courts in certain circuits had treated themselves as empowered to make fresh determinations on the merits of asylum claims as though no administrative adjudication had occurred. As I have analyzed, that practice was difficult to reconcile with both the Administrative Procedure Act's framework for judicial review of agency action and the structural principle that executive branch agencies, not Article III courts, exercise primary fact-finding authority in administrative adjudications.

## The Separation of Powers Dimension

The doctrinal significance of *Urias* extends beyond the specific immigration context. In my view, the decision is best understood as an application of foundational separation of powers principles to the immigration adjudicatory system. Administrative law judges within DHS are officers of the executive branch; their factual determinations are products of the executive branch's adjudicatory capacity within a congressionally established regulatory framework. When Article III courts conduct de novo review of those determinations, they effectively substitute judicial fact-finding for executive fact-finding in a domain that Congress has assigned to the executive branch.

The substantial evidence standard preserves the judiciary's essential function — ensuring that agency determinations are supported by evidence and not arbitrary — while respecting the executive branch's role as the primary adjudicator of factual questions within its delegated statutory authority. This is the balance that the APA was designed to strike, and it is the balance that the *Urias* Court unanimously reaffirms.

The practical dimension is equally significant. De novo review in forum-selected district courts had created both structural and docket-management problems: applicants could seek favorable jurisdictions through strategic filing choices, federal district court dockets absorbed immigration cases that had already been fully adjudicated administratively, and the possibility of a more favorable independent review created incentives for protracted collateral litigation. The substantial evidence standard eliminates these incentives by ensuring that the administrative adjudication is the primary proceeding, not a preliminary hurdle to a subsequent federal trial.

## The Significance of Unanimity

In my analysis, the unanimity of the *Urias* decision warrants separate attention. The Court's nine justices span a wide range of jurisprudential approaches to administrative law, statutory interpretation, and immigration policy. That all nine joined Justice Jackson's opinion signals that the legal question — the proper standard of review for administrative asylum determinations — was resolved on administrative law grounds of general applicability, rather than on the basis of views about immigration policy per se.

Administrative law scholars have long argued that the standard of review for agency factual determinations should not vary based on subject matter — that the substantial evidence standard is equally applicable to agency findings on immigration as to agency findings on labor relations, environmental regulation, or securities law. *Urias* confirms this position. The doctrinal consistency the decision enforces should, over time, produce greater predictability in immigration adjudications and reduce the extent to which forum selection drives litigation strategy.

## Implications

The *Urias* decision will substantially affect the procedural landscape of immigration litigation. Applicants who have exhausted the DHS administrative process and seek federal judicial review will no longer be able to obtain a fresh evidentiary hearing before a potentially more sympathetic district court. Review will instead be confined to the administrative record, with deference accorded to the agency's factual findings. This change in the procedural terrain will likely reduce the volume of immigration litigation that reaches federal district courts, concentrate judicial attention on questions of law rather than fact, and restore the administrative system's finality on questions for which Congress has assigned adjudicative responsibility to the executive branch.

For administrative law practitioners and scholars, the decision reinforces the principle that judicial deference to agency factual findings is not a feature of particular subject areas — it is a constitutional and statutory norm that reflects the institutional allocation of fact-finding authority between the executive and judicial branches. *Urias v. Bondi*, whatever its practical effects on immigration enforcement, stands as a significant reaffirmation of that structural principle.
