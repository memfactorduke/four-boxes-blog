## Summary

Litigation concerning President Donald J. Trump's authority to remove Lisa Cook from her position as a Governor of the Federal Reserve System has placed before the federal courts — and potentially before the United States Supreme Court — a constitutional question of profound national importance: whether the Federal Reserve System's current structure, insulating its governors from at-will presidential removal, is consistent with the separation of powers as established in Article II of the United States Constitution. I have long maintained, and continue to maintain, that serious constitutional questions surround the Federal Reserve's independence from executive oversight. The fight over Ms. Cook's removal has now brought those questions to a head in ways that could have transformative consequences not only for monetary policy but for the entire constitutional framework governing independent agencies — a framework with direct implications for the Second Amendment community's ongoing battles over the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives and other administrative bodies that regulate firearms.

## Background

The Federal Reserve System, established by Congress through the Federal Reserve Act of 1913, is the central bank of the United States. It exercises authority over the Nation's monetary policy, including setting the federal funds rate, overseeing member banks, and regulating the money supply. The Federal Reserve System is governed by a Board of Governors whose members are nominated by the President and confirmed by the Senate but who, under existing statute, may only be removed by the President "for cause" — meaning they serve terms that are insulated from at-will presidential removal.

Lisa Cook was nominated to the Federal Reserve Board of Governors by President Joseph Biden and confirmed by the United States Senate, with Vice President Kamala Harris casting the tie-breaking vote in her role as President of the Senate. President Trump, upon taking office, sought to remove Ms. Cook from her position. Ms. Cook challenged the removal in federal court, arguing that the Federal Reserve Act's for-cause removal protection insulates her from at-will presidential dismissal and that her removal was therefore unlawful.

The dispute raises foundational questions about the constitutional permissibility of statutory schemes that purport to limit the President's removal authority over officers of the United States.

## The Constitutional Framework

The United States Constitution, in Article II, vests the executive power in the President. It further charges the President with the duty to "take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed." The Supreme Court recognized in **Myers v. United States**, 272 U.S. 52 (1926), that the President possesses inherent authority to remove executive officers — a power necessary to ensure that the President can effectively exercise the executive authority that the Constitution vests exclusively in the office.

**Humphrey's Executor v. United States**, 295 U.S. 602 (1935), created what appeared at the time to be an exception to the Myers principle, holding that Congress may insulate officers of certain "independent" agencies from at-will presidential removal if those agencies exercise "quasi-legislative" or "quasi-judicial" functions rather than purely executive functions.

The Supreme Court has, in recent years, substantially narrowed **Humphrey's Executor**. In **Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau**, 591 U.S. 197 (2020), the Court held that a single-director independent agency — the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau — was structured unconstitutionally because for-cause removal protection for a single director who wields substantial executive power was irreconcilable with Article II's vesting of executive power in the President alone. The Court in **Seila Law** was careful to note that it was limiting **Humphrey's Executor** to its facts: multi-member commissions exercising quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial functions.

**Collins v. Yellen**, 594 U.S. 220 (2021), extended **Seila Law**'s reasoning to the Federal Housing Finance Agency. The trajectory of the Court's separation of powers jurisprudence is unmistakable: the constitutional permissibility of for-cause removal protection depends on the specific structure and function of the agency involved, and the trend is toward greater presidential control over the executive branch.

## The Federal Reserve's Distinctive Position

The Federal Reserve presents a more complex case than the CFPB or the FHFA. It is a multi-member body — the structure that **Humphrey's Executor** addressed — and it exercises functions that blend elements of executive action (monetary policy implementation), quasi-legislative rulemaking, and quasi-judicial adjudication. Its constitutional defenders argue that it falls comfortably within the **Humphrey's Executor** exception.

The constitutional critics of the Federal Reserve's independent structure — a tradition that stretches back to debates among the Founding Fathers over the appropriate role of a federal bank, through Andrew Jackson's veto of the Second Bank of the United States, to the debates surrounding the Federal Reserve Act itself — contend that the Federal Reserve's power over monetary policy is a quintessentially executive function. Control of the Nation's monetary policy — the setting of interest rates, the regulation of the money supply, the oversight of the banking system — is not quasi-legislative or quasi-judicial in any meaningful sense. It is the exercise of discretionary governmental power with immediate, concrete effects on the daily economic lives of every American.

## The Second Amendment Connection

I have consistently emphasized to the Second Amendment community that constitutional law operates as an interconnected web. When the Supreme Court issues a ruling expanding or contracting the scope of one constitutional principle, the effects ripple throughout the constitutional order. The separation of powers litigation over the Federal Reserve's structure is directly relevant to Second Amendment advocates for a simple reason: the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives — the agency primarily responsible for implementing federal firearms regulations — is itself an executive agency whose leadership serves at the pleasure of the President.

A strong and constitutionally sound executive removal power means that the President can hold ATF leadership accountable for unlawful or unconstitutional regulatory overreach. A weakened removal power, or a constitutional norm under which independent agencies may insulate themselves from presidential oversight, creates conditions under which bureaucratic actors may continue to regulate firearms in ways that are contrary to both the law and the Second Amendment without effective accountability to the elected executive.

## Implications

The litigation over Lisa Cook's removal will likely require the federal courts to confront directly whether the Federal Reserve's for-cause removal protection remains constitutional in light of **Seila Law** and **Collins**. If the Supreme Court takes up this question — and the national importance of the Federal Reserve and the directness of the conflict with recent separation of powers precedent make Supreme Court review probable — the outcome could reshape the constitutional landscape governing independent agencies more broadly.

The stakes extend well beyond monetary policy. A ruling that the Federal Reserve's for-cause removal protection is unconstitutional under Article II's vesting clause would call into question the constitutional foundation of numerous other agencies that have claimed similar insulation from presidential control. The constitutional web that connects the separation of powers to the Second Amendment is real and consequential. What happens to the Federal Reserve's constitutional structure will, in ways both direct and indirect, affect the future of Second Amendment rights in America.
