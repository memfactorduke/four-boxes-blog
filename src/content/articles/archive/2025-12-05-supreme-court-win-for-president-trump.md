## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has intervened to stay and reverse a three-judge district court panel decision that had invalidated Texas's mid-decade congressional redistricting plan — a plan designed to add as many as five additional Republican seats to the House of Representatives ahead of the 2026 midterm elections. The Court's action, which vindicates the dissenting position taken by Fifth Circuit Judge Jerry Smith, reaffirms the fundamental distinction between impermissible racial gerrymandering and constitutionally permissible partisan gerrymandering. The ruling carries significant implications not only for the control of the House of Representatives but also for the long-term protection of Second Amendment rights, which depends in substantial part on maintaining Republican legislative majorities capable of blocking or repealing anti-gun legislation.

## Background

The context for this litigation begins with a well-documented asymmetry in congressional representation. For decades, Democratic-controlled state legislatures in the Northeast and elsewhere have drawn congressional maps with the deliberate purpose of maximizing Democratic representation — a practice that has resulted in situations where states with roughly even splits between Republican and Democratic voters send exclusively Democratic delegations to Washington. The entire Northeast corridor — Connecticut, Massachusetts, New Hampshire, Vermont, Maine, Rhode Island — has effectively returned zero Republican House members despite Republican candidates routinely receiving approximately forty percent of the popular vote in those states.

Against this backdrop of systematic Democratic gerrymandering, President Trump urged Republican-controlled states to exercise their own constitutional authority to draw favorable congressional maps. In response to this call, Texas Governor Greg Abbott and the Texas state legislature undertook a mid-decade redistricting exercise designed to add five Republican House seats ahead of the 2026 elections.

The League of United Latin American Citizens (LULAC) promptly filed suit against Governor Abbott in federal court, arguing that Texas's redistricting was not a straightforward exercise of permissible partisan politics but was instead unlawfully motivated by racial discrimination, in violation of the Voting Rights Act, the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause, and the Fifteenth Amendment's prohibition on racial discrimination in voting.

## The Three-Judge Panel's Error

A three-judge district court panel, by a two-to-one vote, sided with the plaintiffs. The majority concluded that Texas's redistricting was motivated not by pure partisan politics — which the Supreme Court has clearly held is permissible — but by race. On the basis of this finding, the panel ordered that Texas's congressional maps revert to the pre-redistricting lines that had existed prior to the summer of 2025, which would have had the effect of preserving five Democratic House seats that would otherwise likely have been eliminated.

The critical legal error committed by the panel majority was conflating racial and partisan motivation. As the Supreme Court made clear in its 2019 decision in **Rucho v. Common Cause**, federal courts have no jurisdiction to adjudicate claims of partisan gerrymandering. Partisan political advantage — Republicans drawing maps to elect more Republicans, Democrats drawing maps to elect more Democrats — is constitutionally permissible and presents a political question beyond the competence of federal courts to resolve. The Voting Rights Act and the constitutional amendments that protect against racial discrimination do not forbid parties from preferring their own members over their opponents; they forbid discrimination on the basis of immutable racial characteristics.

The distinction is fundamental but can be manipulated. Because race and partisanship are often correlated — Black voters, for example, vote overwhelmingly Democratic — a party drawing maps to maximize partisan advantage will inevitably draw maps that also affect the racial composition of districts. Anti-gun litigants and their allies in the Democratic Party have long sought to exploit this correlation by characterizing legitimate partisan redistricting as racial discrimination, thereby bringing it within the reach of the Voting Rights Act and the Equal Protection Clause.

## Judge Jerry Smith's Vindication

The lone dissenter on the three-judge panel, Fifth Circuit Judge Jerry Smith, had articulated the legal error committed by the majority with precision: Texas's redistricting was a straightforward exercise of partisan politics — constitutionally unreviewable under **Rucho** — and the majority's characterization of it as racial discrimination was not supported by the record. The Supreme Court's intervention amounts to a direct vindication of Judge Smith's position, an outcome that I had anticipated and predicted in prior commentary.

## The Supreme Court's Action

The Supreme Court stepped in to reverse the three-judge panel, holding that the lower court had misapplied the governing legal standards. The Court's ruling clears the path for Texas's redistricting to take effect, allowing the state to proceed with the congressional maps that should yield five additional Republican House seats after the 2026 elections.

This action reflects the Supreme Court's continued commitment to the principle established in **Rucho** that partisan gerrymandering is beyond the reach of federal courts. Courts have no authority to second-guess a state legislature's decision to draw maps that favor one party over another, and the attempt to recharacterize partisan motive as racial motive — without adequate factual support — cannot be permitted to circumvent this clear rule.

## Implications for Second Amendment Rights

The connection between congressional redistricting and Second Amendment rights is direct and consequential. The House of Representatives has been one of the primary legislative battlegrounds over gun rights for decades. During periods when Democrats have controlled the House, anti-gun legislation has advanced — the 1994 Assault Weapons Ban passed the House with a Democratic majority, and the Bipartisan Safer Communities Act of 2022 drew support from House Democrats in disproportionate numbers.

The maintenance of a Republican House majority serves as a structural check on the most aggressive anti-gun legislative proposals. Conversely, a Democratic House majority creates the institutional conditions in which comprehensive gun control legislation — magazine bans, so-called assault weapons bans, universal background check requirements, red flag law mandates — can be passed and sent to a Senate that may be more susceptible to pressure than its usual composition would suggest.

Adding five Republican House seats through constitutionally permissible redistricting does not merely represent a partisan political advantage. It represents a meaningful strengthening of the structural protection for Second Amendment rights in the legislature. The same voters who returned Donald Trump to the presidency with a mandate to protect the right to keep and bear arms are the voters whose congressional representation is restored to its proper proportionality by the Texas redistricting.

## The Broader Pattern

This case fits within a broader pattern of legal warfare against Republican electoral success that the Second Amendment community must understand and engage with. Anti-gun and anti-Republican litigants have demonstrated a consistent willingness to bring constitutional and statutory claims that are, at their core, attempts to prevent the democratic process from producing outcomes they find undesirable. The redistricting litigation against Texas is a paradigmatic example: unable to prevail at the ballot box in Texas, opponents of Republican governance turned to federal courts in an attempt to preserve Democratic representation that the voters of Texas had not chosen to provide.

The Supreme Court's intervention to correct the three-judge panel's error is therefore not merely a victory for partisan politics. It is a victory for the principle that elections have consequences — that when the people of Texas, acting through their elected legislature and governor, choose to draw congressional lines in a particular way, federal courts should be slow to override that choice absent clear and convincing evidence of racial discrimination.

Second Amendment advocates must remain vigilant across all fronts of this fight — in the courts, in the state legislatures, in Congress, and in the executive branch. The protection of the right to keep and bear arms is ultimately a political project as much as a legal one, and victories like this one strengthen the political foundation on which durable legal protection must rest.
