## Summary

On August 8, 2025, a three-judge panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit issued a unanimous decision in **United States v. Bridges**, holding that machine guns — fully automatic firearms capable of firing continuously so long as the trigger is depressed and ammunition remains in the magazine — are not protected arms under the Second Amendment and that the federal ban on possession of machine guns manufactured after May 19, 1986, is constitutionally valid. The panel's fifty-page opinion analyzed the question under both the *Heller* "dangerous and unusual weapons" framework and the *Bruen* text, history, and tradition standard, concluding that machine guns fall outside the scope of Second Amendment protection under either analytical approach. While this outcome will strike some in the Second Amendment community as a defeat, the decision is, as a matter of current constitutional law and long-term strategic positioning, precisely the result that Second Amendment advocates should want from lower federal courts at this stage of the litigation landscape.

## Background

The federal ban on machine guns manufactured after May 19, 1986 — commonly known as the Hughes Amendment to the Firearm Owners Protection Act of 1986 — prohibits civilians from acquiring or possessing any machine gun manufactured after that date. Machine guns manufactured before the cutoff date may be transferred to civilians but must be registered in the National Firearms Act registry and may be transferred only through a licensed dealer following payment of a $200 tax and completion of ATF Form 4. Because no new machine guns have entered the civilian registry since 1986, the supply of transferable machine guns is fixed, and the cost of registered pre-1986 machine guns has risen dramatically — often exceeding ten thousand dollars for a common submachine gun.

In **United States v. Bridges**, the defendant was charged with illegal possession of a machine gun under 26 U.S.C. § 5861(d) and/or 18 U.S.C. § 922(o). The defense challenged the constitutionality of the federal machine gun ban, arguing that machine guns are protected arms under the Second Amendment as interpreted in *Heller* (2008), *Bruen* (2022), and *Rahimi* (2024).

## The Sixth Circuit's Analysis

The three-judge panel unanimously rejected the constitutional challenge on two independent grounds.

First, the panel held that it was bound by prior Sixth Circuit precedent that had already held, before *Bruen*, that the federal machine gun ban was constitutional. Circuit precedents bind subsequent panels of the same circuit absent intervention by the full court sitting en banc or a contrary Supreme Court decision that effectively undermines the prior holding. The panel concluded that neither *Bruen* nor *Rahimi* had so thoroughly altered the analytical framework that the prior Sixth Circuit machine gun precedent could be disregarded.

Second, and independently, the panel analyzed the question under both *Heller* and *Bruen* and concluded that machine guns are not constitutionally protected in any event. Under *Heller*, the Supreme Court noted that the Second Amendment does not protect weapons that are "dangerous and unusual" — weapons that are not in common use by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes. Machine guns presently in lawful civilian possession number fewer than two hundred thousand — an extraordinarily small number relative to the total population of firearms in the United States, which numbers in the hundreds of millions. By any reasonable measure of what constitutes "common use," machine guns do not qualify. They are, relatively speaking, unusual instruments that are dangerous in ways that extend beyond the ordinary self-defense and sporting applications that the Second Amendment primarily protects.

Under the *Bruen* historical tradition framework, the panel found that no well-established historical tradition exists that would support extending Second Amendment protection to fully automatic weapons. Machine guns, as a category of technology, did not exist in their modern form at the founding or during the Reconstruction era. The analysis of historical analogues is therefore necessarily attenuated.

## Why This Ruling Is the Right Result for Second Amendment Advocates

I want to be direct about a point that may seem counterintuitive to some in the Second Amendment community: the Sixth Circuit's decision in **United States v. Bridges** is the correct outcome for our cause given the present state of the law and the composition of the Supreme Court.

The strategic landscape of Second Amendment litigation requires careful management of the questions that reach the Supreme Court. We cannot currently secure five votes on the Supreme Court for the proposition that machine guns — fully automatic weapons — are constitutionally protected under the Second Amendment. This is the reality of the current Court, whatever one may believe is the philosophically or legally correct answer in the abstract. A Supreme Court ruling that machine guns are unprotected arms is constitutionally manageable; a Supreme Court ruling that would use the machine gun question as a vehicle for narrowing *Heller* or *Bruen* more broadly would be catastrophic for Second Amendment rights across the board.

The paramount objective of Second Amendment litigation at this moment is to secure a Supreme Court ruling confirming that semi-automatic rifles — AR-15s, AK-pattern rifles, and similar firearms — are fully protected arms under the Second Amendment. Those are the weapons that several states have banned or are attempting to ban. Those are the weapons that are in common use by tens of millions of Americans for lawful self-defense, sporting, and other lawful purposes. Those are the weapons whose constitutional protection must be vindicated before we can declare Second Amendment law secure.

Preserving the machine gun question for a more favorable future composition of the Court — while obtaining favorable rulings now on the questions that matter most immediately — is the correct strategic posture. Every lower court ruling that machine guns are unprotected under existing doctrine is a ruling that reduces the probability that the Supreme Court will take a machine gun case, which reduces the probability that a suboptimal ruling on machine guns will be issued, which preserves our maneuvering room on the issues we must win.

## The Positive Legal Content of the Decision

Beyond its strategic value, the Sixth Circuit's opinion contains substantial positive legal content for the Second Amendment community.

The panel's analysis under *Bruen* is rigorous and its acknowledgment of the "dangerous and unusual" limitation is precise. By explicitly confining the machine gun ruling to weapons that are not in common use and that do not serve the core self-defense function at the heart of *Heller*, the panel implicitly confirms that weapons in common use for lawful purposes — the semi-automatic rifles at issue in the major pending Second Amendment cases — receive robust constitutional protection. Every case that carefully defines the machine gun exception is a case that simultaneously defines the protected core to which that exception does not apply.

## Implications

The Sixth Circuit's ruling in **United States v. Bridges** has limited immediate practical effect — the federal machine gun ban remains in force, as it has been continuously since 1986. The decision's significance lies primarily in its contribution to the developing body of Second Amendment appellate precedent and in the strategic implications discussed above.

Going forward, the Second Amendment litigation community should continue to support lower court rulings that hold machine guns unprotected while focusing litigation resources on the cases most likely to produce Supreme Court rulings protecting semi-automatic rifles and other commonly owned firearms. The machine gun question will be revisited in a future era, when the Court's composition is more favorable and when the doctrinal foundation built through successful semi-automatic rifle cases provides a stronger platform from which to argue.
