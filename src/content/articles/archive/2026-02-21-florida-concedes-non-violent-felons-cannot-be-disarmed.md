## Summary

Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier filed a supplemental brief in the Florida District Court of Appeal confessing error on a question of constitutional significance: whether the Second Amendment permits the categorical disarmament of all felons, including those whose convictions reflect no history of violence or adjudicated dangerousness. The brief concludes that while the Second Amendment permits disarmament of felons whose convictions demonstrate that they are dangerous, it does not support the categorical disarmament of all persons with felony convictions as a class. This institutional acknowledgment—from a state attorney general exercising his constitutional duty to defend the law faithfully—represents a significant doctrinal development in the ongoing recalibration of felon-in-possession statutes following the Supreme Court's decisions in **District of Columbia v. Heller** and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**.

## Background

The underlying case involves Christopher Morgan, who in 2007 was convicted in Pennsylvania of carrying a firearm without a license—a felony under Pennsylvania law involving no act of violence, no threat to any person, and no conduct beyond the regulatory violation itself. In 2022, Morgan was stopped in a Florida traffic stop during which he disclosed both the firearm in his vehicle and his prior Pennsylvania conviction. Florida charged him with violating the state's felon-in-possession statute, a law that tracks the language of 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1), the federal prohibition on firearm possession by any person previously convicted of a crime punishable by imprisonment for a term exceeding one year.

Morgan challenged his conviction on Second Amendment grounds, arguing that his prior offense—a licensing violation with no violent conduct—did not establish the kind of dangerousness that the historical tradition of firearms regulation required as a predicate for disarmament. He lost at the trial level and appealed to the Florida District Court of Appeal, where the case was scheduled for oral argument on February 16, 2026.

On February 11, 2026, before oral argument, Attorney General Uthmeier filed a Notice Regarding Position and Motion for Leave to File Supplemental Brief. In it, the Attorney General stated:

> "On further reflection, the attorney general is of the view that the conviction violated Morgan's Second Amendment right to keep and bear arms, properly understood. The Second Amendment permits the government to dispossess felons whose convictions indicate that the felon is dangerous—but not merely all felons as a categorical matter."

## The Originalist Foundation

The Attorney General's position is grounded in the same historical methodology that the Supreme Court adopted in *Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and elaborated in *Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). Under the *Bruen* framework, the government bears the burden of demonstrating that any restriction on the right to keep and bear arms is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. The relevant inquiry is whether founding-era or Reconstruction-era law provides a genuine analogue to the challenged restriction—not whether the restriction can be rationalized as serving some legitimate interest, but whether it reflects a historically recognized pattern of regulation.

The historical record on this question is, in my analysis, reasonably clear. At the founding, the legal mechanisms for restricting firearms rights were predicated on demonstrated dangerousness, not on the commission of any offense that happened to carry a felony classification. Affray laws penalized the misuse of weapons to terrorize or threaten. Surety laws required individuals judicially found likely to endanger others to post bond or face disarmament. Neither tradition supports a categorical rule disarming all persons convicted of any felony offense, regardless of whether the offense involved violence, threatened violence, or provided any evidence of propensity for physical harm.

The founding-era distinction is well illustrated by the principles John Adams articulated in defending the British soldiers following the Boston Massacre in 1770. Adams acknowledged that the assembled colonists had every right to be armed in that setting—the right was the ordinary condition of a free citizen. The limit was misuse: arms employed offensively, to terrorize or threaten, fell outside the protection of the right. That same basic principle—peaceable possession is presumptively protected; its deprivation requires an individualized finding of danger—is the operating framework the Florida Attorney General has now applied.

## The Scope of the Confession of Error

The brief is notable both for what it concedes and for how precisely it frames the constitutional principle. The Attorney General did not argue that the Second Amendment categorically prohibits all disarmament of convicted felons. The position is more precise: the Second Amendment permits disarmament of felons whose convictions are indicative of danger to others or to themselves, but it does not permit categorical disarmament of the entire class of persons with felony records without reference to the nature of the underlying offense.

This framing aligns with the framework the Supreme Court gestured toward in its 2024 decision in **United States v. Rahimi**, where the Court held that the government may disarm an individual consistent with the Second Amendment where that person has been adjudicated, through a proceeding with due process protections, to pose a credible threat of physical violence. *Rahimi* did not resolve the felon-in-possession question directly, but its emphasis on individualized adjudication of danger as the constitutional criterion for disarmament is directly relevant to the analysis the Florida Attorney General has now advanced.

In my view, the Attorney General's formulation is constitutionally correct on its core point, though I would note that the brief references only danger to "other people" as the constitutional predicate for disarmament. The founding-era surety tradition, and the broader constitutional framework, would also support disarmament of individuals adjudicated to be a danger to themselves—a ground the brief does not address but which the Supreme Court would, I am confident, recognize as within the permissible scope of the historical tradition.

## Institutional Significance

The significance of the Florida Attorney General's filing extends beyond its effect on Christopher Morgan's case. An elected state attorney general, discharging the constitutional obligation to "exercise conscientious judgment in the discharge of his duties," has concluded that the categorical felon-in-possession framework cannot be squared with the Second Amendment as properly understood. This is not a position advanced by a Second Amendment advocacy organization in litigation, though such organizations have pressed this argument in courts across the country. It is a position taken by the officer of a state charged with defending that state's laws—an officer who, in this case, determined that fidelity to the Constitution required acknowledging that the state was wrong.

The legal landscape on categorical felon-in-possession statutes has been shifting since *Bruen*. Federal courts have reached varying conclusions about whether and how 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) can be applied to non-violent offenders under the *Bruen* historical methodology. The Third Circuit's decision in **Range v. Attorney General**, which held that an individual whose prior conviction was for making a false statement in connection with food stamp benefits could not be permanently disarmed, represents one significant data point. The Florida Attorney General's brief represents another: an institutional voice from within state government confirming that the categorical framework is constitutionally unsustainable when applied to individuals with no history of violent conduct or adjudicated dangerousness.

## The Distinction Between Dangerous and Non-Dangerous Felons

It is important to be precise about the contours of the principle the Florida filing advances. The Second Amendment community's position on felon-in-possession statutes is not that dangerous individuals—those with violent criminal records, those who have been adjudicated as posing a threat to others, those whose offenses reflect a demonstrated capacity for violence—may not be disarmed. The position is that the government's authority to disarm must be calibrated to the historical tradition of individualized, danger-predicated restriction, rather than extended categorically to everyone who has ever been convicted of an offense denominated a felony under any state or federal law.

The categorical approach encompasses, without differentiation, individuals convicted of violent crimes and individuals convicted of regulatory offenses, professional licensing violations, minor property crimes, and other non-violent conduct that happens to carry a felony classification. In many jurisdictions, the felony threshold has expanded considerably beyond what the founding generation would have recognized as grounds for civic disqualification. Applying a permanent, categorical firearms disability to all such individuals—without reference to whether any specific offense reflects dangerous propensity—cannot be grounded in a historical tradition that consistently required individualized assessment before disarmament.

## Implications

The incremental nature of constitutional change in the felon-in-possession context should not obscure its significance. The *Bruen* framework has provided courts with a methodology that is genuinely capable of distinguishing between historically grounded firearms restrictions and modern categorical expansions that lack historical support. The Florida Attorney General's brief demonstrates that this methodology is now operating at the institutional level: state governments themselves are applying it to reconsider the constitutional validity of laws they have previously defended.

The practical stakes are considerable. Felon-in-possession statutes affect a substantial and diverse population of individuals, many of whom—like Christopher Morgan—pose no demonstrated danger to anyone. As I have examined the founding-era record, the principle that emerges is consistent: the government may restrict firearms rights in response to demonstrated, adjudicated danger. It may not disarm as a matter of administrative convenience or categorical classification divorced from any assessment of the individual's actual propensity for violence. The Florida Attorney General has now applied that principle with appropriate rigor—and in doing so, has added a meaningful institutional voice to the constitutional recalibration that *Bruen* and *Rahimi* have set in motion.
