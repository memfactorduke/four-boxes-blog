## Summary

In an extraordinary and potentially decisive development for Second Amendment rights in the Third Circuit, the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit has sua sponte — that is, on its own initiative, without a request from any party — ordered en banc review of the pending challenge to New Jersey's prohibitions on so-called assault weapons and magazines with a capacity greater than ten rounds. The full Third Circuit has vacated the three-judge panel proceeding and has scheduled oral argument before the entire court for October 15, 2025. This development is, in my professional assessment, highly significant and favorable for the Second Amendment cause. The only principled reason why a court of appeals would intervene in this manner — removing a case from a panel that was almost certainly about to rule against the Second Amendment and reassigning it to the full court — is that a majority of the full court believes the panel would have gotten it wrong.

## Background

**Association of New Jersey Rifle & Pistol Clubs v. Platkin** is the lead challenge to New Jersey's comprehensive restrictions on semi-automatic firearms and large-capacity magazines. New Jersey's law prohibits the possession of semi-automatic rifles that the legislature designates as "assault weapons" and bans detachable magazines with a capacity exceeding ten rounds. These restrictions affect a broad class of commonly owned firearms and accessories.

The Third Circuit is the federal appellate court with jurisdiction over New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware. As a court that oversees a tri-state region including the notably anti-gun state of New Jersey, the Third Circuit has historically been an unfavorable venue for Second Amendment litigation.

Following oral argument before a three-judge panel on July 1, 2025, the outlook for Second Amendment advocates appeared bleak. The composition of the panel strongly suggested the court would rule two-to-one against the Second Amendment challengers, upholding New Jersey's restrictions. Based on the trajectory of the oral argument and the panel composition, that adverse ruling appeared virtually certain.

That ruling was never issued. Instead, the full Third Circuit intervened sua sponte.

## The Legal Significance of Sua Sponte En Banc Review

The Latin phrase *sua sponte* means "of one's own accord." In federal appellate procedure, a court acts sua sponte when it takes action on its own initiative rather than in response to a party's request. Sua sponte en banc review — the full court inserting itself into a pending case before a three-judge panel has issued its decision — is extraordinarily rare.

Under Federal Rule of Appellate Procedure 35, en banc review is appropriate when "necessary to secure or maintain uniformity of the court's decisions" or when "the proceeding involves a question of exceptional importance." Sua sponte en banc review prior to the issuance of a panel opinion is rarer still, because ordinarily a court of appeals does not interrupt a panel's deliberative process unless there is a compelling reason to do so.

The compelling reason in this case is evident: a majority of the active judges of the Third Circuit apparently concluded, before the panel issued its opinion, that the panel was on the verge of issuing a decision that would be wrong. Otherwise, there would be no rational basis for the court to intervene. If the full court agreed with the expected panel outcome, it would have no reason to displace the panel's authority — it would simply wait for the panel opinion and, if any judge disagreed, allow the standard en banc petition process to proceed in the ordinary course.

The inference is strong: the majority of the Third Circuit's active judges — judges who include a significant number of appointees of Democratic presidents, including appointments by Presidents Biden and Obama — believe that New Jersey's restrictions cannot be sustained under the Second Amendment as the Supreme Court has interpreted it in **Heller**, **McDonald**, and **Bruen**. That is an extraordinary development.

## What This Means for the Bruen Framework

The Third Circuit's sua sponte intervention is particularly significant given the broader landscape of circuit court decisions on assault weapons bans in the post-**Bruen** environment. As I have discussed at length in connection with the Second Circuit's ruling in **National Association for Gun Rights v. Connecticut**, multiple circuit courts have shown a troubling willingness to resist the **Bruen** framework, deploying selective quotation and analytical distortion to arrive at predetermined outcomes favorable to gun control.

The Third Circuit's intervention suggests that, at least in that circuit, a different dynamic is at work. The willingness of a majority of the full Third Circuit to preemptively remove a case from a panel expected to misapply **Bruen** indicates that a functioning judicial majority in the Third Circuit takes seriously the Supreme Court's command that lower courts apply the text-and-history methodology that **Bruen** mandates.

This matters beyond New Jersey. The Third Circuit sits in a region where Second Amendment litigation has historically yielded unfavorable results. A strong en banc ruling from the full Third Circuit applying **Bruen** faithfully — and potentially striking down New Jersey's assault weapons and magazine capacity bans — would create favorable precedent binding on district courts in New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware, and would add to the growing body of circuit court authority that the Supreme Court may eventually use as a vehicle for clarifying the national standard.

## The Role of Democratic-Appointed Judges

I want to note, without overstating the point, that the Third Circuit currently includes a significant number of judges appointed by Democratic presidents. The fact that the sua sponte en banc order apparently commands a majority among the full court means that at least some of those Democratic-appointed judges appear to be applying **Bruen**'s text-and-history framework rather than engaging in outcome-driven resistance to it.

This is, if the inference is correct, a significant development. One of the persistent concerns among Second Amendment advocates has been that the post-**Bruen** resistance to the Supreme Court's framework reflects not merely judicial error but a systematic ideological unwillingness among circuit judges appointed by Democratic presidents to faithfully apply the controlling law. If there are judges on the Third Circuit — regardless of their appointing president — who take seriously their oath to apply the law as the Supreme Court has expounded it, that is genuinely encouraging news.

## Implications

The full Third Circuit's oral argument on October 15, 2025, will be an important event in the ongoing development of Second Amendment jurisprudence. If the full court rules for the Second Amendment challengers — striking down or enjoining New Jersey's assault weapons and magazine capacity bans — the decision will be a major precedential victory for the Second Amendment.

Even if the full court rules against the challengers, the fact that the sua sponte intervention occurred suggests a genuine division of views within the court that may eventually produce a ruling, perhaps on a subsequent en banc petition, more favorable to Second Amendment rights.

The Second Amendment community should watch the October 15 argument closely. The questions asked by the judges, the tenor of the colloquy, and the eventual ruling will together provide important evidence about the direction of Second Amendment jurisprudence in the Third Circuit — and, by extension, about the prospects for protecting the right to keep and bear arms in some of the Nation's most restrictive jurisdictions.
