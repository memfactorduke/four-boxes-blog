## Summary

On September 1, 2025, the Delaware Superior Court issued a significant ruling in **Berney v. Delaware Department of Safety and Homeland Security**, holding that adults between the ages of eighteen and twenty are entitled to the full protection of the right to keep and bear arms under the Delaware State Constitution. The decision was authored by Judge Retta Greenstreet and addressed Delaware's application of age-based restrictions on firearms possession and acquisition to this cohort of young adults. While the ruling was grounded in Delaware's state constitutional right to keep and bear arms — which was substantially revised and reinforced by a 1987 constitutional amendment — rather than the federal Second Amendment, the decision represents an important victory for the principle that the right to keep and bear arms extends fully to all adults, regardless of whether they fall below the age of twenty-one. This ruling adds a significant brick to the growing wall of legal precedent protecting the firearms rights of young Americans.

## Background

Delaware is not a state one typically associates with Second Amendment victories. As the home state of former President Biden, Delaware has been governed by elected officials generally hostile to firearms rights. It is therefore all the more significant that a Delaware state court has delivered a ruling affirming the full scope of firearms rights for young adults — a population that has been targeted by recent state and federal legislation imposing age-based restrictions on firearm purchase, possession, and carry.

The legal landscape affecting 18-to-20-year-olds and firearms has been actively litigated throughout the federal court system in recent years. Multiple federal circuits have addressed the question of whether age-based restrictions on firearms purchases and carry are consistent with the Second Amendment, producing divergent results. The majority of the constitutional challenges have been brought under the federal Second Amendment, applying the text-and-history methodology established by the Supreme Court in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008) and reinforced by **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022). The question in those cases has been whether the historical tradition of firearms regulation in America includes any analogous age-based restrictions that would support modern laws prohibiting adults under twenty-one from purchasing or carrying firearms.

The Delaware case takes a different but complementary approach: rather than litigating under the federal Second Amendment, the plaintiffs argued under Delaware's own state constitutional right to keep and bear arms. Delaware's constitution contains a robust arms provision that was significantly strengthened in 1987, when the state adopted a constitutional amendment reaffirming the individual right to keep and bear arms for self-defense. This state constitutional provision provides an independent and parallel legal basis for challenging age-based firearms restrictions, independent of the federal constitutional question.

## The Constitutional Basis for the Court's Ruling

The Delaware Superior Court's ruling recognized that the constitutional text protecting the right to keep and bear arms makes no exception for adults between the ages of eighteen and twenty. Under both the Delaware State Constitution and the federal Second Amendment, the right belongs to "the people" — and adults between the ages of eighteen and twenty are unambiguously members of "the people" as that term is used in the constitutional context.

From a historical standpoint, the case against age-based restrictions for adults is compelling. At the time of the founding, and throughout the nineteenth century, eighteen-year-olds were commonly expected to bear arms in the militia and were not subject to any age-based restriction preventing them from possessing or purchasing firearms. Indeed, the legal age of majority has historically been tied to full civil participation — service in the military, entry into contracts, exercise of the franchise — and it is constitutionally anomalous to recognize adults as fully capable of military service and constitutional civic participation while simultaneously denying them a fundamental constitutional right.

The Bruen text-and-history framework strongly favors this conclusion. The government bears the burden of demonstrating a historical tradition of analogous age-based restriction that would justify denying adults between the ages of eighteen and twenty their constitutional right. That historical record, when honestly examined, does not support such restrictions.

## The Particular Importance of Second Amendment Rights for Young Adults

I have consistently argued, and the Delaware court's ruling reinforces, that the Second Amendment community must pay particular attention to the rights of young adults — not merely as a matter of abstract principle, but as a matter of practical urgency. Young adults between the ages of eighteen and twenty present a distinctive profile of vulnerability and need that makes the right to keep and bear arms for self-defense especially important for them.

Young adults, by virtue of their economic circumstances, often find themselves living in less safe residential environments than older Americans who have had decades to accumulate resources and choose their housing accordingly. They frequently work late-night shifts at hospitals, factories, and service establishments that require them to commute through unsafe neighborhoods at hours when the risk of violent crime is highest. They have less money, less established security, and less political voice than older Americans who have advocated for their own Second Amendment rights for decades. And they are the future of the Second Amendment movement — the next generation of gun owners, voters, and advocates who will either carry forward the constitutional culture that has produced the victories of **Heller**, **McDonald**, and **Bruen**, or who will have grown up in a legal environment where their rights were denied and diminished.

Age-based restrictions that prevent adults — legal adults, fully capable of military service, voting, and entry into binding contracts — from exercising the most fundamental right of self-defense are constitutionally indefensible and practically harmful to the people most in need of the protection the Second Amendment provides.

## The Relationship Between State and Federal Constitutional Rights

The **Berney** decision is grounded in Delaware's state constitutional provision, not the federal Second Amendment. This distinction is legally significant in several respects.

State constitutional rights to keep and bear arms exist independently of the federal Second Amendment and are interpreted under state constitutional law. Many state constitutions provide broader or more explicit protection for firearms rights than the federal Second Amendment, and state court rulings under state constitutional provisions are generally not subject to federal review unless they also involve a federal constitutional question.

The 1987 Delaware constitutional amendment, which substantially strengthened the state's arms provision, provides a particularly robust textual basis for the conclusion that Delaware citizens — all Delaware citizens who are legal adults — possess a right to keep and bear arms for self-defense that the state may not abridge through age-based restrictions directed at this cohort.

The ruling in **Berney** also illustrates an important broader principle: the Second Amendment movement is best served by pursuing parallel tracks of litigation — federal constitutional challenges under **Heller** and **Bruen** alongside state constitutional challenges under the many robust state arms provisions that have been enacted or amended over the past three decades. Every brick in the wall of Second Amendment protection, whether placed there by federal or state court decision, strengthens the overall structure of constitutional protection for the right to keep and bear arms.

## Implications

The Delaware Superior Court's ruling in **Berney** is a meaningful victory that deserves recognition in the broader landscape of Second Amendment jurisprudence, even though it is grounded in state rather than federal constitutional law.

For Delaware residents between the ages of eighteen and twenty, the ruling provides immediate legal protection against state-level age-based restrictions on firearms acquisition and possession under the Delaware Constitution. For the Second Amendment movement nationally, it provides an additional judicial precedent — from an unexpected jurisdiction — recognizing that age-based restrictions on adult firearms rights are constitutionally suspect and inconsistent with the constitutional text and history of firearms rights in America.

More broadly, the decision reinforces the message that the Second Amendment movement must litigate aggressively in state courts as well as federal courts, must develop state constitutional arguments alongside federal constitutional arguments, and must prioritize the rights of young adults as a strategic and moral imperative.
