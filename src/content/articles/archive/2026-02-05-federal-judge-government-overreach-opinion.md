## Summary

Fifth Circuit Judge Don Willett—former Texas Supreme Court Justice, now a federal appellate judge covering Texas, Louisiana, and Mississippi—has undertaken a sustained judicial campaign through a series of concurrences to reintroduce what constitutional scholars call the "structural Constitution" into Second Amendment analysis. In cases touching on the Hughes Amendment's post-1986 machine gun ban (18 U.S.C. § 922(o)) and the broader felon-in-possession prohibition (18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1)), Judge Willett argues that Congress's Article I authority over firearms regulation has expanded far beyond what the Constitution's architecture of enumerated and limited powers permits. In my analysis, Willett's jurisprudential project is not merely interesting academic commentary—it represents a serious challenge to the constitutional foundation of the modern federal gun control regime.

## Background

Constitutional law courses typically divide their subject matter into two conceptual categories: individual rights—the Bill of Rights, the Fourteenth Amendment—and the structural Constitution—Articles I through III, federalism, separation of powers, enumerated powers. In practice, for those concerned with protecting the right to keep and bear arms, almost all the attention flows to the former. The Second Amendment is a rights provision; the doctrinal debate focuses on its scope, its historical meaning, and the permissible limits of state and federal regulation.

Judge Willett is insisting that this bifurcated focus is incomplete and potentially dangerous. The structural Constitution, he argues, is not merely a set of process rules governing who may exercise what governmental power. It is itself a liberty-protecting mechanism—in Justice Scalia's formulation, the Constitution's greatest contribution to liberty is not the Bill of Rights but the separation of powers and the system of divided sovereignty between federal and state governments.

Willett has pressed this argument in concurrences in at least two significant Fifth Circuit cases: **United States v. Wilson**, involving the Hughes Amendment's prohibition on civilian transfer of machine guns manufactured after May 19, 1986, and **United States v. Henry**, involving whether a prior drug possession conviction constitutes a sufficient basis to impose a lifetime firearms disability under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1).

## The Structural Constitution and Article I's Enumerated Powers

The federal government is, by design, a government of enumerated and therefore limited powers. Article I, Section 8 grants Congress specific, defined powers. The Tenth Amendment reserves to the states and the people all powers not delegated to the federal government. This is the foundational architecture: a federal government of defined, bounded authority, surrounded by the broader general police power authority of the states.

Over the course of the twentieth century, particularly following the New Deal Court's expansive interpretation of the Commerce Clause beginning in the late 1930s, this architecture has been substantially eroded. Congress has legislated in areas far removed from any plausible connection to the enumerated power basis nominally invoked—usually the Commerce Clause—for the legislation. Federal criminal law, in particular, has expanded into areas that, under the Constitution's original design, would have been exclusively the province of state law.

Gun control legislation is a prominent example. The federal Gun Control Act of 1968, the Hughes Amendment to the Firearm Owners Protection Act of 1986 (which imposed the machine gun ban), and the Brady Handgun Violence Prevention Act of 1993 all rest on the Commerce Clause as their constitutional foundation. In many of these cases, the connection to interstate commerce is thin to the point of being purely formal.

Judge Willett's concurrences call this out directly. In the **United States v. Wilson** case, he wrote:

> "The Constitution's enduring genius lies less in its promises than in its design. As Justice Scalia once reminded a Senate committee, 'Even the most oppressive regimes profess liberty, but only a government of divided powers can give life to parchment guarantees.' That design, the Constitution's Madisonian architecture of separated powers, divided sovereignty, and enumerated authority was shrewdly crafted to constrain government even as it empowers it. And each generation must decide whether to honor those structural limits as boundaries to uphold or to treat them as obstacles to outwit."

This is not abstract constitutional philosophy. It is a direct challenge to the premise that Congress may impose categorical, nationwide criminal prohibitions on firearm possession whenever it attaches a Commerce Clause label to the legislation.

## Justice Scalia's Senate Testimony and Its Contemporary Relevance

Judge Willett's invocation of Justice Scalia is deliberate and significant. Scalia's observation—that even oppressive regimes profess liberty, but only divided government can make liberty real—captures a fundamental constitutional insight that has been largely forgotten in modern Second Amendment discourse.

The Second Amendment community has, correctly, invested enormous resources in the doctrinal development of Second Amendment rights since **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008). But rights provisions, standing alone, are insufficient protection. A Congress that can use an expansive Commerce Clause reading to regulate virtually any firearm-related conduct, attach criminal penalties to violations, and thereby functionally disarm the civilian population without formally amending the Second Amendment, has effectively circumvented the constitutional protection that amendment provides.

Judge Willett's structural Constitution project represents an attempt to close this gap—to require that federal firearms legislation rest on a genuine enumerated power basis, not merely a nominal Commerce Clause hook.

## The Machine Gun Ban: A Test Case for Article I Limits

The Hughes Amendment's prohibition on civilian acquisition of machine guns manufactured after May 19, 1986—codified at 18 U.S.C. § 922(o)—is an especially interesting case for testing the limits of Congress's Article I authority. The prohibition was enacted through a procedurally chaotic legislative process and applies not merely to commercial transactions but to simple possession of firearms manufactured after the statutory cutoff.

The Commerce Clause basis for this prohibition is, to put it charitably, attenuated. The mere possession of a firearm that happened to be manufactured after a certain date, without any connection to a current commercial transaction, is difficult to characterize as an exercise of Congress's power to regulate interstate commerce. Judge Willett has flagged this concern explicitly, noting that the Hughes Amendment creates what is essentially a generalized criminal prohibition with minimal connection to any plausible Commerce Clause rationale.

If the structural Constitution argument gains traction—and Willett's concurrences are clearly aimed at planting seeds for future litigation—it could provide an independent basis for challenging the machine gun ban that does not depend on the Second Amendment's text-and-history analysis. A law that Congress lacks the enumerated power to enact is unconstitutional regardless of whether it would separately violate the Second Amendment.

## Implications

I have reviewed Judge Willett's concurrences in detail, and they represent serious constitutional scholarship. Whether the Supreme Court will ultimately adopt the structural Constitution framework as an independent basis for scrutinizing federal firearms legislation is uncertain—the Court has been reluctant to impose rigorous limits on Congress's Commerce Clause authority since the brief Lopez/Morrison period in the 1990s. But the argument is legitimate, important, and deserves to be developed in the lower courts before being pressed upward.

The most important implication for Second Amendment advocates is strategic: the path to protecting the right to keep and bear arms is not exhausted by Second Amendment doctrine alone. Article I limits on Congress's enumerated powers, properly enforced, are themselves a bulwark against federal firearms overreach. Judge Willett is right to insist that constitutional structure is part of the analysis—and right to invoke Justice Scalia's reminder that divided government is not a bureaucratic inconvenience but the primary mechanism through which constitutional liberty is actually secured.
