## Summary

The en banc oral argument in **Coons v. Attorney General of New Jersey** and **Siegel v. Attorney General of New Jersey** before the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit did not go well for the State of New Jersey. In my assessment, New Jersey's lawyers faced pointed and skeptical questioning from multiple judges, and at least one judge on the panel appeared to cite a historical law that does not, upon examination, actually exist in the historical record — a remarkable development that illustrates both the weakness of the state's historical case and the degree of judicial resistance that Second Amendment rights continue to encounter even from jurists nominally applying Bruen's originalist framework.

## Background

The Third Circuit is the federal appellate court of intermediate jurisdiction over Pennsylvania, Delaware, and New Jersey. It sits predominantly in Philadelphia and has historically been a court that has given limited deference to Second Amendment rights. New Jersey is among the most restrictive jurisdictions in the nation when it comes to the right to keep and bear arms — a state that, before **New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), effectively denied the public-carry right to ordinary citizens and then responded to Bruen's mandate with a sweeping redesign of its gun laws intended to preserve as much restriction as possible within whatever space the Supreme Court's decision left open.

New Jersey's post-Bruen legislative response designated approximately 24 to 25 broad location categories as government-mandated gun-free zones — what I have consistently called "sensitive places," though that term is a regulatory euphemism for something more accurately described as mass prohibition on the exercise of a constitutional right in public space. The prohibited locations include beaches, colleges, schools, childcare facilities, hospitals, nursery schools, youth sport events, museums, libraries, zoos, theaters, casinos, stadiums, and healthcare facilities. The state also enacted a so-called "vampire rule" — my shorthand for the requirement that, even with a valid carry license, a citizen cannot bring a firearm onto any private property open to the public without the express, affirmative consent of the property owner. That provision inverts the constitutional baseline by treating the exercise of a fundamental right as an opt-in permission that every gas station, grocery store, and laundromat operator must separately grant.

## Procedural History

Federal district court Judge Renée Marie Bumb issued a ruling favorable to the Second Amendment, enjoining many of New Jersey's restrictions and finding that the state could not identify founding-era historical analogs sufficient to justify its expansive gun-free zone scheme. The case proceeded to a three-judge Third Circuit panel that included Judge David Porter — the strongest originalist voice on the court — but also two judges far less sympathetic to Second Amendment claims. The panel majority, in a September 2025 decision, reversed the district court and upheld most of New Jersey's scheme. Judge Porter dissented forcefully. The full Third Circuit subsequently granted en banc rehearing, aided in part by the recent confirmation of two new Trump-appointed judges, Judge Jennifer Mascott and Judge Emil Bove, whose addition shifted the court's composition.

## What Happened at Argument

By all accounts available from the oral argument transcript and recordings, New Jersey's lawyers struggled. The state's theory — that its sweeping sensitive-places designations are consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation — ran directly into the problem that founding-era history simply does not support the comprehensive prohibition of public carry that New Jersey has enacted. Bruen itself requires the government to identify "distinctly similar" historical regulations when a challenged law addresses a "general societal problem that has long existed." New Jersey cannot point to anything in the historical record that resembles a ban on carrying firearms at beaches, libraries, museums, zoos, or the full constellation of locations the state has designated.

Of particular note, one judge on the en banc panel — whose questioning appeared, in my view, to be oriented toward finding support for New Jersey's position — cited what appears to be a historical law that does not actually exist in the historical record. When an advocate defending a constitutional restriction must rely on fabricated or nonexistent historical precedent, that is not a minor technical error. It is a fundamental indictment of the historical case for the restriction itself. If the founding era actually supported comprehensive sensitive-places gun bans, advocates would not need to construct fictional history to defend them.

The questioning from other members of the bench pressed New Jersey on the scope of its designations and on the temporal question — whether founding-era or Reconstruction-era history should control — that I have addressed at length in my scholarship. Chief Judge Chagares, in particular, challenged New Jersey's argument that Reconstruction-era analogs should prevail when they conflict with founding-era evidence, citing Bruen's explicit instruction that late-nineteenth-century history has "minimal probative value" in such conflicts.

## The Vampire Rule and Its Constitutional Infirmity

The so-called vampire rule presents a somewhat different constitutional challenge from the location-based sensitive-places designations, but it is no less infirm. The rule converts the public-carry right that Bruen recognized into a conditional entitlement dependent on the consent of every individual property owner whose land or premises a citizen might wish to enter. The constitutional right to bear arms in public for self-defense does not evaporate upon crossing the threshold of a privately owned business open to the general public. No historical analog from the founding era supports such a rule. To the contrary, the founding-era understanding was that the right to bear arms in public was a general liberty, not one that required the affirmative permission of each property owner along one's path through public commercial life.

This provision is currently pending before the Supreme Court in **Wolford v. Lopez**, a case arising from Hawaii's analogous vampire-rule provision. I expect the Supreme Court to strike it down. The Third Circuit may find it unnecessary to address the vampire rule directly if the Supreme Court acts first.

## Implications

The en banc argument in **Coons** and **Siegel** produced several indicators favorable to the Second Amendment. New Jersey's lawyers were pressed hard on the foundations of their historical case. At least one judge sympathetic to the state's position appeared to resort to historical argument that is not grounded in actual historical sources — a telling sign of the weakness of the state's evidentiary position. The Chief Judge's direct challenge to the Reconstruction-era argument is significant.

The Third Circuit's ultimate decision will almost certainly be reviewed by the Supreme Court regardless of outcome. If the en banc court upholds New Jersey's scheme, it will conflict with the framework Bruen established and invite certiorari. If it strikes down significant portions of the scheme, New Jersey will petition for review. Either way, the Supreme Court will likely have occasion to elaborate on the scope of the sensitive-places exception and the limits on state authority to redesign gun laws as end-runs around the right Bruen recognized.

What is clear from the argument is that New Jersey's comprehensive approach to sensitive-places designation — covering virtually the entire geography of public life in the state — is indefensible under a faithful application of Bruen's text-and-history test. The historical record from 1791 does not support it, and the state's advocates cannot construct a sufficient historical case because the founding-era evidence simply does not exist.
