## Summary

The United States Department of Justice has officially announced the formal creation of a Second Amendment section within the Civil Rights Division — the first dedicated institutional unit of its kind in the federal government's history devoted to protecting and advancing the right to keep and bear arms. In her announcement, Assistant Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon provided specific details about the unit's priorities and initial enforcement actions. I want to both celebrate this landmark development and offer what I believe is a necessary and constructive framework for evaluating the DOJ's Second Amendment performance going forward.

---

*The following analysis is based on Assistant Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon's formal announcement of the DOJ Second Amendment section and the unit's initial enforcement activities as of December 2025.*

---

## Background

The formal establishment of a dedicated Second Amendment unit within the DOJ Civil Rights Division is, in my assessment, a historic development. To appreciate its significance, consider the context: the Second Amendment was ratified in 1791. For nearly 234 years, no administration of either party created a dedicated federal institutional structure within the Department of Justice specifically charged with protecting the right to keep and bear arms as a civil right.

As Dhillon noted in her announcement, the closest historical analogue may be the Freedmen's Bureau established in the 19th century, which was used, among other purposes, to protect the Second Amendment rights of formerly enslaved African Americans against the systematic disarmament campaigns of Southern state governments. Attorney General Pam Bondi has stated publicly that the Second Amendment is not a "second-class right" — and the creation of this unit translates that statement from rhetoric into institutional reality.

## What the Unit Has Already Done

In her announcement, Dhillon reported on the Civil Rights Division's initial Second Amendment enforcement actions:

**Los Angeles Lawsuit.** The Division has filed suit against the City of Los Angeles for imposing unreasonable, multi-year delays on concealed carry permit applications. Under **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022), the Second Amendment protects the right to carry firearms in public for self-defense, and a jurisdiction that weaponizes administrative delay to effectively nullify that right cannot survive constitutional scrutiny. This lawsuit was overdue.

**Barnett v. Raoul (Seventh Circuit).** Dhillon personally argued the **Barnett** case before the Seventh Circuit in Chicago, challenging Illinois's ban on semi-automatic rifles, including AR-15s. This represents the kind of direct, personal engagement by senior DOJ leadership with Second Amendment litigation that is unprecedented in the Division's history.

**Hawaii Challenge.** The Division filed an additional challenge to Hawaii's unreasonable firearm restrictions, adding another front to the DOJ's offensive posture against anti-gun state regimes.

**Amicus Briefs.** The DOJ, through the Solicitor General's office, has filed amicus briefs in multiple circuit court cases supporting the constitutional protection of firearms that various states have attempted to ban.

The unit's current focus areas, as Dhillon described them, include: multi-thousand-dollar application fees that price law-abiding citizens out of their constitutional rights; unreasonably long permit processing delays; and bans on firearms that are presumptively protected under **Bruen**'s "common use" standard.

## A Framework for Evaluating DOJ Performance

I want to be direct about something: applause for good performance should be vigorous and sincere. But the Second Amendment community should also be discerning and analytical — not reflexively deferential to any administration simply because it uses the right language. With that in mind, I want to propose a framework by which citizens, advocates, and commentators should evaluate the DOJ's Second Amendment performance over time.

**Category One: Clear Wins.** When the DOJ files well-crafted lawsuits against anti-gun jurisdictions, argues constitutional cases competently in the circuits, and secures injunctions blocking unconstitutional firearms restrictions, it deserves recognition and support. The Los Angeles lawsuit and the Barnett argument fall squarely in this category. These are good decisions that should be applauded unambiguously.

**Category Two: Areas for Improvement.** There are situations where the DOJ is moving in the right direction but could do better — perhaps by making more aggressive legal arguments, taking on additional cases, or advancing more robust Second Amendment theories in its briefs. When I see these opportunities, I will identify them. Constructive criticism of friends is not disloyalty; it is how you make friends stronger.

**Category Three: Wrong Paths.** There are also situations where the DOJ makes strategic decisions that are, in my view, legally incorrect or counterproductive. The most significant example is where the DOJ defends federal gun-control statutes that are constitutionally questionable. I understand the "Take Care" clause obligation — the executive branch has an institutional duty to defend validly enacted statutes unless they have been declared unconstitutional by a court. But there are arguments and strategic choices available to the DOJ that can minimize the damage done while complying with institutional obligations, and I will note when I believe the DOJ is choosing the wrong path.

## The Significance of the Freedmen's Bureau Analogy

I want to pause on a historical point that Dhillon mentioned in her announcement and that I find both accurate and important. The Freedmen's Bureau was established after the Civil War precisely to protect the constitutional rights of newly freed Black Americans — including their Second Amendment right to keep and bear arms. Southern state governments systematically disarmed freed slaves through "Black Codes," and the Freedmen's Bureau used federal authority to push back.

The parallel to today is instructive. Anti-gun "blue state" governments — California, New York, New Jersey, Illinois, Hawaii, and others — are today engaged in a similarly systematic campaign of disarmament, targeting law-abiding citizens through prohibitive fees, endless delays, and categorical bans on commonly owned firearms. The use of federal Civil Rights Division authority to counter these regimes is not only legally sound; it is historically consonant with the Division's foundational mission.

## Critical Considerations Going Forward

The creation of this unit raises important questions about durability. An administrative unit created by one administration can be dismantled by the next. The more important long-term goal is securing court decisions — at the circuit level and ultimately at the Supreme Court — that establish binding precedent protecting Second Amendment rights regardless of who controls the executive branch.

This is why the DOJ's litigation strategy matters so much. Every well-argued brief, every court victory, every favorable circuit precedent builds the constitutional infrastructure that will outlast any particular administration. I will be watching closely to ensure that the Second Amendment unit's litigation choices maximize the long-term jurisprudential value of its work — not merely its short-term political optics.

## Implications

The formal launch of the DOJ Second Amendment unit is a landmark development that deserves recognition. For the first time in American history, the federal government has institutionalized the protection of the right to keep and bear arms as a civil rights priority within the Department of Justice.

The announcement of specific enforcement actions — the Los Angeles lawsuit, the Barnett argument, the Hawaii challenge — demonstrates that this is not merely symbolic. The unit is operational, it is litigating, and it is on offense. That is exactly what the Second Amendment community has needed and demanded for decades.

The work is just beginning. There are hundreds of unconstitutional firearms restrictions across this country that need to be challenged. The DOJ has a long road ahead. But the institutional foundation is now in place, and for that I am genuinely encouraged.
