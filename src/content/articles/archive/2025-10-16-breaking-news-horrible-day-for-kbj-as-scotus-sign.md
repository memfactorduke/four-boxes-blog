## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit — the federal appellate court with jurisdiction over Delaware, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania — convened today for en banc oral argument in **Cheeseman v. Polillo** and **Association of New Jersey Rifle & Pistol Clubs v. Attorney General of New Jersey**, the consolidated challenge to New Jersey's ban on AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles and its prohibition on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds. The entire Third Circuit heard argument today rather than a three-judge panel — a procedurally significant development that I have been highlighting for some time as a potentially major breakthrough for the Second Amendment. I am pleased to report that Judge Jennifer Mascot, the newest member of the Third Circuit, did indeed participate in today's argument as I had hoped and anticipated. In my assessment, the questions posed at oral argument were overall favorable to the Second Amendment, and I believe the Second Amendment community has a strong likelihood of prevailing in this case.

## Background: How We Arrived at En Banc Review

Understanding why this case is before the full Third Circuit rather than a standard three-judge panel requires some background. Some time ago, the cases were argued before a three-judge panel in the Third Circuit. That panel circulated a draft opinion internally to the rest of the court, as is standard practice under Third Circuit rules before a panel decision is published. The draft opinion, as I assessed it, was clearly adverse to the Second Amendment — the panel that drew those cases was, in my view, an anti-Second Amendment panel that was going to rule against the challengers.

However, the full Third Circuit reviewed the draft and voted, internally, not to allow it to be issued. Rather than permitting that panel decision to become binding precedent across the circuit, the full court voted to rehear the cases en banc — meaning all of the active judges on the Third Circuit would participate. The three-judge panel opinion was thereby vacated and today's argument before the full court replaced it.

This sequence of events was significant for a specific reason: I had been publicly urging attention to the fact that there was an open seat on the Third Circuit — a vacancy based in Delaware — that President Donald Trump had the opportunity to fill. Trump nominated Jennifer Mascot, a former law clerk to both Justice Clarence Thomas and Justice Brett Kavanaugh, and a law professor with deep Second Amendment expertise, to fill that seat. After confirmation by the United States Senate and the signing of her commission — which President Trump completed before departing on his Middle East trip — Judge Mascot was sworn in. As of this morning, she participated in the en banc oral argument. That participation matters enormously for the composition of the panel that will decide these cases.

## The Legal Framework: Heller, Common Use, and the Burden

The governing framework for these cases derives from **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008) and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022). **Heller** established that the Second Amendment protects the right to keep and bear arms that are in common use by Americans for lawful purposes. The historical tradition of regulating arms — the only basis on which the government may restrict the Second Amendment after **Bruen** — permits the banning only of weapons that are both dangerous and unusual. Weapons in common use among law-abiding Americans are by definition not unusual, and therefore cannot constitutionally be banned simply because a state legislature regards them as dangerous.

New Jersey's position at oral argument inverted this framework. The state argued, in substance, that the burden falls on Second Amendment claimants to demonstrate that the banned firearms are not dangerous — essentially arguing that the government's power to ban arms is presumptive and that gun owners must affirmatively establish their constitutional right. That argument is incompatible with **Heller** and **Bruen**, both of which make clear that when the government prohibits arms, the burden falls on the government to justify that prohibition through historical tradition.

One of the judges pressed New Jersey's counsel on this point in a manner I found analytically precise: if New Jersey's standard is "dangerous or unusual" — meaning the state can ban weapons that are either dangerous or unusual, as a disjunctive test — then the state can ban all firearms, because by definition every firearm is dangerous. New Jersey then attempted to retreat to the position that the standard is "unusually dangerous," a conjunctive formulation. But that formulation creates its own problem: determining whether something is "unusually" dangerous requires establishing a baseline of what usual dangerousness looks like among the class of commonly held arms. When tens of millions of Americans own and lawfully use AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines, those items are by definition within the category of usual arms. They cannot simultaneously be unusual.

## New Jersey's Burden Argument

A second major argument New Jersey pressed was that the burden of proof should rest with the Second Amendment claimants — that it was the obligation of Cheeseman, the Association of New Jersey Rifle & Pistol Clubs, and the other plaintiffs to affirmatively demonstrate that AR-15s and standard-capacity magazines are not in common use or are not protected arms.

This argument is, in my analysis, flatly inconsistent with settled Second Amendment doctrine. Under **Heller**, the definition of "arms" within the Second Amendment's protection is broad: anything that can be used offensively or defensively for lawful purposes. AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles and magazines that facilitate their operation unambiguously fall within that definition. Once those items are established as arms within the constitutional category, and once the government enacts a ban on them, the burden shifts to the government — not the right-holder — to justify that ban by identifying a historical tradition of analogous regulation.

The Supreme Court's framework in **Bruen** reinforced this burden allocation. It is the government's obligation to come forward with historical analogues demonstrating that arms bans of this type have a tradition in American law going back to the founding era. New Jersey did not carry that burden credibly in my assessment, and the questions from the bench during oral argument reflected skepticism about the state's historical evidence.

## Jennifer Mascot's Participation

I want to explain why Judge Mascot's participation in this argument is significant beyond her individual vote. The Third Circuit's jurisprudence on the Second Amendment has historically been hostile to gun rights. The en banc configuration of the court, with the participation of a judge who is both a former Clarence Thomas clerk and a former Brett Kavanaugh clerk — and who brings deep academic expertise in originalist Second Amendment analysis — changes the intellectual character of the deliberations. In appellate courts, the quality of the legal arguments made during deliberation and in opinion drafting matters enormously. Judge Mascot's presence ensures that the full force of the Second Amendment's original meaning and the proper application of **Heller** and **Bruen** will be brought to bear throughout the court's internal discussions.

## The Connection to Louisiana v. Callais

Today's Third Circuit argument occurs against the backdrop of the Supreme Court's pending decision in **Louisiana v. Callais**, which was argued at the Supreme Court yesterday. As I have analyzed extensively, a decision in **Callais** invalidating Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act's requirement of race-conscious redistricting could result in the elimination of approximately nineteen majority-minority congressional districts, potentially shifting the House of Representatives toward a more durable Republican majority for years. That structural political shift would provide enormous legislative protection for Second Amendment rights at the federal level. The Third Circuit's AR-15 ban case and the Supreme Court's redistricting case thus both represent, through very different legal mechanisms, pathways to securing the right to keep and bear arms against ongoing political and legal attacks.

## Implications

Today was a significant day for the Second Amendment. En banc argument in the Third Circuit on New Jersey's AR-15 ban, with a reconstituted court that includes Judge Mascot, represents the best opportunity this circuit has presented in years for a pro-Second Amendment decision that could invalidate some of the most aggressive firearms restrictions in the northeastern United States. If the Third Circuit rules that New Jersey's AR-15 ban and its ten-round magazine limit violate the Second Amendment, the decision would bind the entire circuit covering Delaware, New Jersey, and Pennsylvania, and would create significant pressure on other circuits and the Supreme Court to address similar bans in states like California, Maryland, and Illinois.

I will monitor the court's deliberations and provide analysis of the decision when it is issued.
