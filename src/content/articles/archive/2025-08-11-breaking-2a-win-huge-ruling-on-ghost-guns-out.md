## Summary

On August 11, 2025, the Minnesota Supreme Court issued a significant ruling in **State of Minnesota v. Veagel**, holding that a Minnesota criminal statute prohibiting possession of a firearm not identified by a serial number applies only to firearms that are required under federal law to bear a serial number — and that a privately made firearm not subject to federal serialization requirements does not fall within the scope of that criminal prohibition. The case arose when a defendant involved in a motor vehicle accident was found in possession of, among other items, an AR-style privately made firearm with no serial number. The Minnesota Supreme Court's decision, while decided on statutory construction grounds rather than Second Amendment constitutional grounds, represents a meaningful legal development for the Second Amendment community and offers a clear illustration of how textual fidelity in statutory interpretation can produce outcomes favorable to the right to keep and bear arms.

## Background

Minnesota law contains a criminal provision, enacted in 1994, that criminalizes the possession of a firearm that is not "identified by a serial number." The state prosecuted Mr. Veagel, alleging that the AR-style privately made firearm found in his vehicle — a firearm assembled from commercially available components without a serial number, commonly referred to by gun control advocates as a "ghost gun" — violated this statute.

The term "ghost gun" is, in my view, a propagandistic label designed to generate fear and hostility toward a category of firearms that has a long, lawful, and constitutionally protected tradition in American life. Americans have made their own firearms since before the founding of the Republic. The right to manufacture one's own arms for personal use has never been questioned historically and is, at its core, an expression of the same right to keep and bear arms that the Second Amendment protects. The fact that a privately made firearm does not carry a serial number issued by a commercial manufacturer does not transform it into a criminal instrument.

The legal question in **State v. Veagel** was purely one of statutory interpretation: did Minnesota's 1994 criminal statute — which prohibited possession of a firearm "not identified by a serial number" — apply to a privately made firearm that was never commercially manufactured and therefore never subject to any federal or state requirement to bear a serial number?

## The Federal Serialization Framework

To understand the Minnesota Supreme Court's reasoning, one must first understand the federal framework governing serial number requirements. Two federal statutes are relevant.

The first is the National Firearms Act, which governs so-called NFA items — short-barreled rifles, short-barreled shotguns, suppressors, machine guns, and certain other regulated firearms. Under the NFA, serialization requirements attach regardless of who manufactures or possesses the item. A privately made NFA item must bear a serial number.

The second is the Gun Control Act of 1968, which imposes serialization requirements on federally licensed manufacturers and dealers. Critically, the Gun Control Act's serialization mandate applies to FFLs — those licensed in the business of manufacturing and selling firearms. It does not impose a blanket requirement on private individuals who manufacture firearms for their own personal use. A person who lawfully manufactures a firearm for personal use that does not fall within the NFA's definition of a regulated item is not required under federal law to serialize that firearm.

This is not a loophole or an oversight. It is the intended operation of the federal statutory framework as enacted by Congress. The ATF's 2022 attempt to administratively expand the serialization requirement to cover privately made firearms — an attempt that was subsequently challenged in federal court — does not alter the text of the underlying statutes as they existed when the Minnesota legislature enacted its 1994 criminal provision.

## The Minnesota Supreme Court's Holding

The Minnesota Supreme Court interpreted its 1994 statute to incorporate the federal serialization framework by reference. The court held that Minnesota's criminal prohibition on possession of a firearm "not identified by a serial number" applies only to firearms that are required under federal law to bear a serial number. Because a privately made firearm not subject to NFA regulation is not required under federal law to bear a serial number, it does not fall within the scope of the Minnesota criminal provision.

In reaching this conclusion, the court was notably candid about the policy dimensions of the issue. The majority opinion acknowledged that "personally manufactured firearms that are not required to have serial numbers pose real dangers to public safety" and that regulation of such weapons "is an important and complex public policy issue." The court further observed that the Minnesota legislature had considered but declined to enact a specific prohibition on privately made firearms in 2023 — meaning that the legislature had the opportunity to address the issue directly and chose not to.

The court's conclusion followed from this legislative history: "the final decision on whether and how to regulate ghost guns rests with the legislature." Courts do not make policy; they interpret statutes as written. The 1994 Minnesota law, as written, did not encompass firearms not required by federal law to bear a serial number, and the court correctly declined to rewrite the statute to achieve a result that the legislature had not enacted.

## The Significance of Statutory Fidelity

This decision is a model of the kind of disciplined, textually faithful statutory interpretation that produces correct outcomes regardless of the political valence of the result. The Minnesota Supreme Court did not reach out to invalidate all regulation of privately made firearms. It did not issue a sweeping Second Amendment ruling. It simply read the statute as written, examined the legislative history, noted that the legislature had consciously declined to extend the prohibition to privately made firearms, and concluded that the criminal provision did not reach the defendant's conduct.

This approach — fidelity to the text actually enacted by the legislature — is the correct judicial methodology, and it frequently produces results favorable to Second Amendment rights precisely because many of the most aggressive gun control measures are enacted hastily, drafted imprecisely, and enforced far beyond their textual scope. A court willing to hold the government to the actual words of the law it enacted is, in effect, a check on regulatory overreach even when it does not reach constitutional questions.

## Implications

The Minnesota Supreme Court's ruling in **State v. Veagel** carries several significant implications.

First, it establishes that in Minnesota, the state's criminal prohibition on unserialized firearms does not apply to privately made firearms not subject to federal serialization requirements. This is a direct legal protection for individuals in Minnesota who lawfully manufacture their own firearms for personal use.

Second, the decision confirms that the appropriate vehicle for regulating privately made firearms is the legislature, not prosecutorial overreach under statutes that do not encompass the targeted conduct. If Minnesota wishes to prohibit the possession of privately made unserialized firearms, it must pass a law that says so. Courts will not fill the gap by expanding criminal statutes beyond their text.

Third, and perhaps most broadly, this case illustrates that the legal fight over privately made firearms will be won or lost in legislatures and in courts interpreting the precise language of the laws enacted. The Second Amendment community should remain vigilant against legislative efforts to broadly prohibit privately made firearms, while taking confidence from decisions like **State v. Veagel** that demonstrate the courts' willingness, when properly presented with the issue, to hold the government to the text of the law as written.
