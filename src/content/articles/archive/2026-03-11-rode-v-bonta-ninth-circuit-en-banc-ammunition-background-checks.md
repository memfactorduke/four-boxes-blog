## Summary

I've analyzed a significant development where the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit has selected an eleven-judge en banc panel to hear *Rhode v. Bonta*, a Second Amendment challenge to California's ammunition background check law. In a development that Second Amendment advocates view as unusually favorable, eight of the eleven judges drawn for the panel were appointed by Republican presidents, with only three appointed by Democratic presidents. The case arrives at the en banc stage after the Second Amendment prevailed at both the district court level and before a three-judge panel, which struck down the law in a 2-1 decision. The Trump Administration's Department of Justice, through its Second Amendment Unit, has filed an amicus brief supporting the challengers and urging the court to affirm the panel decision invalidating the law. Oral argument is scheduled for March 25, 2025.

## Background

California enacted a law requiring that all purchasers of ammunition submit to a background check before completing a transaction. The law stands as an outlier in American firearms regulation -- it is, by all accounts, the only law of its kind in the country that imposes a full background check requirement on ammunition purchases. The stated purpose of the law is to prevent prohibited persons from acquiring ammunition, but its practical effect is to impose additional costs, delays, and burdens on every ammunition buyer in the state, including law-abiding citizens who have already passed background checks to purchase their firearms.

Second Amendment plaintiffs challenged the law in federal district court. The trial court conducted an analysis under the framework established in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), and concluded that the ammunition background check system was unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The state of California appealed to the Ninth Circuit, where a three-judge panel affirmed the district court's ruling in a 2-1 decision. The majority held that the law impermissibly burdened the right to keep and bear arms. One judge dissented, finding the law constitutional.

California then sought en banc rehearing -- a procedural move that has become routine in the Ninth Circuit whenever the Second Amendment prevails at the panel stage. The Ninth Circuit granted en banc review, and the eleven-judge panel was selected.

## The En Banc Panel Composition

In my analysis, the composition of an en banc panel in the Ninth Circuit carries outsized significance. Unlike every other federal circuit, where en banc review involves all active judges on the court, the Ninth Circuit's size requires that en banc panels consist of only eleven randomly selected judges -- the chief judge and ten judges drawn by lot. The identity of those judges can be outcome-determinative, particularly in politically charged areas of law like the Second Amendment.

The panel drawn for *Rhode v. Bonta* is, by Ninth Circuit standards, remarkably favorable for the Second Amendment. Eight of the eleven judges were nominated by Republican presidents, and three were nominated by Democratic presidents. This ratio is nearly unprecedented for a Second Amendment en banc case in a circuit that has historically been hostile to firearms rights.

However, as I've examined it, the raw partisan composition of the panel does not guarantee the outcome. One of the Republican-appointed judges on the panel, Judge VanBebber, dissented from the original three-judge panel decision, voting to uphold California's law. If that judge maintains his position, the effective split would be seven potential votes in favor of the Second Amendment to four against -- still a comfortable margin, but contingent on the remaining Republican appointees adhering to a rigorous reading of the constitutional text. As I caution, some Republican-appointed judges adopt "law and order" postures that lead them to defer to government regulation of firearms, regardless of what the Second Amendment requires.

Of particular note, Judge Lawrence VanDyke -- widely regarded as one of the strongest Second Amendment voices on the Ninth Circuit and a rumored candidate for a future Supreme Court vacancy -- is among the eleven judges selected. His presence on the panel raises the possibility of a muscular pro-Second Amendment majority opinion that could reshape Ninth Circuit precedent, in my view.

## The Department of Justice Amicus Brief

In my analysis, adding further weight to the challengers' position, the Department of Justice under Attorney General Pam Bondi has filed an amicus brief supporting the Second Amendment challenge. The brief was filed through the DOJ's Second Amendment Unit, headed by Harmeet Dhillon. The brief argues that the three-judge panel correctly held California's ammunition background check system unconstitutional and urges the en banc court to affirm that decision.

As I see it, the DOJ's intervention is significant for several reasons. Federal courts often accord substantial weight to the views of the United States as amicus curiae, particularly in cases involving constitutional rights. Moreover, the DOJ's position signals that the executive branch views California's ammunition background check law as an outlier that exceeds the bounds of permissible firearms regulation. There is speculation that Dhillon herself may seek oral argument time to present the government's position before the en banc panel.

## The Plain Text and the Right to Ammunition

As I've analyzed it, the constitutional analysis in *Rhode v. Bonta* turns on whether California's ammunition background check law implicates the plain text of the Second Amendment. The text provides that "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed." The right to "keep" means the right to possess firearms; the right to "bear" means the right to carry firearms in public for self-defense.

As the Seventh Circuit recognized in *Ezell v. City of Chicago*, 651 F.3d 684 (7th Cir. 2011), the right to keep and bear arms necessarily encompasses the right to train with firearms. The logic is straightforward: a right to possess and carry firearms for self-defense is meaningless without the ability to achieve and maintain proficiency. And proficiency requires practice -- which, in turn, requires ammunition.

In my view, ammunition is thus constitutionally protected not merely as a commercial product but as an indispensable component of the right itself. A firearm without ammunition is inoperable -- useless for home defense, for carrying in public, or for training. Any regulation that burdens the acquisition of ammunition therefore directly implicates the plain text of the Second Amendment. California's background check requirement imposes a financial cost and a procedural barrier on every ammunition purchase, functioning as what I describe as a form of poll tax on the exercise of a fundamental right.

## The Significance of State Versus Federal Law

As I emphasize on The Four Boxes Diner, there is a pattern in the Supreme Court's Second Amendment jurisprudence that has significant implications for *Rhode v. Bonta*: the Court has been far more willing to strike down state and local firearms laws than federal statutes. In *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), the Court struck down the District of Columbia's handgun ban -- a local law. In *McDonald v. City of Chicago*, 561 U.S. 742 (2010), the Court struck down Chicago's handgun ban -- a municipal ordinance. In *Bruen*, the Court struck down New York's discretionary concealed-carry licensing regime -- a state law. In *Caetano v. Massachusetts*, 577 U.S. 411 (2016), the Court vacated Massachusetts's ban on stun guns -- another state law.

By contrast, when the Court has confronted federal firearms statutes -- such as in *United States v. Rahimi*, 602 U.S. 680 (2024), involving 18 U.S.C. section 922(g)(8) -- it has shown greater deference to Congress. As I see it, this pattern suggests that California's ammunition background check law, as a state enactment, is precisely the type of outlier regulation the Supreme Court is most inclined to invalidate, should the case eventually reach the high court.

The outlier nature of the law compounds this vulnerability. The Supreme Court has repeatedly signaled skepticism toward firearms regulations that lack analogues in other jurisdictions. In my analysis, a law that stands alone among all fifty states invites heightened scrutiny and suggests that it is not grounded in any genuine historical tradition of firearms regulation.

## The En Banc Court's Broader Authority

An important but often overlooked dimension of en banc review is the scope of the panel's authority. An en banc panel is not limited to deciding the case before it on narrow grounds. It possesses the legal authority to overrule prior circuit precedent -- including decisions of previous en banc panels. In theory, a favorable ruling in *Rhode v. Bonta* could extend beyond the ammunition background check issue and revisit or abrogate earlier Ninth Circuit decisions hostile to the Second Amendment.

Whether this particular panel will exercise that authority remains uncertain. As I see it, I consider it unlikely that the court will use *Rhode v. Bonta* as a vehicle for sweeping doctrinal revision. But the mere existence of a favorable en banc panel in the Ninth Circuit -- a court long regarded as a graveyard for Second Amendment claims -- represents a meaningful shift in the institutional landscape.

## Looking Ahead

Oral argument in *Rhode v. Bonta* is scheduled for March 25, 2025. As I see it, the case presents a strong vehicle for a Second Amendment victory at the en banc level: the law is an outlier, it has been struck down twice, the federal government supports the challengers, and the panel composition favors the Second Amendment. While no outcome is guaranteed -- and Second Amendment litigants in the Ninth Circuit have learned to temper expectations -- the constellation of factors in this case provides genuine grounds for optimism that California's ammunition background check law will not survive en banc review, in my judgment.
