## Summary

February 2026 marks the tenth anniversary of the death of Justice Antonin Scalia, whose majority opinion in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), stands as the foundational modern text of Second Amendment jurisprudence and as the defining example of originalism applied at the highest level of American constitutional adjudication. In my analysis, Heller was not merely a case about handguns in the District of Columbia — it was a methodological manifesto that transformed how courts, lawyers, and scholars understand the relationship between constitutional text, historical meaning, and the rule of law. A decade after Scalia's death, his intellectual legacy remains the indispensable framework through which Second Amendment litigation is conducted.

## Background

Justice Antonin Scalia died on February 13, 2016. He had served on the United States Supreme Court for nearly thirty years, having been appointed by President Ronald Reagan in 1986. Before joining the Court, he served on the U.S. Court of Appeals for the D.C. Circuit and taught law at the University of Chicago, where his scholarship on statutory interpretation and constitutional methodology laid the intellectual groundwork for what would become the modern originalist movement.

The opinion for which Scalia is perhaps most consequentially remembered by the Second Amendment community is his 2008 majority opinion in **District of Columbia v. Heller**. The case arose from a challenge to the District of Columbia's categorical ban on handgun possession and its requirement that lawfully owned long guns be kept unloaded and either disassembled or secured with a trigger lock — effectively rendering them unavailable for self-defense even in the home. Dick Heller, a special police officer authorized to carry a firearm on duty, applied to register a handgun he wished to keep at home for self-defense. The District refused. Heller filed suit seeking to enjoin the District's handgun ban and related restrictions. The district court dismissed. The D.C. Circuit reversed, holding that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to possess firearms. The Supreme Court granted certiorari.

## The Heller Opinion: Text, Structure, and History

Speaking from the bench when the decision was announced on June 26, 2008, Justice Scalia summarized the Court's holding: "We hold that the Second Amendment guarantees an individual right to have and use arms for self-defense in the home, and that the District's handgun ban, as well as its requirement that firearms in the home be rendered inoperative, violates that right."

That holding required the Court to work through the Second Amendment's full text: "A well regulated Militia, being necessary to the security of a free State, the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed." The amendment's structure — a prefatory clause followed by an operative clause — had long been the source of interpretive dispute. Those who argued for a purely collective or militia-based right relied heavily on the prefatory language. Scalia's opinion dispatched that argument by applying a foundational principle of construction that was well-established in 1791: a prefatory clause cannot limit the scope of the operative text. The prefatory clause announces a purpose; the operative clause secures the right. The militia purpose does not restrict the individual scope of "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms."

The opinion then proceeded through an extensive examination of the text's original public meaning — how the words of the amendment were understood by those who wrote, debated, and ratified it. Scalia analyzed the phrase "keep and bear Arms" as it was commonly used in the eighteenth century; examined the role of the right to arms in English common law, going back to the 1689 English Bill of Rights; reviewed state constitutional provisions from the founding era that confirmed the individual character of the right; and traced the historical development of the right through colonial America and into the early republic.

## Originalism as Constitutional Method

What made Heller significant beyond its holding was its methodological rigor. Justice Scalia wrote a 154-page opinion — including dissents — that demonstrated in granular detail what it means to practice originalism as a disciplined judicial methodology rather than as a rhetorical posture.

Originalism, as Scalia understood and practiced it, is the theory that the meaning of a constitutional provision is fixed at the time of its adoption, and that courts are bound to apply that original meaning rather than updating it to reflect contemporary preferences or policy goals. This is not a formalistic or mechanical exercise. It requires deep historical learning, careful analysis of linguistic usage, and serious engagement with the sources — legislative history, legal treatises, newspaper commentary, state ratification debates — that illuminate what constitutional text meant to those who made it law.

The alternative, as Scalia frequently observed, is not neutrality but simply unacknowledged policy-making by unelected judges. If constitutional meaning is not fixed by text and history, then it is determined by whatever a contemporary judicial majority believes to be wise, fair, or consistent with "evolving standards." That approach converts the constitution into an instrument of judicial will rather than a constraint upon it. The predictability, consistency, and democratic legitimacy of constitutional law depend on the commitment to original meaning that Heller exemplified.

## Heller's Lasting Doctrinal Framework

Heller established several doctrinal principles that continue to structure Second Amendment litigation. First, the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms for traditionally lawful purposes, most notably self-defense. Second, the right is not unlimited — Scalia acknowledged that certain regulations, including prohibitions on carrying in sensitive places, conditions on commercial sale, and laws disarming felons and the mentally ill, are "presumptively lawful." Third, the handgun is constitutionally protected because it is "the quintessential self-defense weapon" — the type of arm in common use by law-abiding Americans for lawful purposes.

**McDonald v. City of Chicago**, 561 U.S. 742 (2010), extended Heller's holding by incorporating the Second Amendment against state governments through the Fourteenth Amendment, making the individual right to keep and bear arms a fundamental right binding on all fifty states.

**New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), built on Heller by rejecting the means-end scrutiny framework that many lower courts had substituted for the text-and-history analysis Heller prescribed. Bruen held that the proper test for Second Amendment challenges is whether the challenged regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation in America — the very methodology Scalia pioneered in Heller.

## Implications

Justice Scalia's death in 2016 came at a moment when the Second Amendment's doctrinal future hung in the balance. Had the vacancy he left been filled immediately by a justice hostile to Heller's framework, the individual right recognized in 2008 might have been substantially narrowed or effectively abandoned. Instead, the subsequent appointments to the Supreme Court have produced a majority that has reaffirmed and extended Heller — most dramatically in Bruen.

The intellectual architecture Scalia constructed in Heller — text, original public meaning, historical tradition — is now the law of the land for Second Amendment analysis at every level of the federal judiciary. Lower courts that have resisted that framework face increasing pressure from a Supreme Court that has shown itself willing to summarily reverse decisions that depart from it.

I have reviewed and written extensively about Scalia's methodology and its application to the full range of Second Amendment issues that courts face today. What strikes me most, a decade after his death, is the extent to which the project he undertook in Heller was not merely a case about one city's gun regulations but a sustained argument about the nature of constitutional law itself. The Second Amendment's vindication in the federal courts depends in substantial measure on maintaining fidelity to that argument. Ten years on, Antonin Scalia's work in Heller remains the indispensable foundation.
