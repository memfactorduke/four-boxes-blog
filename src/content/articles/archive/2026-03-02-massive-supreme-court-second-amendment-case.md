## Summary

In my analysis, the Supreme Court's oral argument in **United States v. Hammani** — spanning approximately two hours — proved more favorable to Second Amendment interests than the case's problematic factual record might have suggested. Multiple Justices demonstrated pointed skepticism toward the government's expansive reading of 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3), and the doctrinal distinction between "unlawful user" and "addicted to" emerged as a central point of contention with substantial constitutional significance. As I have argued, the textual precision with which the Court engages this definitional question will determine whether the resulting opinion narrows § 922(g)(3)'s reach in a constitutionally meaningful way, even if the provision is ultimately upheld on its face.

## Background

**United States v. Hammani** challenges the constitutionality of 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3), which prohibits firearm possession by any person "who is an unlawful user of, or addicted to, any controlled substance as defined in section 102 of the Controlled Substances Act." The provision contains two grammatically and conceptually distinct prongs: (1) "unlawful user" status, which may be established by proof of a pattern of ongoing illegal drug use; and (2) "addicted to" status, which connotes a physiological or psychological dependency that may persist independently of recent drug use. These prongs, as I have previously examined, carry different constitutional implications under the historical-analogue framework established by **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022).

The defendant, Ali Hammani, was convicted on the basis of evidence that he habitually used marijuana and cocaine, and the government's record also contains allegations of connections to the Iranian Revolutionary Guard and involvement in drug trafficking. As I noted in my pre-argument analysis, the factual profile of this case is among the least sympathetic available for a Second Amendment challenge, and the Solicitor General's decision to seek certiorari in this vehicle rather than in a case involving a more conventional defendant raises questions about the government's constitutional strategy.

## The Justices' Engagement with the "Unlawful User" Definition

The oral argument's most consequential development, in my assessment, was the intensity with which multiple Justices engaged the question of what "unlawful user" means as a constitutional matter under *Bruen*. The critical doctrinal question — whether a person must be an active, current user at the time of firearm possession, or whether some more attenuated history of drug use suffices — was pressed from the bench with considerable specificity.

Several Justices appeared concerned that the government's reading of "unlawful user" could sweep in millions of Americans whose drug use was remote in time and context from any firearm possession, extending far beyond any plausible Founding-era historical analogue. The government's position — that habitual, ongoing use creates a status-based disqualification that persists throughout the period of habitual use — was met with skepticism across what appeared to be the ideological spectrum of the Court.

In my analysis, this line of questioning reflects a serious doctrinal concern: if "unlawful user" is read broadly enough to include anyone who used a controlled substance at any point prior to possessing a firearm, then § 922(g)(3) becomes a categorical disarmament statute of extraordinary breadth, one with no discernible Founding-era counterpart. The Court appeared attentive to this risk.

## The "Addicted to" Prong and Founding-Era Analogues

The second prong of § 922(g)(3) — "addicted to" a controlled substance — generated a distinct line of questioning that intersects with the government's historical-analogue argument. As I have analyzed, the government's most plausible Founding-era analogue is the category of persons deemed "lunatics" under colonial and early state law, who were subject to civil commitment and, as a practical matter, disarmament during periods of incapacity.

The government's analogical argument proceeds by characterizing habitual drug use and addiction as states of persistent cognitive and volitional impairment comparable to Founding-era conceptions of mental illness. The Justices appeared willing to take this analogy seriously for the "addicted to" prong, where the impairment is arguably continuous and comparable to the lunacy model. However, extending the same analogy to the "unlawful user" prong — where a person may be a periodic, non-dependent user without any discernible continuous impairment — was met with greater skepticism.

The government's fallback argument — that the disarmament is effectively voluntary, because the individual may recover Second Amendment rights by ceasing drug use — is not without force under the lunacy analogy (where restoration of rights upon recovery was the historical norm), but it has the potential to become circular: the government is arguing that the constitutional validity of mandatory disarmament is sustained by the ease with which the disarmed person could theoretically comply. The Justices' questioning suggested awareness of this tension.

## The Originalist Framework Applied

As I have consistently argued, the *Bruen* framework requires more than a superficial resemblance between modern firearms regulations and Founding-era practices. The Court must assess whether the historical analogues are sufficiently comparable in "burden" and "justification" to the challenged regulation to establish a genuine tradition of analogous restriction. A regulation that disarms a broad class of people based on a highly attenuated connection to the historical lunacy category — for example, anyone who has smoked marijuana on multiple occasions, without any evidence of impairment at the time of firearm possession — likely cannot satisfy that standard.

In my view, the most constitutionally defensible reading of § 922(g)(3) would confine it to persons who are, at the time of firearm possession, in a state of active intoxication or continuous dependency that impairs judgment in a manner comparable to Founding-era lunacy. That reading would preserve the statute's application to persons genuinely incapacitated by substance dependence while immunizing from prosecution those whose connection to drug use is remote, episodic, or historical. Whether the Court is prepared to write such a limiting construction into the statute, rather than simply upholding it facially, is the central doctrinal question that the *Hammani* decision will need to answer.

## The *Clay v. Louisiana* Collateral Development

In parallel with the *Hammani* argument, the Court's issuance of *Varial v. Texas* in the October sitting bears noting for its implications for the remaining undecided case from that sitting, *Clay v. Louisiana*. As I have analyzed, the authorship pattern suggests that Justice Alito is the likely author of the *Clay* majority opinion. *Clay* presents the question of whether Section Two of the Voting Rights Act of 1965 can constitutionally mandate race-conscious redistricting in tension with the Fourteenth Amendment's Equal Protection Clause. A ruling that curtails such mandates could affect the partisan composition of the House of Representatives by twelve to nineteen seats — a consequence with direct relevance to the legislative environment in which Second Amendment protections must be maintained and defended.

## Implications

In my assessment, the oral argument in **United States v. Hammani** demonstrated that the Court is seriously engaged with the question of § 922(g)(3)'s constitutional scope, and that a majority may be inclined to articulate meaningful limiting principles on the "unlawful user" prong even if the statute is facially upheld. The distinction the Justices drew between active, continuous use or addiction and more attenuated patterns of prior use suggests an awareness that the statute, as currently applied by the government, reaches beyond any supportable Founding-era analogue. The resulting opinion — which the Court will issue in due course — is unlikely to strike the provision in its entirety, but it may produce doctrinal boundaries that constrain prosecutorial overreach in ways that protect the Second Amendment rights of law-abiding citizens whose sole disqualification is an episodic history of marijuana use. The development of that limiting principle, however imperfect the factual vehicle through which it arrives, may represent *Hammani*'s most durable contribution to Second Amendment jurisprudence.
