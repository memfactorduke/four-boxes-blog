## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit is preparing to hear en banc oral argument in the consolidated challenge to New Jersey's ban on AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles and magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds — a case I have been covering closely and regard as one of the most consequential pending Second Amendment cases in the country. In advance of that argument, I want to provide a detailed analysis of the legal framework the Third Circuit must apply, explain why New Jersey's core arguments are inconsistent with binding Supreme Court precedent, and describe what a correct application of **District of Columbia v. Heller** and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** requires the court to do. The resolution of these cases will directly affect the Second Amendment rights of millions of Americans in New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware — and potentially far beyond.

## Background: New Jersey's Bans and the Road to En Banc Review

New Jersey has enacted two statutes at issue in this litigation. The first prohibits the possession of semi-automatic rifles that the state classifies as "assault firearms" — a category that encompasses the AR-15 platform and similar designs. The second restricts magazine capacity to ten rounds, prohibiting possession of any magazine capable of holding more than that number. Both prohibitions carry criminal penalties.

The cases were originally argued before a three-judge panel of the Third Circuit. The panel circulated a draft opinion internally — standard practice before publication — and the draft was adverse to the Second Amendment. Rather than allow that anti-Second Amendment panel opinion to issue and become binding circuit precedent, the full Third Circuit voted internally to grant en banc rehearing. That vote stopped the panel opinion from issuing and resulted in today's en banc oral argument before the court's full complement of active judges.

The significance of this procedural history cannot be overstated. The full Third Circuit made an institutional judgment that the panel's draft ruling was sufficiently important — and sufficiently contested — that the entire court, not just three judges, should decide the question. That decision creates the possibility of a precedential ruling that thoroughly applies **Heller** and **Bruen** and that commands the authority of the circuit's full membership.

## The Correct Legal Framework: Heller and What It Actually Says

The starting point for any Second Amendment analysis involving an arms ban is **Heller**, not **Bruen**. This is a point I have made in my article in the Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy, and it is often misunderstood in Second Amendment litigation. **Bruen** established the historical tradition test as the governing framework for evaluating firearms regulations generally. But when the specific question is whether a state may completely prohibit an entire category of arms, **Heller** itself provides the dispositive rule.

**Heller** defined "arms" for Second Amendment purposes broadly: anything that can be used offensively or defensively for lawful purposes. Under that definition, AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles are unambiguously arms. Magazines that enable those rifles to function — that facilitate armed self-defense by allowing the user to fire multiple rounds — are likewise arms or constitute integral components of arms. There is no serious argument that these items fall outside the constitutional text.

**Heller** then established the principle that governs arms ban cases: the government may not prohibit the possession of arms that are in common use by Americans for lawful purposes. The Court explained that the historical tradition of arms regulation in this country has permitted the banning only of weapons that are both dangerous and unusual. Weapons that are in common use — meaning they are widely owned and employed by law-abiding citizens for self-defense, hunting, sport shooting, and other lawful activities — cannot be deemed unusual, and therefore cannot be banned consistent with the Second Amendment.

**Heller** applied this rule to handguns and held that the District of Columbia could not ban them because handguns are the most popular defensive firearm in America and are in common use by law-abiding citizens. The logical extension of that holding to AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles is straightforward: there are tens of millions of these rifles in civilian hands in the United States. They are owned and used by law-abiding Americans for home defense, competition shooting, hunting, and recreation in numbers that dwarf the total ownership of many other categories of firearms. They are, by any rational measure, in common use.

## The "Dangerous and Unusual" Standard: New Jersey's Analytical Errors

New Jersey has pressed several arguments in this litigation that are, in my view, incompatible with the governing framework. Understanding why those arguments fail requires unpacking the "dangerous and unusual" formulation with some precision.

**The disjunctive reading.** New Jersey argued at various points that the government may ban arms that are "dangerous or unusual" — using "or" as a disjunctive conjunction, meaning either characteristic alone would suffice. Under this reading, any firearm could be banned simply because it is dangerous. But all firearms are dangerous. That is their essential characteristic. A standard that permits the banning of any dangerous arm would permit the banning of every arm, obliterating the Second Amendment's core protection. No serious reading of **Heller** supports this position, and it was appropriately challenged at oral argument.

**The "unusually dangerous" reformulation.** Recognizing the problem with the disjunctive reading, New Jersey attempted to reformulate the standard as "unusually dangerous" — meaning the state can ban arms that are dangerous to an unusual degree. This argument is superficially more coherent but fails on its own logic. To determine whether something is "unusually" dangerous, one must first establish what level of dangerousness is usual. The baseline for usual dangerousness in the context of commonly kept and borne arms is necessarily derived from the weapons that Americans actually own and use in large numbers. Because tens of millions of Americans own AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles, these weapons occupy the category of usual arms. Their dangerousness — however one might quantify it — is therefore the dangerousness of usual arms, not of unusual ones. New Jersey cannot satisfy the "unusually dangerous" standard by pointing to features that are characteristic of the most widely owned class of rifles in the United States.

**The conjunctive standard from Heller.** The correct formulation, as **Heller** stated and as I argued in the Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy, is "dangerous and unusual" — both conditions must be satisfied. An arm that is widely owned and in common use cannot be unusual by definition. Because AR-15-style rifles are in common use, they fail the "unusual" prong of the conjunctive test, and the state's ability to ban them is categorically foreclosed regardless of any dangerousness argument the state advances.

## The Burden of Proof Question

A critically important question in this litigation — one with implications for every future arms ban case — is who bears the burden of proof. New Jersey argued that the burden should fall on the Second Amendment challengers to demonstrate that the banned arms are not dangerous, or alternatively that they are in common use. This argument inverts the constitutional allocation of burdens in a manner that is inconsistent with **Heller** and **Bruen**.

The correct framework operates as follows. The Second Amendment protects the right to keep and bear arms. When a state enacts a categorical prohibition on a class of arms, that prohibition presumptively infringes on a constitutionally protected right. Under **Bruen**, the government must then justify the prohibition by identifying a historical tradition of analogous regulation from the founding era or the Reconstruction era. The burden is the government's. It is not the obligation of the gun owner to prove the absence of such a tradition; it is the government's obligation to prove its presence.

This burden allocation reflects the basic constitutional structure of rights protection in America. When the government restricts speech, it bears the burden of justifying that restriction under the applicable First Amendment standard. The same principle applies to the Second Amendment after **Bruen**. States that seek to prohibit classes of arms bear the burden of establishing that such prohibitions have historical grounding — and that burden is substantial, because the historical record does not support broad prohibitions on weapons in common civilian use.

New Jersey attempted to introduce historical analogues at oral argument, but those analogues — drawn from laws regulating specific dangerous or unusual weapons historically, or from common law restrictions on affright — do not map onto a categorical ban on the most commonly owned class of rifle in the modern United States. A historical tradition of banning weapons that posed unusual dangers in their specific context does not establish a tradition of banning weapons that are in standard civilian use.

## What Correct Application of Heller Requires

A correct application of **Heller** and **Bruen** to this case compels a ruling in favor of the Second Amendment challengers. The analytical steps are straightforward:

First, AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines are arms within the meaning of the Second Amendment — they are designed and used for offensive and defensive purposes and fall within the constitutional text.

Second, these arms are in common use by tens of millions of law-abiding Americans for lawful purposes including self-defense, hunting, and competitive shooting. They are therefore not unusual under **Heller**'s framework.

Third, because they are in common use and therefore not unusual, they cannot be banned under the "dangerous and unusual" test regardless of any dangerousness argument the state advances.

Fourth, the government bears the burden of establishing a historical tradition of analogous prohibition, and it cannot carry that burden because no such tradition exists for weapons in widespread civilian use.

The Third Circuit should rule that New Jersey's ban on AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles and its restriction on magazine capacity violate the Second Amendment.

## Implications

The stakes in this case are significant. New Jersey has some of the most aggressive gun control laws in the nation. A ruling striking down the AR-15 ban and the magazine restriction would directly affect millions of New Jersey residents who are currently prohibited from possessing some of the most commonly owned firearms and accessories in America. It would also create binding precedent for the Third Circuit covering Delaware and Pennsylvania, and would add significant appellate authority to the growing body of post-**Bruen** decisions striking down similar laws in other circuits.

More broadly, this case will test whether the Third Circuit can be trusted to faithfully apply **Heller** and **Bruen** now that its composition has changed. The participation of Judge Jennifer Mascot — who brings originalist Second Amendment expertise from her academic career and her clerkships with Justices Thomas and Kavanaugh — provides genuine reason for optimism that the court will approach these questions with the fidelity to text and history that the Constitution requires.
