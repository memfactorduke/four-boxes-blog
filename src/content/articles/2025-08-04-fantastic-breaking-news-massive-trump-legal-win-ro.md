## Summary

On August 5, 2025, a federal district court judge in the Eastern District of Texas issued a sixty-one-page opinion allowing a major antitrust lawsuit — brought by the state of Texas and more than a dozen red-state attorneys general — against BlackRock, Inc., Vanguard Group, and State Street Corporation to proceed. The lawsuit alleges that these three Wall Street investment giants, which together exercise proxy voting authority over trillions of dollars in index fund assets, engaged in concerted anticompetitive conduct — specifically by coordinating their use of shareholder proxy votes to suppress the output and profitability of American coal companies in furtherance of a shared ESG agenda. The court's ruling that the complaint states a viable antitrust claim under the Sherman Act is among the most consequential legal developments of 2025 for the Second Amendment community, for reasons that are not immediately obvious but are deeply significant once understood: if the antitrust theory succeeds, it will provide a powerful legal weapon to protect the firearms industry from the same ESG-driven financial strangulation that has been applied to the coal sector and that BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street would almost certainly apply to gun companies given the opportunity.

## Background

### The Index Fund Structure and Proxy Power

Over the past two decades, index funds have become the dominant vehicle for individual and institutional investment in American equity markets. An index fund is a passively managed investment product that tracks a specified market index — the S&P 500, the Nasdaq-100, or a sector-specific benchmark — by holding proportional positions in all of the constituent securities. Index funds are administered by asset managers, and the three largest administrators in this space — BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street — collectively hold or manage an astonishing proportion of the shares in virtually every major publicly traded American company.

When an investor purchases shares in a BlackRock or Vanguard index fund, that investor holds a beneficial interest in the fund, but the legal title to the underlying shares — and the voting rights attendant to those shares — rests with the asset manager. This legal structure gives BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street the ability to cast proxy votes at the annual shareholder meetings of thousands of American corporations on behalf of the tens of millions of ordinary investors whose retirement accounts, pension funds, and personal savings are invested in those funds. The magnitude of this proxy power is extraordinary: on any given shareholder vote at a major American corporation, the combined proxy votes controlled by these three institutions may represent twenty, twenty-five, or thirty percent of the total shares outstanding.

### The ESG Agenda and the Coal Industry

The lawsuit alleges that BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street did not use this proxy power in the financial interests of the investors whose shares they controlled. Instead, the complaint alleges, these institutions deployed their coordinated proxy power to advance an ESG — Environmental, Social, and Governance — agenda that prioritized suppressing the production and profitability of American coal companies, even at the expense of the financial returns to which the underlying investors were entitled.

Specifically, the allegations focus on the use of coordinated proxy votes — cast by the three institutions in concert across their collective holdings in coal company shares — to restrict capital expenditures, limit production expansion, and reduce shareholder returns in furtherance of a shared goal of reducing American coal output. The theory is that this coordination, conducted across competing asset managers with combined control over a significant fraction of coal company shares, constitutes horizontal price-fixing or output restriction under the Sherman Act — the classic form of antitrust violation.

### Why This Matters for the Gun Industry

The connection to the Second Amendment and the firearms industry may not be immediately apparent. I want to explain it clearly.

The same three companies — BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street — that own substantial equity positions in coal companies also own substantial equity positions in the publicly traded firearms and defense companies that manufacture and sell firearms to civilians. These include companies like American Outdoor Brands, Vista Outdoor, Smith & Wesson's parent company, and others whose shares trade on American stock exchanges. These companies, their management teams, their boards of directors, and their ability to raise capital are all subject to the same proxy voting pressure that the lawsuit alleges was weaponized against the coal industry.

The firearms industry has already experienced a version of this: major financial institutions have denied banking services, credit card processing, and capital access to gun companies based on their business. The ESG framework that animates these decisions treats lawful firearms manufacturers as reputationally radioactive from the perspective of asset managers committed to "responsible investment" principles. If BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street are permitted — without legal consequence — to coordinate their proxy votes to suppress the coal industry, nothing prevents them from applying the same coordinated pressure to the gun industry. Proxy votes can be used to replace pro-Second Amendment board members with gun-hostile directors, to restrict capital expenditures that would expand production, to reduce compensation for executives who resist ESG demands, and ultimately to drive firearms companies into financial distress.

## The Legal Theory and the Court's Ruling

The antitrust theory advanced by the state attorneys general is grounded in the Sherman Act, which prohibits contracts, combinations, and conspiracies in restraint of trade. The complaint alleges that BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street — ostensibly competing asset managers — effectively coordinated their proxy voting strategies through participation in ESG-related investor coalitions and informal communications, producing the functional equivalent of a conspiracy to suppress output in the coal sector.

The sixty-one-page opinion by the Eastern District of Texas judge does not adjudicate the merits of this theory. What the court did was to hold that the complaint adequately pleads a claim under the Sherman Act that is plausible on its face — the minimum required to survive a motion to dismiss. This is a significant legal determination. The court found that the complaint's allegations, if proven at trial, would constitute actionable antitrust violations. The case now proceeds to discovery, where the attorneys general will have the opportunity to obtain documents, communications, and testimony that may shed light on the extent to which BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street coordinated their conduct.

The court's sixty-one-page opinion is itself a legal road map. A thoughtful, thorough federal judge has now identified the legal theory, the factual predicate, and the analytical framework under which these companies may be held liable for coordinated ESG-driven suppression of American industry. That road map is available not only to state attorneys general but to private plaintiffs — including firearms industry participants — who may have been damaged by similar ESG coordination directed at the gun sector.

## Implications

The antitrust case against BlackRock, Vanguard, and State Street is potentially one of the most consequential legal proceedings of the Trump era for the Second Amendment community.

First, if the case proceeds to trial and produces a verdict or settlement holding these institutions liable for ESG-driven antitrust violations in the coal sector, it will establish a legal precedent and a practical deterrent that substantially reduces the likelihood that the same institutions will deploy coordinated proxy power against the firearms industry. Antitrust liability is not a cost that even the world's largest asset managers can easily absorb, and the deterrence value of a successful Sherman Act verdict extends far beyond the specific defendants and the specific industry involved.

Second, the discovery process in this litigation may reveal the full extent of coordination among ESG-committed institutional investors — coordination that has been conducted through private communications, coalition memberships, and informal networks largely invisible to outside observers. If that coordination is documented and disclosed, it will provide the factual foundation for parallel antitrust actions covering other industries, including firearms.

Third, and most broadly, this case represents the application of established American legal principles — the antitrust laws that have protected competitive markets since 1890 — to a genuinely new form of market manipulation. The proposition that powerful financial institutions may not conspire to restrict output in lawful American industries, regardless of the ideological justification they offer for doing so, is both legally sound and practically essential for the preservation of a free economy that includes a robust and financially viable American firearms industry.
