## Summary

The Second Amendment community erupted this week over a statement by a Department of Justice trial attorney in the Northern District of Texas indicating that, as of April 8, 2026, the Trump administration is maintaining the Biden-era definition of what constitutes a firearm frame or receiver. The reaction online has been sharp — accusations of betrayal, suggestions that the Trump DOJ is perpetuating Biden's so-called "ghost gun" regime indefinitely.

I want to pump the brakes. What actually happened here is far more mundane than the social media furor suggests, and understanding it requires grasping both the procedural posture of the pending *Vandertok* litigation and the basic administrative law principle that governs rulemaking when a federal agency lacks a Senate-confirmed director. This is not a hill worth dying on — not yet, anyway. Let me explain why.

## The Vandertok Litigation and Its Current Posture

The *Vandertok* case involves an Administrative Procedure Act challenge to ATF's final rule redefining what qualifies as a "frame or receiver" under federal firearms statutes. The Supreme Court resolved a narrow slice of the dispute, holding that the rule did not violate the APA's statutory or procedural requirements in the specific respects before it. What the Court emphatically did not do was bless the entirety of the rule or foreclose further challenges. Significant portions of the underlying litigation remain live.

After the Supreme Court's decision, the case was remanded and is now pending before Judge Reed O'Connor in the Northern District of Texas. Because the Trump administration signaled early on that it might change or revoke the Biden-era rule — which would render the litigation moot under Article III's case-or-controversy requirement — the parties agreed to stay proceedings. Federal courts lack jurisdiction over hypothetical disputes; if there is no live rule to challenge, there is no justiciable controversy.

## The April 2 Joint Status Report

On April 2, 2026, the parties filed a joint status report in response to Judge O'Connor's March 19 order requesting an update on where things stand. That report contained a significant disclosure: ATF had advised that it "plans to take agency action to amend the challenged rule." On that basis, most parties agreed to continue the stay for another 90 days while the rulemaking process unfolds.

There was one critical exception. The Second Amendment Foundation and Defense Distributed declined to wait. They want to press forward to summary judgment now — to litigate and win on the merits rather than sit on their hands while the administrative process runs its course. That is a perfectly defensible strategic choice. There are arguments on both sides, and reasonable lawyers can disagree about whether pressing forward or awaiting the regulatory change is the smarter play. But the point is: the litigation is not dead, and two significant plaintiffs are actively pushing to resolve it.

## What the April 8 Statement Actually Said — and What It Didn't

Because the parties had agreed to file a proposed summary judgment briefing schedule by April 9, 2026, a DOJ trial attorney sent the parties an email on April 8 providing a factual update. The substance of the statement was this: as of April 8, 2026, the government has decided to maintain the current definition of firearm frame or receiver and is not making changes at this time.

That statement was factually accurate as of the date it was made. It is a litigation update — the kind of thing lawyers say to each other when a court is demanding to know the current posture of a case before setting a briefing schedule. It is not a permanent policy pronouncement. It is not a statement from the ATF Director, the Attorney General, or the White House. It is a low-level trial attorney describing the status quo as of a specific date, for the specific purpose of scheduling motion practice in a pending federal case.

Reading that email as a definitive signal that the Trump administration has abandoned any intent to revise the frame-or-receiver rule is a category error. Litigation status updates are not regulatory policy.

## The Real Reason: No Confirmed ATF Director

Here is what the social media commentary has almost universally overlooked: there is a sound — and longstanding — administrative law reason why ATF is not moving on rulemaking right now. ATF does not have a Senate-confirmed director.

Robert Sakata was nominated to lead ATF in January 2026. His confirmation hearing before the Senate occurred in February. By all indications, he is on track to be confirmed. But the Senate has not yet acted, and until it does, Sakata is not the official agency head for purposes of federal administrative law.

This matters because of a clear executive order issued by President Trump on January 20, 2025, at the very outset of his administration. That order directed all executive departments and agencies not to propose or issue any rule until a department or agency head appointed or designated by the president reviews and approves it. The order was not invented by Trump — it mirrors directives issued by Barack Obama, George W. Bush, and other presidents. It reflects a basic principle of administrative governance: agencies should not be promulgating binding rules without confirmed leadership accountable to the Senate and the president.

The ATF, as an executive agency, is bound by that directive. Without a confirmed director, it is functionally prohibited from initiating new rulemaking — including any revision to the frame-or-receiver definition. That is not a failure of political will. It is the standard operating procedure of the administrative state, and it is the Trump administration's own order being followed.

## What This Means for the Frame-or-Receiver Rule

The logical inference — and I want to be clear that I have no inside information here — is that ATF is waiting for Sakata's confirmation before it moves on the frame-or-receiver rule. Once he is confirmed and seated as director, the executive order's hold on new rulemaking is lifted, and there is nothing structurally preventing ATF from initiating a proceeding to amend or rescind the Biden-era definition.

That may happen quickly, or it may not. But the current posture reflects institutional procedure, not policy surrender. The Senate needs to act. Once it does, we will have a clearer picture of where the Trump ATF intends to take this.

Meanwhile, the *Vandertok* litigation continues. SAF and Defense Distributed are pressing forward on the merits. The case is very much alive. And there are legal arguments yet to be adjudicated that the Second Amendment community has every reason to pursue.

## Calibrating the Response

I understand the frustration. The Second Amendment community has waited a long time for regulatory relief on the frame-or-receiver question, and any signal — however ambiguous — that relief may be delayed reads as a setback. But the appropriate response to ambiguous signals is careful analysis, not alarm.

A single factual statement by a low-level trial lawyer negotiating a scheduling order in Texas is not a permanent policy declaration — and treating it as one does the Second Amendment community no favors. It redirects energy toward a fight that isn't ripe, potentially alienates allies in the administration, and obscures what is actually a more complicated and still-developing situation.

Watch what happens when Sakata is confirmed. Watch whether ATF initiates rulemaking. Watch how the summary judgment briefing in *Vandertok* unfolds. Those are the metrics that matter. Right now, all we have is a scheduling update.
