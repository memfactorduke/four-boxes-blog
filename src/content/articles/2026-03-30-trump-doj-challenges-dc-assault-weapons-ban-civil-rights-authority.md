## Summary

The Trump Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi, has filed a brief opposing the District of Columbia's motion to dismiss a federal lawsuit challenging D.C.'s ban on semi-automatic rifles, including AR-15s and AK-47-pattern firearms. The DOJ argues that 34 U.S.C. Section 12601, the federal pattern-or-practice statute, authorizes the Department to sue local governments whose law enforcement officers systematically deprive persons of constitutional rights -- including the Second Amendment right to keep and bear arms. The brief contends that D.C.'s assault weapons ban is unconstitutional and that its routine enforcement constitutes actionable law enforcement misconduct under the statute.

## Background

The District of Columbia maintains a prohibition on the registration and possession of semi-automatic rifles classified as "assault weapons," including AR-15s and AK-47-pattern firearms. Under D.C. law, residents who attempt to register such weapons are denied, and individuals found in possession of a banned firearm face criminal penalties including incarceration of up to one year.

The Trump Administration's Department of Justice, led by Attorney General Pam Bondi and with Harlan Dillon heading the DOJ's Office of Civil Rights, filed suit against the District of Columbia arguing that this ban violates the Second Amendment. Rather than proceeding under a conventional constitutional challenge brought by individual plaintiffs, the DOJ invoked 34 U.S.C. Section 12601, a federal statute that makes it unlawful for any governmental authority -- or its agents -- to engage in a pattern or practice of law enforcement conduct that deprives persons of rights secured by the United States Constitution. This novel approach, as I view it, represents an innovative use of existing federal authority to advance Second Amendment protections.

The District of Columbia responded with a motion to dismiss, contending that the DOJ lacks statutory authority to bring this type of claim. D.C.'s central argument was that Section 12601 was intended to address police misconduct issues such as excessive force and Fourth Amendment violations, not to challenge firearms regulations under the Second Amendment. The DOJ's newly filed brief directly rebuts that position.

## The DOJ's Opposition Brief

### The Statutory Framework

The DOJ's brief centers on the plain text of 34 U.S.C. Section 12601, which provides in pertinent part:

> It shall be unlawful for any governmental authority, or any agent thereof, or any person acting on behalf of a governmental authority, to engage in a pattern or practice of conduct by law enforcement officers that deprives persons of rights, privileges, or immunities secured or protected by the Constitution or the laws of the United States.

The DOJ argues that the statute's reach is not limited to any particular constitutional provision. By its terms, Section 12601 protects all rights "secured or protected by the Constitution." The Second Amendment, as the Supreme Court has repeatedly held, secures an individual right to keep and bear arms. Thus, the DOJ contends, a law enforcement pattern of enforcing an unconstitutional firearms ban falls squarely within the statute's scope.

### Undisputed Elements

The DOJ's brief identifies four factual predicates that D.C. does not contest:

1. The District of Columbia is a governmental authority within the meaning of the statute.
2. The Chief of Police and the Metropolitan Police Department are agents of the District.
3. The Chief and MPD act on behalf of the District when enforcing its laws.
4. D.C. law enforcement officers routinely enforce the assault weapons ban.

Given these undisputed facts, the DOJ argues that the only remaining question is whether the assault weapons ban is unconstitutional. If it is, then its systematic enforcement necessarily constitutes a pattern or practice of conduct depriving persons of their constitutional rights.

### D.C.'s Attempt to Narrow the Statute

The brief characterizes D.C.'s position as an effort to read a limitation into Section 12601 that does not exist in the statutory text. The DOJ writes that D.C. and its amici "take umbrage with the fact that Section 12601 protects the fundamental constitutional right to keep and bear arms from law enforcement misconduct." Their argument, the DOJ contends, amounts to a "desire to limit the statute's protection to exclude a right that they disfavor," which "is not a valid ground to dismiss the United States' complaint."

Historically, the Department of Justice has deployed Section 12601 in contexts involving police use of excessive force, racial discrimination in policing, and similar Fourth and Fourteenth Amendment concerns. The Trump DOJ's application of the same statute to Second Amendment violations represents a novel and potentially consequential expansion of the provision's operational use -- though, as the brief emphasizes, the statutory text supports no distinction among constitutional rights.

## The Second Amendment Merits

While the threshold question before the court is whether the DOJ has statutory authority to bring the case, the brief also previews the merits of the underlying Second Amendment claim.

### AR-15s as Protected "Arms"

The DOJ argues that semi-automatic rifles are plainly "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment. Drawing on *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), the brief notes that the Supreme Court defined "arms" to encompass "all instruments that constitute bearable arms, even those that were not in existence at the time of the founding." AR-15-pattern rifles, the DOJ contends, "fall squarely within this category."

### The Common-Use Test

The brief invokes the common-use framework established in *Heller* and left undisturbed by subsequent decisions in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), and *United States v. Rahimi*, 602 U.S. 680 (2024). Under this test, the Second Amendment protects weapons "in common use" by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes. With an estimated 24 million or more AR-15-style rifles in civilian hands in the United States, the DOJ argues these firearms readily satisfy the common-use threshold.

### Smith & Wesson v. Mexico

The brief cites *Smith & Wesson Brands, Inc. v. Estados Unidos Mexicanos*, 602 U.S. 515 (2024), in which Justice Kagan, writing for the Court, observed that AR-15 rifles and AK-47 rifles are "both widely legal and bought by many ordinary consumers." The DOJ deploys this language to underscore that even justices who have not been associated with expansive Second Amendment interpretations have acknowledged the lawful prevalence of these firearms. The statement that "manufacturers cannot be charged with assisting in criminal acts just because Mexican cartel members like those guns, too" reinforces the premise that AR-15s are ordinary consumer products, not weapons whose civilian possession can be categorically prohibited.

### Justice Kavanaugh's Heller II Dissent

The brief also draws on then-Judge Brett Kavanaugh's dissent in *Heller v. District of Columbia* (Heller II) at the D.C. Circuit level. In that opinion, Judge Kavanaugh employed a text, history, and tradition methodology -- anticipating the analytical framework the Supreme Court would later adopt in *Bruen* -- and concluded that semi-automatic rifles such as the AR-15 are in common use and therefore protected by the Second Amendment. The DOJ quotes his observation that it is "analytically difficult to distinguish the AR-15s at issue here from the handguns at issue in *Heller*," since both are semi-automatic and both are used by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes including self-defense in the home.

## Broader Implications

The DOJ's litigation strategy in this case carries significance well beyond the District of Columbia.

**A new enforcement paradigm.** If the court permits this action to proceed, it would establish that the federal government can use pattern-or-practice authority to challenge state and local firearms laws that violate the Second Amendment. This would give the DOJ a powerful tool to intervene against jurisdictions that maintain laws inconsistent with *Heller*, *Bruen*, and their progeny -- without requiring individual plaintiffs to bear the burden and expense of constitutional litigation. I view this as a potentially transformative development in Second Amendment enforcement.

**Ripple effects on assault weapons bans.** Several states and municipalities maintain bans on semi-automatic rifles similar to D.C.'s prohibition. A ruling on the merits that AR-15-pattern rifles are constitutionally protected under the common-use test would provide significant persuasive -- and potentially controlling -- authority for challenges to those bans. The DOJ's arguments in this brief, drawing on Supreme Court precedent from *Heller* through *Smith & Wesson v. Mexico*, lay a comprehensive foundation for such a holding, and I anticipate these arguments will prove decisive.

**Second Amendment parity.** The DOJ's core statutory argument -- that Section 12601 protects all constitutional rights without distinction -- advances the broader principle that Second Amendment rights are entitled to the same enforcement mechanisms and institutional support as other fundamental rights. This reflects the Supreme Court's admonition in *Bruen* that the Second Amendment is not "a second-class right" subject to "an entirely different body of rules." This is a position I have long advocated through my analysis on The Four Boxes Diner.

## What Comes Next

The District of Columbia's motion to dismiss is now fully briefed and awaits a ruling from the trial court. If the motion is denied, the case will proceed to discovery and ultimately a determination on the merits of whether D.C.'s assault weapons ban violates the Second Amendment. That merits determination, conducted under the text, history, and tradition framework mandated by *Bruen*, could produce a landmark ruling on the constitutional status of semi-automatic rifle bans -- an issue that remains unresolved at the Supreme Court level.
