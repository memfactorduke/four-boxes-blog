## Summary

In my analysis, the Hughes Amendment to the Firearms Owners Protection Act of 1986 — codified at 18 U.S.C. § 922(o) — presents two distinct but related constitutional vulnerabilities: a textual flaw in the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives' administrative interpretation of the statute's governmental exception, and a deeper structural defect in Congress's authority to enact the prohibition in the first instance. I had the opportunity to discuss both vulnerabilities at length with Stephen Halbrook, the preeminent scholar and litigator in Second Amendment law, whose work was cited by Justice Scalia in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and who personally litigated the statutory interpretation challenge to § 922(o) before the Eleventh Circuit in the late 1980s and early 1990s. His analysis, both of the statute's procedural origins and its legal defects, warrants careful examination.

## Background

The Firearms Owners Protection Act of 1986 (FOPA) was, at its core, a remedial statute — a legislative correction of documented ATF overreach that had been the subject of congressional hearings and widespread concern among firearms industry participants and civil liberties advocates. The Senate passed FOPA without the Hughes Amendment; the machine gun prohibition was introduced on the floor of the House on the second day of debate, at the close of deliberations, by Representative Bill Hughes of New Jersey. What Halbrook described as "chaos" ensued: debate was cut off, a voice vote was taken with no recorded tally, and the amendment was incorporated into a bill that President Reagan signed into law, despite the amendment's eleventh-hour and procedurally anomalous insertion.

The restriction did not take immediate effect, affording a roughly three-month window during which manufacturers produced a significant volume of machine guns for registration under the National Firearms Act of 1934 (NFA). Those pre-1986 registered machine guns remain legal to transfer; the effect of § 922(o) is to freeze the civilian supply, which has caused registered NFA machine gun values to appreciate dramatically — creating a market anomaly directly attributable to the registration cutoff.

## The ATF's Reinterpretation of the Governmental Exception

Section 922(o)'s prohibition is not absolute on its face. The statute provides that it "does not apply to the transfer to or by, or possession under the authority of, the Federal Government or a State or a political subdivision thereof." The textual question — what does "under the authority of" mean — proved central to Halbrook's subsequent litigation.

As I have examined, the ATF's position is that "under the authority of" should be read to mean "for the benefit of" a governmental entity. Those four words — "for the benefit of" — appear nowhere in the statute. Congress did not vote on them. No legislative record supports their insertion. The ATF derived this alternative phrasing from its own regulatory preferences and imposed it on the text in a manner that directly contravenes the cardinal rule of statutory interpretation: courts give effect to the words Congress actually chose, not to words an agency wishes Congress had chosen. Justice Scalia's textualist methodology — applied consistently across his jurisprudence and codified in his treatise *Reading Law: The Interpretation of Legal Texts* — would categorically reject such an interpretive maneuver.

In the litigation Halbrook pursued on behalf of his client JD Jones, Jones had submitted an application to manufacture a machine gun under the NFA — the regulatory pathway that had governed machine gun transfers for over five decades. The ATF denied the application on the ground that the manufacture was not "for the benefit of" a governmental unit, even though the application itself was filed with, and subject to approval by, the ATF Director. As I see it, the irony is significant: an act requiring federal regulatory approval and oversight is, by ordinary linguistic usage, an act performed "under the authority of" the federal government. The Northern District of Georgia initially recognized as much, agreeing with Halbrook's interpretation. The Eleventh Circuit, however, reversed, accepting the government's textual addition and leaving § 922(o) with its current reach.

## The Principle of Non-Repeal by Implication

A second interpretive flaw compounds the ATF's position. Federal law has long recognized the canon against implied repeal: absent a clear expression of congressional intent, later statutes are not read to repeal earlier ones by implication. The NFA, enacted in 1934, established an affirmative regulatory framework under which machine guns could be registered, taxed, and transferred with ATF approval. That framework was not expressly repealed by FOPA. A proper reading of § 922(o), consistent with this canon, would interpret the later statute in harmony with the NFA's existing registration provisions rather than as an implicit elimination of the entire civilian machine gun market.

As I have reviewed the statutory structure, 18 U.S.C. § 925(a)(1) — enacted prior to § 922(o) — already provides categorical exceptions to federal firearms restrictions for governmental use. Interpreting § 922(o)'s governmental exception to require a "benefit of government" showing would render § 925(a)(1)'s governmental exception at least partially redundant. Statutory interpretation principles disfavor redundancy, lending further support to the reading that "under the authority of" in § 922(o) means governmental authorization — not governmental benefit.

## The Constitutional Defect: Congressional Power

Beyond the statutory interpretation questions lies a structural constitutional problem. As I have argued, § 922(o) presents a meaningful challenge under Congress's enumerated powers. The statute identifies no commerce clause predicate on its face; it contains no jurisdictional element requiring a connection to interstate commerce, and its legislative history does not establish the kind of substantial effects showing that *United States v. Lopez*, 514 U.S. 549 (1995), demands. The NFA, by contrast, was enacted under the taxing power and contains explicit taxing provisions — a constitutional foundation that § 922(o) conspicuously lacks.

Halbrook noted that ATF Director Higgins testified before Congress in 1985 that NFA-registered machine guns had not presented a criminal enforcement problem. If the NFA's existing registration, taxation, and accountability framework had already achieved whatever public safety objectives Congress might have identified, the Hughes Amendment's additional prohibition serves no marginal safety purpose. An enactment that lacks both a clear Article I foundation and any demonstrable public safety rationale is particularly vulnerable to constitutional challenge — and, in my analysis, the Second Amendment independently forecloses the outcome even if some Article I hook could be identified, given the broad protection *Heller* and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), afford to arms in common use.

## State Legislative Initiatives

The constitutional uncertainty surrounding § 922(o) has prompted legislative activity at the state level. West Virginia and Kentucky have each considered mechanisms that would facilitate, within their respective jurisdictions and within the NFA's regulatory framework, access to fully automatic firearms for qualified citizens. These initiatives proceed on two premises: first, that if § 922(o) is unconstitutional, the restriction it imposes is void and the NFA framework alone governs; and second, that states, exercising their retained police powers, may be positioned to establish parallel regulatory frameworks that provide the governmental authorization that could satisfy even the ATF's "for the benefit of" construction.

As I see it, these legislative efforts represent a strategically sound approach to forcing the constitutional question through channels that create concrete, litigable records — a methodology that has proven effective in Second Amendment litigation since *Heller*.

## The Path Forward

The fundamental problem Halbrook identified — an administrative agency adding words to a statute that Congress never enacted, applied to accomplish a firearms restriction that implicates both structural constitutional limits on congressional power and the substantive protections of the Second Amendment — remains unresolved. The Eleventh Circuit's acceptance of the ATF's interpretive addition has never been directly reconsidered in the post-*Bruen* framework, which now requires courts to assess firearms regulations against the nation's historical tradition rather than deferring to governmental assertions of purpose.

In my view, the most promising avenue for future litigation combines the textual statutory argument — courts must apply the words Congress chose, not words agencies prefer — with a *Bruen*-framework challenge establishing that no historical tradition supports a categorical ban on civilian ownership of a class of arms in common use. The two arguments are complementary: the statutory argument, if accepted, resolves the case without reaching the constitutional question; the constitutional argument, if necessary, applies the framework the Supreme Court has now established. Either path leads to the same destination — a recognition that § 922(o) as interpreted and enforced exceeds what the law and the Constitution permit.

## Implications

Halbrook's decades of scholarship and litigation on Second Amendment jurisprudence have shaped constitutional doctrine in ways that will continue to reverberate. His analysis of the Hughes Amendment — grounded in textual fidelity, structural constitutional principles, and the originalist methodology the Supreme Court has now adopted as governing law — identifies vulnerabilities that remain available for future litigants. The registration freeze imposed by § 922(o) has created a civilian machine gun market defined by artificial scarcity, not by any genuine public safety rationale. Whether through renewed direct challenges to the statute's constitutionality or through state-level initiatives that force the question in new litigation postures, the legal landscape for challenging the Hughes Amendment has improved considerably since the Eleventh Circuit last addressed the question. The analytical framework Halbrook constructed — and that I have examined here — provides the foundation for that renewed challenge.
