## Summary

Virginia's new Democratic legislative majority, operating under a governor with an intelligence agency background, has advanced legislation that would ban the sale, transfer, manufacture, and importation of so-called "assault weapons"—a category that encompasses ordinary semi-automatic centerfire rifles, most prominently the AR-15 and its variants. In my analysis, this legislation is unconstitutional on its face under both **District of Columbia v. Heller** and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**: these are firearms in common use for lawful purposes, and there is no founding-era historical tradition of prohibiting the civilian possession of the militia-relevant arms of the age. The passage of this legislation through the Virginia House should be understood as the opening act of a sustained constitutional confrontation.

## Background

Virginia, under Governor Abigail Spanberger and a Democratic legislative majority, is pursuing one of the most ambitious state-level gun control agendas in the country. The centerpiece of the current session's legislative push is a comprehensive assault weapons prohibition, with the bill's Senate analog sponsored by State Senator Saddam Salem of the 37th District—Fairfax County—and co-sponsored by State Delegate JJ Singh, among others. The bill would ban, effective July 1st, the importation, sale, manufacture, and transfer of assault firearms and large-capacity ammunition feeding devices throughout the Commonwealth.

Under the bill's operative definition, an "assault firearm" is essentially a semi-automatic centerfire rifle capable of accepting a detachable magazine holding more than 15 rounds. This definition is specifically crafted to encompass the AR-15 and similar firearms—the most popular rifle platform in the United States, with an estimated 24 to 30 million units in civilian hands.

## The Constitutional Framework

The analysis under current Supreme Court doctrine begins with **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), which established two propositions directly relevant here. First, the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms for self-defense, untethered from militia service. Second, and critically, the government may not prohibit the possession of firearms that are "in common use at the time" for lawful purposes. **Heller** drew this "common use" standard from the Court's 1939 decision in **United States v. Miller**, 307 U.S. 174 (1939), which had suggested that the Second Amendment's protection extends to arms bearing a reasonable relationship to the preservation or efficiency of a well-regulated militia.

Semi-automatic centerfire rifles, and the AR-15 platform in particular, are unambiguously "in common use" in the relevant sense. They are the most commonly sold rifle category in America, owned by tens of millions of law-abiding citizens for self-defense, hunting, recreational target shooting, and home protection. A firearm this widely owned and used does not become constitutionally unprotected merely because legislators append to it the loaded label "assault weapon."

**New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), adds the text-and-history requirement: when the government seeks to restrict conduct protected by the Second Amendment's plain text, it must demonstrate that the restriction is "consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation." The burden falls on the government to produce founding-era historical analogues—laws enacted around the time of the Second Amendment's ratification that imposed comparable burdens for comparable reasons.

Virginia cannot meet this burden. There is no founding-era historical tradition of prohibiting civilian possession of the militia-relevant long arms of the day. Quite the opposite: the founding generation was deeply invested in widespread civilian ownership of effective firearms, precisely because those arms were essential to the militia system that the Second Amendment was designed to protect. The muskets and rifles of the founding era were the military-grade weapons of their time. To suggest that the founding generation would have prohibited civilian ownership of militarily useful long arms is to invert the entire constitutional logic of the Second Amendment.

## The Legislative Record: What the Sponsors Reveal About Their Intentions

In my analysis, the legislative record surrounding Virginia's assault weapons legislation is as constitutionally significant as the statutory text itself. The legislation's sponsors have been notably, systematically silent about the Second Amendment, the Constitution's text, and the founding-era tradition that **Bruen** requires courts to consult. The legislative justification offered is entirely framed in terms of "gun violence" as a public health crisis requiring emergency intervention.

State Delegate Dan Helmer, speaking in support of the bill on the House floor, advanced claims about the weapons' danger without engaging the constitutional framework. State Senator Salem's press release characterizes gun violence as an "epidemic" while making no reference to the constitutional dimension of what his legislation proposes.

This rhetorical pattern is not accidental. Legislators who know their bill cannot survive constitutional scrutiny tend to avoid constitutional discussion. They rely instead on the institutional dynamics of the Fourth Circuit—a court that has historically been willing to uphold firearms restrictions that cannot withstand rigorous **Bruen** analysis—to give their legislation a longer life than it deserves.

## The Fourth Circuit Problem

Virginia sits within the Fourth Circuit's jurisdiction. I have previously analyzed the Fourth Circuit's track record on Second Amendment claims, and it is not encouraging for rights-protective outcomes. Senior Judge J. Harvie Wilkinson III, a Reagan appointee who has functioned as the intellectual center of gravity on the circuit for decades, has written opinions and made statements at oral argument that reflect deep skepticism of Second Amendment claims. His comments at argument in prior assault weapons cases—including claims about the physical destructiveness of certain firearms that are, charitably, not supported by the evidentiary record—illustrate the institutional obstacles that constitutional challenges to Virginia's legislation will face.

The result is likely to be a period of legal limbo: the legislation may be enacted, may survive initial preliminary injunction proceedings, and may even survive a Fourth Circuit merits decision—before the constitutional question is resolved definitively by the Supreme Court.

## Implications

The constitutional question posed by Virginia's assault weapons ban is, under **Heller** and **Bruen**, not a close one. Semi-automatic centerfire rifles are in common use. There is no founding-era historical tradition of prohibiting them. They are the modern analog of the militia-relevant arms of the founding period. A categorical ban on their sale and transfer would, if enforced, disarm Virginians of the most popular rifle platform in the country.

That the legislation has advanced through the Virginia House does not reflect constitutional soundness. It reflects political will operating within a state government currently structured to pursue an anti-gun agenda without meaningful internal constraint. The constitutional resolution will come through the courts, not the legislature.

Second Amendment advocates must be prepared for the possibility that Virginia's ban will be enacted and will function as law for several years while litigation proceeds. The strategic imperative is to ensure that the case reaching the Supreme Court is presented in the most favorable possible posture—with the clearest possible record of the firearms' common use, the weakest possible government historical analogues, and plaintiffs whose sympathetic facts make the constitutional question stark and unavoidable.
