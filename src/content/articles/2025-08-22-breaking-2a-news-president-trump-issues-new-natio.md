## Summary

President Donald J. Trump has publicly and forcefully reaffirmed his administration's commitment to the Second Amendment right to keep and bear arms, with specific reference to the right of law-abiding citizens to carry firearms for self-defense throughout the country — including in Washington, D.C., which has historically been among the most restrictive jurisdictions in the Nation with respect to civilian firearms carry. This reaffirmation comes in the context of ongoing law enforcement operations in the District of Columbia authorized by President Trump. I want to address two distinct legal matters that this development raises: first, the constitutional basis for the right to carry in the Nation's capital; and second, an important distinction regarding the meaning of "illegal guns" in the context of current law enforcement operations in D.C. — a distinction that has created some unnecessary confusion within the Second Amendment community.

## Background

Washington, D.C. has for decades operated under among the most restrictive firearms regulations in the United States. The District's permitting requirements for carry, its registration requirements, and its practical barriers to civilian firearms ownership were the subject of the Supreme Court's foundational Second Amendment ruling in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008). In **Heller**, the Supreme Court struck down the District's handgun ban and its requirement that all firearms be kept disassembled or trigger-locked in the home.

The right to carry firearms outside the home was subsequently addressed — and firmly established — by the Supreme Court in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), which held that the Second Amendment protects the right of law-abiding citizens to carry firearms for self-defense in public, and that states and the District of Columbia may not deny that right on discretionary grounds.

In this context, President Trump's reaffirmation of his support for the right of law-abiding citizens to carry firearms in Washington, D.C., and throughout the country, is a constitutionally grounded policy position. The Second Amendment, as interpreted by the Supreme Court, protects exactly this right. The President's public support for its exercise is both constitutionally consistent and politically significant.

## The "Illegal Guns" Terminology: A Necessary Clarification

In connection with the Trump administration's ongoing law enforcement operations in the District of Columbia — aimed at addressing violent crime, gang activity, and the significant public disorder that characterized D.C. during the prior administration — the U.S. Attorney for the District of Columbia, Jeanine Pirro, has on occasion used the phrase "illegal guns" to describe firearms recovered from individuals arrested in connection with these operations.

This phrase has generated concern within the Second Amendment community, and I want to address that concern directly while also providing the necessary context that, in my view, places the U.S. Attorney's language in the correct light.

The Second Amendment community rightly maintains a vigilance about the language used to describe firearms regulation. The phrase "illegal guns" is often deployed by gun control advocates as a rhetorical device to delegitimize lawful civilian firearms ownership — creating the false impression that there is an inherent category of firearms that are "illegal" apart from the specific legal status of the individual possessing them. This rhetorical move is one that I have long criticized, and the concern it generates in the Second Amendment community is legitimate.

However, in the specific context of the D.C. law enforcement operations, I believe U.S. Attorney Pirro's use of the phrase "illegal guns" is a shorthand — imprecise, and perhaps in need of refinement — for a specific and important legal concept: firearms in the possession of individuals who are legally prohibited from possessing them under federal law.

## 18 U.S.C. § 922(g) and the Prohibited Persons Framework

Federal law, specifically 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1), makes it a federal felony for any person who has been convicted of a crime punishable by imprisonment for a term exceeding one year to possess any firearm or ammunition. This provision, commonly referred to as the felon-in-possession statute, applies regardless of whether the firearm itself is otherwise a lawful firearm. A Glock handgun that is entirely legal for a law-abiding citizen to purchase and possess becomes, in the hands of a convicted felon, a violation of federal law.

The individuals being arrested in connection with the D.C. law enforcement operations are, by and large, not law-abiding citizens exercising their Second Amendment rights. They are career felons and gang members — individuals who are legally prohibited from possessing firearms under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g) regardless of the type of firearm involved. When U.S. Attorney Pirro refers to "illegal guns" in this context, she is not articulating a policy that threatens the Second Amendment rights of law-abiding citizens. She is, albeit in imprecise shorthand, describing the possession of firearms by federally prohibited persons.

This is a distinction that matters enormously. A policy targeting prohibited persons who possess firearms illegally is entirely consistent with the Second Amendment. The Supreme Court in **Heller** specifically stated that nothing in its opinion "should be taken to cast doubt on longstanding prohibitions on the possession of firearms by felons and the mentally ill." The felon-in-possession statute, and its enforcement against violent career criminals, is constitutionally sound and indeed essential to the legitimate regulatory framework that the Second Amendment community itself supports.

## The Importance of Precision

With that said, I do want to note that precision in language matters, and U.S. Attorney Pirro would be well-served to use more careful terminology. Rather than "illegal guns" — a phrase with anti-Second Amendment connotations and a history of rhetorical misuse — the appropriate formulation is "firearms possessed by prohibited persons" or "unlawful possession of firearms by convicted felons." This framing accurately describes the legal violation without suggesting that the firearms themselves are somehow inherently illegal or that there is a category of "dangerous" guns that law-abiding citizens should be prevented from owning.

The Second Amendment community's instinct to scrutinize government language about firearms is sound and should continue. But in this particular instance, the context strongly suggests that the D.C. law enforcement operations are targeting violent criminals who are already legally prohibited from possessing firearms — not law-abiding Second Amendment rights holders.

## Implications

President Trump's public reaffirmation of the right to carry in Washington, D.C., and throughout the Nation, reflects the administration's genuine commitment to the Second Amendment. Combined with the administration's DOJ position in cases like **United States v. Peterson** — where the DOJ acknowledged that suppressors are constitutionally protected arms — the Trump administration has consistently staked out pro-Second Amendment legal positions in a manner that will have lasting effects on the development of Second Amendment jurisprudence.

The ongoing D.C. law enforcement operations, properly understood, are consistent with the Second Amendment. Targeting violent career criminals who illegally possess firearms is not only constitutionally permissible — it is precisely the kind of law enforcement activity that the Second Amendment community should support as a complement to the broader constitutional right of law-abiding citizens to keep and bear arms for self-defense.
