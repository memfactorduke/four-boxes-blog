## Summary

A major oral argument in *United States v. Alsen* before the Eleventh Circuit Court of Appeals has exposed what I can only describe as a systemic and recurring failure within the Trump Department of Justice: career attorneys are repeatedly misapplying the *Bruen* two-step methodology in Second Amendment litigation, conflating the plain-text analysis with the historical-tradition analysis in ways that are flatly inconsistent with Supreme Court precedent.

This is not an isolated mistake. I have now documented this exact same methodological error across multiple circuits and multiple cases — and a clear pattern has emerged. When Harmeet Dhillon and her Second Amendment team are on the brief, the DOJ gets the analysis right. When their names are absent, the methodology breaks down in ways that could, if left unaddressed, establish damaging precedent that outlasts any individual case outcome.

## The *Bruen* Framework Is Not Optional

To understand what is going wrong, one must first understand the framework the Supreme Court established in *Bruen* (2022) and *Heller* (2008). The test is straightforward in structure: first, determine whether the plain text of the Second Amendment covers the conduct at issue. Second, if the plain text covers the conduct, the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that the challenged regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation in this country.

These are two distinct analytical steps, and the burden allocation is not interchangeable. At step one — plain-text analysis — the court engages in a definitional and linguistic inquiry. The only question is whether the item at issue is a "bearable arm" within the meaning of the Second Amendment. As the Supreme Court held in *Caetano v. Massachusetts* (2016), the Second Amendment "extends, prima facie, to all instruments that constitute bearable arms, even those that were not in existence at the time of the founding." Justice Alito, concurring in *Caetano*, characterized that language as hard to imagine speaking "more directly to the point." *Heller* itself defined "arms" to include "anything that a man wears for his defense or takes into his hands or useth in wrath to cast at or strike another."

The "common use" test — the inquiry into whether a weapon is in common use by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes — does not belong at step one. It is a product of historical analysis. As *Heller* made clear, the historical tradition of firearms regulation includes "the historical tradition of prohibiting the carrying of dangerous and unusual weapons." That tradition is the *source* of the common-use test. "Dangerous and unusual" is a conjunctive standard — a weapon may not be banned unless it is both dangerous and unusual. And a weapon cannot be unusual if it is in common use. Therefore, the government's failure to demonstrate that a weapon is not in common use is a failure at step two — the historical-tradition burden — not a threshold question of textual coverage.

## What the DOJ Argued in *Alsen* — and Why It Is Wrong

In *United States v. Alsen*, the defendant was charged with and pleaded guilty to possessing a machine gun in violation of 18 U.S.C. § 922(o). He raised a Second Amendment challenge, arguing that machine guns are protected arms. The DOJ's brief to the Eleventh Circuit contained the following passage:

> "The Second Amendment does not protect dangerous and unusual weapons not typically possessed for lawful purposes. The Second Amendment's text does not preclude § 922's regulation of machine guns because the amendment's protection only applies to weapons in common use for lawful purposes. Indeed, machine guns have long been considered dangerous and unusual weapons, not weapons that are in common use for lawful purposes."

This framing is incorrect as a matter of Supreme Court doctrine. The DOJ is treating "common use for lawful purposes" as a textual threshold — as though the Second Amendment's text itself excludes weapons not in common use. But that is not what *Heller* held. *Heller* placed the common-use analysis within the historical tradition, as a tool for identifying which weapons fall within the "dangerous and unusual" category that can be regulated consistent with history. When the DOJ argues that the Second Amendment's "text" limits protection to weapons in common use, it is collapsing the two-step *Bruen* analysis into a single step and, crucially, relieving the government of its burden at step two.

The plain-text question in *Alsen* was simple: is a machine gun a bearable arm? Of course it is. A machine gun can be worn, held, and carried. It is an instrument that a person takes into his hands. That is the end of the step-one inquiry. The burden then shifts to the government to demonstrate — through historical evidence — that machine guns are dangerous and unusual within the meaning of that historical tradition. Whether machine guns are in common use is relevant to that historical inquiry, not to the threshold textual question.

## The Same Error in *Chris Brown v. BATF*

This is not a one-off mistake confined to one regional U.S. Attorney's office. I covered a parallel error this week in the *Chris Brown v. BATF* case out of the Eighth Circuit, brought by the National Rifle Association, the Firearms Policy Coalition, the American Suppressor Association, and the Second Amendment Foundation, challenging provisions of the so-called "One Big Beautiful Bill" relating to suppressors and short-barreled rifles.

In its opposition brief in that case, the DOJ argued that the common-use test belongs at *Bruen*'s first step — the plain-text step — citing *United States v. Trials* from the Eighth Circuit. That reliance is doubly flawed. First, the cited passage in *Trials* was dicta — a throwaway line not essential to the holding. Second, and more fundamentally, circuit court dicta cannot override binding Supreme Court precedent. *Heller* and *Bruen* are unambiguous about where the common-use analysis sits in the doctrinal structure.

## The DOJ Got It Right in the Seventh Circuit

What makes this pattern so troubling is the direct contrast with positions the DOJ has taken in other circuits when Harmeet Dhillon's team was involved. In the Seventh Circuit's *Barnett v. Raoul* — the challenge to Illinois's assault-weapons ban — the DOJ's brief, bearing Dhillon's name, executed the *Bruen* methodology correctly.

On plain text, the brief applied *Heller*'s definition of arms: "Like other rifles have since the founding, AR-15s fall squarely within this category of arms, they are worn and carried for offensive or defensive action in case of conflict with others, thus satisfying *Heller*'s definition of arms." Full stop. That is the step-one analysis — definitional and linguistic.

Then, correctly shifting to the historical-tradition inquiry, the Dhillon team argued that there is no way Illinois could show AR-15s are not in common use, and therefore the State cannot establish that they are dangerous and unusual. The brief was explicit: the common-use test "is historically based" and "is supported by the historical tradition of prohibiting the carrying of dangerous and unusual weapons," citing Justice Alito's concurrence in *Caetano*. That is the correct analysis, done correctly, in the correct sequence, with the burden placed correctly on the government.

This is not a subtle distinction. The Seventh Circuit brief and the Eleventh Circuit brief are contradictory on a foundational methodological question, and they both come from the same Department of Justice under the same President.

## The Stakes: Methodology Matters More Than Any Single Case

I want to be direct about why I consider this a potentially catastrophic problem, even if the immediate case outcomes in *Alsen* or *Chris Brown* might not be of the greatest consequence to the broader Second Amendment community.

Federal judges rely on the positions of the United States government. When the DOJ files a brief that mischaracterizes Supreme Court methodology, there is a risk — not a small one — that circuit courts will adopt that erroneous framing, particularly if neither side correctly argues the right methodology. Bad precedent established in the circuit courts does not stay there. It compounds. It gets cited in future cases. It shapes how lower courts approach Second Amendment challenges for years, potentially decades.

We can afford to lose individual cases involving machine guns or suppressors. What the Second Amendment community cannot afford is a systematic corruption of the *Heller*/*Bruen* methodological framework — one that shifts burdens back onto rights-claimants, treats the plain-text step as a substantive limitation rather than a definitional inquiry, and effectively neuters the government's historical-tradition burden at step two. That would be a doctrinal catastrophe, and it would have been accomplished not by hostile courts or opposing counsel, but by our own Department of Justice.

I should also note that the Solicitor General's office under John Sauer and the positions taken by Jeanine Pirro have been consistent with correct *Bruen* methodology. The problem appears concentrated among career attorneys operating outside the direct supervision of Trump's political appointees in the Second Amendment space.

## A Structural Fix Is Needed

The solution is not complicated, though implementing it requires institutional will. Someone at the Department of Justice — ideally within Harmeet Dhillon's Civil Rights Division or in close coordination with it — needs to be assigned a dedicated oversight function: reviewing every brief filed in every federal court that implicates Second Amendment methodology before it is submitted. Not to dictate outcomes in every case, but to ensure that the methodology is correct, that the *Bruen* two-step framework is properly applied, and that the government's burden at the historical-tradition step is not being improperly offloaded onto the rights-claimant at the plain-text step.

This is not about babysitting. It is about institutional coherence. The Trump administration is, by any fair measure, the most pro-Second Amendment administration in modern American history. The solicitor general has declined to enforce assault-weapons bans in the District of Columbia. Harmeet Dhillon's team filed a correct and aggressive brief in *Barnett*. But somewhere below that level of leadership, career lawyers are either misunderstanding the doctrine, disagreeing with it, or operating without adequate supervision — and the result is a string of briefs that contradict the administration's own legal positions and undercut the rights of American gun owners in federal courts.

My rule of thumb has become simple: if Harmeet Dhillon's name — or the name of someone from her Second Amendment team — is not on the brief, proceed with caution. The pattern is too consistent to be coincidental, and the consequences of allowing it to continue are too serious to ignore.
