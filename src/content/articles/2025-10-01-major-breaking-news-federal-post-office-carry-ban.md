## Summary

A federal district court in the Northern District of Texas, sitting in Fort Worth, has issued a significant ruling in **Firearms Policy Coalition v. Bondi** — striking down the federal prohibition on carrying firearms into United States Post Office facilities as unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The plaintiffs, including the Firearms Policy Coalition and the Second Amendment Foundation, brought this challenge on behalf of their members who sought to exercise their Second Amendment right to carry a firearm for self-defense while conducting ordinary daily business at the post office. The district court's ruling — that the federal statute and regulations prohibiting firearms at post offices lack any basis in the historical tradition of analogous regulation at the time of the Founding — is well-reasoned, constitutionally grounded, and represents one of the most significant district-court-level Second Amendment victories in the post-**Bruen** landscape. I want to analyze this ruling in careful detail, address its scope and limitations, and explain what it means for the millions of law-abiding Americans who currently cannot carry a firearm at the post office.

## Background

The federal prohibition on carrying firearms into United States Postal Service facilities is not found in a statute enacted in a careful, modern legislative debate about sensitive places and Second Amendment jurisprudence. It derives from regulations and statutory provisions enacted in the late 1960s and early 1970s — decades before the Supreme Court's landmark decisions in **District of Columbia v. Heller** and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** established the constitutional framework that now governs Second Amendment analysis.

The post office, as a matter of practical experience, is indistinguishable from any other retail location. It is a place where ordinary Americans go to mail packages, purchase stamps, pick up held mail, and conduct mundane business transactions. It has no particular security infrastructure — no metal detectors, no armed guards, no security checkpoints. Post office employees are not law enforcement officers. The building is not a courthouse, a prison, or any other facility that has historically been associated with heightened security justifications for firearms restrictions.

Yet because of a regulatory scheme enacted more than a half-century ago, law-abiding citizens who hold valid concealed-carry permits — who have been vetted through background checks and whatever state licensing requirements apply — are legally prohibited from carrying their firearms for self-defense in a post office, even in the most pro-Second Amendment states in the country. A Texan with a license to carry who is fully authorized to carry in grocery stores, gas stations, and banks is nonetheless prohibited from carrying that same firearm into the local post office.

## The Plaintiffs' Challenge

The Firearms Policy Coalition and the Second Amendment Foundation, along with individual members of those organizations, brought suit challenging the post office carry prohibition as a facial and as-applied violation of the Second Amendment. The plaintiffs' core argument was straightforward: under the **Bruen** framework, the government bears the burden of identifying a historical tradition of analogous regulation at the time of the Founding. There is no such historical tradition for prohibiting carry in post office facilities, because the concept of a federal postal service with restricted-firearms premises did not exist in anything like its modern form at the Founding, and in any event the Founding-era legal culture recognized no general principle that government-operated premises were categorically off-limits to armed citizens.

The constitutional analysis begins, as **Bruen** requires, with the plain text of the Second Amendment. The Second Amendment guarantees the right of the people to keep and bear arms. Carrying a firearm for self-defense at a post office is plainly within the scope of the right to bear arms — it is the quintessential exercise of the right that the Second Amendment was written to protect. Therefore, the burden falls on the government to justify the restriction by reference to a historical tradition of analogous regulation.

## The District Court's Analysis

The district court's opinion in **Firearms Policy Coalition v. Bondi** is, in my assessment, a well-constructed application of the **Bruen** framework. The court began, properly, with the plain text question: is carrying a handgun in a post office covered by the Second Amendment? The answer is clearly yes. The court then placed the burden on the government to identify historical analogues.

The government's historical arguments failed. The government could not point to Founding-era traditions of prohibiting firearms in government postal facilities, because no such tradition existed. The early American postal system operated through a network of privately operated post roads and post riders, not government-owned facilities with firearms restrictions. The model of the modern post office — a government-owned, government-staffed retail premises — was not the postal reality of 1791.

The government also argued that post offices are analogous to courthouses and other government buildings that courts have recognized as historically sensitive places. The district court correctly rejected this analogy. Courthouses have a well-documented historical tradition of firearms restrictions going back to the common law — the prohibition on bringing weapons into court was well-established in English and colonial American legal practice. A post office has no comparable historical pedigree of restricted firearms access.

## The Scope of the Injunction: Nationwide Application

One of the most legally significant aspects of this ruling is the question of the injunction's scope. The plaintiffs — the Firearms Policy Coalition and the Second Amendment Foundation — are national organizations with members in every state. Their argument, and a genuinely important one, is that the federal post office carry prohibition is a federal law of nationwide application, and that an injunction prohibiting enforcement of that law should apply nationwide to all members of those organizations.

This is a legitimate and well-grounded argument. The Second Amendment Foundation and the Firearms Policy Coalition are not local organizations with limited membership. They have members in Texas and Florida, in California and New York, in every state and territory of the United States. If the federal statute is unconstitutional — and the district court has held that it is — it is unconstitutional as applied to every one of those members, not merely to those who happen to reside in the Northern District of Texas.

The district court's ruling on the scope of the injunction is a critical element of this decision, and I expect it to be heavily litigated on appeal. The Trump administration's Department of Justice will presumably seek to limit the injunction's scope, consistent with the administration's general preference for narrow rather than nationwide injunctions. The Second Amendment plaintiffs, in contrast, will argue that the nationwide scope is appropriate and legally required.

I believe the plaintiffs have the better of this argument. Federal constitutional violations do not respect state lines, and federal statutory provisions that violate the Second Amendment are unconstitutional throughout the United States, not merely within the jurisdiction of the specific court that first strikes them down.

## Prior Litigation and the Developing Trend

This is not the first federal court decision to challenge the post office carry prohibition. There have been other cases — criminal prosecutions and civil challenges — in which the constitutionality of the prohibition has been raised and addressed with varying outcomes. Some prior cases found the prohibition constitutional; others raised serious doubts about its validity under the **Bruen** framework.

What makes the **Firearms Policy Coalition v. Bondi** decision particularly significant is its timing, its scope, and the quality of the legal analysis. Coming in the wake of **Bruen** and with the benefit of several years of post-**Bruen** litigation experience, this ruling applies the historical analysis with the rigor and precision that **Bruen** demands. It is the strongest judicial statement yet that the post office carry prohibition is constitutionally untenable.

## Implications

The district court's ruling in **Firearms Policy Coalition v. Bondi** represents a significant and consequential victory for Second Amendment rights. The federal prohibition on carrying firearms at post offices is one of those quiet, taken-for-granted gun restrictions that most Americans have never thought to question — it has simply existed in the regulatory background for decades. The plaintiffs' successful challenge demonstrates that the **Bruen** framework is capable of reaching these legacy restrictions, not just the high-profile new laws enacted after **Bruen** in states like New York, California, and Hawaii.

This case will certainly be appealed to the Fifth Circuit, and from there it may well reach the Supreme Court. The resolution of the post office carry ban question — along with the broader sensitive-places questions being litigated in **Wolford v. Lopez** and related cases — will ultimately define the constitutional scope of the government's authority to restrict carry in public and quasi-public spaces. The trajectory of that litigation is, on the whole, favorable for the Second Amendment, and this district court ruling is a significant and welcome data point in that direction.
