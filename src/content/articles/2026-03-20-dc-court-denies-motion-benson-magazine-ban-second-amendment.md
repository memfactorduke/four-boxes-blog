## Summary

The D.C. Court of Appeals has denied the District of Columbia's emergency motion to expedite its petition for rehearing en banc and to suspend the precedential effect of the court's recent decision in *Benson v. United States*. As I've analyzed, that decision held that magazines holding more than ten rounds are protected arms under the Second Amendment and cannot be banned. The denial preserves a critical split of authority among senior courts that may provide the basis for Supreme Court review of magazine capacity restrictions nationwide.

## The Benson Decision and Its Significance

Just weeks ago, a three-judge panel of the D.C. Court of Appeals issued a landmark ruling in *Benson v. United States*, holding that the District of Columbia's ban on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds violates the Second Amendment. The opinion, spanning over fifty pages, conducted a thorough textual and historical analysis consistent with the framework established by the Supreme Court in *District of Columbia v. Heller* and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*.

The ruling was particularly significant because it was supported by the Trump administration's Department of Justice. In September 2025, the U.S. Attorney for the District of Columbia filed a critical motion arguing that the D.C. magazine ban was unconstitutional under the Second Amendment and that the federal government would no longer enforce the statute. The D.C. Court of Appeals agreed.

As I've observed, the decision created what Second Amendment advocates have long sought: a split of authority among the nation's highest courts on the question of magazine capacity restrictions. While multiple federal circuits -- including the First, Fourth, Seventh, and Ninth Circuits -- have upheld magazine bans, the D.C. Court of Appeals now stands as the first senior appellate court to hold that such bans are unconstitutional. That split historically serves as a primary basis for the Supreme Court to grant certiorari and resolve the conflict.

## The District's Emergency Motion

Recognizing the stakes, the District of Columbia moved quickly. Its attorneys filed an emergency motion asking the D.C. Court of Appeals to take two extraordinary steps: first, to expedite its petition for rehearing en banc so that the full court could reconsider the panel decision at an accelerated pace; and second, to suspend the precedential status of the *Benson* decision while that rehearing petition was pending.

The District argued that the ruling had opened the floodgates for magazines to surge into the District, creating what it described as an immediate and severe risk to public safety. It pointed to an incident in California, where a federal court's temporary injunction of that state's magazine restrictions allegedly led to more than a million magazines entering the state during a brief window.

## Benson's Opposition

The attorney for Tyrie Benson filed a forceful opposition that the court ultimately found persuasive. The response characterized the District's claimed reasons for emergency action as "hyperbolic" and "within the district's own control."

On the public safety argument, Benson's counsel made a pointed observation: if ceasing enforcement of the magazine ban would truly prompt an immediate surge of magazines into the District, that surge should have occurred six months earlier, when the Trump administration ceased enforcing the ban based on its view that the statute was unconstitutional. No public safety crisis had materialized in the intervening months.

The opposition further noted that magazines holding more than ten rounds are in common and ubiquitous use by law-abiding citizens across the country, accounting for roughly half of all magazines in civilian hands. The non-enforcement and invalidation of the D.C. law simply brought the District in line with Virginia and the thirty-seven other states that impose no regulation on magazine capacity.

Benson's attorney also highlighted a telling omission: the D.C. City Council, which had been aware of the legal challenges since at least September, had taken no legislative action to address the situation. If the council genuinely believed there was a public emergency, it had the power to act -- yet it chose inaction.

## The Court's Ruling

The D.C. Court of Appeals denied the District's motion in its entirety. The court declined to suspend the precedential effect of the *Benson* decision and refused to expedite the rehearing petition. Instead, it ordered that the case proceed through the normal course of appellate procedure, inviting the Department of Justice and Benson's counsel to submit briefs on whether en banc review should be granted.

The denial signals that the court is not prepared to treat this Second Amendment ruling as warranting extraordinary procedural treatment. En banc review in the D.C. Court of Appeals is exceedingly rare, occurring roughly once per year, and typically in narrow criminal procedure cases rather than broad constitutional questions.

## The Road Ahead

With the precedential value of *Benson* intact, the case continues to create strategic dilemmas for opponents of the Second Amendment. If the D.C. Court of Appeals denies rehearing en banc, the District will face a difficult choice: accept the loss and allow the precedent to stand, or seek certiorari from the Supreme Court -- where, given the current composition of the Court, the risk of a nationwide ruling invalidating magazine bans is substantial.

The Department of Justice is expected to file a brief opposing en banc review, consistent with its position that the magazine ban is unconstitutional. That posture would make it politically and practically difficult for the full court to grant rehearing over the objection of the federal prosecutorial authority responsible for enforcing D.C. criminal law.

For the Second Amendment community, in my view, the *Benson* decision remains a critical brick in the wall of precedent supporting the right to keep and bear commonly owned arms. Whether the case ultimately reaches the Supreme Court through this procedural path or through parallel litigation in the federal circuits, the split of authority it has created substantially increases the likelihood that the nation's highest court will address magazine capacity restrictions in the near future.
