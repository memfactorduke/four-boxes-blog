## Summary

The Trump administration's Department of Justice, under the direction of Attorney General Pam Bondi, has filed a substantial brief before the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit in the consolidated AR-15 ban cases of *Cheeseman v. New Jersey* and *Association of New Jersey Rifle and Pistol Clubs v. New Jersey Attorney General*. The brief, submitted to the Third Circuit sitting en banc with oral argument scheduled for October 15, 2025, takes the position that New Jersey's categorical prohibition on AR-15 semi-automatic rifles and magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds violates the Second Amendment. The Department's argument centers on the principle that the Second Amendment protects all arms in common use for lawful purposes, that the government bears the burden of justifying any restriction, and that New Jersey has not met — and cannot meet — that burden.

## Background

The Second Amendment provides that "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed." The Supreme Court in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), established that this right protects law-abiding citizens' possession of commonly owned arms for self-defense and other lawful purposes. In *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the Court clarified the applicable analytical framework: courts must determine whether a challenged regulation burdens protected conduct, and if so, the government bears the burden of demonstrating that the regulation is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation.

New Jersey has enacted a categorical ban on AR-15 semi-automatic rifles and a restriction limiting magazine capacity to ten rounds. These restrictions have been challenged in federal court, and the Third Circuit — covering New Jersey, Delaware, and Pennsylvania — has granted en banc review, meaning that all active judges will hear the cases together. The Department of Justice's decision to file a brief fully supporting the Second Amendment plaintiffs represents a significant institutional commitment to the constitutional claims at issue.

## Analysis of the Department of Justice's Constitutional Arguments

The Department of Justice's brief identifies several core constitutional arguments that I regard as essential to the proper analysis of AR-15 ban cases.

**The Common Use Principle.** The brief argues that the Second Amendment protects all arms in common use by law-abiding Americans for lawful purposes. This principle, established in *Heller*, operates as a constitutional floor: if an arm is commonly possessed for lawful purposes, the government may not categorically prohibit it regardless of what historical analogues it can marshal or what policy justifications it advances. The AR-15 rifle is, by any measure, in common use — tens of millions are owned by law-abiding citizens for self-defense, sporting purposes, and other lawful uses. The constitutional conclusion follows directly: New Jersey's categorical ban is incompatible with the Second Amendment.

**The Government Bears the Burden of Proof.** The brief correctly articulates that the burden of justifying any restriction on Second Amendment rights rests with the government, not with the citizen. This allocation of the burden of proof has practical significance: it is not the plaintiff's obligation to prove that an AR-15 ban is unconstitutional, but the government's obligation to prove it is constitutional by identifying a sufficiently analogous historical tradition. When the government cannot carry that burden — as is the case here, given the absence of any founding-era or Reconstruction-era tradition of categorical semi-automatic rifle bans — the law must fall.

**The Common Defense Rationale.** The brief addresses the Second Amendment's prefatory clause, which identifies a well-regulated militia as necessary to the security of a free state. The Founders' conception of the right to keep and bear arms encompassed not merely individual self-defense but also the collective capacity of the citizenry to provide for the common defense. Restricting law-abiding citizens to firearms and magazine configurations inferior to those used by military and law enforcement personnel is inconsistent with this constitutional design — particularly where, as here, the restricted arms are the same class of weapons the Founders recognized as essential to a well-armed citizenry.

**The Logical Consequences of Magazine Restrictions.** The brief addresses the slippery slope that magazine capacity restrictions create: if a government can ban magazines holding more than ten rounds, it can equally ban magazines holding more than seven, more than five, or more than one. The constitutional line must be drawn at the common use threshold, or the practical effect is a progressive erosion of the right to keep and bear arms through successive reductions in magazine capacity.

## The Institutional Significance of the DOJ's Position

The Department of Justice's filing in this case is significant not merely for its legal content but for its institutional meaning. The federal government — the most powerful litigant in the American judicial system — has placed itself squarely on the side of Second Amendment plaintiffs in what may become the most consequential firearms rights litigation of the decade. Attorney General Pam Bondi and the officials who signed this brief — including those responsible for constitutional litigation at the Department — have represented to the Third Circuit that the position of the United States is that New Jersey's AR-15 ban violates the Constitution.

This institutional alignment is particularly important before the en banc Third Circuit. Courts of appeals are not indifferent to the views of the United States government on questions of constitutional law. The Department's brief will be read carefully by every active judge on the Third Circuit, and its arguments will frame the constitutional choices the court must make. A brief from the United States that argues for the unconstitutionality of a state gun ban is not a document that courts can set aside without serious engagement with its analysis.

## The Structural Advantage of the Third Circuit En Banc Proceedings

As I have previously analyzed, the Third Circuit presents a structural advantage that is absent in other circuits where AR-15 ban challenges are proceeding. Unlike the Ninth Circuit sitting en banc — which heard California's magazine ban challenge in *Duncan v. Bonta* in an environment dominated by judges appointed by presidents who favored restrictive gun laws — the Third Circuit's en banc composition at the time of the October 15, 2025 argument may include a more balanced array of judges, particularly if additional Trump appointees are confirmed before the argument date.

The geographic composition of the Third Circuit is also relevant: the circuit's jurisdiction encompasses Pennsylvania, a state with a substantial tradition of firearms ownership and one in which the political culture is more receptive to Second Amendment rights than New Jersey. The court is not composed exclusively of jurists whose professional and political formation occurred in a single-state environment hostile to firearms rights.

## Implications

The Department of Justice's brief in *Cheeseman* and *Association of New Jersey Rifle and Pistol Clubs* is, in my assessment, one of the most important pro-Second Amendment filings in the history of the Department. It represents a full-throated institutional endorsement of the constitutional principles that *Heller* and *Bruen* established: that the Second Amendment protects commonly owned arms, that the government bears the burden of justifying restrictions, and that categorical bans on popular firearms have no foundation in the historical tradition of firearms regulation in America.

The en banc argument on October 15, 2025 is a pivotal moment. The Second Amendment rights of millions of law-abiding citizens in New Jersey — and, through the circuit split that will result from conflicting decisions across circuits, ultimately of all Americans — depend on the Third Circuit's faithfulness to the constitutional framework the Supreme Court has articulated. The Department of Justice's brief ensures that the full weight of the federal government supports the conclusion that this framework requires the invalidation of New Jersey's AR-15 and magazine ban.
