## Summary

The United States Supreme Court, in an 8-1 decision in *Charles v. Salazar*, struck down a Colorado conversion therapy law on First Amendment grounds, holding that the state could not transform protected speech into regulable "conduct" simply by relabeling it. While the case arose in a First Amendment context, the Court's reasoning carries potentially significant implications for Second Amendment litigation -- particularly challenges to state laws that classify ordinary semi-automatic rifles as "assault weapons" and standard-capacity magazines as "large-capacity magazines." The decision's categorical rejection of what Justice Gorsuch called a "labeling game" may undermine a core premise of firearms restrictions that depend on politically derived statutory definitions to remove commonly owned arms from constitutional protection.

## Background

*Charles v. Salazar* involved a licensed talk therapist in Colorado who wished to counsel clients experiencing gender dysphoria. Under Colorado law, licensed practitioners were prohibited from engaging in so-called conversion therapy -- meaning they could encourage a patient's gender transition but could not explore whether the patient might benefit from a different course of treatment. The therapist, Kristen Chelsey Charles, challenged the law as a violation of her First Amendment right to free speech.

Colorado defended the statute by characterizing the therapist's conversations with clients not as speech but as "professional conduct" subject to state regulation. The state argued that because the therapist held a professional license, the state could regulate her therapeutic interactions as conduct rather than expression, thereby avoiding First Amendment scrutiny.

The case reached the Supreme Court after the Tenth Circuit Court of Appeals upheld the law, though Judge Timothy Tymkovich (referenced in the opinion through his dissent, attributed in the transcript to "Judge Hart") dissented, observing that as applied to the plaintiff, the law regulated only what she may and may not say -- not any physical conduct.

## The Supreme Court's Decision

Writing for an 8-1 majority, Justice Neil Gorsuch rejected Colorado's characterization in unambiguous terms. The opinion identified the state's core argument as an attempt to redefine speech as conduct through definitional manipulation.

Justice Gorsuch wrote that the lower court dissent had correctly identified the problem: "the majority's effort to recast Miss Charles's speech as professional conduct amounted to little more than a labeling game." The Court then adopted that characterization and elevated it to a governing principle.

The opinion addressed Colorado's defense directly:

> The state of Colorado's premise is simply mistaken. In many applications, the state's law banning conversion therapy may address conduct such as aversive physical interventions. But here, Miss Charles seeks to engage only in speech. And as applied to her, the law regulates what she may say. Her speech does not become conduct just because the state of Colorado may call it that.

Justice Gorsuch continued:

> Nor does her speech become conduct just because it can also be described as a "treatment," a "therapeutic modality," or anything else for that matter. The First Amendment is no word game, and the rights it protects cannot be renamed away or their protections nullified by mere labels.

The Court held that Colorado's law, as applied to the plaintiff, constituted viewpoint discrimination in violation of the First Amendment.

## Implications for Second Amendment Litigation

While *Charles v. Salazar* is a First Amendment case, the analytical framework it establishes has direct relevance to pending and future Second Amendment challenges to so-called assault weapon bans and magazine capacity restrictions.

**The definitional parallel.** Every jurisdiction that bans commonly owned semi-automatic rifles does so by first redefining them. An ordinary semi-automatic rifle -- mechanically indistinguishable in function from a semi-automatic handgun protected under *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008) -- is reclassified as an "assault weapon" based on the presence of certain cosmetic or ergonomic features such as a pistol grip, adjustable stock, or barrel shroud. Similarly, standard-capacity magazines -- the magazines that ship from the factory with most firearms -- are relabeled as "large-capacity magazines" to justify their prohibition.

The logic is structurally identical to what Colorado attempted in *Charles v. Salazar*. Just as Colorado sought to reclassify speech as "professional conduct" to escape First Amendment protection, states with assault weapon bans seek to reclassify ordinary arms as "assault weapons" to escape Second Amendment protection. In both instances, the government's argument depends on the judiciary accepting a politically motivated statutory definition at face value.

**The Court's rejection of definitional manipulation.** The Supreme Court's statement that constitutional "rights ... cannot be renamed away or their protections nullified by mere labels" applies with equal force across the Bill of Rights. If Colorado cannot transform speech into conduct by legislative definition, it follows that states cannot transform commonly owned firearms into unprotected weapons of war through the same mechanism.

As Justice Clarence Thomas has previously observed -- a point I have emphasized in my own scholarship -- the term "assault weapon" is itself "a political term developed by anti-gun publicists," not a technical or historically grounded firearms classification. The *Charles v. Salazar* majority's hostility toward government relabeling as a constitutional avoidance strategy reinforces the argument that courts must look past statutory nomenclature to the underlying reality of what is being regulated.

**Timing and pending cases.** The decision arrives at a critical moment. Multiple challenges to state assault weapon bans and magazine restrictions are advancing through the federal courts, and the Supreme Court may soon consider whether to grant certiorari in one or more of these cases. If and when the Court hears such a challenge, the reasoning of *Charles v. Salazar* provides Second Amendment litigants with a powerful cross-doctrinal argument: the same Court that refused to let Colorado redefine speech as conduct is unlikely to permit states to redefine ordinary rifles as assault weapons simply by legislative fiat.

## The "Made-Up Category" Argument

The connection between *Charles v. Salazar* and the assault weapon debate has been developed at length in my legal scholarship. In an article published in the Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy, I argued that "assault weapon" bans constitute "unconstitutional laws for a made-up category of firearms." My central thesis is that ordinary semi-automatic rifles, like the semi-automatic handguns protected in *Heller*, are constitutionally protected arms, and that protection "does not disappear merely because the anti-gun lobby chooses to label -- or perhaps more accurately mislabel -- these ordinary firearms as assault weapons."

I further argued that the definitional process itself is the mechanism of circumvention: "Because of the semi-automatic rifle's appearance, coupled with certain features that are arbitrarily included in some 'assault weapon' ban statutes, an ordinary rifle gets converted definitionally into an assault weapon." The parallel to what the Supreme Court rejected in *Charles v. Salazar* -- the conversion of one constitutionally relevant category into another through definitional relabeling -- is direct.

## Looking Ahead

The practical significance of *Charles v. Salazar* for firearms litigation will depend on how lower courts and, ultimately, the Supreme Court apply its reasoning outside the First Amendment context. The decision does not address the Second Amendment directly. However, the principle it articulates -- that constitutional rights are defined by substance, not by the labels governments attach to the conduct they wish to regulate -- is not cabined to any single amendment.

Should the Supreme Court grant certiorari in an assault weapon ban case in the coming term, litigants challenging such bans will almost certainly cite *Charles v. Salazar* for the proposition that the Court should evaluate semi-automatic rifles based on what they are -- commonly owned arms functionally similar to constitutionally protected handguns -- rather than what state legislatures have chosen to call them. Given the 8-1 margin in *Charles v. Salazar*, that argument appears to command a substantial majority of the current Court, and I expect this reasoning will prove decisive in future Second Amendment cases.
