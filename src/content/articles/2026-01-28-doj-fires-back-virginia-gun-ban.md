## Summary

Associate Attorney General Harmy Dhillon, head of the civil rights division and Second Amendment unit at the Trump Department of Justice, publicly responded to Virginia's proposed SB 749 — a bill that would ban possession of magazines holding more than ten rounds without any grandfather clause for current owners — with an unambiguous statement: "This is a blatant violation of Supreme Court precedent and it will not stand." In my analysis, Dhillon is entirely correct. A magazine ban of this scope constitutes not merely a restriction on a firearm accessory but a categorical ban on an entire class of firearms — one that cannot survive scrutiny under **District of Columbia v. Heller** and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen**. The DOJ's response, swift and unequivocal, is exactly the kind of executive-branch engagement with rogue state gun legislation that the Second Amendment community has needed.

## Background: Virginia SB 749

Following the recent election in which Democrats gained substantial majorities in the Virginia General Assembly and installed a new governor, the Commonwealth's legislature moved quickly on a package of gun restrictions. Among the most aggressive provisions of SB 749 as amended is the elimination of any grandfather clause for current possessors of magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds. Under the bill as amended, keeping any such magazine in Virginia would constitute a Class 1 misdemeanor, punishable by up to twelve months in prison. Millions of Virginians currently own what the bill labels "large capacity magazines" — a term that I and serious Second Amendment practitioners uniformly reject in favor of the accurate descriptor: **standard capacity magazines**.

The characterization matters. A magazine holding fifteen, seventeen, or thirty rounds is not some exceptional high-capacity modification. It is the magazine that comes factory-standard with the firearm from the manufacturer. Reduced-capacity magazines exist only in states where legislatures have artificially mandated them. The market — that is, the collective judgment of law-abiding Americans exercising their Second Amendment rights — has consistently chosen standard-capacity magazines because they represent the design optimized for the firearm's intended lawful use, including self-defense.

## Why a Magazine Ban Is a Firearm Ban

The most important analytical point in this debate is one that courts and commentators frequently miss, and I want to state it precisely: **a ban on magazines holding more than ten rounds is not merely a ban on a component. It is a categorical ban on an entire class of firearms.**

Consider the logic. If it is unlawful to possess a magazine capable of holding more than ten rounds, then it is effectively unlawful to possess any firearm that requires such a magazine to function as designed. That is, any semi-automatic pistol or rifle that operates on a standard-capacity magazine becomes functionally banned when its magazine is prohibited — because you cannot legally possess the magazine integral to the firearm's designed operation. The result is a categorical ban on firearms capable of firing more than ten rounds without manual reloading. Stated differently: a magazine ban is a gun ban, just written to look like something else.

**Heller** is explicit that categorical bans on arms in common use by law-abiding Americans for lawful purposes are categorically unconstitutional. The standard-capacity magazines at issue in Virginia are possessed by tens of millions of Americans for home defense, recreational shooting, and competition. Their prevalence places them squarely within the "common use" protection that **Heller** established and **Bruen** reaffirmed.

## The Definition of "Arm" Under Bruen

**Bruen** defines an "arm" as any instrument that facilitates armed self-defense. A magazine holding more than ten rounds is precisely such an instrument: it is an object whose function is to enable a firearm to perform the defensive role for which it was designed. Limiting a magazine to ten rounds does not merely reduce capacity in the abstract; it degrades the defensive utility of the firearm, forcing the defender to reload in circumstances where the number of attackers or the duration of a threat may exceed what ten rounds can address. The instrument that enables the firearm to function as a defensive arm is therefore itself protected under the plain text of the Second Amendment as an arm.

This analytical point — that a suppressor, a magazine, or other functional component is itself an arm under **Bruen**'s definition — has been advanced by the Trump DOJ in briefs in **Barnett v. Raoul** in the Seventh Circuit and **Cheeseman v. Platkin** in the Third Circuit. The consistency of the DOJ's position across these cases reinforces the credibility of the legal framework.

## The Burden-of-Proof Question

A point that I have emphasized repeatedly bears repeating in this context: the burden of proving that a standard-capacity magazine is not in common use — and thus might potentially fall within the "dangerous and unusual" exception that could support a ban — rests on the government, not on the rights claimant.

The **Bruen** methodology is explicit: once a rights claimant demonstrates that the challenged restriction implicates the plain text of the Second Amendment, the burden shifts to the government to establish a historical tradition of analogous regulation. If the government seeks to defend a ban by arguing the item is dangerous and unusual, it must prove that. It cannot simply assert rarity or dangerousness and require the plaintiff to disprove it. Courts that reverse this burden — demanding that Second Amendment plaintiffs affirmatively demonstrate common use before the government must engage in historical analysis — are departing from **Bruen**'s framework.

Anti-gun courts have persistently made this error. The Supreme Court will not. When magazine-ban cases reach the Court — and they will — the burden-of-proof question will be dispositive, and the answer will not favor Virginia.

## The DC Precedent: Non-Enforcement as Policy

The DOJ's rebuke of Virginia's magazine ban is reinforced by concurrent action in the District of Columbia, where U.S. Attorney Jeanine Pirro has announced that the DC ban on magazines holding more than ten rounds will not be prosecuted, having determined that the ban "cannot be reconciled with the Second Amendment." The Trump DOJ has thus established a consistent institutional position across all its relevant capacities: it will not defend magazine bans in court, it will rebuke state legislatures that enact them, and it will decline to enforce federal-district analogs.

This is how executive-branch engagement with unconstitutional state laws should function. The administration is not merely filing briefs; it is signaling through multiple channels that the regulatory environment for magazine bans has fundamentally shifted, and that states that proceed with enactment face both litigation and the absence of federal cooperation.

## Implications for Virginia and Beyond

Virginia's SB 749 will almost certainly be enacted given the current legislative majority. It will also, equally certainly, be challenged in federal court. Whether that challenge reaches the Fourth Circuit or eventually the Supreme Court, the legal outcome — under any faithful application of **Heller** and **Bruen** — is not seriously in doubt. The more interesting question is timing: how quickly the courts move, and whether the Fourth Circuit can be persuaded to apply **Bruen** honestly rather than through the inventive evasions for which it has become known.

The DOJ's swift public response to SB 749's amendment removing the grandfather clause signals that the administration is monitoring state gun-control legislation in real time and is prepared to engage immediately. That institutional posture — visible, rapid, and legally precise — is a meaningful deterrent to other states that might otherwise pursue similar restrictions in the expectation of federal passivity. The era of federal acquiescence to anti-gun state legislation is over, and Virginia is encountering that reality directly.
