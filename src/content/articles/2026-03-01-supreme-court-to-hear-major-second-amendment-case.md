## Summary

In my analysis, the Supreme Court's grant of certiorari in **United States v. Hammani** presents the Court with an important but procedurally problematic vehicle for resolving whether 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3) — which prohibits firearm possession by unlawful users of, or persons addicted to, controlled substances — is consistent with the Second Amendment as interpreted under **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). As I have argued, the factual record in *Hammani* is poorly suited to establishing favorable Second Amendment precedent, and I am skeptical that the Solicitor General's decision to seek certiorari in this case reflects sound constitutional strategy. Nevertheless, the oral argument scheduled for tomorrow will determine the contours of the Court's inquiry, and the constitutional question presented — whether Founding-era historical analogues support categorical disarmament of unlawful drug users — is one the Court must eventually resolve.

## Background

Section 922(g)(3) of Title 18 renders it unlawful for "any person who is an unlawful user of or addicted to any controlled substance (as defined in section 102 of the Controlled Substances Act)" to "possess in or affecting commerce, any firearm or ammunition." Marijuana remains classified as a Schedule I controlled substance under the Controlled Substances Act, notwithstanding legislative developments at the state level and ongoing administrative review of that classification. A person who is a consistent, habitual user of marijuana — or who is addicted to it — is therefore subject to federal prosecution under § 922(g)(3) if they possess a firearm.

Ali Hammani was convicted under this provision. The government's trial record includes evidence that Hammani had connections to the Iranian Revolutionary Guard, engaged in drug dealing involving cocaine and marijuana, and was a habitual user of controlled substances. These facts create a factual context that is, in my assessment, among the least sympathetic conceivable for a Second Amendment challenge. The Supreme Court's precedents do not decide constitutional questions in a vacuum; the factual record shapes the way the relevant legal questions are framed and the breadth of the ruling that follows.

## The Statutory Framework

The provision at issue contains two grammatically and conceptually distinct prongs. The first prong prohibits possession by persons who are "unlawful user[s] of" a controlled substance — a category the courts of appeals have generally defined by reference to a pattern of ongoing use, though the precise temporal scope varies across circuits. The second prong prohibits possession by persons "addicted to" a controlled substance, which connotes a physiological or psychological dependency that may persist independently of active drug use at the moment of possession. These two categories carry different constitutional implications under the *Bruen* framework, and the Court's willingness to address them separately in tomorrow's argument will determine the analytical precision of any resulting opinion.

The government has proceeded on the theory that Hammani's habitual marijuana and cocaine use placed him within both prongs of the statute at the time of his firearm possession. The constitutional question — whether the Second Amendment, properly understood in light of Founding-era historical tradition, permits Congress to categorically disarm individuals within these categories — is one the Supreme Court has not previously addressed directly.

## The Strategic Question: Cert Selection Under *Bruen*

As I have examined the history of Supreme Court Second Amendment litigation, the most durable and favorable precedents have arisen from cases with sympathetic plaintiffs. **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), involved Dick Heller, a federal security professional who sought only to keep a handgun in his home for self-defense. **Bruen** involved ordinary citizens who were denied carry licenses despite having no disqualifying history. The plaintiffs in those cases embodied the paradigmatic Second Amendment rights-bearer: a law-abiding citizen seeking to exercise a constitutional right.

**United States v. Hammani** presents a materially different profile. As I have argued, when the Solicitor General selects a case for Supreme Court review, the choice of vehicle is as consequential as the legal argument being advanced. More sympathetic § 922(g)(3) vehicles exist — cases such as *Range v. Attorney General*, which involved a Pennsylvania man whose disqualification under § 922(g)(1) derived from a minor food stamp offense committed thirty years prior. A case like *Range* invites the Court to articulate the narrowest possible reading of permissible disarmament categories. *Hammani*, by contrast, provides the Court with a factual context in which an expansive affirmance would be easier to reach without appearing to disfavor ordinary, law-abiding citizens. In my judgment, pursuing this case to the Supreme Court was a strategic error if the objective is to establish favorable, durable Second Amendment precedent.

## The Constitutional Question: Founding-Era Analogues

The *Bruen* framework requires the government to demonstrate that a challenged firearms regulation is "consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation." For prohibitions applicable to specific categories of persons, this means identifying historical analogues from the Founding era that demonstrate a comparable tradition of disarmament.

The government's strongest historical argument will draw on Founding-era provisions addressing "lunatics" — persons deemed to suffer from mental illness or cognitive incapacity that impaired their fitness to exercise the right to bear arms. Colonial and early state laws permitted civil commitment and, as a practical matter, disarmament of such individuals during periods of incapacity. The government will argue that habitual unlawful drug use produces a comparable impairment of judgment and volitional control, and that the analogy to Founding-era lunacy provisions justifies Congress's authority to disarm habitual users and addicts.

In my analysis, this argument has greatest force as applied to the "addicted to" prong of § 922(g)(3), where the physiological dependency is, by definition, continuous rather than episodic. The analogy to lunacy — which the Founding era understood as a condition that impaired judgment persistently, not merely at moments of acute episode — fits more naturally to the profile of someone in a state of active physical addiction. The extension of the same analogy to all "unlawful users," including those whose drug use is periodic or remote in time from the moment of firearm possession, is more attenuated and likely to draw scrutiny at oral argument.

## The Government's Voluntary-Cessation Argument

The Department of Justice will also advance a structural argument that distinguishes § 922(g)(3) from permanent disarmament provisions: the prohibition is, in the government's framing, self-terminating. A person subject to § 922(g)(3) may recover Second Amendment rights by ceasing unlawful drug use and resolving any addiction — the disarmament is a consequence of a continuing behavioral choice, not a permanent status-based exclusion. The government will draw an analogy to the Founding-era understanding that individuals who recovered from lunacy could be restored to full legal capacity, including the right to bear arms.

As I have examined this argument, it has some structural coherence — it avoids the most objectionable feature of permanent categorical bans — but it also carries a risk of appearing circular. The government is effectively arguing that the constitutional validity of mandatory disarmament is sustained by the theoretical ease with which the disarmed person could comply. Whether the Court finds this reasoning persuasive will depend on how strictly it applies the requirement that Founding-era analogues be genuinely comparable in both burden and justification.

## Implications

In my assessment, the oral argument in **United States v. Hammani** will determine how precisely the Court engages the textual and historical distinctions within § 922(g)(3). The most likely outcome is that the Court upholds the provision's facial validity while articulating limiting principles on the "unlawful user" prong that constrain the statute's reach to persons actively engaged in a pattern of ongoing drug use at or near the time of firearm possession. A ruling of that character — which preserves the government's ability to disarm persons in a state of active addiction or habitual use while immunizing episodic or historical users from prosecution — would represent an imperfect but meaningful application of originalist principles to a genuinely difficult constitutional question.
