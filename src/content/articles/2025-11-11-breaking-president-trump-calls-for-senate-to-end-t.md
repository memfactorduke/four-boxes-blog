## Summary

President Donald J. Trump has called on Senate Republicans to eliminate the legislative filibuster in connection with efforts to reopen the federal government during the recent shutdown. While I am an enthusiastic supporter of President Trump and his agenda, I respectfully disagree with this particular position. From the perspective of the Second Amendment community, the elimination of the legislative filibuster would represent a grave, potentially irreversible risk to the constitutional right to keep and bear arms. Examining the Democratic Party's stated intentions — court packing, a national "assault weapons" ban, and statehood for the District of Columbia and Puerto Rico — makes clear why Second Amendment supporters should approach any proposal to eliminate the filibuster with the greatest of caution.

## Background

The Senate filibuster is a procedural tradition unique to the United States Senate — it does not exist in the House of Representatives. In its modern form, the filibuster effectively requires sixty votes for "cloture" — the procedural motion to end debate and proceed to a vote on substantive legislation. This sixty-vote threshold applies to major legislative enactments: tax bills, gun control measures, healthcare legislation, and the like.

Notably, the filibuster as it currently applies has already been substantially eroded. Judicial nominations — including Supreme Court nominations — were exempted from the sixty-vote cloture requirement in 2013 and 2017, respectively. What remains is the sixty-vote requirement for substantive legislation, and it is that remaining protection that President Trump has urged Senate Republicans to eliminate.

The immediate context was the government shutdown: Senate Democrats, led by Minority Leader Chuck Schumer, were blocking passage of a continuing resolution to fund the government. President Trump argued that Republicans should eliminate the filibuster to pass a budget with a simple majority. As it happened, sufficient Democratic senators broke ranks to reopen the government without eliminating the filibuster, rendering the immediate question moot — but the underlying debate remains very much alive.

## The Democratic Agenda Without the Filibuster

To understand why Second Amendment supporters should resist eliminating the legislative filibuster, one must reckon with what the Democratic Party has publicly, repeatedly, and explicitly stated it will do when it next controls the Senate with a simple majority and the filibuster is gone.

The stated agenda includes four catastrophic items from a Second Amendment perspective:

**First**, enactment of a nationwide "assault weapons" ban and a ban on standard-capacity magazines. This is not speculation — it is Democratic Party platform policy, endorsed by virtually every Democratic senator.

**Second**, statehood for the District of Columbia and Puerto Rico. Both territories would immediately send two Democratic senators each to Congress, guaranteeing a four-seat Democratic gain in the Senate — likely making it functionally impossible for Republicans to ever again hold a Senate majority sufficient to block anti-gun legislation.

**Third**, national legislation prohibiting voter identification requirements, designed to reshape the electorate in ways that would entrench Democratic majorities.

**Fourth** — and most consequential for the courts — expansion of the Supreme Court from nine to thirteen or fifteen justices, followed by the immediate appointment and confirmation of multiple progressive justices. James Carville, the prominent Democratic strategist, stated as recently as this week that Democrats intend to do precisely this when they next hold unified government.

## The Court-Packing Threat to the Second Amendment

This last point demands special attention. The Supreme Court's landmark decisions in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008), **McDonald v. City of Chicago** (2010), and **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022) established that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms and mandates a text-and-history methodology for evaluating the constitutionality of firearms regulations. Those decisions rested on narrow majorities.

If Democrats pack the Supreme Court with five or six additional progressive justices — and then immediately enact a national assault weapons ban — that newly constituted Court will possess more than sufficient votes to uphold the ban in its entirety. The result would be that American citizens could be legally compelled to surrender their commonly owned firearms, and the constitutional protection established in Heller and Bruen would be effectively nullified.

I stated publicly during the 2016 presidential campaign, while serving as a media surrogate for Donald Trump, that this was precisely the trajectory a Hillary Clinton presidency would set in motion. The threat is no less real today. Democrats have been transparent about their intentions. We should take them at their word.

## The Asymmetric Risk

The question is not whether Republicans could benefit from eliminating the filibuster in the near term. Arguably, they could pass legislation they currently cannot. The question is whether the long-term risk to the Second Amendment justifies that short-term gain.

Eliminating the filibuster now would guarantee that when Democrats next achieve unified government — which they will, eventually — they can enact their entire anti-Second Amendment agenda with fifty-one Senate votes. There would be no procedural brake. No minority protection. No requirement for even a degree of bipartisan consensus before fundamental rights are legislatively destroyed.

The filibuster, whatever its imperfections, has served as one of the last institutional safeguards against the most sweeping anti-gun legislation in American history. Democrats have made clear they want it gone. The fact that they want it gone is perhaps the most compelling argument that Second Amendment supporters should want it preserved.

## Implications

The elimination of the legislative filibuster is not a partisan procedural adjustment. For the Second Amendment community, it is an existential question about whether the constitutional right to keep and bear arms can survive the next Democratic government. History, and Democratic politicians' own statements, give us every reason to believe it cannot — if the sixty-vote threshold disappears.

I respect and admire President Trump enormously, and I believe he has been the most consequential president for the Second Amendment in modern history through his Supreme Court appointments alone. On this one question, however, I believe the long-term interests of Second Amendment rights — and of constitutional governance generally — counsel against eliminating the legislative filibuster.

The current majority is temporary. The constitutional damage from an unconstrained future Democratic majority would not be.
