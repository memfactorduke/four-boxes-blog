## Summary

A proposed bill in the West Virginia legislature, drafted with the support of Gun Owners of America, would establish a state office authorized to acquire and transfer fully automatic machine guns to qualified citizens. In my analysis, this proposal rests on a textual foundation in federal law that has been consistently misunderstood: the Hughes Amendment to the Firearm Owners Protection Act of 1986, codified at 18 U.S.C. § 922(o), explicitly excludes transfers made "by" a state from its general prohibition on civilian machine gun transfers. That exclusion is not a drafting anomaly—it reflects the longstanding statutory and regulatory treatment of government entities as distinct from the "persons" subject to the prohibition. The Trump administration's Department of Justice has, in the course of other litigation, confirmed this interpretation in formal filings.

## Background

The Hughes Amendment, enacted as part of the Firearm Owners Protection Act of 1986, added 18 U.S.C. § 922(o) to the federal firearms statutes. The provision established what became widely understood as a near-total prohibition on civilian transfers of machine guns manufactured after May 19, 1986. The pre-existing registry of transferable machine guns—those manufactured and registered before that date—became a closed market, and the combination of finite supply and continued demand caused registered machine gun prices to appreciate substantially over the following decades. The conventional understanding of § 922(o) held that post-1986 machine guns were permanently unavailable to private citizens.

That conventional understanding, as I have reviewed the statutory text and regulatory framework, is incomplete. The Hughes Amendment contains a carve-out that has received insufficient attention, and recent developments in both federal litigation and state legislative activity have brought its significance into focus.

## The Statutory Text of 18 U.S.C. § 922(o)

The operative language of the Hughes Amendment provides:

> "Except as provided in paragraph (2), it shall be unlawful for any person to transfer or possess a machine gun."

Paragraph (2) then specifies the exceptions:

> "This subsection does not apply with respect to: (A) a transfer to or by or under the authority of the United States or any department or agency thereof or a State or a department, agency, or political subdivision thereof."

The critical phrase in paragraph (2)(A) is the disjunctive formulation "to or by." The statute does not merely exempt transfers *to* government entities—as one might expect if the purpose were simply to allow law enforcement acquisitions. It explicitly exempts transfers *by* government entities as well. The natural reading of "a transfer... by... a State" encompasses a state's transfer of a machine gun to a private citizen. If the legislature had intended to limit the exception to law enforcement and government-to-government transactions, it would not have included the word "by."

This textual observation is not a novel interpretive move. It follows directly from the ordinary meaning of the statutory language. A state may acquire a post-1986 machine gun, and a state may transfer a post-1986 machine gun. Both transactions fall within the explicit statutory exception. The prohibition on transfer and possession applies to "any person"—a term that, as I examine below, does not include state governments.

## The Regulatory Definition of "Person"

The scope of the Hughes Amendment's prohibition is further delimited by the regulatory definition of "person" applicable to National Firearms Act proceedings. 27 C.F.R. § 479.11 defines "person" for NFA purposes as:

> "A partnership, company, association, trust, corporation including each responsible person associated with such entity, an estate, or an individual."

The definition enumerates private-law entities and individuals. State governments are absent from the list. This regulatory definition is not incidental: it is reproduced on the face of ATF Form 5320.1, the application to make and register NFA firearms, which explicitly states that the form is not applicable to government entities. The ATF has, in its own administrative practice, consistently treated government entities as outside the definition of "person" for purposes of the machine gun transfer prohibition. This administrative interpretation is consistent with the statutory text and reinforces the conclusion that § 922(o)'s prohibition reaches only private-law entities and individuals.

## DOJ Confirmation in Recent Litigation

The interpretation I have outlined received authoritative executive branch confirmation in the case of **New Jersey v. Bondi**, pending in the United States District Court for the District of Maryland. In that litigation, the Department of Justice filed a brief stating that federal law "expressly provides that the machine gun prohibition does not apply to any transfer to or by the United States or any department or agency thereof," and specifically noted the "parallel language dealing with states in the same statute." The DOJ's brief further explained that this exclusion applied whether the transferor was "the US government agency or a state agency or the state itself," and concluded that "whether it be the US government agency or a state agency or the state itself is not bound by the Hughes Amendment."

This is a formal legal position taken by the executive branch in federal litigation. The Trump administration's Department of Justice has, in the course of defending a specific legal dispute, confirmed on the record that states are not bound by the Hughes Amendment's prohibition on machine gun transfers. That acknowledgment has consequences that extend beyond the immediate litigation, because it validates the statutory interpretation on which West Virginia's proposed legislation rests.

## The West Virginia Proposal

The proposed West Virginia legislation would create an Office of Public Defense charged with acquiring fully automatic machine guns—whether through purchase from manufacturers or manufacture in a state armory—and transferring them to qualified citizens. The bill's drafters have been deliberate in grounding the legislation in the statutory authority I have identified: the legislation specifies that all machine gun transfers made under its authority "shall be made by the State of West Virginia" and identifies the transfers as made "under the authority of the State of West Virginia, consistent with 18 U.S.C. § 922." The drafters are not seeking an implicit reading of the statute; they are invoking the explicit exception on its face.

The proposal contemplates two acquisition pathways. The state could purchase post-1986 machine guns directly from federally licensed manufacturers, which regularly sell to government customers under the existing exception. Alternatively, the state could manufacture machine guns in a state-operated armory. Both approaches are within the scope of the statutory exception as written. The state government is the transferor in each case, and § 922(o)(2)(A) explicitly exempts transfers by states from the prohibition.

## The Broader Constitutional Context

The *Bruen* framework, which requires the government to justify firearms restrictions by reference to the historical tradition of firearms regulation, bears on the constitutional landscape within which West Virginia's proposal operates. As I have noted in other analytical contexts, the founding generation understood the right to keep and bear arms to encompass weapons of genuine military utility—muskets, rifles, and the ordinary arms of a militiaman—not merely weapons suitable for sporting or self-defense purposes against individual criminals. The historical case for excluding machine guns, as a class of weapons in common use for lawful purposes, from Second Amendment protection is contested, and several federal courts have begun to examine that question with greater rigor in the post-*Bruen* era.

West Virginia's proposal does not require resolution of that constitutional question, because it operates entirely within the space the Hughes Amendment's own text creates. But the constitutional backdrop is relevant to understanding why the proposal matters as a matter of Second Amendment development: it represents a state government acting affirmatively within the scope of a federal statutory exception to expand firearms access for its citizens, using the legal architecture the federal statute itself provides. This approach to incrementally expanding Second Amendment protections through careful statutory analysis and state-level action reflects the kind of methodical, legally grounded advocacy that produces durable constitutional change.

## Implications

The West Virginia machine gun legislation, if enacted and successfully defended, would establish a precedent with potentially significant national consequences. Other states with sympathetic legislatures and legal environments could adopt similar frameworks, each operating within the § 922(o)(2)(A) exception. The aggregate effect would be to restore civilian access to post-1986 machine guns in states that elect to participate—a result that would represent a substantial shift from the status quo that has prevailed since 1986.

The legal foundation for this result rests on three pillars, each independently supportable: the plain text of 18 U.S.C. § 922(o)(2)(A), which expressly exempts transfers by states; the regulatory definition of "person" in 27 C.F.R. § 479.11, which excludes government entities; and the DOJ's own litigation position confirming that states are not bound by the Hughes Amendment. In my assessment, this is a textually coherent interpretation of an existing federal statute, not a novel constitutional theory or an aggressive reading of an ambiguous provision. The statute says what it says, and what it says is that states may transfer machine guns to citizens.

The significance of this development lies not only in the immediate policy question of machine gun access, but in what it demonstrates about the methodology of constitutional restoration through careful legal analysis. Incremental advances grounded in statutory text, regulatory interpretation, and executive branch confirmation accumulate into structural change—brick by brick, precedent by precedent, until the constitutional landscape has shifted in ways that would have seemed implausible at the outset.
