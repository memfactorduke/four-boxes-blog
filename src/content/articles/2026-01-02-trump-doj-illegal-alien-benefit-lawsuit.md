## Summary

The Trump Department of Justice has filed a major lawsuit in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of Virginia challenging the Commonwealth of Virginia's practice of classifying illegal aliens as state residents and extending to them in-state tuition rates and state-administered financial assistance at public colleges and universities, while requiring American citizens from other states to pay higher out-of-state tuition. The DOJ's complaint alleges that this practice violates federal law — specifically 8 U.S.C. § 1623, enacted as part of the Personal Responsibility and Work Opportunity Reconciliation Act of 1996 — which provides that no alien not lawfully present in the United States shall be eligible for any post-secondary education benefit on the basis of residence within a state unless a citizen or national of the United States is similarly eligible. In my assessment, this lawsuit is legally well-founded, strategically important, and directly connected to the protection of the constitutional rights of American citizens, including the right to keep and bear arms.

## Background: Virginia's In-State Tuition Policy

Virginia law classifies certain illegal aliens as state residents for tuition purposes. Specifically, Virginia's Education Code provisions allow illegal aliens — or the children of illegal aliens — who attended a Virginia high school to qualify for in-state tuition rates at Virginia's public colleges and universities. The same benefit extends to eligibility for state-administered financial assistance.

The consequence of this classification is that an American citizen from Georgia, North Carolina, Maryland, or any other state who applies to attend a Virginia public university must pay out-of-state tuition — a rate that can be tens of thousands of dollars higher per year than the in-state rate. That American citizen has less access to state-subsidized higher education in Virginia than a foreign national who is unlawfully present in the country but attended a Virginia high school. The American citizen is, in the DOJ's apt phrase, placed "at the back of the bus" relative to illegal aliens by the very state that they — as Americans — helped fund through federal tax dollars.

## The Federal Statutory Framework: 8 U.S.C. § 1623

The governing federal statute was enacted in 1996 under the leadership of Speaker Newt Gingrich in the House and signed into law by President Bill Clinton. It provides, without exception, that an alien who is not lawfully present in the United States shall not be eligible on the basis of residence within a state for any post-secondary education benefit unless a citizen or national of the United States is eligible for such benefit without regard to whether such citizen or national is such a resident.

The statute's plain text is clear. The operative prohibition is triggered when a state extends a benefit — here, in-state tuition rates — to an unlawfully present alien based on residence within the state, while that same benefit is not available to American citizens who are not residents of the state. Virginia's statute does exactly this: it classifies illegal aliens as Virginia residents and thereby makes them eligible for in-state tuition, while American citizens from other states remain ineligible.

As the DOJ complaint states: "Federal law prohibits states from providing aliens who are not lawfully present in the United States with any post-secondary education benefit that is denied to US citizens. There are no exceptions. Virginia violates it nonetheless."

## Federal Preemption and the Supremacy Clause

Beyond the direct statutory violation, Virginia's policy is preempted by federal immigration law under the Supremacy Clause of the United States Constitution. The regulation of immigration is a federal function. Congress has made a specific policy determination, reflected in § 1623, that states may not use public funds to extend benefits to unlawfully present aliens that are not available to American citizens on equal terms. A state statute that conflicts with this federal policy is preempted and unenforceable.

The DOJ's lawsuit seeks permanent injunctive relief enjoining the enforcement of the provisions of Virginia's Education Code that directly conflict with federal immigration law. The legal basis for this relief is well established: where state law conflicts with federal law in an area where Congress has explicitly legislated, the state law must yield.

## The Connection to Second Amendment Rights

I have consistently argued that the single most important structural threat to the Second Amendment — and to all constitutional rights of American citizens — is illegal immigration at scale. This is not primarily a cultural argument; it is a constitutional arithmetic argument.

The presence of tens of millions of foreign nationals who are unlawfully in this country creates political and apportionment effects that distort the balance of constitutional power. Congressional apportionment — which determines the allocation of seats in the House of Representatives and, correspondingly, votes in the Electoral College — is based on total population counts, not on citizen counts. States with large concentrations of unlawfully present foreign nationals receive additional congressional seats to which they would not otherwise be entitled based solely on their citizen population. This distortion shifts political power toward states — California, Illinois, New York — that are most aggressively anti-Second Amendment, and away from states that are most protective of constitutional rights.

Beyond the apportionment effect, policies like Virginia's in-state tuition preference actively incentivize illegal immigration by extending public benefits to those who violate immigration law. Every such incentive that is successfully challenged and enjoined reduces the political and economic pull factors that sustain illegal immigration at scale.

## The Significance of the Trump DOJ's Posture

Under the Obama and Biden administrations, the federal government not only declined to challenge state policies that extended benefits to illegal aliens but actively encouraged them. The Trump DOJ's decision to sue Virginia reflects a fundamental reorientation: the Department of Justice is deploying the legal power of the federal government to enforce federal immigration law against state non-compliance, in the same tradition as the enforcement actions that secured the Supremacy Clause's force in earlier generations.

This is consistent with a broader pattern. The Trump DOJ has filed lawsuits challenging anti-Second Amendment state laws in multiple jurisdictions. It has filed pro-Second Amendment amicus briefs in the federal circuits. It has established a Second Amendment unit tasked specifically with litigating to protect the right to keep and bear arms. The Virginia lawsuit fits within this broader framework: the Trump administration is using federal legal authority to reverse policies — whether they directly restrict firearms or indirectly undermine constitutional governance through immigration policy — that harm American citizens.

## Implications

The DOJ's lawsuit against Virginia is legally well-grounded, factually straightforward, and constitutionally important. The federal statute prohibiting in-state tuition benefits for unlawfully present aliens is unambiguous. Virginia's violation of it is equally unambiguous. If successful, this lawsuit will remove one significant incentive for illegal immigration, reduce the political distortions that large populations of unlawfully present foreign nationals create, and reaffirm the principle that American citizenship entitles Americans to benefits and protections that are not available to those who have violated federal law to enter or remain in this country. In my assessment, this is precisely the kind of enforcement action that the rule of law requires — and that the protection of all constitutional rights, including the right to keep and bear arms, ultimately depends upon.
