In my analysis, the Trump Department of Justice has taken a significant and favorable position on the scope of so-called "sensitive places" under the Second Amendment, conceding in federal litigation that the government bears a genuine and demanding burden to justify any location-based restriction on the right to carry under the historical tradition framework established by the Supreme Court in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. ## Background: Sensitive Places Under Bruen

The Supreme Court's 2022 decision in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen* established that the Second Amendment protects the right of law-abiding citizens to carry firearms outside the home for self-defense. The Court held that this right may not be restricted through subjective, discretionary licensing regimes that require applicants to demonstrate a particularized need for self-protection. Instead, the government must issue permits to all applicants who satisfy objective eligibility criteria.

The *Bruen* majority also recognized, in a passage that has generated significant subsequent litigation, that governments have historically restricted the carrying of firearms in certain "sensitive places" such as schools and government buildings. The Court acknowledged this historical tradition as a permissible basis for location-specific carry restrictions, but it also expressly cautioned that the sensitive places exception must be grounded in historical practice and cannot be stretched to encompass such broad categories of locations as to effectively eliminate the right to carry in public altogether.

The definition of "sensitive places" has been one of the most hotly contested questions in post-*Bruen* Second Amendment litigation. Lower courts have reached widely varying conclusions about which locations qualify, with some courts adopting expansive readings that would allow virtually any densely populated area or government-adjacent location to be designated a gun-free zone, and other courts applying a more rigorous historical analysis that limits the exception to locations with genuine historical antecedents.

## The Trump DOJ's Revised Position

The Trump Department of Justice, under the leadership of Attorney General Pam Bondi and in coordination with Solicitor General John Sauer, has adopted a legal position in federal litigation that applies the *Bruen* historical tradition framework rigorously to sensitive places claims. Rather than defending expansive gun-free zone designations on broad public safety grounds, the administration has acknowledged that the government bears a genuine burden to identify historical antecedents for each specific location or category of location it seeks to designate as a sensitive place.

In my analysis, this is the constitutionally correct approach. The *Bruen* framework does not permit governments to simply invoke the label "sensitive place" as a talisman to exempt any desired location from the Second Amendment's protections. The government must demonstrate, through historical evidence, that the specific type of location was treated as a sensitive place at or near the time of the Founding, or that it shares the essential characteristics that made certain locations sensitive places in historical practice.

## What Locations Qualify as Sensitive Places

In my assessment, the historical record supports a relatively confined category of genuine sensitive places. The Supreme Court in *Bruen* cited schools and government buildings as paradigmatic examples -- locations where there is a historical tradition of restricting the carry of arms that predates the modern era. Legislative chambers, courthouses, and polling places also have historical support.

What the historical record does not support, in my analysis, is the expansion of sensitive place designations to include all public transit systems, all bars and restaurants that serve alcohol, all public parks, all commercial entertainment venues, or all densely populated urban areas. These expansions -- which several states, including New York and New Jersey, have attempted following *Bruen* -- reflect an attempt to use the sensitive places exception to recreate, in substance, the very carry restrictions that *Bruen* invalidated.

The Trump DOJ's concession that the government must justify sensitive place designations through historical evidence, rather than through interest-balancing or invocations of public safety, is therefore a direct challenge to these overreaching state laws. It aligns the federal government's position with the originalist methodology that *Bruen* mandates and that the Second Amendment community has long advocated.

## Implications for Pending Litigation

The Trump administration's revised position on sensitive places will have direct consequences for a significant number of pending Second Amendment cases in federal courts across the country. In each of those cases, the federal government's litigating position -- whether as a party, as an amicus, or through the positions of the Solicitor General's office in briefing filed to the Supreme Court -- carries considerable weight.

When the federal government adopts and advocates for a rigorous historical tradition standard in sensitive places litigation, lower courts are more likely to apply that standard faithfully. Conversely, when the federal government expands or endorses overbroad sensitive place designations, it emboldens state and local governments to persist in constitutionally questionable restrictions.

By aligning its litigation strategy with the *Bruen* majority's original methodology, the Trump DOJ is, in my assessment, making a genuine and substantive contribution to the development of Second Amendment doctrine in the post-*Bruen* era.

## The Relationship to the Right to Carry

In my view, the sensitive places question cannot be understood in isolation from the fundamental right to carry that *Bruen* established. If the sensitive places exception is construed so broadly as to encompass the overwhelming majority of locations where a law-abiding citizen might reasonably be present, then the right to carry recognized in *Bruen* becomes a hollow formality -- a right that exists on paper but cannot be exercised in practice.

The Second Amendment protects a real and meaningful right to carry arms for self-defense. That right has the most significance in precisely the locations and circumstances where self-defense may be needed most urgently. Defining sensitive places narrowly, in accordance with historical practice, ensures that the right to carry retains genuine substance and is not gradually legislated out of existence through the accumulation of location-specific restrictions.

The Trump DOJ's adoption of a demanding historical standard for sensitive place designations is, therefore, not merely a legal position in pending litigation. It is a statement about the administration's understanding of the Second Amendment as a right that the executive branch has an obligation to protect, consistent with its oath to uphold the Constitution.
