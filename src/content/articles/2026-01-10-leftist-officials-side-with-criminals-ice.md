## Summary

A pattern of statements and conduct by certain local government officials — including public denunciations of federal Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) agents as "fake" law enforcement and explicit refusals to cooperate with federal immigration enforcement — illustrates in stark terms the foundational flaw in a central premise of gun control advocacy: the claim that law-abiding citizens need not possess firearms for self-defense because government law enforcement will protect them. When elected officials declare that they will not recognize or cooperate with federal agents performing lawful duties, and when the legal framework itself does not require police to protect any individual citizen, the argument that self-reliance is unnecessary collapses entirely.

## Background

### The Premise of Gun Control: Government as Protector

The core argument advanced by gun control proponents is, at its heart, a substitution argument: that the individual's need for a firearm for self-defense is diminished — or eliminated — because the state provides protection through law enforcement. On this view, the private possession of firearms is unnecessary, because 911 is always available and police will respond to protect life and property.

This argument has long been legally and practically dubious. In **Town of Castle Rock v. Gonzales**, 545 U.S. 748 (2005), the Supreme Court held, unambiguously, that individuals have no constitutional right to police enforcement of a restraining order — or, by extension, to any affirmative police protection. The Court's ruling reflects a broader common-law and constitutional principle: law enforcement agencies have a duty to the public generally, not to any specific individual. The police are not, in any legally cognizable sense, your personal bodyguard.

**Warren v. District of Columbia**, 444 A.2d 1 (D.C. Ct. App. 1981), reached the same conclusion at the state level, holding that the District of Columbia had no special duty to protect private citizens from criminal harm. Courts across the country have repeatedly applied this principle, rejecting tort claims by crime victims who argue that law enforcement failed to protect them.

The legal framework is thus clear: the government is under no obligation to protect any given individual citizen. The decision whether and how quickly to respond to any given call for assistance is discretionary. The police may be minutes away when seconds matter.

### When Officials Actively Undermine Enforcement

The current episode raises an even more troubling scenario: not merely the failure of officials to protect citizens, but the active obstruction of federal law enforcement by local officials who disagree with federal policy.

The conduct on display in Philadelphia exemplifies this dynamic. Philadelphia Sheriff Rochelle Bilal publicly characterized ICE agents as "fake made-up wannabe law enforcement" who wear masks "to commit crime." She pledged that the Philadelphia Sheriff's Office would not cooperate with ICE enforcement operations in the city, and that any ICE agents who "commit a crime" in Philadelphia would be prosecuted by the local district attorney. She framed ICE enforcement of federal immigration law — the removal of individuals unlawfully present in the United States — as itself criminal conduct.

This is not a policy disagreement expressed through legitimate political channels. It is a local official telling federal law enforcement agents, exercising authority explicitly granted by Congress, that they are criminals and that the local government will treat them as such. The implications for public safety and for the rule of law are profound.

## The Constitutional and Legal Framework

### Federal Immigration Enforcement Authority

The authority of the federal government to enforce immigration law is not a matter of dispute in constitutional law. Article I, Section 8 of the Constitution grants Congress the power to "establish a uniform Rule of Naturalization." Congress has exercised that power through a comprehensive statutory scheme — the Immigration and Nationality Act — that vests enforcement authority in federal agencies, including ICE. Federal agents carrying out lawful enforcement operations under this statutory scheme are engaged in constitutionally authorized activity.

The Supreme Court has repeatedly affirmed the primacy of the federal government in immigration matters. In **Arizona v. United States**, 567 U.S. 387 (2012), the Court held that states may not unilaterally impose their own immigration enforcement regimes in ways that conflict with federal law. The flip side of that principle — that state and local officials may not obstruct or undermine federal immigration enforcement — follows directly.

When a local sheriff characterizes lawful ICE operations as criminal conduct and pledges to arrest federal agents, she is not exercising legitimate local authority. She is obstructing the enforcement of federal law.

### The Duty-to-Protect Doctrine and Its Implications for Self-Defense

The interaction between the duty-to-protect doctrine and the conduct of officials who actively obstruct federal law enforcement produces a result of the utmost significance for Second Amendment analysis. If police have no legal duty to protect any individual citizen — and they do not — then citizens cannot rely on law enforcement as a substitute for personal self-defense capability. And if, in addition, local officials actively choose to undermine federal law enforcement for political reasons, the practical availability of government protection is further diminished.

In some jurisdictions, the officials most hostile to the Second Amendment are simultaneously the officials most willing to obstruct federal law enforcement and most sympathetic to policies that have historically correlated with higher rates of violent crime. This is not a coincidence. It reflects a coherent ideological posture in which individual citizens are rendered dependent on government institutions that those same officials have weakened, politicized, or compromised.

### The Case of Renee Good

The incident involving Renee Good — a woman shot by police in Minneapolis while in her vehicle during an ICE enforcement operation — illustrates the human cost of confused and politically driven law enforcement. Whatever the specific facts of that incident, it represents a situation in which law enforcement interactions went tragically wrong in the context of politically charged circumstances.

I submit that every such incident — in which the safety of law-abiding citizens is compromised by the politicization of law enforcement — makes the case for Second Amendment rights more urgent, not less. When citizens cannot trust that law enforcement will protect them, and when some officials have signaled their willingness to obstruct even federal law enforcement, the individual's ability to defend herself takes on heightened constitutional importance.

## The Second Amendment as the Answer to Government Failure

### Self-Defense as a Fundamental Right

**District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), established that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms for self-defense — most prominently, self-defense in the home. The Court's reasoning was grounded in the recognition that self-defense is "the central component" of the Second Amendment right and that the home is where the need for self-defense is most acute.

But the logic of **Heller** extends beyond the home. **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), extended Second Amendment protection to public carry for self-defense. The right to defend oneself does not vanish at the front door.

What **Heller** and **Bruen** together recognize — implicitly but powerfully — is that the individual's right to defend herself cannot be made contingent on the availability and willingness of government protection. The Second Amendment guarantees a right to effective self-defense capability precisely because the government cannot guarantee individual safety, and the Founders knew this.

### What the Philadelphia Sheriff's Statements Reveal

When the Philadelphia Sheriff publicly declares that she will obstruct federal law enforcement and will not protect residents who encounter agents engaged in lawful federal operations, she is — perhaps unwittingly — providing the most compelling possible argument for the individual right to keep and bear arms. Her statements demonstrate, with crystalline clarity, that there are circumstances in which government officials will not protect citizens, will not cooperate with those who seek to do so, and may even treat law-abiding law enforcement as enemies.

In such circumstances, the argument that citizens do not need firearms because the government will protect them is not merely unpersuasive. It is dangerous. It is an argument that leaves law-abiding citizens disarmed and dependent on officials who have demonstrated their unwillingness to provide the protection that gun control proponents claim makes individual armament unnecessary.

## Implications

### The Erosion of the Substitution Argument

The combination of the legal framework — no duty to protect individuals — and the political reality — officials willing to obstruct enforcement for ideological reasons — fatally undermines the substitution argument at the heart of gun control advocacy. Law-abiding citizens cannot rely on government protection as a substitute for personal self-defense capability, both because no such legal obligation exists and because political reality has made such protection unpredictable.

### The Importance of Understanding the Political Landscape

I have long argued that understanding who is actually seeking to restrict Second Amendment rights — and what their broader political agenda entails — is essential context for evaluating gun control proposals. When the same political figures who seek to restrict civilian firearms ownership are also those who weaken law enforcement, obstruct immigration enforcement, and politicize public safety agencies, the Second Amendment becomes not merely an individual right but a structural safeguard against the consequences of those policies.

The right to keep and bear arms is, at its core, the right of self-reliance — the recognition that each citizen bears ultimate responsibility for her own safety and that no government, however well-intentioned, can guarantee that safety. Officials who simultaneously seek to disarm citizens and undermine the law enforcement agencies that might otherwise protect them are, whether they recognize it or not, advocating for a state of affairs in which law-abiding citizens are left defenseless.

The Second Amendment exists precisely to prevent that outcome.
