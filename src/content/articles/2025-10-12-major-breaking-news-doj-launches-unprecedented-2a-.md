## Summary

On the evening of Saturday, October 11, 2025, Harlan Dillon, the Assistant Attorney General for Civil Rights at the United States Department of Justice and head of the DOJ's Second Amendment Task Force, published a series of posts on the social media platform X directly defending Attorney General Pam Bondi's record on Second Amendment matters. The statement is remarkable both for its content and for the unusual circumstances of its publication — an unscheduled, weekend evening communication from a Senate-confirmed senior official at the nation's top law enforcement agency. The posts lay out in specific terms what the Bondi DOJ has done to advance Second Amendment rights, address the controversy surrounding the membership list order in **Ree v. ATF**, and describe the administration's broader originalist litigation posture. I regard this statement as highly significant and largely correct.

## Background

Throughout the week leading up to this statement, portions of the Second Amendment community engaged in increasingly heated criticism of Attorney General Bondi following news coverage of the membership list provision in the **Ree v. ATF** final judgment. Some commentators called for Bondi's removal and characterized the provision — which was entered sua sponte by the district court and was not requested by the DOJ — as evidence that the Trump administration was attempting to construct a gun owner registry. I consistently and publicly disagreed with those characterizations, explaining that the DOJ had not sought this information, had specifically disclaimed any interest in obtaining it, and had moved jointly with the plaintiffs to vacate the provision.

By Saturday evening, Dillon took the unusual step of issuing a public defense of the Attorney General's Second Amendment record. The timing and manner of the communication — a weekend social media thread rather than a formal press release — suggests both the seriousness with which the DOJ regards the reputational damage inflicted by the controversy and the genuine commitment of senior officials to setting the record straight with the Second Amendment community directly.

## The Dillon Posts: Key Points

### The Ree Case and Membership Lists

Dillon's statement confirms the facts I had already reported and analyzed. The DOJ did not request the membership lists at issue in **Ree v. ATF**. The court entered the disclosure provision on its own initiative, without asking the parties whether they agreed to it. When the DOJ saw the provision, it immediately communicated its opposition to the plaintiffs and agreed to file a joint motion to vacate. The district court granted that motion. No membership list belonging to any Second Amendment organization will be disclosed to the federal government.

Dillon further explains the constitutional principles at stake. Compelled disclosure of the membership lists of advocacy organizations implicates the First Amendment's protection of freedom of association — a right the Supreme Court recognized in **NAACP v. Alabama** (1958) and has consistently protected in the decades since. The DOJ's position is that these constitutional principles apply with full force to gun rights organizations. Asking gun owners to register themselves with the federal government by virtue of their organizational membership is, as Dillon puts it, "inconsistent with Department of Justice policy."

### The Seventh Circuit Argument

Among the most significant disclosures in Dillon's statement is confirmation that she personally appeared before the United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit to argue on behalf of the United States in opposition to Illinois's assault weapons ban in **Harrel v. Raoul**. Illinois's law bans a broad category of semi-automatic firearms, including the AR-15 — the most popular rifle in the United States — as well as standard-capacity magazines and various accessories.

The constitutional case against Illinois's ban is straightforward under **Bruen** and **Heller**. The AR-15 is in common use by law-abiding citizens throughout the United States. Under **Heller**, arms in common use are presumptively protected. Under **Bruen**, the government bears the burden of demonstrating that its regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation at the time of the Founding and Reconstruction. There is no historical tradition of categorical bans on the most commonly owned rifles in America. As Dillon notes in her statement, Justice Thomas has written in favorable terms about the AR-15 as a firearm used for legitimate purposes by law-abiding citizens that is presumptively entitled to Second Amendment protection. The DOJ's argument to the Seventh Circuit is that Illinois's ban must be struck down.

### Originalism as the Guiding Framework

Dillon's statement also addresses the jurisprudential philosophy animating the DOJ's approach to Second Amendment litigation and constitutional litigation generally. The administration is committed to originalism — the interpretive methodology that derives constitutional meaning from the original public understanding of the text at the time of enactment. For the Second Amendment, this means that constitutional analysis must begin and end with what the phrase "right of the people to keep and bear Arms" meant to those who ratified it in 1791.

This commitment to originalism is not merely academic. It has direct, practical consequences for litigation strategy. An originalist DOJ will press for the application of **Bruen's** historical tradition test in every circuit, will oppose watered-down applications of that test, and will argue against interpretive frameworks that give courts discretion to ignore historical evidence in favor of policy-based balancing tests. As I have argued extensively, the single most important thing the Second Amendment community can do to protect and expand constitutional gun rights is to ensure that the institutions of the federal government — the courts, the executive branch, and the legislature — are populated by individuals committed to faithful application of the Constitution's original meaning.

### The DOJ's Overall Record

Dillon also references the DOJ's broader litigation record under Attorney General Bondi. Across all areas of litigation, the Trump DOJ has achieved an extraordinary record at the United States Supreme Court — approximately 25 wins and zero losses. This includes victories in immigration, executive power, and other high-stakes constitutional cases argued by Solicitor General John Sauer. Sauer, as the Senate-confirmed official responsible for representing the United States before the Supreme Court, works directly under Attorney General Bondi.

This record matters for the Second Amendment community for a straightforward reason: any Supreme Court case involving Second Amendment rights will be argued or supported by Sauer's office. A DOJ with a 25-0 record at the Supreme Court is a DOJ worth keeping. The political and practical reality is that Attorney General Bondi is not going to be removed from office, and the case for removing her — even if such a thing were desirable — cannot survive the arithmetic of Senate confirmation. The focus of the Second Amendment community should be on ensuring that the administration continues to advance Second Amendment rights, not on demanding leadership changes that are both impractical and counterproductive.

## Implications

The Saturday night statement by Harlan Dillon is, in my assessment, an important moment in the relationship between the Department of Justice and the Second Amendment community. For the first time in memory, a senior DOJ official has taken to public social media to specifically and systematically defend the administration's record on Second Amendment matters in response to criticism from gun rights advocates. This reflects both the seriousness with which senior DOJ officials regard the Second Amendment community and the administration's genuine commitment to Second Amendment rights.

The statement also serves a clarifying function. The controversy over the membership list provision in **Ree v. ATF** was fueled in significant part by inaccurate reporting and uninformed commentary. Dillon's statement, issued by an official with direct personal knowledge of the relevant facts, corrects the record. The DOJ did not seek a gun owner registry. It moved swiftly to undo the district court's sua sponte order. It is affirmatively litigating against assault weapons bans. It is committed to originalism. These are facts the Second Amendment community should know and should weigh in assessing the administration's overall record.
