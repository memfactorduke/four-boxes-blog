> "The Supreme Court heard argument today in *Wolford v. Lopez*, and if the questions from the bench tell us anything, Hawaii's so-called 'vampire rule' is about to get a stake through its constitutional heart."
>
> — Professor Mark W. Smith, Four Boxes Diner Host

Today, January 20, 2026, the Supreme Court of the United States heard oral argument in *Wolford v. Lopez*, No. 24-1046 — the most important Second Amendment carry case the Court has taken since *Bruen*. At issue: Hawaii's statute requiring that anyone who wishes to carry a firearm on private property open to the public first obtain "express authorization" from the property owner. Walk into a restaurant armed without a posted sign or verbal permission, and you are violating Hawaii law. Gun-rights observers have taken to calling it the "vampire rule" — you need an explicit invitation before your right to carry crosses the threshold. After watching the argument today, my read is that Hawaii came with very little to show for its position, and the conservative justices were not shy about saying so.

## What the Vampire Rule Actually Does

Hawaii's law, challenged by Jason Wolford, Alison Wolford, Atom Kasprzycki, and the Hawaii Firearms Coalition, essentially flips the default. Under ordinary property law, you may enter any place open to the public unless you are excluded. Under Hawaii's scheme, gun owners are excluded everywhere unless they are specifically invited. That inversion has no parallel in how we treat any other constitutional right.

Think about it: a journalist carrying a recorder into a shopping mall does not need the mall owner's express consent. A churchgoer carrying a Bible does not need posted authorization. A political activist does not need a sign on the door before wearing a campaign button into a restaurant. Only the Second Amendment right requires the gun owner to pre-clear exercise of the right with each private property owner in turn. That structural inequality is what *Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), was designed to correct: firearms regulations must be grounded in the Nation's historical tradition, and there is simply no Founding-era tradition of requiring armed citizens to collect express invitations from shopkeepers before entering.

The Ninth Circuit, in *Wolford v. Lopez*, 116 F.4th 959 (9th Cir. 2024), upheld the law anyway. Today's argument suggests that will not stand.

## Six Justices Who Noticed the Problem

The reaction from the bench was, to put it plainly, not kind to Hawaii. Petitioners were represented by Alan Beck. The United States came in on the side of the gun owners through Principal Deputy Solicitor General Sarah Harris, which is itself a significant signal about how the Trump DOJ views this case. Hawaii's counsel was Neal Katyal — a brilliant appellate advocate and a formidable opponent — and even he struggled to find solid ground.

Justice Kavanaugh was direct: he said he simply did not "see the kind of broad tradition here" that would be required for Hawaii's law to survive under *Bruen*. That is not a skeptical question looking for a better answer. That is a conclusion. When Kavanaugh says the historical record doesn't support the law, he is describing what he is going to write.

Chief Justice Roberts noted that the Second Amendment has functioned as a "disfavored right" in the lower courts — a criticism that has been building since *Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and that *Bruen* was explicitly designed to address. Roberts was not asking whether the Second Amendment had been mistreated. He was telling Katyal that it had.

Justice Barrett pressed Katyal on the logic of the rule: if Hawaii can require express authorization before carry on commercial property open to the public, can it require the same authorization before carry in private homes? Katyal said no. The follow-up — "Really?" — did not reflect agreement.

## The 1865 Louisiana Problem

Here is where the argument went somewhere remarkable. Hawaii, in its briefs, leaned heavily on an 1865 Louisiana statute that required property owners' consent before anyone could enter with firearms. That law, as every serious constitutional scholar knows, was enacted specifically to disarm freedmen and formerly enslaved people in the Reconstruction South — part of the wave of Black Codes designed to deny Second Amendment rights to Black Americans and leave them defenseless against the Ku Klux Klan and racist local law enforcement.

Justice Gorsuch, with characteristic precision, observed that many people who ordinarily treat laws like that one as they would "garlic in front of a vampire" — wanting nothing to do with them — were now citing it as constitutional support for a modern gun restriction. The irony was pointed, and it was not lost on the courtroom. Justice Alito amplified it: the Louisiana law, he noted, was designed to prevent the formerly enslaved from defending themselves. That Hawaii would ask the Supreme Court to uphold its gun ban by analogy to an anti-Black Reconstruction statute tells you a great deal about the weakness of the historical case.

## My Read Coming Out of Argument

Oral argument is not a verdict, and I have been wrong before when reading tea leaves. But a case in which six conservative justices express consistent skepticism about the historical record, in which the respondent's counsel is visibly searching for answers, and in which the United States has filed an amicus brief for the challengers — that is not a case that is going to be a 5-4 squeaker for the gun-banners. My initial read: *Wolford* will be reversed, and the opinion is likely to include language further tightening what kinds of "sensitive places" regulations survive *Bruen* scrutiny.
