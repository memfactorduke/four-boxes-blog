## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit has upheld Fairfax County, Virginia's ban on firearms in public parks, and the manner in which it did so is constitutionally fallacious. The panel, relying on the Supreme Court's 1987 decision in **United States v. Salerno**, held that because the county's no-guns-in-parks ordinance could be constitutionally applied to parks that happen to contain school facilities — where firearms bans may be permissible — the entire ordinance could not be struck down in a facial challenge. This reasoning is deeply flawed. It misapplies **Salerno**, mischaracterizes what a facial challenge requires in the Second Amendment context, and produces an outcome that the Supreme Court's methodology in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** plainly does not permit. The Fourth Circuit's sleight of hand transforms a critical conceptual distinction — facial challenge versus as-applied challenge — into a vehicle for preserving a sweeping gun ban in 416 of 420 Fairfax County parks where no school is present.

## Background

In **LeFave v. County of Fairfax**, plaintiffs challenged Fairfax County's ordinance prohibiting the possession of firearms in county parks. The plaintiffs argued that they wished to carry firearms in parks for self-defense, that the plain text of the Second Amendment covers such conduct, and that the County could not demonstrate a historical tradition of firearms regulation that would justify a blanket ban on guns in public parks at the time of the founding.

This is, on its face, a strong Second Amendment argument. Parks — which in the founding era were commonly called greens or commons — were ubiquitous in early American life. The American Revolution itself was ignited on the Green at Lexington and Concord. There is no meaningful historical tradition of banning firearms in public outdoor spaces that would satisfy the Bruen framework. The government cannot point to founding-era analogues for ordinances barring armed citizens from the public commons.

Faced with an argument it could not credibly answer on the merits, the Fourth Circuit instead resolved the case on an antecedent procedural ground: the nature of the challenge itself.

## Facial Challenges and As-Applied Challenges: The Basic Distinction

A facial challenge to a law is an argument that the law is unconstitutional in all its applications — that there is no set of circumstances under which it could be validly enforced. A successful facial challenge results in the law being struck down in its entirety, as written.

An as-applied challenge, by contrast, is an argument that a law, which might be constitutional as applied to others, is unconstitutional as applied to the specific plaintiff in the specific circumstances of the case. A successful as-applied challenge results only in the law being unenforceable against that particular plaintiff — the law itself remains on the books.

The Supreme Court addressed the facial challenge standard in **United States v. Salerno** (1987), stating that a facial challenge succeeds only if the plaintiff demonstrates that there is "no set of circumstances" in which the challenged statute could constitutionally apply. Under this demanding standard, if any constitutional application of the statute exists, the facial challenge fails.

## The Fourth Circuit's Reasoning and Its Fatal Flaw

The Fourth Circuit applied the Salerno standard as follows: Fairfax County has 420 parks. Four of those parks contain school facilities. The Supreme Court has indicated, in dicta, that governments may prohibit firearms in school zones. Therefore, the no-guns-in-parks ordinance has at least one constitutional application — parks containing schools. Because at least one constitutional application exists, the facial challenge must fail under Salerno, and the entire ordinance survives.

This reasoning is wrong for multiple reasons.

**First**, the application of Salerno in the Second Amendment context is incorrect. The Supreme Court in Bruen did not speak in Salerno terms. Bruen established a categorical text-and-history inquiry. The question is whether the historical tradition of firearms regulation permits a blanket ban on carrying firearms in public parks as a general matter — not whether some parks might be defensible on other grounds. The Fourth Circuit is using Salerno to graft a survivability escape valve onto the Bruen analysis that the Supreme Court never authorized.

**Second**, the Fourth Circuit's reasoning proves too much. Under its logic, virtually any gun ban covering a broad category of locations could survive facial challenge as long as the category includes at least one sub-location where guns could constitutionally be banned. A city could ban firearms on all sidewalks, and the ban would survive facial challenge as long as the city had a few sidewalks outside schools. A state could ban firearms in all commercial establishments, and the ban would survive as long as some establishments are in "sensitive places." The principle has no limiting principle.

**Third**, and most critically, the four parks that contain schools are not the parks at issue in this case. There are 416 parks in Fairfax County where no school is present. The plaintiffs want to carry in those parks. The government cannot point to a founding-era analogue for banning firearms in open public spaces — the greens, commons, and public grounds that were part of daily American life in 1791. The Fourth Circuit's ruling means that because guns can be banned in four parks, guns may be banned in all 420 — including the 416 where the school rationale is entirely inapplicable.

## The Salerno Problem in Second Amendment Law

**United States v. Salerno** was decided in a pre-Bruen world. Its "no set of circumstances" test may have logical application in other constitutional contexts. But it sits uncomfortably with the Second Amendment's historical-tradition methodology, which asks whether a regulation has founding-era support — a categorical legal question, not a case-by-case application question.

The better approach, consistent with Bruen, is to ask whether the challenged regulation as written has a founding-era historical analogue that would justify its categorical application. A blanket public parks ban does not. That parks existed and were used by armed citizens in 1791, and that no historical tradition of public parks disarmament can be identified, should be dispositive of the facial challenge under Bruen — regardless of what Salerno says about facial challenges in other constitutional settings.

The Fourth Circuit's reliance on Salerno is, in this context, a methodological sleight of hand: by invoking Salerno, the court avoids engaging with the Bruen historical-tradition question altogether, substituting a procedural escape hatch for the substantive Second Amendment analysis that Bruen demands.

## The As-Applied Alternative

Having rejected the facial challenge, the Fourth Circuit implicitly left open the possibility of an as-applied challenge. Plaintiffs who wish to carry in specific Fairfax County parks that contain no school facilities could, in theory, bring an as-applied challenge arguing that the ordinance cannot constitutionally be applied to them in those locations.

This is cold comfort. As-applied challenges are expensive, slow, and produce relief only for the individual plaintiff. They do not protect other county residents who want to carry in the same parks. They require litigation cycles that can take years. And in the interim, the unconstitutional ban remains in force against everyone except the successful as-applied challenger.

This is precisely the dynamic I discussed in the context of legislative versus adjudicative facts: anti-gun courts use procedural mechanisms — whether the legislative/adjudicative distinction or the facial/as-applied distinction — to ensure that unconstitutional gun bans stay on the books as long as possible, at maximum cost and delay to Second Amendment advocates.

## Implications

The Fourth Circuit's **LeFave** ruling illustrates why the Supreme Court must either clarify the applicability of Salerno in the Second Amendment context or grant certiorari in an appropriate case to address the facial challenge standard directly under Bruen.

The practical stakes are high. If the Salerno "no set of circumstances" standard can be deployed to defeat facial challenges to sweeping gun bans whenever the banned location category includes any sub-location where gun bans might be permissible, then Bruen's historical-tradition methodology will be functionally available only to as-applied challengers. That outcome would dramatically limit the scope of Second Amendment litigation victories and leave most challenged gun regulations on the books indefinitely.

The Second Amendment community must recognize this dynamic and push back against it — in briefing, in academic commentary, and in petitions for certiorari asking the Supreme Court to clarify that Bruen's categorical historical inquiry controls over a misapplied Salerno standard in the firearms context.
