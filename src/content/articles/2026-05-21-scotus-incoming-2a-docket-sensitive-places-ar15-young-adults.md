The Second Amendment Foundation and the Firearms Policy Coalition just filed a cert petition at the U.S. Supreme Court in *Kipke v. Moore*, challenging Maryland's so-called "sensitive places" laws — the government-mandated gun-free zones that proliferated after *Bruen* as an excuse to hollow out carry rights. That filing is the headline, but it's the broader SCOTUS docket picture that I want to work through today, because understanding where *Kipke* fits means understanding every other 2A case sitting on the Court's doorstep.

## The Two Cases Already Argued — Wolford and Hemani

Two Second Amendment cases have already been argued this term and will be decided by June 2026. The first is *Wolford v. Lopez*, 116 F.4th 959 (9th Cir. 2024), which challenges Hawaii's "vampire rule" — a law that flips the default presumption so that a licensed carrier cannot bring a firearm into any business open to the public without the owner's express permission. That inverts how constitutional rights work. The presumption should be that you may exercise your rights, not that you must beg permission at every gas station and grocery store. I expect that rule to fall.

The second is *United States v. Hemani*, which puts 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(3) — the unlawful-drug-user firearms ban — before the Court. I have been critical of Solicitor General D. John Sauer's decision to seek cert here, and I'll say it plainly again: this is a poor vehicle. The *Rahimi* Court, 602 U.S. 680 (2024), held that a finding of physically violent dangerousness can justify temporary disarmament. Whether casual marijuana use clears that bar is a stretch — and risking Second Amendment jurisprudence on that question was not necessary. I want to be clear that Todd Blanche, as Acting Attorney General, sits above Sauer in the DOJ hierarchy. If a similarly bad 2A vehicle surfaces in the future, Blanche has the authority — and I'd argue the obligation — to say no.

## Young Adults: GVR or Grant?

The young-adult cases have been held at the Court for months, and my read is that one of two things is happening. Either the Court GVRs them — vacating the lower court judgments and remanding for reconsideration in light of whatever *Wolford* and *Hemani* produce — or the justices intend to grant cert and take these up fresh next term.

The two cases are *NRA v. Bondi*, challenging Florida's Marjory Stoneman Douglas High School Public Safety Act after the en banc Eleventh Circuit upheld its under-21 firearm-purchase restrictions 8–4, and *West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF*, challenging 18 U.S.C. §§ 922(b)(1) and (c)(1)'s federal bar on FFLs selling handguns to 18–20-year-olds. Critically, the Fifth Circuit struck down those same federal provisions in *Reese v. ATF*, No. 23-30033 (5th Cir. Jan. 30, 2025). That circuit split is exactly the kind of trigger that pulls a grant.

My position here is straightforward: once you're a legal adult, you can vote, sign contracts, join the military, and get married. The argument that you somehow forfeit the right to defend yourself until age 21 is constitutionally incoherent, and I think the Court agrees.

## The AR-15 Backlog — Vehicles Matter

Here is where I want to be precise, because the AR-15 docket is crowded and not all vehicles are equal. *Viramontes v. Cook County* has been relisted more than ten times and is the most talked-about cert petition — but the Seventh Circuit's opinion has vehicle problems, with the panel faulting the plaintiffs for not developing the record. That burden-shifting is backwards: under *Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the government bears the burden of justifying restrictions by reference to historical tradition, not the other way around. Still, if I had to bet, the Court waits for cleaner vehicles.

Those cleaner vehicles are *Barnett v. Raoul* in the Seventh Circuit and *Cheeseman v. Platkin* in the Third Circuit. Judge Stephen McGlynn built a solid evidentiary record in the Southern District of Illinois before *Barnett* went up. And *Cheeseman*, heard en banc with new Trump appointees Judge Emil Bove and Judge Jennifer Mascott on the panel, has a real shot at producing a circuit split — the Third Circuit could well rule that AR-15s are protected arms under the Second Amendment, which forces the Court's hand. When one of those decisions comes down, I expect a cert grant to follow quickly.

Justice Brett Kavanaugh said as much when cert was denied in *Snope v. Brown* last June: the Court "should and presumably will address the AR-15 issue soon, in the next Term or two." Justices Thomas, Alito, and Gorsuch would have granted in *Snope* outright. The votes for a merits win on AR-15s are there — we just need the right case in front of them.

## Magazines, Sensitive Places, and My Order of Battle

The magazine cases — *Duncan v. Bonta* (California LCM ban) and *Gator's Custom Guns v. Washington* — have been relisted repeatedly with no action. One telling signal: when *Snope* was denied, Justice Kavanaugh issued a statement about AR-15s. No justice issued a comparable statement about magazines. That asymmetry tells me something about where Kavanaugh's enthusiasm actually lies.

I am not entirely confident we can carry Roberts, Kavanaugh, and Barrett on the magazine question today. I think we win eventually — magazines plainly facilitate armed self-defense and are "arms" under any honest reading of *Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and *Bruen* — but I do not want to test that before we bank the AR-15 win. A per curiam on plain-text coverage of magazines is not impossible, but my strong preference is: AR-15 first, then magazines and suppressors together.

*Kipke v. Moore* is the sensitive-places flagship, and it came out of a Fourth Circuit panel that included Judge J. Harvie Wilkinson III — someone I think several justices would enjoy correcting on Second Amendment methodology. But sensitive places, too, can wait. Win AR-15. Win young adults. Then take apart the gun-free-zone infrastructure case by case.
