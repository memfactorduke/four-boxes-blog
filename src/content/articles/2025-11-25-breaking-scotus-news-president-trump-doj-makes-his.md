## Summary

The United States Department of Justice, acting through the Office of the Solicitor General, has filed a merits brief in the United States Supreme Court in **Wolford v. Lopez**, arguing that Hawaii's so-called "vampire rule" — a law that effectively prohibits licensed carriers from carrying firearms on any private property open to the public without first obtaining unambiguous advance permission from the property owner — is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The brief represents a significant and welcome exercise of federal advocacy at the Court's highest level in support of the constitutional right to carry arms for self-defense outside the home.

## Background

In **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the Supreme Court held that the Second Amendment protects the right to carry a handgun for self-defense outside the home and that any governmental restriction on that right must be consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. **Bruen** struck down New York's "proper cause" requirement for concealed carry licenses as incompatible with this standard.

Hawaii's pre-**Bruen** carry licensing regime was equally restrictive — effectively making public carry unavailable to ordinary citizens. Following **Bruen**, Hawaii was required to dismantle that scheme. It did so on paper. But Hawaii simultaneously enacted a new restriction, codified as what critics have termed the "vampire rule," that effectively accomplishes the same suppression of the carry right through a different mechanism.

Under Hawaii's post-**Bruen** law, even a person who holds a valid concealed carry license is prohibited from carrying a firearm on any privately owned property that is open to the public — including gas stations, convenience stores, coffee shops, pharmacies, grocery stores, and virtually every other commercial establishment — unless the owner or manager of that specific property provides "unambiguous written or verbal authorization" or posts conspicuous signage affirmatively permitting firearms.

## The "Vampire Rule" and Its Constitutional Deficiency

The term "vampire rule" captures the practical operation of this scheme with precision. Like the folkloric vampire who cannot enter a dwelling without an invitation, a licensed carrier in Hawaii cannot enter any business without affirmative permission — and most businesses will never think to provide such permission because the question will never occur to them.

The result is that a lawful carrier in Hawaii is functionally prohibited from carrying outside the home in most real-world circumstances. The carry license has been rendered a nullity. This is exactly what the Supreme Court condemned in **Bruen**: a licensing regime that "effectively eliminates the right to public carry."

As the DOJ brief correctly observes, Hawaii's prior licensing scheme was invalidated by **Bruen** for this reason. Hawaii then responded not by implementing a genuine permit system, but by "loosening its licensing restrictions yet simultaneously enacting a new restriction that effectively nullifies those licenses and prevents public carry." The vampire rule is, in essence, Hawaii's attempt to accomplish through a "default no-carry" rule what it could no longer accomplish through a restrictive licensing regime.

## The DOJ Brief: Opening Arguments

The Solicitor General's brief opens with the following statement, which I consider both accurate and important:

> "The United States has a substantial interest in the preservation of the right to keep and bear arms and in the proper interpretation of the Second Amendment."

This framing matters. For too many years, the federal government appeared indifferent or even hostile to the Second Amendment rights of ordinary Americans. The statement that the United States has a "substantial interest" in preserving those rights signals a fundamental reorientation of the executive branch's constitutional posture.

The brief's substantive analysis proceeds from **Bruen**'s holding that the right to carry a handgun for self-defense outside the home "ranks among the Second Amendment's most basic guarantees." On this foundation, the DOJ argues that the government "cannot enact licensing regimes that effectively eliminate the right to public carry" and cannot restrict firearms access "without showing that the restriction fits within a discernible tradition of firearms regulation."

Hawaii has made no such showing. The vampire rule has no historical analogue. There is no tradition in American history of requiring citizens to obtain affirmative property-owner permission before exercising a fundamental constitutional right in ordinary commercial spaces open to the public.

## The Practical Absurdity of the Vampire Rule

The DOJ brief gives concrete expression to a practical problem that is immediately apparent to anyone who lives in the real world. Where, precisely, is a licensed carrier in Hawaii supposed to carry a firearm if that carrier cannot bring the weapon to a gas station, a convenience store, a coffee shop, a pharmacy, a grocery store, or a restaurant — because none of those establishments has thought to post a sign permitting firearms?

The answer, of course, is effectively nowhere outside the home except on public streets and sidewalks. Hawaii has thus accomplished through a private-property default rule what it could not accomplish directly: the functional elimination of the right to bear arms for self-defense in the daily life of its citizens.

The DOJ brief captures this dynamic when it states that Hawaii has effectively made it "a crime for licensees to carry firearms on private property open to the public — the very places where licensees would go in their daily lives."

## The Significance at the Supreme Court Level

The filing of a merits brief by the Solicitor General's office is a meaningful institutional act. The Solicitor General appears before the Supreme Court in every term and has established credibility as a voice of considered legal analysis. When the federal government tells the Supreme Court that a state law is unconstitutional, the Court takes that position seriously.

I have been critical of some of this administration's litigation choices at the trial court level — the DOJ's brief defending the NFA in the Southern District of Texas, discussed separately, raised significant concerns. But the Supreme Court is the most important court in the country, and it is at that level that getting the analysis right matters most. At the Supreme Court level, in this case, the Department of Justice has gotten it right.

## Implications

**Wolford v. Lopez** presents the Supreme Court with an opportunity to reaffirm and clarify **Bruen**'s practical scope. If the Court holds that Hawaii's vampire rule is unconstitutional — as I believe it should — that ruling will effectively foreclose any future attempt by anti-gun states to accomplish through default-prohibition rules what they are forbidden to accomplish through restrictive licensing.

The DOJ's decision to file a strong brief in support of the Second Amendment at the Supreme Court level, while simultaneously creating the Second Amendment Rights Section within the Civil Rights Division, reflects a coherent and encouraging pattern. The administration appears committed to using the full weight of federal legal authority to defend the right to keep and bear arms at the courts that matter most.

The citizens of Hawaii, and every American who exercises the constitutional right to public carry, deserve nothing less.
