## Summary

The Daily Wire has reported that officials within the Trump administration's Department of Justice have been internally discussing whether federal firearms law can be applied to restrict individuals who identify as transgender — on the theory that gender dysphoria constitutes a mental disorder under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4) — from possessing firearms. This report has generated significant controversy. I want to provide the definitive legal analysis: what federal law actually provides, what the Constitution actually permits, what the Supreme Court has said in **United States v. Rahimi** and related cases, and what this means for the Second Amendment community's strategic interests.

## Background

The Daily Wire reported, in the wake of a series of mass shootings involving transgender-identifying perpetrators, that DOJ officials have been reviewing "ways to ensure that mentally ill individuals suffering from gender dysphoria are unable to obtain firearms while they are unstable and unwell." A source inside the DOJ stated that the discussions center on the proposition that those who identify as transgender suffer from gender dysphoria, which the source characterized as "a mental disorder."

The incidents that prompted this discussion include, according to the reporting, the Annunciation Church shooting in Minnesota and the Nashville school shooting in Tennessee, both of which allegedly involved transgender-identifying individuals.

## The Statutory Framework: 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4)

The relevant federal provision is 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(4), which prohibits any person "who has been adjudicated as a mental defective or has been committed to a mental institution" from possessing, shipping, transporting, or receiving firearms or ammunition.

This provision has two distinct operative clauses. The first covers individuals who have been "adjudicated as a mental defective." Under the applicable federal regulations, this means a determination by a court, board, commission, or other lawful authority that a person, as a result of marked subnormal intelligence or mental illness, incompetency, condition, or disease, is a danger to himself or to others, or lacks the mental capacity to contract or manage his own affairs. The second clause covers individuals who have been "committed to a mental institution," meaning a formal involuntary commitment by a court, board, commission, or other lawful authority.

Both clauses share a critical feature: they require prior action by a legal authority. Neither clause applies based on a diagnosis alone, no matter how serious the diagnosis. A person who has been diagnosed with schizophrenia but has never been adjudicated as a mental defective or committed to a mental institution is not a prohibited person under § 922(g)(4). A person who has been diagnosed with gender dysphoria but has never been through either of those legal processes is likewise not a prohibited person under this provision.

## The Constitutional Analysis: Rahimi and Historical Tradition

The Supreme Court's 2024 decision in **United States v. Rahimi** is the controlling authority on the constitutional dimension of mental health disqualifications from firearms possession. In **Rahimi**, the Court upheld the federal statute that disarms individuals subject to domestic violence restraining orders where a court has made a specific finding that the individual poses a credible threat to the physical safety of an intimate partner.

The Court's holding rests on two pillars. First, the Constitution's historical tradition — extending from the Founding era forward — encompasses the authority to disarm individuals who have been found by legal process to pose a danger to others. Second, the finding of dangerousness must be individualized: a court or equivalent legal authority must have made a specific determination that this particular individual, based on specific facts, poses a credible and current threat of physical harm.

The historical tradition the Court identified is instructive. At the time of the Founding, English and American law recognized the authority to disarm "lunatics" — individuals who were not merely mentally ill but who were violently dangerous, and who had been determined by competent authority to be so. The categories of "idiots" and "lunatics" in early American law referred not to people with any mental abnormality, but specifically to people whose mental condition made them a physical danger to themselves or others.

This historical tradition supports the constitutional validity of § 922(g)(4) as applied to individuals who have been formally adjudicated as mental defectives or committed to mental institutions through processes that carry procedural safeguards. It does not support any broader categorical prohibition.

## What the Constitution Permits: The Individualized Adjudication Requirement

The constitutional line, as **Rahimi** draws it, is clear. The government may restrict firearms possession by an individual who has been found — through an individualized legal process with appropriate procedural safeguards — to be a danger to himself or to others. The government may not restrict firearms possession by a category of individuals based on shared demographic or diagnostic characteristics, without any individualized determination of dangerousness.

Applied to the question of gender dysphoria: the Constitution does not permit a categorical prohibition on firearms possession by all individuals who have received a gender dysphoria diagnosis. The vast majority of such individuals have never been adjudicated as mental defectives, have never been committed to mental institutions, and have never been found by any court to pose a danger to anyone. A categorical prohibition sweeping in all of them would be constitutionally indefensible.

What the Constitution does permit — and what existing law already provides — is application of § 922(g)(4) to specific individuals who have been formally adjudicated as mental defectives or committed to mental institutions, whatever the underlying condition that prompted that adjudication or commitment. If a court in Florida, invoking the Baker Act, commits an individual to a mental institution after finding that the individual is a danger to himself or to others, and if that commitment is properly reported to the National Instant Criminal Background Check System, that individual is already a prohibited person under federal law. The fact that gender dysphoria was one of the conditions involved in that determination does not change the legal analysis — but the adjudication or commitment itself must have occurred.

## The Baker Act and Civil Commitment

Florida's Baker Act is an illustrative example of the state-law civil commitment framework that interacts with federal firearms law. Under the Baker Act, an individual may be involuntarily committed for mental health evaluation and treatment if a law enforcement officer, mental health professional, or judge finds that the individual is mentally ill and poses a real and present danger to himself or others. An involuntary commitment under the Baker Act is a "commitment to a mental institution" for purposes of § 922(g)(4).

The critical point is that the Baker Act commitment requires an individualized finding of dangerousness. Not every person with a mental health diagnosis qualifies. The law demands a specific determination, made by a qualified professional or a judge, that this particular individual, at this particular time, poses a danger. That is the constitutional floor that **Rahimi** establishes, and it is a floor that the Baker Act satisfies.

## The Strategic Implications for the Second Amendment Community

There is an important strategic argument that the Second Amendment community should consider. The greatest practical threat to Second Amendment rights is not the occasional overreach of gun-control advocacy organizations — it is mass shootings. Every mass shooting, regardless of the political or demographic identity of the perpetrator, generates enormous pressure for gun-control legislation, and that pressure has historically resulted in restrictions on the law-abiding majority who bear no responsibility for the crimes of a few.

The strategically sound position for the Second Amendment community is this: vigorously oppose any categorical, status-based prohibition that lacks constitutional foundation; simultaneously support rigorous enforcement of the individualized adjudication framework that already exists in § 922(g)(4); and demand that states improve their reporting of mental health adjudications and commitments to the NICS system so that people who are already legally prohibited from possessing firearms actually appear in the background check database.

If genuinely dangerous individuals — those who have been found by courts to pose a credible danger — are actually prevented from purchasing firearms through the background check system, the result is fewer mass shootings, less political pressure for broad gun-control legislation, and stronger public support for the Second Amendment. The constitutional framework already provides the tools; what is needed is the political will to enforce it faithfully and consistently.

## Implications

The DOJ's internal discussions about applying the existing mental health disqualification framework to individuals diagnosed with gender dysphoria is legally and constitutionally defensible only to the extent that it is limited to individuals who have been formally adjudicated as mental defectives or committed to mental institutions through processes with adequate procedural safeguards. A categorical prohibition based on diagnosis alone is constitutionally untenable and should be opposed by the Second Amendment community as vigorously as any other attempt to create extra-constitutional prohibited-persons categories.

The existing law is both constitutionally sound and practically sufficient, if faithfully enforced. The focus of reform efforts should be on ensuring that qualifying adjudications and commitments are actually reported to NICS — a systematic failure that has allowed numerous mass shooters to purchase firearms despite being legally disqualified — rather than on expanding the scope of the prohibition in ways that the Constitution does not support.

The Second Amendment protects all law-abiding Americans. That protection is best served by a legal framework that is constitutionally principled, rigorously enforced, and clearly distinguished from the kind of categorical, demographic-based disarmament that the gun-control movement has long sought to impose.
