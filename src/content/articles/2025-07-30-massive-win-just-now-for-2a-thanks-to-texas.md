## Summary

The state of Texas, acting at the urging of President Trump and Vice President Vance, has redrawn its congressional district maps in advance of the 2026 midterm elections. That decision, while not a judicial ruling or a new piece of legislation, is among the most consequential recent developments for the long-term protection of Second Amendment rights in America. The reason is arithmetic: Republicans currently hold the House of Representatives by a margin of 219 to 212, Democrats need only approximately six additional seats to reclaim the majority, and historical patterns strongly suggest that the party controlling the White House tends to lose House seats in midterm elections. If Democrats flip the House in 2026, Hakeem Jeffries — whom Everytown for Gun Safety named its "gun sense lawmaker of the year" in 2024 — becomes Speaker of the House, and every piece of pro-Second Amendment legislation dies on arrival. Texas's decision to act now, before the 2026 cycle, is a meaningful step toward preserving a legislative environment in which Second Amendment rights can be protected and extended.

## Background

The United States House of Representatives consists of 435 members apportioned among the fifty states by population, as measured by the decennial census mandated by the Constitution. Each state legislature is responsible, within constitutional and statutory constraints, for drawing the boundaries of its congressional districts — a process that directly determines how many members of each party are likely to be elected from that state.

The process of drawing district lines in a manner advantageous to the controlling political party is commonly known as gerrymandering, a practice with roots in the early republic. While courts have addressed the most egregious racial gerrymandering as a constitutional violation, the Supreme Court has held that partisan gerrymandering — drawing lines to advantage one political party over another — presents a political question beyond the reach of federal judicial review. That holding means that states retain broad discretion to draw congressional maps in ways that reflect the partisan priorities of their controlling legislatures.

Historically, Democratic-controlled states including California, Illinois, and New York have used this discretion aggressively. California's congressional maps, drawn by a body ostensibly designed to reduce political influence, have consistently produced outcomes favorable to Democrats. Illinois and New York have pursued similarly aggressive gerrymanders, effectively removing competitive districts and engineering large delegations of Democratic representatives from states whose overall political composition might otherwise support more balanced delegations.

## The 2026 Stakes for the Second Amendment

The present House composition — 219 Republicans to 212 Democrats — is narrow by historical standards. Democrats require only approximately six net gains to flip the majority. Under historical patterns, the party holding the White House at the time of a midterm election tends to lose House seats. Republicans are not immune from this phenomenon. The combination of a narrow margin and historical headwinds creates a genuine and serious possibility that Democrats will reclaim the House majority after the 2026 midterms.

The identity of the next Speaker of the House matters enormously to the Second Amendment community. Hakeem Jeffries, the current House minority leader and the person who would almost certainly become Speaker in the event of a Democratic majority, has been recognized by Everytown for Gun Safety — the primary anti-Second-Amendment advocacy organization in the United States — as its federal "gun sense lawmaker of the year" for 2024. His legislative record includes support for banning so-called assault weapons, limiting standard-capacity magazines, restricting the ability of eighteen-to-twenty-year-old adults to acquire firearms, and banning so-called ghost guns. There is no category of firearms restriction that Jeffries has declined to support. A House led by Jeffries would present an institutional blockade against every category of pro-Second Amendment legislation.

The legislative agenda at stake is substantial. The Second Amendment community has identified several major legislative priorities for the current Congress: removing suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns entirely from the National Firearms Act through a subsequent reconciliation bill; enacting national concealed carry reciprocity; and defending the gains achieved in the One Big Beautiful Bill. None of these objectives can be accomplished — or defended — through a House controlled by an anti-Second-Amendment majority leadership.

## Texas's Decision to Act

The state of Texas currently holds thirty-eight congressional seats. The composition of Texas's congressional delegation has significant arithmetic implications for the national House majority. Texas has historically elected a large Republican delegation, but the specific configuration of district lines determines how many of those seats are competitive, how many are safe Republican seats, and how many might be vulnerable to a swing toward Democrats in a favorable national environment.

The decision by Texas — at the express urging of the Trump-Vance administration — to redraw its congressional maps before the 2026 cycle reflects a strategic recognition that the current maps may not be optimally configured to withstand the electoral environment a midterm cycle typically produces. A state with as large and diverse a congressional delegation as Texas has substantial latitude to configure its districts in ways that protect existing Republican incumbents and potentially create additional favorable Republican seats, effectively raising the floor of the Republican delegation's minimum expected performance in an unfavorable election year.

Republicans have in many prior election cycles declined to match the aggressive gerrymander strategies employed by Democratic legislatures in California, Illinois, and New York. That asymmetry has contributed to structural disadvantages in the national House composition that do not reflect the underlying partisan composition of the national electorate. Texas's willingness to redraw its maps represents a belated but substantive effort to compete on the same terms that have long been employed by the opposing party.

## The Relationship Between Electoral Outcomes and Second Amendment Rights

The Second Amendment community sometimes views elections and legislative outcomes as separate from, or secondary to, the work of litigation and constitutional adjudication. That compartmentalization is a strategic error. The courts do not operate in a vacuum. Judicial appointments — at every level of the federal judiciary — are made by elected presidents and confirmed by elected senators. The composition of the lower federal courts, which hear the vast majority of Second Amendment cases that will never reach the Supreme Court, depends entirely on who holds the White House and the Senate.

Similarly, the legislative path for removing items from the NFA, for enacting national reciprocity, and for defending against future gun control proposals depends on who controls the House. The litigation victories achieved in *Bruen* and its progeny can be partially preserved through the courts regardless of the legislative environment, but they cannot be extended, and they can be threatened, by legislation that a hostile House majority could pass.

The Texas redistricting is, in the direct and immediate sense, a political act undertaken by elected officials in a legislative process. But for the Second Amendment community, it is also a defensive act that seeks to protect the legislative environment in which Second Amendment-protecting laws can be passed and anti-Second-Amendment laws can be blocked. Understanding that connection — between maps, elections, legislative majorities, and constitutional rights — is essential for anyone who takes the Second Amendment's protection seriously.
