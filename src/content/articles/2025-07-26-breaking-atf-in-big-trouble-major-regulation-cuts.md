## Summary

The Trump administration, working through the Department of Government Efficiency and in coordination with artificial intelligence tools deployed across the executive branch, is conducting a systematic review of the entire Code of Federal Regulations with the stated objective of eliminating fifty percent of existing federal regulations by the first anniversary of the current administration. Reports indicate that as many as fifty or more ATF-related regulations have been identified as candidates for revision, reduction, or elimination. The legal foundation for this initiative is straightforward and sound: the AI-assisted review process works by comparing the statutory text that Congress enacted — which is the supreme law of the land beneath the Constitution — against the regulations that agencies have promulgated pursuant to those statutes, and identifying provisions in which agency regulation has exceeded the scope of the statutory authority conferred by Congress. In the post-*Loper Bright* legal environment, in which federal courts no longer defer to agency interpretations of ambiguous statutory language, this exercise of identifying and eliminating ultra vires regulatory provisions is not merely a policy preference. It is a constitutional and administrative law imperative. For the Second Amendment community, the prospect of eliminating fifty or more ATF regulations that lack clear statutory authorization represents among the most consequential near-term developments in the effort to restore the right to keep and bear arms to its full constitutional dimensions.

## Background

The Code of Federal Regulations is the codification of the general and permanent rules published in the Federal Register by the departments and agencies of the federal government. It currently encompasses approximately 185,000 pages across 50 titles, the product of decades of agency rulemaking by unelected bureaucrats operating with varying degrees of fidelity to the statutory authority that Congress actually delegated. Title 27 of the Code of Federal Regulations, which governs Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives, contains the ATF's implementing regulations for the Gun Control Act of 1968, the National Firearms Act, the Arms Export Control Act, and related statutes.

The administrative regulatory state did not arrive fully formed. It grew incrementally, through successive administrations and across generations of agency personnel, as federal agencies exercised — and in many instances exceeded — the statutory authority that Congress delegated to them. The mechanism by which this excess occurred was often subtle: Congress would enact a broadly worded statute, and the agency charged with implementing it would promulgate regulations that interpreted the statutory language in the broadest possible manner, extending the agency's jurisdiction as far as the elastic terms of the statute could plausibly be stretched. Under the doctrine established by the Supreme Court in *Chevron U.S.A., Inc. v. Natural Resources Defense Council*, 467 U.S. 837 (1984), courts were instructed to defer to reasonable agency interpretations of ambiguous statutes — a doctrine that provided substantial judicial cover for aggressive regulatory expansion.

The Supreme Court's 2024 decision in **Loper Bright Enterprises v. Raimondo**, 144 S. Ct. 2244 (2024), overruled *Chevron* deference. Courts are no longer instructed to defer to agency interpretations of ambiguous statutory text. The question of what a statute means is a legal question, and legal questions are for courts to resolve independently. The practical implication of this shift is that the many ATF regulations that were promulgated on the basis of expansive interpretations of ambiguous statutory language — interpretations that courts formerly deferred to and would no longer defer to today — are now legally vulnerable in a way they were not before *Loper Bright*.

## The AI-Assisted Regulatory Review Process

The mechanism being deployed by the Trump administration to identify and eliminate ultra vires regulations is analytically straightforward, even if its scale is unprecedented. Artificial intelligence tools are being used to compare, in parallel across the entire Code of Federal Regulations, two bodies of text: the statutory authorizations enacted by Congress on the one hand, and the regulations promulgated by agencies pursuant to those authorizations on the other. Where the regulatory text prescribes conduct, imposes requirements, or exercises authority that is not traceable to the statutory authorization Congress provided, the regulation is identified as a candidate for elimination or revision as exceeding the agency's statutory authority.

This is not a novel legal theory. The principle that agencies may act only within the authority conferred upon them by Congress — that they may not manufacture authority from ambiguous or general statutory language to impose requirements Congress never authorized — has been a foundational tenet of administrative law for as long as the administrative state has existed. What is new is the scale and speed at which modern artificial intelligence makes it possible to conduct this comparative analysis across the full breadth of federal regulations. The review that would have required years of human legal analysis can now be accomplished with a degree of comprehensiveness that was previously impractical.

Reports indicate that the ATF has been identified as an agency with a particularly significant number of regulations that lack adequate statutory grounding. As many as fifty or more ATF-related regulations have been identified as candidates for revamp, reduction, or elimination. The ATF inspector corps is reported to be subject to reductions of over five hundred positions. Rob Lighter, serving as chief counsel at ATF under the current administration, is among the personnel responsible for ensuring that the agency's enforcement priorities and regulatory posture align with what the relevant statutes actually authorize rather than what prior administrations wished those statutes had authorized.

## The Administrative Law Framework for Regulatory Elimination

The formal elimination of a federal regulation requires notice-and-comment rulemaking under the Administrative Procedure Act, 5 U.S.C. § 553. An agency seeking to rescind or significantly modify an existing regulation must publish a notice of proposed rulemaking in the Federal Register, provide an opportunity for public comment, consider the comments received, and publish a final rule with a reasoned explanation for the regulatory change. This procedural requirement applies whether the agency is adding regulatory obligations or removing them.

The AI-assisted review process being described does not itself accomplish regulatory elimination. What it accomplishes is the identification of the specific regulations that are candidates for elimination — a targeting function that allows the notice-and-comment process to be directed with precision at the provisions most clearly lacking statutory support. The identification phase and the elimination phase are legally distinct. The identification process can be conducted rapidly, using AI to perform the statutory-versus-regulatory comparison at scale. The elimination phase requires the notice-and-comment process, which takes time. But the sequencing is logical: identify the targets first, then proceed through the required procedural steps to eliminate them.

The administration's stated objective of eliminating fifty percent of federal regulations within approximately six months of the initiative's launch reflects the aspiration; actual regulatory elimination will proceed through the required administrative process at whatever pace the agencies are capable of sustaining. What the AI identification process promises is not a shortcut around the APA's procedural requirements, but a means of ensuring that the procedural effort is directed efficiently at the regulations with the most defensible legal basis for elimination.

## The Significance for Second Amendment Rights

The question of ATF regulatory authority is not merely a matter of administrative housekeeping. The ATF has used its regulatory authority — often aggressively and, in recent years, in ways that the courts have found to exceed statutory authorization — to define the scope of federal firearms law in ways that affect every gun owner in the United States. Recent examples illustrate the pattern.

The ATF's rule redefining bump stocks as machine guns was struck down by the Supreme Court in **Garland v. Cargill**, 602 U.S. 406 (2024), on the ground that the statutory definition of "machinegun" in the National Firearms Act did not encompass bump stocks, regardless of the ATF's regulatory interpretation. The Court's analysis was direct: the ATF had stretched the statutory text beyond its ordinary meaning to accomplish a policy result that Congress had not authorized. *Cargill* is an illustration of what systematic AI-assisted review might identify at scale — regulatory provisions that rest on interpretations of statutory language that the actual statutory text will not support.

The ATF's 2022 rulemaking redefining "frame or receiver" for purposes of the GCA's serialization and background check requirements was similarly challenged in the courts on statutory authority grounds, with courts finding that the rule exceeded the agency's delegated authority. The ATF's attempted administrative redefinition of pistols with stabilizing braces as short-barrel rifles subject to NFA registration requirements was vacated by the Fifth Circuit on administrative law grounds.

The pattern is consistent. The ATF, across multiple administrations and across a regulatory history spanning decades, has repeatedly attempted to expand its statutory authority through regulatory interpretation. The courts — particularly in the post-*Loper Bright* environment — have increasingly declined to ratify these expansions. An AI-assisted review process that systematically identifies ATF regulations lacking adequate statutory support would accelerate and systematize what the courts have been doing case by case: enforcing the requirement that agency authority be traceable to congressional authorization.

## The Major Questions Doctrine as Additional Constraint

Beyond the post-*Loper Bright* rejection of *Chevron* deference, the Supreme Court's major questions doctrine provides an additional constraint on ATF regulatory authority. In **West Virginia v. Environmental Protection Agency**, 597 U.S. 697 (2022), the Court held that when an agency claims authority to regulate a matter of vast economic and political significance, it must identify clear statutory authorization for that claimed authority. Vague or general delegations of regulatory authority are insufficient to support major regulatory exercises.

The firearms regulatory context presents questions of significant constitutional and policy magnitude. Regulations that affect the practical exercise of the Second Amendment right across millions of firearms owners are not minor technical matters. Under the major questions doctrine, the ATF cannot derive authority to impose those regulations from ambiguous or general statutory language. The combination of *Loper Bright*'s elimination of *Chevron* deference and the major questions doctrine's demand for clear authorization in high-stakes regulatory contexts substantially narrows the space within which ATF regulations can survive legal scrutiny.

## Implications

The Trump administration's deployment of artificial intelligence to identify ultra vires federal regulations, with particular attention to the ATF, represents a significant structural development in the effort to bring the administrative regulatory state within the bounds of congressionally conferred authority. For the Second Amendment community, this initiative has practical significance that extends beyond its administrative law dimensions.

Every ATF regulation that lacks adequate statutory authorization is a regulation that imposes burdens on law-abiding gun owners without the sanction of the elected legislature that represents them. The Constitution's separation of powers does not permit agencies to legislate through regulation; only Congress may exercise legislative power. The systematic identification and elimination of ATF regulations that exceed the agency's statutory mandate is therefore not merely a technical exercise in administrative law tidiness. It is a restoration of the constitutional order in which the rights of citizens are limited only by the choices of their elected representatives, not by the interpretive preferences of unelected bureaucrats who have, over many decades, accumulated authority that was never legitimately theirs.

The notice-and-comment process required to formally eliminate these regulations will take time, and not all candidates for elimination will survive political and legal challenges during the rulemaking process. But the initiative, if pursued with the comprehensiveness and rigor that AI-assisted analysis makes possible, could produce a landscape of ATF regulation substantially more faithful to the statutory authority Congress actually conferred — and substantially less burdensome to the millions of law-abiding Americans whose exercise of Second Amendment rights has long been constrained by regulations that were never legitimately authorized.
