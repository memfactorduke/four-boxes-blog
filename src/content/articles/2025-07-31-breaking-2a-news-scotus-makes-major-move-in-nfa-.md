## Summary

The Supreme Court of the United States has entered a significant order in **Rush v. United States**, directing the Trump administration's Department of Justice to file a response by September 2, 2025, addressing the question whether the Second Amendment secures the right to possess unregistered short-barrel rifles that are in common use for lawful purposes. The case arises from the criminal conviction of an individual, Mr. Rush, who was found in possession of a short-barrel rifle that had not been registered under the National Firearms Act and on which no NFA tax had been paid. He was sentenced to thirty months in federal prison. The National Rifle Association filed an amicus brief in support of his petition for certiorari. The Court's call for the Solicitor General's views signals meaningful interest in the question presented — interest that would not exist if the Justices were prepared to summarily deny review. This order is a significant development in the ongoing effort to bring the question of NFA-covered arm protection before the Supreme Court for definitive resolution under the text-and-history framework of **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022).

## Background

Federal law, codified at 26 U.S.C. § 5861 and related provisions of the National Firearms Act, requires that any rifle having a barrel shorter than sixteen inches be registered with the federal government and that a $200 tax be paid. Possession of an unregistered short-barrel rifle is punishable by up to ten years' imprisonment and a $250,000 fine. Mr. Rush was prosecuted under these provisions and convicted. His defense raised the Second Amendment as a bar to his prosecution — specifically arguing that short-barrel rifles are constitutionally protected arms and that the NFA's registration and taxation requirements as applied to him cannot survive scrutiny under *Bruen*'s text-and-history framework.

The lower courts have produced deeply inconsistent results in cases challenging NFA restrictions and arms bans more broadly. The Seventh Circuit Court of Appeals — the most reliably problematic federal court of appeals on Second Amendment questions — has issued opinions inconsistent not only with the Supreme Court's controlling precedents but, in some instances, with its own prior decisions. The Fourth Circuit and D.C. Circuit have similarly shown a persistent inability or unwillingness to apply *Bruen* faithfully. The consistent pattern across these courts is a willingness to manufacture analytical escape routes from the text-and-history methodology that *Bruen* made mandatory.

The NRA's decision to file an amicus brief in support of Mr. Rush's petition reflects the organization's recognition that this case presents a vehicle for reaching the Supreme Court on the specific question of whether short-barrel rifles — and by extension, the broader category of NFA-regulated arms — are constitutionally protected. Justice Kavanaugh's concurrence in the denial of certiorari in a recent Fourth Circuit AR-15 case, *Snopes v. Maryland*, stated explicitly that the Court expects to take an arms-ban or arms-restriction case in the near term to resolve definitively what categories of arms the Second Amendment protects. The **Rush** order should be read in that context.

## The Significance of the CVSG Order

When the Supreme Court calls for the views of the Solicitor General — a practice known as a CVSG — it signals that at least some Justices consider the petition worth examining carefully before deciding whether to grant or deny review. The Court does not issue CVSG orders routinely. Such orders are reserved for cases where the Justices want to know, before resolving the certiorari question, what position the executive branch takes on the merits and whether the government considers review appropriate.

In **Rush v. United States**, the CVSG order directed the Trump DOJ to file its response by September 2, 2025. The Court is asking the executive branch to address the question of whether the Second Amendment protects the possession of unregistered short-barrel rifles in common use for lawful purposes. The administration's response will be consequential.

If the Trump DOJ takes the position that short-barrel rifles are constitutionally protected arms — consistent with its prior concession in **United States v. Peterson** that suppressors are protected arms — that position will substantially support granting certiorari and will constrain the government's ability to defend the NFA's registration requirements on the merits. If the DOJ instead defends the NFA registration requirements aggressively and argues against certiorari, that posture will be harder to reconcile with the administration's stated pro-Second Amendment commitments, and it will be noticed by the Second Amendment community.

## The Legal Question Presented

The question in **Rush** — whether the Second Amendment secures the right to possess unregistered short-barrel rifles in common use for lawful purposes — implicates two distinct analytical issues under *Bruen*: whether the covered arm is textually an "arm" within the meaning of the Second Amendment, and if so, whether the NFA's registration and taxation requirements are consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation.

On the first question, the answer is straightforward. A short-barrel rifle is a rifle. A rifle is a firearm. Firearms are, at a minimum, arms within the plain meaning of the Second Amendment's text. Any court that holds otherwise — and some lower courts have gestured in this direction — is engaged in willful misreading of unambiguous text. Short-barrel rifles are owned and used by millions of law-abiding Americans for home defense, recreational shooting, and hunting. They are manifestly in common use for lawful purposes. Under **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), arms in common use for lawful purposes cannot be prohibited.

On the second question — whether the NFA's registration scheme is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation — the analysis is equally unfavorable to the government. The founding era has no analog to a comprehensive federal firearms registration system requiring individuals to submit their names, addresses, photographs, fingerprints, and identifying information to a government database as a precondition to possessing a constitutionally protected arm. Government firearms registration is a twentieth-century invention. It postdates the founding by well over a century. Under *Bruen*, twentieth-century regulatory innovation cannot establish the kind of historical tradition necessary to sustain a burden on Second Amendment-protected rights.

## The Relationship to the AR-15 and Arms-Ban Litigation

The **Rush** order arrives in a broader landscape of cases testing the limits of *Bruen*'s application to specific categories of arms. Justice Kavanaugh's concurrence in the *Snopes* cert denial made clear that the Court is watching this line of cases and intends to take one up for definitive resolution. The question is which case will serve as the vehicle.

A criminal case like **Rush** presents a somewhat different posture from a civil case brought by a gun owner challenging a licensing or registration regime in the abstract. In a criminal context, the defendant has suffered actual conviction and imprisonment; the case is already fully developed; and the equities favor a clear, definitive ruling. The downside of a criminal vehicle is that the facts of the defendant's particular situation — including the nature of any other charges or conduct in the case — may complicate the Court's ability to issue a clean constitutional ruling.

What is clear from the CVSG order is that the Court is not indifferent to the question. The Justices asked for the executive branch's view. That is not a signal of imminent denial. It is a signal of interest, and in the current term's posture — with the *Bruen* framework now three years old and lower courts continuing to reach inconsistent and frequently incorrect results — the time for the Court to step in is arriving.

## Implications

The September 2, 2025, deadline for the Solicitor General's response means the Court will have the executive branch's position in hand well before it returns from summer recess and begins considering petitions for the upcoming term. If the DOJ supports certiorari or at minimum acknowledges the Second Amendment validity of the underlying claim, the probability of the Court granting review increases substantially.

The Second Amendment community should understand that **Rush v. United States** may not ultimately prove to be the vehicle the Court selects for resolving the NFA registration and arms-protection question — the Court has discretion to await a cleaner factual record or a different circuit posture. But the CVSG order confirms that the Court is paying attention. It will not wait indefinitely while lower courts continue to produce results that are irreconcilable with *Bruen*'s clear directives. The question is when, not whether, the Supreme Court will speak clearly on whether NFA-regulated arms — short-barrel rifles, suppressors, and similar items in common use — receive the full protection the Second Amendment guarantees.
