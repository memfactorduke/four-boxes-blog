## Summary

The Trump-Bondi Department of Justice has filed a brief in **Jensen v. ATF** defending core provisions of the National Firearms Act's regulatory scheme for suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns — even after Congress, at President Trump's urging, eliminated the $200 transfer tax on those items as part of major legislation. In my analysis, the brief reflects a significant institutional failure to communicate clear Second Amendment policy directives down the chain of command at DOJ, and it produces constitutional arguments that are inconsistent with the administration's stated commitment to protecting the Second Amendment. The individual line attorneys handling this case are almost certainly not to blame; the problem appears to originate at the policy level.

## Background

**Jensen v. ATF** is a case pending in the Northern District of Texas challenging the constitutionality of the National Firearms Act's regulatory scheme as applied to suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns — specifically the registration, fingerprinting, and background-check requirements that persist even after the $200 tax on such transfers was eliminated by Congress. The case was brought by plaintiffs including the Second Amendment Foundation and the Firearms Policy Coalition.

The National Firearms Act, enacted in 1934, imposes an elaborate regulatory framework on certain categories of firearms and accessories. The constitutional justification for the NFA has historically rested on Congress's taxation power under Article I of the Constitution — a foundation the Supreme Court acknowledged in **Sonzinsky v. United States**, 300 U.S. 506 (1937), and which remained nominally intact through the Obamacare era's discussion of Congress's taxing authority in **National Federation of Independent Business v. Sebelius**, 567 U.S. 519 (2012). The theory was that Congress could regulate firearms as an incident of its power to tax their transfer.

The constitutional landscape changed when Congress enacted major legislation — commonly referred to as the "One Big Beautiful Bill" — that eliminated the $200 transfer tax for suppressors, short-barrel rifles, and short-barrel shotguns. The tax's elimination raises a profound constitutional question: if the NFA's regulatory scheme was justified as an exercise of the taxing power, what Article I authority remains to justify the accompanying registration, fingerprinting, background-check, and waiting-period requirements once the tax itself has been repealed?

## The DOJ Brief and Its Problems

Notwithstanding the elimination of the transfer tax and the administration's strong public support for Second Amendment rights, the DOJ filed a brief in **Jensen** defending the NFA's remaining regulatory requirements. The brief argues, in substance, that the registration and regulatory scheme remains valid even absent the tax.

I have significant concerns about this legal position on multiple grounds. As a threshold matter, the brief fails to grapple seriously with the constitutional question that the tax repeal creates. If the NFA's registration requirements were always justified as ancillary to the taxing power, their continued existence absent a tax demands a new and independent constitutional justification. That justification must be located in Article I of the Constitution, which creates a Congress of enumerated — and therefore limited — powers. The commerce power, the most plausible alternative, is not self-evidently applicable in a manner that would satisfy post-Lopez constitutional scrutiny to purely intrastate transactions involving lawfully manufactured goods.

Beyond the structural constitutional question, the brief fails to engage adequately with the Second Amendment analysis that Heller, McDonald, and Bruen now require. Under Bruen's text-and-history framework, the government bears the burden of demonstrating that a challenged regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation in America as of 1791. Registration mandates, fingerprinting requirements, and multi-month waiting periods applicable to constitutionally protected arms have no historical analog in the founding era. The government's brief, as I have reviewed its reasoning, does not meet that burden.

## Systemic and Institutional Dimensions

I want to be careful to assign blame appropriately here. The line attorneys at DOJ who drafted and filed this brief are almost certainly professional lawyers doing what professional lawyers do: they received a case file, were told to defend the federal government's legal position, and produced a brief accordingly. The Department of Justice has tens of thousands of attorneys, and it is extraordinarily difficult to ensure that every brief touching on Second Amendment issues reflects a coherent and constitutionally sound policy position.

The problem, in my judgment, lies at the policy level. The administration's Second Amendment policy unit within DOJ appears not to have communicated clear, constitutionally grounded direction to the line attorneys handling Second Amendment-adjacent cases in district courts across the country. This is a management and coordination failure, not a failure of individual lawyers.

The practical consequence, however, is the same regardless of where the institutional failure lies: the United States government is now on record defending an NFA regulatory scheme that, in my view, cannot be justified under either the post-tax-repeal Article I analysis or the Second Amendment's text-and-history framework as the Supreme Court has most recently articulated it. That creates a problematic precedent in the Northern District of Texas and potentially in the Fifth Circuit.

## The Broader Constitutional Picture

The NFA's registration requirements impose substantial burdens on the exercise of Second Amendment rights in ways that have no founding-era pedigree. A law-abiding citizen who wishes to acquire a suppressor — a safety device that reduces hearing damage, not a weapon — must currently navigate a months-long process involving fingerprinting, registration with the federal government, and an extensive background check, even after the $200 tax has been eliminated. The constitutional basis for imposing these burdens on a constitutionally protected right, now that the taxing-power justification has been substantially undermined by Congress's own legislative action, is far from obvious.

The Second Amendment Foundation and the Firearms Policy Coalition have correctly identified this as a significant litigation opportunity. Congress's repeal of the transfer tax has altered the constitutional landscape in ways that courts must now carefully examine. The DOJ's brief, by defending the regulatory remnant without engaging the full scope of the constitutional challenge, fails to serve either the administration's stated policy goals or the development of a sound Second Amendment jurisprudence.

## Implications

The administration's overall record on Second Amendment issues is, in my view, strongly positive. I have supported this administration's direction on firearms policy from the beginning, and I believe its general commitment to restoring Second Amendment rights is genuine. That is precisely why I find the DOJ's position in **Jensen v. ATF** so troubling. An administration that is willing to lead Congress to eliminate the NFA transfer tax should not simultaneously defend, in federal court, the regulatory apparatus that was justified primarily by that same tax.

The path forward requires better coordination within DOJ between those setting Second Amendment policy and those filing briefs in district courts. It also requires a more candid constitutional assessment of what Article I authority — if any — now supports the NFA's registration and regulatory requirements for suppressor and short-barrel firearms transfers, and whether those requirements can survive scrutiny under the Second Amendment framework the Supreme Court has established. The Jensen case provides an important vehicle for that assessment. The government's current brief is not equal to the constitutional moment.
