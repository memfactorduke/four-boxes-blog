## Summary

On August 14, 2025, the Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi and the direction of President Donald J. Trump, filed a significant legal brief in the United States District Court in Texas in the case of **Elite Precision Customs LLC v. ATF**, arguing that the federal law prohibiting non-residents of a given state from taking possession of a handgun purchased from a federally licensed dealer located in that state is constitutional under the Second Amendment. The challenge was brought by the Firearms Policy Coalition and allied Second Amendment organizations seeking to invalidate a provision of the Gun Control Act of 1968 that has restricted interstate handgun acquisitions for more than five decades. The Department of Justice's decision to defend this restriction — rather than abandon it, as some in the Second Amendment community had hoped — raises important and difficult questions about the Trump administration's litigation posture on Second Amendment issues, and warrants careful analysis of both the plaintiffs' arguments and the government's response.

## Background

The federal law at issue is codified at 18 U.S.C. § 922(b)(3) and related provisions of the Gun Control Act of 1968, which prohibit federally licensed firearms dealers from transferring a handgun to any person who is not a resident of the state in which the dealer's licensed premises is located. The restriction was enacted as part of the Gun Control Act's broader regulatory framework and was originally applied to all firearms. Congress subsequently amended the law in 1986 to remove long guns from the non-resident restriction, but the handgun prohibition remains in full force today.

The practical consequence of this law is significant. If a resident of Montana travels to Texas and purchases a handgun from a Texas FFL, the dealer cannot transfer physical possession of that handgun to the Montana resident in Texas. Instead, the firearm must be shipped to a licensed dealer in Montana, where the buyer must submit to a separate background check and take possession in his or her home state. The buyer may pay for the firearm in any state; the point-of-sale commercial transaction is fully permitted. What is prohibited is the physical transfer of possession at the out-of-state dealer's location.

The challenge in **Elite Precision Customs LLC v. ATF** argues that this restriction violates the Second Amendment as interpreted by the Supreme Court in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008) and, particularly, in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022). Under the *Bruen* framework, a firearm regulation is constitutionally valid only if the government can demonstrate that it is consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation in the United States at the time of the founding. The plaintiffs argue that no such historical tradition supports a ban on the interstate acquisition of handguns.

## The Plaintiffs' Arguments

The plaintiffs, led by the Firearms Policy Coalition, present a straightforward textual and historical argument. The Second Amendment guarantees the right of the people to keep and bear arms, and the laws at issue — in their own words — make it "impossible for a peaceable American who is not prohibited for any reason from possessing firearms to purchase a handgun from a federally licensed firearms dealer unless that dealer is located in the home state." The plaintiffs further contend that because the government concedes that the challenged laws bar the transfer of a handgun from a licensed dealer to a law-abiding and peaceable citizen in forty-nine states where the dealer is not located, those laws unquestionably burden the fundamental right protected by the Second Amendment. The central question, under *Bruen*, is therefore whether the government's restriction is historically justifiable.

The plaintiffs argue that it is not. No analogous historical tradition of restricting the interstate acquisition of arms at the time of the founding or in the Reconstruction era has been identified. The handgun purchase ban is a creature of the modern federal regulatory state, enacted in 1968 without any grounding in the founding-era understanding of the right to keep and bear arms.

## The Department of Justice's Response

The Trump Department of Justice's response to these arguments reflects a litigation posture that some observers have found surprising. The DOJ does not simply argue that the restriction is supported by historical tradition — it also argues, as a preliminary matter, that the restriction does not constitute a cognizable "infringement" of the Second Amendment in the first instance. The government's position is that an infringement must be something particularly burdensome, and that because buyers may still purchase handguns in their home states, the non-resident restriction is merely a logistical inconvenience rather than a true constitutional violation.

This preliminary argument is legally weak and, in my assessment, incorrect. When one examines the same founding-era dictionaries that the Supreme Court consulted in *Heller* — the lexicographic work of Samuel Johnson, Noah Webster, and other contemporaneous authorities — the term "infringe" carried a broader meaning than the government's brief acknowledges. To infringe a right is to diminish, encroach upon, or obstruct its exercise, not merely to prohibit it outright. A law that prevents a law-abiding citizen from taking possession of a handgun in a state other than his or her state of residence plainly encroaches upon the exercise of the right to acquire and keep arms, even if some other pathway to acquisition remains theoretically available.

The government also advances historical tradition arguments in defense of the restriction, though those arguments face the same evidentiary obstacles that have plagued government efforts to defend modern gun regulations under the *Bruen* standard. The absence of founding-era analogues to a federal interstate acquisition restriction is a substantial hurdle that the government's brief does not convincingly surmount.

## Analysis of the DOJ's Litigation Posture

I want to address directly what this filing reveals about the Trump administration's approach to Second Amendment litigation. It is undeniably true that this filing represents a departure from the hoped-for posture of a Second Amendment-friendly administration — namely, a deliberate refusal to defend a statute that lacks historical grounding. The DOJ under the prior Biden administration would have defended this same restriction, and the Trump DOJ is now doing so as well.

There are, however, reasons to view this situation with some nuance. The decision to defend existing federal statutes — even those with questionable constitutional foundations — reflects a longstanding institutional norm within the Department of Justice that transcends particular administrations. The DOJ possesses a general institutional obligation to defend the constitutionality of Acts of Congress unless there is no reasonable argument in their defense. That institutional commitment does not necessarily reflect the personal views of the Attorney General or the President on the merits of the underlying Second Amendment question.

Moreover, and this is a crucial point that cuts directly in favor of Second Amendment litigants, the decision whether this restriction is unconstitutional ultimately rests not with the Department of Justice but with the federal courts applying the *Bruen* standard. The DOJ's defense of the restriction does not determine the outcome. Federal judges are required to apply *Heller* and *Bruen* as the governing law, and under those precedents, the government bears a heavy burden of demonstrating a well-established historical tradition that supports the restriction. That burden will be difficult to meet here.

## Implications

The litigation in **Elite Precision Customs LLC v. ATF** raises questions that extend well beyond the specific contours of the non-resident handgun purchase restriction.

First, the case provides an important opportunity for federal courts to address, under *Bruen*, the constitutionality of Gun Control Act provisions enacted in 1968 — a period that, as the Supreme Court made clear in *Bruen*, is far too modern to serve as the relevant historical benchmark. The founding era and the Reconstruction era are the appropriate reference points, and 1968 federal legislation simply does not carry the originalist pedigree that *Bruen* demands.

Second, this case illustrates a point that I have repeatedly emphasized: the fight to vindicate Second Amendment rights requires fluency across multiple areas of law, not merely core Second Amendment doctrine. The non-resident handgun restriction is a feature of federal licensing and commerce law as much as it is a gun regulation, and effective advocacy in this space demands an understanding of how the federal firearms licensing framework operates in practice.

Third, if the courts ultimately invalidate the non-resident handgun purchase restriction, the practical consequence for American gun owners would be substantial. Law-abiding citizens traveling across state lines — whether for work, recreation, or any other lawful purpose — would no longer face the logistical burden of shipping a purchased firearm back to their home state. The constitutional guarantee of the right to keep and bear arms would operate without the artificial geographical barrier that the 1968 statute has imposed for more than five decades.
