## Summary

The Trump administration's Department of Justice, through Solicitor General John Sauer, has filed an emergency application with the United States Supreme Court seeking a stay of lower court orders that enjoined the deployment of federalized National Guard troops in Chicago to protect federal immigration enforcement operations and federal property. A federal district court in Chicago ruled that President Trump lacked the authority to federalize the Illinois National Guard under these circumstances, and a three-judge panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit affirmed that decision. The administration now brings the matter to the Supreme Court on an emergency basis, seeking to restore the President's ability to deploy these federal troops while the underlying litigation proceeds. In my analysis, the lower court decisions represent a serious and unjustified encroachment on core executive and commander-in-chief authority, and the Supreme Court has strong grounds to grant the requested stay.

## Background

Federal immigration enforcement officers from ICE — operating within the Department of Homeland Security under the direction of Secretary Kristi Noem — have been conducting active immigration enforcement operations in Chicago, Illinois, a jurisdiction that has placed substantial political obstacles in the path of federal law enforcement. As in other similarly situated deep blue urban jurisdictions, there has been organized resistance to these operations, including protests aimed at obstructing the work of federal law enforcement and incidents of interference with ICE agents and federal facilities.

In response, President Trump, exercising authority under a federal statute that permits the President to federalize state National Guard units, issued a directive federalizing elements of the Illinois National Guard — and separately, National Guard units from Texas — and deploying those troops to Chicago. The stated purposes were twofold: first, to protect federal property and federal law enforcement personnel from attack, riot, and interference; and second, to address what the administration characterized as a condition amounting to rebellion or near-rebellion against federal authority — namely, the coordinated, systematic obstruction of federal immigration law enforcement by state and local officials and private actors.

President Trump was explicit that the National Guard was not being deployed to conduct immigration enforcement itself — not to go door to door, not to locate or arrest illegal aliens. The role of the federalized troops was defensive and protective: to safeguard federal facilities, protect federal agents so they could perform their jobs, and maintain order sufficient to allow the lawful exercise of federal authority.

## The Governor's Lawsuit and the Lower Court Rulings

Governor J.B. Pritzker of Illinois filed suit in federal district court in Chicago, arguing that President Trump lacked the statutory and constitutional authority to federalize the Illinois National Guard under these circumstances. A federal district court judge in Chicago agreed, enjoining the deployment. The Seventh Circuit panel affirmed, holding that the President had not adequately established the legal and factual basis for federalizing the National Guard.

In the immediate aftermath of the Seventh Circuit's ruling, the situation became something of an absurdity: the federalized National Guard soldiers technically could be stationed in Chicago and federalized in name, but the district court's injunction prohibited them from actually being deployed for their intended protective purpose. The soldiers could, as I noted, sit on a bench and watch television — but could not be used to protect federal agents conducting lawful enforcement operations.

This represents, in my view, a profound and constitutionally untenable situation: inferior federal courts telling the Commander-in-Chief of the United States Armed Forces how he may and may not exercise his authority to federalize and deploy the National Guard — a power that Congress has specifically granted to the President by statute.

## The Constitutional and Statutory Framework

The President's authority to federalize the National Guard derives from a federal statute — commonly referred to as a federalization statute or Title 10 authority — that permits the President to call forth National Guard units when, among other things, there is a need to execute federal law, suppress insurrection, or repel invasion. The statute codifies the President's constitutional role as Commander-in-Chief and as the chief executive responsible for ensuring that federal law is faithfully executed.

The President made two independent arguments for why federalization was warranted here. First, the deployment was necessary to protect federal property and federal law enforcement personnel from the ongoing pattern of attacks, protests, and interference that had characterized the anti-enforcement environment in Chicago. Second, the pattern of coordinated state and local resistance to federal immigration law enforcement — with the active involvement or acquiescence of the state's governor and the city's officials — constituted a form of rebellion or insurrection against federal authority that independently justified the deployment.

Whether one agrees with the policy choices being made, the constitutional principle at stake is fundamental: the President has the authority to enforce federal law, and the courts — particularly inferior district courts — do not have the power to micro-manage or enjoin the President's exercise of core commander-in-chief authority over military and national security matters. As I see it, the lower court's reasoning, if accepted by the Supreme Court, would represent a dangerous expansion of judicial power into territory the Constitution reserves to the executive branch.

## The Emergency Application and the Shadow Docket

The administration's emergency application to the Supreme Court invokes what is commonly called the "shadow docket" — the Court's emergency docket through which the justices act on time-sensitive requests for stays, injunctions, and other immediate relief, typically without full briefing, oral argument, or elaborate written opinions. The shadow docket has been the subject of substantial criticism from the left, including from lower court judges who complain, as discussed in a recent New York Times article, about what they perceive as a lack of transparency and reasoning in the Court's emergency orders.

As I have analyzed in other contexts, that criticism is largely pretextual. The shadow docket exists because constitutional emergencies require prompt judicial resolution. When a lower court wrongly enjoins a federal law or executive action, the party harmed by that injunction cannot wait months or years for the ordinary appellate process to run its course. The emergency docket is the mechanism through which the Supreme Court exercises its supervisory responsibility over the lower courts in real time. The fact that some lower court judges dislike being reversed on the emergency docket does not make the process illegitimate.

## Implications

The Supreme Court's resolution of this emergency application will have significant implications beyond the immediate facts of National Guard deployment in Chicago. A ruling in the President's favor would reaffirm the executive branch's authority to deploy federal military assets to protect federal operations against state and local obstruction — an authority that is foundational to the supremacy of federal law. A ruling against the administration would invite further lower court activism in the most sensitive areas of executive authority, including immigration enforcement, border security, and the deployment of military forces in domestic emergencies.

I expect the Supreme Court to grant the emergency stay, consistent with its broader pattern of reversing lower court overreach in the context of Trump administration enforcement actions. The constitutional text and structure strongly support the President's position, and the pattern of lower court activism in this area has already been checked repeatedly by the Supreme Court. This case presents another opportunity for the Court to reaffirm the proper boundaries of judicial authority.
