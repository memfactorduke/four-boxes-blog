## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has granted an emergency stay, by a vote of 6-3, blocking a New York trial court order that directed the state's Independent Redistricting Commission to redraw New York's 11th Congressional District on explicitly race-based grounds. In my analysis, Justice Samuel Alito's concurrence in the stay order applies the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment with precision, characterizing the lower court's directive as racial discrimination that cannot satisfy either of the two compelling interests this Court has recognized as capable of justifying race-conscious governmental action. The decision carries implications beyond the particular redistricting dispute — it raises material questions about the continued viability of statutory race-based redistricting mandates under the Supremacy Clause.

## Background

New York's 11th Congressional District encompasses Staten Island and portions of southwestern Brooklyn, and is represented by Congresswoman Nicole Malliotakis. In January 2026, a New York County trial court issued an injunction ordering the New York Independent Redistricting Commission to redraw the district's boundaries for the express purpose of enabling minority voters to "elect the candidate of their choice." The plaintiffs, a group of voters, had proceeded under the New York state constitution and state statutory law.

The redistricting order was challenged before the United States Supreme Court on the ground that it violated the Equal Protection Clause of the Fourteenth Amendment. The application was initially presented to Justice Sotomayor in her capacity as the Circuit Justice for the Second Circuit, and by her referred to the full Court, which granted the stay. The Court's order reads: "The January 21st, 2026 order entered by the Supreme Court of the state of New York, New York County...is stayed."

## Justice Alito's Concurrence: The Equal Protection Analysis

In my analysis, Justice Alito's concurrence provides the doctrinal foundation for the stay and is the more significant portion of the Court's action. The concurrence begins from the premise established in *Students for Fair Admissions, Inc. v. President and Fellows of Harvard College* and *Students for Fair Admissions, Inc. v. University of North Carolina*, 600 U.S. 181 (2023), that racial classifications in government action are inherently constitutionally suspect and are permissible only in the most extraordinary circumstances. As Alito stated:

> "These cases concern a state court order that blatantly discriminates on the basis of race. The New York Supreme Court, that state's trial level court, ordered the New York Independent Redistricting Commission to draw a new Congressional District for the express purpose of ensuring that minority voters are able to elect the candidate of their choice."

The concurrence characterizes this as "unendorsed racial discrimination" — an "inherently odious" activity under the Fourteenth Amendment's equal protection guarantee. Alito identified only two compelling interests that the Court's precedents recognize as capable of justifying race-based governmental action: mitigating prison-specific risks, and remediating specific identified instances of past discrimination that violate the Constitution or a federal statute. Neither was present in the redistricting context. In Alito's words:

> "It is therefore an understatement to say that applicants here are likely to succeed on the merits of their equal protection claim."

## The Supremacy Clause Dimension

As I have examined, the broader constitutional principle implicated by this case extends beyond the specific redistricting order. The trial court based its injunction on New York state law — specifically, an interpretation of the state constitution and state redistricting statutes. But as Justice Alito's concurrence observes, the Supremacy Clause of the U.S. Constitution, Article VI, Clause 2, establishes that federal constitutional requirements preempt contrary state law. A state court cannot authorize, through statutory interpretation or otherwise, conduct that the federal Equal Protection Clause prohibits.

This principle is particularly significant in the context of the Voting Rights Act, which has been invoked in redistricting litigation as a basis for compelling race-conscious district configurations. The Voting Rights Act is a congressional statute; the Fourteenth Amendment is constitutional text. Where a statutory mandate requires a state to employ racial classification as the predominant factor in drawing district lines — and that classification cannot satisfy strict scrutiny — the constitutional provision prevails. As I have argued, the trajectory of the Supreme Court's equal protection jurisprudence since *Shaw v. Reno*, 509 U.S. 630 (1993), through *Miller v. Johnson*, 515 U.S. 900 (1995), and the 2023 affirmative action decisions, is toward categorical resistance to race as a predominant governmental decisional criterion.

The pending case of *Cala v. Louisiana* presents a related question about race-based redistricting under the Voting Rights Act framework and is likely to further clarify the constitutional limits that this stay order implicates.

## The Distinction Between Partisan and Racial Redistricting

A critical doctrinal distinction bears emphasis here. The Supreme Court held in *Rucho v. Common Cause*, 588 U.S. 684 (2019), that partisan gerrymandering claims present nonjusticiable political questions beyond the reach of federal courts. States retain broad authority to draw district lines that favor one political party over another — such decisions are understood as inherent in the political process and are committed to legislative discretion. The Equal Protection Clause, however, imposes a separate and independent constraint: racial classification as a predominant factor in district design is subject to strict scrutiny regardless of the political justification offered.

In my analysis, the New York trial court's order failed this constitutional test not because it favored one party over another — which the Constitution permits — but because it explicitly directed line-drawing based on race. That distinction, while precise, is constitutionally decisive. The fact that the race-conscious redistricting would predictably also benefit one political party does not cure the constitutional defect; the Equal Protection Clause evaluates the criterion used, not merely the practical outcome.

## Implications for Redistricting Jurisprudence

The Supreme Court's 6-3 grant of the emergency stay signals that at least six members of the Court found a substantial probability that the applicants would prevail on the merits of their equal protection challenge — the governing standard for emergency relief in *Hollingsworth v. Perry*, 558 U.S. 183 (2010). That threshold finding is significant. It confirms that the Court's equal protection jurisprudence applies with full force to state court-ordered redistricting, not merely to legislative redistricting enacted by state legislatures or Congress.

For practitioners engaged in redistricting litigation, the concurrence establishes that a state court's statutory authority does not provide constitutional cover for a race-based redistricting mandate. The Supremacy Clause operates to invalidate state law directives — whether legislative or judicial in origin — that cannot satisfy the compelling interest and narrow tailoring requirements that strict scrutiny demands. The case will proceed on the merits, and the questions Justice Alito identifies as likely to favor the applicants will receive full briefing and argument before a final resolution is reached.
