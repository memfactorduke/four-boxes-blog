## Summary

The phrase "merciless Indian Savages" appears in the Declaration of Independence as one of the grievances leveled against King George III. Anti-gun advocates have attempted to weaponize the Founders' awareness of mass violence as an argument that the Second Amendment was crafted in a naive era, unaware of the dangers of lethal force used at scale. This article examines that historical claim on its merits, traces the documented record of frontier violence known to the Founding generation, and explains precisely why that history reinforces — rather than undermines — the constitutional right to keep and bear arms.

## Background

The Declaration of Independence, signed by the Founders in 1776, contains the following language attributed principally to Thomas Jefferson:

> "He [King George III] has excited domestic Insurrections amongst us, and has endeavoured to bring on the Inhabitants of our Frontiers, the merciless Indian Savages, whose known Rule of Warfare, is an undistinguished Destruction of all Ages, Sexes and Conditions."

This passage reflects a specific historical grievance: the British Crown's deliberate incitement of frontier violence against colonial settlers. The language, however uncomfortable to modern sensibilities, is factually grounded in documented episodes of mass killing that the Founding generation witnessed firsthand and recorded in contemporaneous sources.

## The Anti-Gun Argument and Why It Fails

A recurring argument advanced by progressive scholars and invoked by anti-gun attorneys in Second Amendment litigation holds that the Founders, when they drafted the Second Amendment in 1791, were unfamiliar with the concept of mass murder or mass atrocity. The premise is that human violence has somehow "evolved" since the late eighteenth century — that firearms are uniquely more dangerous now than they were then — and that the Founders therefore could not have anticipated the regulatory challenges posed by modern weapons.

This claim is not merely unpersuasive on theoretical grounds; it is demonstrably, historically false.

## The Documented Record of Frontier Violence Before 1791

The historical record establishes that the Founding generation was intimately familiar with the realities of mass killing well before the ratification of the Second Amendment. Several incidents are particularly instructive.

**The Enoch Brown School Massacre (1764).** In what historians have identified as among the first mass killings of children in a school setting in North American history, schoolmaster Enoch Brown was killed along with approximately nineteen children at a Pennsylvania schoolhouse in 1764 — twelve years before the Declaration of Independence and twenty-seven years before the Second Amendment was ratified. The attack was carried out by a group of Delaware warriors during the violence that followed Pontiac's Rebellion. The Founders lived with this event and others like it as recent memory.

**The Penn's Creek Massacre (1755) and Related Frontier Violence.** Throughout the 1750s and 1760s, the Pennsylvania and Virginia frontiers were repeatedly subjected to attacks in which settlers were killed and taken captive. These events were widely reported and discussed in colonial newspapers and correspondence. The men who signed the Declaration and drafted the Bill of Rights were not abstract philosophers writing in a vacuum — they were citizens of a society routinely confronted with organized lethal violence.

**The Cherry Valley Massacre (1778).** During the Revolutionary War itself, a combined force of British soldiers and Iroquois warriors attacked Cherry Valley, New York, killing approximately thirty civilians. This occurred just thirteen years before the Second Amendment was ratified. The Framers were thus legislating against the immediate backdrop of mass atrocities committed against civilians.

## The Significance for Second Amendment Jurisprudence

The relevance of this history to contemporary Second Amendment litigation extends beyond mere historical interest. In **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), the Supreme Court held that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms and confirmed that this right is rooted in the pre-existing natural right of self-defense. The Court was explicit that the right "existed in 1791" and that the Second Amendment did not create the right but instead memorialized it.

During oral argument in **Heller**, Justice Anthony Kennedy directly raised the issue of frontier violence in questioning the government's attorney for the District of Columbia. Justice Kennedy's inquiry acknowledged that the Founders were keenly aware of the threat posed by violent attack and that the right to keep and bear arms was, in substantial part, a response to exactly those conditions.

The Second Amendment's text — "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed" — is phrased as the affirmation of a pre-existing right, not the creation of a new one. This grammatical structure, recognized by the Supreme Court in **Heller**, confirms that the Founders understood self-defense as a natural right that antedated the Constitution itself.

## The Natural Rights Foundation

The Second Amendment and the broader constitutional framework derive in significant part from natural rights theory, which found its most famous American expression in the Declaration of Independence. Thomas Jefferson's declaration that all men are "endowed by their Creator with certain unalienable Rights" — including life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness — rests on the philosophical tradition of Locke, Blackstone, and others who identified the right of self-preservation as among the most fundamental rights a person possesses.

This natural rights foundation is critical. If the right to keep and bear arms is grounded in the natural right of self-defense, it cannot be legislated away simply because the tools of self-defense change over time. The question under the **Heller** framework and its successor, **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), is whether a proposed firearms regulation is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation — not whether modern weapons present dangers the Founders could not have imagined.

## Implications

The argument that the Founding generation was naive about mass violence is not merely historically wrong — it is strategically dishonest. It attempts to undermine the constitutional status of the Second Amendment not through legitimate textual or historical analysis, but by attributing to the Framers an ignorance that the historical record plainly refutes.

The documented evidence is unambiguous: the men who drafted, debated, and ratified the Second Amendment were personally and professionally acquainted with organized, large-scale violence against civilians. They knew that tomahawks, like any weapon capable of being wielded repeatedly, had no inherent limitation on their destructive capacity. They understood that mass atrocity was a feature of the human condition that no era could claim immunity from.

This historical record strengthens rather than weakens the Second Amendment's protective scope. If the Founders knew what mass violence looked like, chose to recognize a pre-existing natural right of self-defense, and then memorialized that right in the Bill of Rights, they were doing so with full awareness of the stakes. Courts interpreting the Second Amendment today are bound to respect that considered, historically grounded judgment.
