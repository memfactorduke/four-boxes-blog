## Summary

With Attorney General Pam Bondi's departure from the Department of Justice, the Second Amendment community faces a pivotal question: who should lead DOJ next? Mark W. Smith argues that while Bondi's tenure produced historically significant pro-Second Amendment initiatives, critical missteps -- likely originating from the Solicitor General's office and DOJ leadership rather than Bondi herself -- counsel against elevating certain internal candidates, and he identifies Lee Zeldin as a promising alternative.

## Background

Attorney General Pam Bondi has left office, triggering immediate speculation about her successor. The vacancy arrives at a sensitive moment for Second Amendment litigation strategy at the federal level, with multiple high-profile cases pending and the DOJ's newly created Second Amendment Task Force still in its early stages.

Smith's assessment of the Bondi-era DOJ is nuanced. On one hand, he credits the department with an unprecedented set of pro-Second Amendment actions: the establishment of a dedicated Second Amendment unit within DOJ, the filing of amicus briefs supporting firearms rights, litigation against the District of Columbia's bans on so-called large-capacity magazines and AR-15-style rifles, and lawsuits challenging slow-moving firearms licensing regimes in states including California. Harmeet Dhillon, serving as head of the Civil Rights Division, has been a driving force behind much of this work, including oral argument before the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit in a case challenging assault weapon and magazine bans.

On the other hand, Smith identifies what he views as serious strategic errors -- errors he attributes not to Bondi personally, but to other DOJ officials.

## The Case Selection Problem: *Rahimi*, *Range*, and *Reese*

The most pointed criticism concerns the Solicitor General's decision to push *United States v. Rahimi* to the Supreme Court. Smith argues the case presented a deeply unfavorable fact pattern for Second Amendment advocates: the defendant had admitted to cocaine and marijuana use, making him an unsympathetic vehicle for testing the boundaries of 18 U.S.C. Section 922(g), the federal felon-in-possession statute. Smith notes that Justices Alito and Roberts are known to be skeptical of drug-related cases, making the strategic choice to elevate *Rahimi* especially questionable from a pro-Second Amendment perspective.

By contrast, Smith points to *United States v. Range* -- a Third Circuit case involving what he describes as a far more sympathetic fact pattern -- as the type of case that should have been used to develop favorable precedent on Section 922(g). Similarly, *United States v. Reese*, a Fifth Circuit case addressing the Second Amendment rights of 18-to-20-year-olds, represents the kind of case where the government could have sought certiorari on favorable terms rather than allowing less favorable vehicles to reach the Court first.

Beyond case selection, Smith criticizes DOJ's refusal to enter consent decrees favorable to Second Amendment organizations such as Gun Owners of America, the Second Amendment Foundation, and the Firearms Policy Coalition. He also flags what he characterizes as an improper DOJ litigation position on the scope of remedies -- specifically, arguing that individuals who were not members of a plaintiff organization on the date a lawsuit was filed or judgment entered cannot benefit from the resulting relief. Smith contends this position contradicts decades of Supreme Court precedent on organizational standing and associational remedies.

## Who Should Not Be Attorney General

Smith identifies three categories of candidates he believes would be detrimental to Second Amendment interests, while emphasizing that his objections are strategic rather than personal.

**Todd Blanch**, currently the Deputy Attorney General and interim head of DOJ, is Smith's primary concern. Blanch, a former federal prosecutor in the U.S. Attorney's Office for the Southern District of New York, is characterized as a capable lawyer and someone who served President Trump well, but one whose prosecutorial background and institutional orientation make him unlikely to aggressively advance Second Amendment priorities. Smith suspects Blanch, along with the Solicitor General's office, is the source of the problematic litigation decisions described above.

**Solicitor General John Sauer** and anyone from his office fall into the same category. Smith attributes the poor case selection and resistance to favorable settlements to some combination of the SG's office and career DOJ attorneys.

**Harmeet Dhillon**, perhaps counterintuitively, is also on Smith's "do not elevate" list -- but for the opposite reason. Smith views Dhillon as far too valuable in her current role leading the Civil Rights Division to be moved. Pulling her into the attorney general's position would, in his estimation, waste her specialized skill set on administrative and managerial duties -- budget testimony before Congress, oversight of U.S. Attorneys' offices nationwide, sentencing guidelines, and death penalty cases -- while removing the driving force behind DOJ's most productive Second Amendment work. Smith draws an analogy to the business principle that a top salesperson should not necessarily be promoted to sales manager. If a future administration under a President Vance or President Rubio were to take office, elevating Dhillon at that point might make sense; the current timing does not.

## Potential Candidates

### Lee Zeldin

Smith identifies former Congressman Lee Zeldin, currently serving as EPA Administrator, as his leading candidate. Zeldin holds an A rating from the NRA, opposes assault weapon bans, supports the right to carry, took a favorable position on Operation Choke Point, and opposes semi-automatic rifle bans. Smith also points to Zeldin's willingness to confront entrenched bureaucratic interests at the EPA -- including his reversal of the endangerment finding on greenhouse gas emissions -- as evidence of the fortitude needed to resist career DOJ attorneys who may be undermining Second Amendment litigation strategy from within.

### Unconventional Ideas

Smith floats two speculative possibilities. First, if Justice Clarence Thomas or Justice Samuel Alito were to retire at the end of the current Supreme Court term, either could potentially serve as attorney general -- though Smith acknowledges this is purely hypothetical and he has no information suggesting either justice plans to step down.

Second, Smith raises the possibility of a political arrangement in which Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton becomes the U.S. Attorney General while Senator John Cornyn retains his Senate seat, resolving the ongoing Texas Republican primary contest. Smith does not endorse this idea but offers it as a scenario worth considering.

## The Senate Confirmation Constraint

Any nominee must be confirmed by the U.S. Senate, and Smith emphasizes that the current Republican majority is not a reliable MAGA majority. He identifies several potential defections: Senator Lisa Murkowski of Alaska, Senator Susan Collins of Maine (who faces a difficult reelection), retiring Senator Thom Tillis of North Carolina (who has increasingly broken with President Trump), and potentially Senator John Cornyn of Texas should he lose his primary to Ken Paxton. This political reality, Smith notes, is precisely why losing a confirmed attorney general was strategically risky -- there is no guarantee the Senate will confirm a successor more favorable to the Second Amendment than Bondi was.

## Implications

The attorney general vacancy presents both opportunity and risk for Second Amendment advocates. The opportunity lies in installing a leader who will not merely permit pro-Second Amendment work at DOJ but will actively ensure that the Solicitor General's office selects favorable vehicles for Supreme Court review and that career attorneys do not undermine favorable settlements and consent decrees. The risk is that a confirmation battle in a politically fractured Senate could produce a nominee less sympathetic to firearms rights than Bondi -- or that the confirmation process itself becomes a vehicle for anti-Second Amendment political theater. The selection will likely signal whether the administration's Second Amendment litigation strategy was a product of genuine institutional commitment or the initiative of a few key officials who may or may not remain in place.
