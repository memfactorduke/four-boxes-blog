At the 2026 NRA Annual Meetings in Houston, the Department of Justice confirmed a personnel reform that sounds administrative and is actually structural: attorneys hired into the new Second Amendment Rights Section will be permitted to work out of their local U.S. Attorney's offices rather than relocate to Washington, D.C. Announced by Assistant Attorney General Harmeet K. Dhillon and Acting Attorney General Todd Blanche, the change quietly eliminates the single largest filter that has kept pro-Second Amendment lawyers out of the Civil Rights Division for decades.

## The Announcement

The news broke during the DOJ's high-profile presence at the 155th NRA Annual Meetings, held April 16–19 at the George R. Brown Convention Center in Houston. Dhillon, who leads the Civil Rights Division, and Blanche, who became Acting Attorney General on April 2, 2026 after President Trump dismissed Pam Bondi, signaled that the new Second Amendment Rights Section — launched December 4, 2025, and currently led by Acting Chief Andrew Darlington — would recruit nationally and allow remote work through the federal U.S. Attorney office network.

In practical terms, a qualified attorney in Houston, Billings, or Salt Lake City can now apply to work for the section, be hired into the Civil Rights Division, and conduct that work from the nearest U.S. Attorney's office. The lawyer does not have to sell a home, move a family, or take children out of school to serve in a federal civil rights role.

## Why D.C. Location Has Been a Structural Filter

The requirement to live and work inside the Beltway has long functioned as an invisible screen on the federal hiring pool — particularly for the Civil Rights Division. The economics alone are prohibitive. A lawyer leaving a private-sector practice in a mid-sized red-state city typically takes a significant pay cut to join DOJ. Layer on Washington-area housing costs, spousal employment disruption, and school transitions, and the move is financially untenable for many mid-career attorneys with families and established practices.

The geographic concentration compounds the effect. The D.C. metro — the District itself, suburban Maryland, and Northern Virginia — votes heavily Democratic in every federal cycle. The resident talent pool from which DOJ has historically drawn skews decisively in one political direction, and a division tasked with civil rights litigation has tended to reflect the ideological preferences of the region that supplies its applicants. Dhillon has publicly noted that roughly 75 percent of the Civil Rights Division's existing career staff departed after she took over, underscoring how narrowly the prior pipeline was drawn.

A lawyer who believes the Second Amendment is a genuine individual right in need of vigorous federal enforcement is statistically more likely to live in Texas, Florida, Tennessee, Montana, or Utah than in Arlington or Bethesda. Requiring that lawyer to physically relocate to a region where the political and professional culture is openly hostile to that view has been a powerful — if unstated — disqualifier.

## Inside the Second Amendment Rights Section

The Second Amendment Rights Section is a young and deliberately ambitious unit. Created inside the Civil Rights Division in December 2025, it marks the first time in the division's 68-year history that a standing office has been tasked with affirmatively litigating on behalf of gun owners. It has already filed suits against state and local governments, including an action against Los Angeles County over concealed carry permit processing delays, and Dhillon has telegraphed additional litigation targeting Virginia's recently enacted firearms measures.

At the NRA's National Firearms Law Seminar days earlier, Dhillon publicly urged attorneys to apply to the section through the DOJ careers portal, warning that the volume of unconstitutional state legislation is outstripping the division's current staffing. Remote hiring is the logical second step. Without it, the section's recruitment pitch — join a new civil rights unit and litigate cutting-edge constitutional cases — runs into the same relocation wall that has thinned the applicant pool for decades.

## Personnel Is Policy

The adage that personnel is policy is nowhere more true than at DOJ, where line attorneys exercise enormous discretion over which cases are filed, how they are briefed, and which arguments are pressed. A section staffed with lawyers drawn from states where the Second Amendment is taken seriously as a matter of ordinary constitutional practice will produce different briefs, different theories, and different results than a section staffed exclusively from the D.C. suburbs.

There is also a retention dimension. Attorneys who can remain embedded in their home legal communities — continuing to practice alongside state bars, judges, and colleagues who share their constitutional commitments — are measurably more likely to stay in federal service long enough to build institutional expertise. The Second Amendment Rights Section will need that continuity if it is to establish a durable litigation footprint.

Whether the reform produces the results Dhillon and Blanche envision depends on execution: how quickly the section can vet and onboard candidates, whether Congress sustains the division's funding, and whether the courts continue to apply *Bruen* with the rigor its text demands. But as a management move, it is rare and overdue — a federal agency actively widening, rather than narrowing, the geographic and ideological pool from which it draws. For the Second Amendment bar, it is an invitation worth taking seriously.
