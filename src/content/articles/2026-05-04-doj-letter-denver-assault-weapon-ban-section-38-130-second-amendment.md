> "Denver has run an unconstitutional AR-15 ban for 37 years by hiding behind blue-state circuit courts. The DOJ just stripped that cover away — and Denver knows it."
>
> — Professor Mark W. Smith, Four Boxes Diner Host

Major news out of Washington and Denver. On April 28, 2026, Assistant Attorney General Harmeet K. Dhillon, head of the DOJ Civil Rights Division, sent a letter to Denver Mayor Mike Johnston and City Attorney Miko Brown threatening a federal Second Amendment lawsuit unless Denver agrees by 5 p.m. on May 5, 2026, to stop enforcing its so-called assault weapon ban — Denver Revised Municipal Code Section 38-130, on the books since 1989 under then-Mayor Federico Peña. This is the Trump DOJ's new Second Amendment Section doing exactly what I have argued for years it should do: dropping binding Supreme Court precedent on an anti-gun jurisdiction that has been hiding behind sympathetic lower courts. The game is over.

## What the Letter Actually Says

The Dhillon letter is short, surgical, and quotes *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), front and center. It reminds Denver that "[a]rms in common use by law-abiding citizens are protected by the Constitution and may not be categorically banned," notes that Denver's ordinance bans "AR-15 style semi-automatic rifles" — tens of millions of which are owned by law-abiding Americans — and concludes flatly that "the ordinance violates the Second Amendment."

Then comes the threat. Dhillon writes that she "has authorized the filing of a complaint in federal district court against the city and the Denver Police Department." The only off-ramp is a consent decree in which Denver agrees to stop enforcing the ban and concedes its unconstitutionality. That is what real Second Amendment enforcement looks like — not press releases, not amicus briefs, but federal civil-rights litigation backed by DOJ.

## Denver's Response — The Inferior-Court Two-Step

Denver wrote back the same way every blue jurisdiction writes back: by pretending Supreme Court precedent does not exist and pointing instead at lower-court rulings. The reply addressed to "Ms. Dhillon" calls the DOJ's request "baseless, irresponsible, and a clear overreach of the federal government's power," then runs through a list of crime statistics that have nothing to do with whether *Heller* permits a categorical ban on common-use rifles.

The legal core of Denver's response is this:

> "All six federal appellate courts that have considered assault weapons or large capacity magazine prohibitions following *Bruen* have upheld them. ... While the Tenth Circuit has not yet ruled on an assault weapon or LCM case, in *United States v. Morgan*, a three-judge panel did unanimously hold that a statute precluding the transfer of possession of a machinegun did not violate the appellant's Second Amendment right."

Look at that list. The First (Boston), Second (NYC), Fourth (Maryland), Seventh (Chicago), Ninth (San Francisco), and D.C. Circuits — six of the most reliably anti-gun appellate courts in the country, sitting in jurisdictions whose politicians enact gun bans precisely because they know their judges will rubber-stamp them. That is not constitutional analysis. That is an echo chamber. And *United States v. Morgan*, No. 24-3141 (10th Cir. Sept. 2, 2025), is a machinegun case under 18 U.S.C. § 922(o) — not a semi-automatic rifle case. Citing it against an AR-15 ban is a category error.

## The Inferior-Court Game — and Why It Is Finally Failing

Article III says it plainly: there is "one supreme Court, and ... such inferior Courts as the Congress may from time to time ordain and establish." Inferior courts are inferior. When the Supreme Court has spoken — and in *Heller* it said clearly that arms in common use cannot be categorically banned — no number of First, Fourth, or Ninth Circuit panels can paper over that holding.

I have been making this argument since *Bruen* came down. The blue-state appellate courts know what *Heller* says. They simply do not care, because they bet the Supreme Court can only correct so many constitutional errors in a term. *Bianchi v. Brown*, 111 F.4th 438 (4th Cir. 2024) (en banc), upholding Maryland's ban, was the most embarrassing example — and when cert was sought as *Snope v. Brown* in 2025, the Court denied review, with Justices Thomas, Alito, and Gorsuch noting they would have granted. That denial bought the lower courts time. It did not vindicate them. These are the same circuits, by the way, that would have happily upheld the gutted reading of Section 2 of the Voting Rights Act the Court just dismantled in *Louisiana v. Callais*, No. 24-109 (Apr. 29, 2026).

## Burden of Proof — The Point Denver Keeps Missing

Denver also points to the dismissal of Barry Arrington's 2022 federal challenge to Colorado's large-capacity magazine ban, dismissed with prejudice in 2024 after the plaintiffs reportedly could not muster "common use" evidence. That outcome is not a vindication of the ban — it is a vindication of how badly the lower federal courts have inverted *Bruen*.

The burden of proof in an arms-ban case sits on the government. Period. As I laid out in my Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy article on the in-common-use test: once a modern law implicates the plain text of the Second Amendment — and a flat ban on a class of arms unmistakably does — the burden shifts to the government to prove a historical tradition. *Heller* named that tradition: dangerous and unusual. To meet it, the government must prove the arm is *not* in common use for lawful purposes. If it cannot, the ban falls.

Think of it like a criminal trial. If the government charges you with murder, you can sit there and watch birds out the window — the government has to prove its case beyond a reasonable doubt. Same idea here. The Second Amendment claimant does not have to come forward with anything. With tens of millions of AR-15s in private hands, "not in common use" is a case Denver cannot win.

## Where This Lands

Denver will fight. They will quote those six circuit opinions like scripture. None of it will matter at the Supreme Court. If the justices grant cert in an AR-15 case next term — and I believe they will — there are at least six votes to apply *Heller* and *Bruen* honestly. I would not be shocked by 9-0. Meanwhile, AAG Dhillon and the Second Amendment Section have done what no prior administration would: pick a fight with an anti-gun city using the federal civil-rights apparatus. Section 38-130 has stood 37 years. Its days are numbered.
