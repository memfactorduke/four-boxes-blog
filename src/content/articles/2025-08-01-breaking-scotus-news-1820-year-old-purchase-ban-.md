## Summary

The West Virginia Citizens Defense League and the Second Amendment Foundation, represented by the prominent constitutional litigation firm Cooper & Kirk, have filed a petition for certiorari in **West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF**, asking the Supreme Court of the United States to resolve whether a federal law that prohibits licensed firearms dealers from selling handguns and handgun ammunition to law-abiding adults between the ages of eighteen and twenty violates the Second Amendment. The petition arrives before the Court on the back of a fully developed and evenly split circuit conflict: three federal circuits have struck down the federal handgun purchase ban as applied to this age group, and three have upheld it. That three-to-three split, standing alone, is a classic occasion for Supreme Court intervention. My view is that the Court is likely to grant certiorari, and when it does, the text-and-history methodology of **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), compels the conclusion that the federal ban cannot stand. Eighteen-to-twenty-year-old Americans are full members of "the people" whose rights the Second Amendment guarantees, and the government cannot produce a founding-era tradition of disarming this age cohort.

## Background

Federal law, as administered and enforced by the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives, prohibits licensed firearms dealers — federal firearms licensees, or FFLs — from selling handguns or handgun ammunition to any individual under the age of twenty-one. This prohibition applies uniformly to all law-abiding Americans in the eighteen-to-twenty age range, without regard to military service, prior experience, or any individual determination of dangerousness.

The West Virginia Citizens Defense League, joined by members of that organization and affiliated plaintiffs in the eighteen-to-twenty age range, challenged the prohibition in federal court. The litigation produced a Fourth Circuit decision upholding the ban — authored by Judge Harvey Wilkinson, who has demonstrated consistent hostility to Second Amendment claims — that applied a historical tradition analysis yielding conclusions at odds with the decisions reached by a majority of circuits that have confronted the same question.

Cooper & Kirk, one of the most capable constitutional litigation firms in the country, is handling the petition for certiorari on behalf of the plaintiffs. The Second Amendment Foundation is co-petitioner. This is a well-funded, well-staffed, and strategically positioned petition that deserves and is likely to receive serious consideration from the Court.

## The Circuit Split

Six federal circuits have now addressed whether the Second Amendment protects the rights of law-abiding adults between the ages of eighteen and twenty to acquire or keep and bear firearms. The split is precisely three-to-three, and the three circuits on each side represent geographically diverse areas of the country.

The Fifth Circuit, the Eighth Circuit, and the Third Circuit have each concluded, applying *Bruen*'s text-and-history methodology, that eighteen-to-twenty-year-old adults possess full Second Amendment rights. These courts have correctly recognized that the textual reference to "the people" in the Second Amendment is not age-conditioned — it encompasses all Americans who are members of the political community, not only those who have reached a particular birthday threshold established by twentieth-century legislation.

The Fourth Circuit — in the opinion authored by Judge Wilkinson that underlies the present petition — and the Tenth and Eleventh Circuits have reached the opposite conclusion. The Tenth Circuit covers states including Colorado and New Mexico; the Eleventh Circuit, which covers Florida, Georgia, and surrounding states, produced what was a notably fractured decision authored by Judge William Pryor, an otherwise reliable jurist on constitutional questions, who nonetheless reached an outcome that I believe is analytically insupportable under *Bruen*.

A three-to-three circuit split on a federal constitutional question directly implicating an enumerated constitutional right is among the most compelling grounds for Supreme Court review. The current state of the law — in which an eighteen-year-old in Texas may purchase a handgun from a licensed dealer but the identical transaction is prohibited for an eighteen-year-old in West Virginia — is constitutionally intolerable and practically unsustainable. The Court will almost certainly take this case.

## The Textual Argument

The Second Amendment's text does not limit its protection to persons above a specified age. It guarantees "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms." In **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), the Supreme Court analyzed the phrase "the people" with reference to the Constitution's other uses of that phrase — in the First Amendment, the Fourth Amendment, the Ninth Amendment, and the Tenth Amendment — and concluded that "the people" refers to a class of persons who are part of the national community and who have otherwise developed sufficient connection with the United States to be considered part of that community.

Eighteen-year-old Americans are unambiguously members of the national community. They are citizens, subject to the full obligations of citizenship including military service, jury duty, and the payment of taxes. They vote in federal elections. They enter into contracts. The suggestion that this class of persons is somehow excluded from the "people" to whom the Second Amendment speaks has no textual foundation and reflects a willingness to constrain constitutional text by reference to post-hoc legislative choices rather than by reference to the document's original public meaning.

## The Historical Argument

Under *Bruen*, once a claimant establishes that the Second Amendment's text covers the challenged conduct, the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that the regulation is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. The government must identify a well-established and representative founding-era analog to the challenged law.

The federal handgun purchase ban for eighteen-to-twenty-year-olds has no founding-era analog. At the time of the founding, young men in this age range were not merely permitted to keep and bear arms — they were affirmatively expected to do so. The founding generation contemplated that members of the militia, who were drawn from the population of able-bodied males and who were expected to appear for service bearing their own arms, would include persons in this age range. The Second Amendment was drafted and ratified in a world where an eighteen-year-old was fully expected to own and carry a firearm, and in some contexts was legally required to possess one.

The federal ban on handgun sales to this age group is a twentieth-century invention with no ancestry in founding-era law. The government will not be able to identify a colonial or early republic statute that categorically prohibited the acquisition of firearms by law-abiding adults in the eighteen-to-twenty range. Absent such a historical tradition, the *Bruen* framework requires the law to fall.

## The Right to Acquire as a Concomitant of the Right to Keep and Bear

One of the significant subsidiary questions this case presents is whether the right to keep and bear arms — protected by the Second Amendment's text — encompasses the concomitant right to acquire firearms, including through commercial purchase from a licensed dealer.

This question is analytically straightforward. The right to "keep" arms is the right to possess them. The right to "bear" arms is the right to carry them. Both rights are hollow without the ability to acquire the arms to be kept and carried. No person is born in possession of a firearm. The right to possess necessarily implies the right to obtain. Imposing a categorical prohibition on the purchase of firearms from licensed dealers is functionally equivalent, for the affected individual, to prohibiting possession — because for most people, commercial purchase from an FFL is the only practical means of lawful acquisition.

This principle has implications extending well beyond the specific question of age-based purchase restrictions. The Court's articulation of a right to acquire as an incident of the right to keep and bear arms would establish a foundation for challenges to a range of regulatory schemes that burden the commercial acquisition of firearms, including various background check requirements, waiting periods, and serialization mandates.

## Implications

The **West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF** petition represents an important opportunity for the Supreme Court to speak directly to several questions that lower courts have handled inconsistently since *Bruen*: the scope of "the people," the application of the text-and-history methodology to age-based restrictions, and the extent to which the right to keep and bear arms encompasses the right to acquire. A well-crafted opinion in this case would be enormously valuable to the development of Second Amendment doctrine.

The Trump administration will be required to file a response to the petition and, if certiorari is granted, to defend the federal statute. The administration's posture on this question will be closely watched. A Department of Justice that declines to defend the age-based purchase prohibition, or that files a response acknowledging the validity of the Second Amendment challenge, would significantly accelerate the litigation and signal to the Court that the executive branch does not view the prohibition as constitutionally defensible.

Whatever the administration's position, the legal merits favor the petitioners. The founding-era historical record does not support categorical disarmament of any class of law-abiding American adults, and the Court should say so.
