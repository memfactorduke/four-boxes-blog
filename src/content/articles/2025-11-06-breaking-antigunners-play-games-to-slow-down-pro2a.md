## Summary

One of the most insidious and frequently misunderstood tactics employed by anti-gun government defendants in Second Amendment litigation is the deliberate conflation of "legislative facts" with "adjudicative facts." This distinction — rooted in foundational principles of constitutional adjudication — is not merely academic. When lower courts allow, or even encourage, parties to treat Second Amendment historical-tradition questions as adjudicative facts requiring extensive discovery, expert testimony, and trial-level proceedings, they are doing more than making a legal error. They are strategically prolonging litigation, depleting Second Amendment advocacy resources, and delaying the vindication of fundamental constitutional rights for as long as possible. Understanding this distinction is essential for anyone seeking to understand why Second Amendment cases move slowly and why that slowness benefits only those who oppose the right to keep and bear arms.

## Background

The Supreme Court's decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022) established a clear methodology for evaluating Second Amendment challenges to firearms regulations. Courts must first assess whether the plain text of the Second Amendment covers the challenged conduct; if it does, the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that the regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in America at the founding.

This text-and-history methodology was designed, in part, to cabin the discretion of lower court judges who had — under the pre-Bruen means-ends balancing framework — exercised essentially unconstrained power to weigh the "importance" of gun control regulations against the burden they imposed on Second Amendment rights. Bruen replaced that policy-laden inquiry with a historical inquiry: what did the founding generation understand the Second Amendment to permit?

The distinction between legislative facts and adjudicative facts is central to how that historical inquiry should — and should not — proceed.

## What Are Legislative Facts?

Legislative facts are facts that bear on the justification, meaning, or constitutionality of a law or constitutional provision. They are general in nature, not specific to the parties before the court. They include historical facts about why a constitutional provision was adopted, what contemporaneous sources reveal about its meaning, and what the legal and social context was at the time of ratification.

Examples of legislative facts in the Second Amendment context include: the text of the Second Amendment itself; the meaning of the words "keep," "bear," and "arms" at the time of ratification; the writings of founders such as Alexander Hamilton and James Madison in the Federalist Papers; the history of disarmament laws — or the absence thereof — in colonial and early American legal practice; and Cesare Beccaria's influential treatise on crimes and punishment, which Jefferson shared with Madison and which illuminates founding-era thinking on the relationship between armed citizens and government.

Legislative facts are, by their nature, determined by courts through legal analysis — by examining historical sources, treatises, records of legislative debate, and analogous laws from the founding era. They do not require trials, expert witnesses, depositions, or the apparatus of fact-finding that governs adjudicative proceedings.

## What Are Adjudicative Facts?

Adjudicative facts, by contrast, are facts about the specific parties and specific events at issue in a particular case. Did the plaintiff go through the red light? Did the defendant serve the underage patron alcohol? Is the specific plaintiff in this gun case actually eighteen years old, actually a resident of the relevant state, and actually seeking to purchase the type of firearm at issue?

Adjudicative facts are what trials are for. They are specific, particularistic, and dependent on evidence about the actual parties before the court. They are appropriately resolved through discovery, testimony, and the evidentiary machinery of the adversarial process.

## The Deliberate Conflation

The critical point — and the source of the problem in Second Amendment litigation — is that anti-gun government defendants, and the judges sympathetic to their position, routinely seek to characterize Second Amendment historical-tradition questions as adjudicative facts requiring full trial-level development. They argue that whether a founding-era analogue exists for a modern gun control law is a "disputed factual question" that requires expert witnesses, discovery, and years of litigation before any ruling can be made.

This characterization is legally incorrect. The question of whether the founding generation would have recognized a particular type of firearms regulation as consistent with the Second Amendment is a legislative fact. It is a question about historical law and legal context — the kind of question courts answer every day through legal analysis of historical sources, without depositions and without trials. It is no more an "adjudicative fact" than the meaning of the Commerce Clause or the original understanding of the Eighth Amendment's "cruel and unusual" standard.

## Why This Matters: The Strategic Logic of Delay

One must never lose sight of the strategic reality governing Second Amendment litigation. In the overwhelming majority of cases, Second Amendment plaintiffs want their cases decided quickly. They are challenging gun control laws that, pending resolution of the litigation, remain in force and continue to burden their constitutional rights. Every day a case remains unresolved is another day the challenged law operates to restrict what the plaintiff can lawfully possess, carry, or purchase.

The government — and anti-gun defendants generally — want exactly the opposite. They want cases to move as slowly as possible. While a case drags through years of discovery, expert testimony, trial court proceedings, and successive appeals, the law they are defending remains on the books and continues to restrict Second Amendment rights. Delay is, for the anti-gun side, a form of victory: they deprive plaintiffs of the practical enjoyment of their constitutional rights for as long as the litigation continues.

The conflation of legislative facts with adjudicative facts is the legal mechanism by which this delay is engineered. By successfully arguing that historical tradition questions require extensive trial-level fact development, government defendants can transform what should be a relatively swift legal analysis into multi-year litigation campaigns consuming enormous resources on both sides — resources that the taxpayer-funded government can sustain far more easily than private Second Amendment litigants.

## Anti-Gun Judges as Enablers

Judges who are ideologically sympathetic to gun control understand this dynamic and exploit it. By accepting the government's framing of historical-tradition questions as adjudicative facts requiring trial, these judges accomplish two things simultaneously: they delay the potential invalidation of the challenged law, and they impose asymmetric resource costs on Second Amendment plaintiffs that may deter future challenges.

The Supreme Court's Bruen framework was intended to make Second Amendment adjudication more legally determinate and less subject to judicial policy preferences. But lower courts that treat the historical inquiry as an occasion for open-ended fact discovery are, in practice, reconstructing the very discretionary analysis that Bruen eliminated — just under a different label.

## Implications

Attorneys litigating Second Amendment cases must affirmatively resist any attempt by government defendants to characterize Second Amendment historical-tradition questions as adjudicative facts. These are legislative facts. They are matters of legal and historical analysis, not of particularistic trial-level fact-finding. Courts should resolve them as a matter of law, on a developed legal record, without the delay and expense of discovery and expert battles.

Second Amendment supporters and advocacy organizations must understand this dynamic so they can recognize delay tactics when they encounter them, support litigation strategies that aggressively challenge improper fact-characterization arguments, and hold lower courts accountable when they permit government defendants to weaponize the distinction between legislative and adjudicative facts as a tool for constitutional delay.

The right to keep and bear arms is a fundamental constitutional right. It is not a subject fit for indefinite judicial limbo engineered through procedural manipulation. Courts that permit such manipulation are not discharging their constitutional function — they are facilitating the evasion of a binding Supreme Court precedent by other means.
