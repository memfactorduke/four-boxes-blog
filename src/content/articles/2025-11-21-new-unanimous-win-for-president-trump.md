## Summary

A three-judge panel of the United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit, including Judge Frank Easterbrook, has unanimously voted to stay the eight-page preliminary injunction entered by Judge Sarah Ellis of the United States District Court for the Northern District of Illinois, which had purported to govern in granular operational detail how Immigration and Customs Enforcement agents must conduct themselves in the Chicago area. The Seventh Circuit's unanimous decision — reaching across ideological lines to include judges not known as reliable conservatives — reflects the constitutional indefensibility of a single district court judge's attempt to assume supervisory control over a federal law enforcement agency that answers to the President of the United States.

## Background

The Trump administration, working through the Department of Homeland Security, ICE Director Tom Homan, and DHS Secretary Kristi Noem, has been engaged in a sustained national effort to enforce the nation's immigration laws — including the arrest and deportation of persons present in the United States illegally. That effort has been, predictably, resisted in the courts by progressive plaintiffs and their allies.

Among the resistance efforts was a lawsuit filed in the Northern District of Illinois before Judge Sarah Ellis, a federal district court judge whose background included being born in Canada to parents born in Jamaica. The lawsuit sought to constrain ICE's conduct in the Chicago area, and Judge Ellis responded by entering a sweeping, eight-page preliminary injunction that did not merely impose broad limitations on ICE operations — it regulated ICE agents' conduct at the operational and granular level.

Judge Ellis's injunction required ICE agents to wear visible identification at all times, mandated the use of body cameras, required agents to issue two verbal warnings before deploying any crowd control device, and otherwise prescribed in specific terms how federal law enforcement officers must perform their duties. In effect, Judge Ellis appointed herself as the operational supervisor of a federal executive branch agency.

## The Constitutional Problem with Judicial Operational Micromanagement

The United States Constitution vests executive power in the President, not in the federal district courts. Article II, Section 1 states: "The executive Power shall be vested in a President of the United States of America." Article II, Section 3 further provides that the President "shall take Care that the Laws be faithfully executed."

The President's authority over federal law enforcement agencies is nearly plenary. The agencies of the executive branch — including DHS, ICE, and their component units — derive their authority from the President and are accountable to the President, not to individual federal district court judges. When a district court judge issues an injunction specifying in granular operational detail how federal agents must conduct themselves in the field, that judge is usurping executive authority that the Constitution assigns to a different branch of government.

This is the central constitutional defect in Judge Ellis's injunction. The order was not merely a broad prohibition on a class of governmental conduct — which might have been within the court's equitable authority in an appropriate case. It was a detailed operational directive that functioned as a set of regulations issued by one branch of government over another branch's internal operations.

## The Seventh Circuit's Response

The Seventh Circuit panel — consisting of Judge Michael Brennan, Judge Frank Easterbrook, and Judge Michael Scudder — unanimously agreed that Judge Ellis had overstepped her judicial authority and entered an order staying her preliminary injunction.

The participation of Judge Frank Easterbrook in this unanimous result deserves particular note. Judge Easterbrook is not known as a reliable conservative on matters of executive power or immigration, and his work on the Seventh Circuit has drawn criticism from Second Amendment advocates on other issues. The fact that even Judge Easterbrook concluded that Judge Ellis had exceeded the bounds of permissible judicial authority speaks to how manifestly indefensible her injunction was. When a judge who is not inclined toward restraint finds a judicial act to have been an overreach, the overreach was severe.

The Seventh Circuit's order effectively freezes Judge Ellis's injunction, restoring the operational freedom that ICE requires to perform its statutory duties under the supervision of the President and his delegated officers.

## The Pattern of Judicial Lawfare

Judge Ellis's injunction in the Northern District of Illinois is not an isolated incident. It reflects a deliberate pattern of judicial resistance to the Trump administration's immigration enforcement agenda. Progressive legal organizations have identified federal district courts in favorable jurisdictions — Chicago, San Francisco, New York, Seattle — as vehicles for obtaining broad preliminary injunctions that delay or impede the administration's lawful exercise of executive power.

The tactic is strategically rational from the plaintiffs' perspective: even a preliminary injunction that is ultimately reversed has real practical effect, because it takes time for the Supreme Court or a circuit court to correct the lower court's error. During the months or years of litigation, the injunction operates as a veto over the executive's enforcement agenda.

There is an old legal joke, revived recently in a dissent by Judge Jerry Smith, that captures the attitude underlying this pattern: What is the difference between God and a federal district court judge? The answer is that God does not believe he is a federal district court judge. Judge Ellis's eight-page operational directive suggests she may not share that humility.

## The Role of the Seventh Circuit

The Seventh Circuit's unanimous stay is important not only for its immediate practical effect — restoring ICE's operational freedom in Chicago — but also for the institutional signal it sends.

Courts of appeals serve a gatekeeping function with respect to district court overreach. When a district court judge issues an order that exceeds the bounds of judicial authority, the circuit court has both the power and the duty to correct that error. The Seventh Circuit's unanimous action here demonstrates that at least some members of the appellate bench, across ideological lines, recognize and are willing to enforce the separation of powers that the Constitution requires.

Judge Brennan, in particular, has distinguished himself as one of the stronger judges in the Seventh Circuit on questions of constitutional structure and executive power. His participation in the unanimous stay is consistent with his prior record. The willingness of Judges Easterbrook and Scudder to join in a unanimous result amplifies the significance of the ruling.

## Implications

The Seventh Circuit's stay of Judge Ellis's injunction represents a meaningful institutional correction of a district court that exceeded its authority. ICE can now resume its statutory enforcement functions in the Chicago area without being subject to the operational micromanagement that a single unelected judge attempted to impose.

The broader pattern of judicial resistance to the Trump administration's immigration agenda will continue. More injunctions will be sought, and some will be granted by friendly district courts. But the Seventh Circuit's unanimous action in this case demonstrates that the courts of appeals retain the capacity and the willingness to enforce constitutional limits on judicial authority — even when doing so benefits an administration that the progressive legal establishment opposes.

That is, as it should be, how the constitutional system of separation of powers is supposed to function.
