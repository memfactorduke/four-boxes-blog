## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit has held, in a 2-1 decision authored by Judge James Ho, that 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) — the federal felon-in-possession statute — is unconstitutional as applied to Edward Cockerham. Cockerham's predicate conviction was a Mississippi state felony for failure to pay child support, an offense for which he served zero days in prison and which he subsequently remedied by paying the outstanding debt in full. The Fifth Circuit held that the Second Amendment's historical tradition of firearms regulation does not support the permanent, categorical disarmament of individuals whose prior conviction involved no connection to physical violence or dangerousness. Judge Ho's opinion contains notable language, including the declaration that Second Amendment analysis "must be guided by history, not hoplophobia" — a term denoting an irrational fear of weapons.

This decision continues the development of as-applied Second Amendment jurisprudence in the wake of **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022) and builds upon the Third Circuit's earlier ruling in **Range v. Attorney General** (2023), in which a nonviolent food stamp fraud conviction was held insufficient to justify lifetime firearms disarmament.

## Background

Federal law at 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) prohibits any person who has been convicted of "a crime punishable by imprisonment for a term exceeding one year" from possessing a firearm. The statute's operative test turns on the potential punishment for the predicate offense — not on whether the defendant was actually imprisoned, and not on whether the predicate offense bore any relationship to violence or physical dangerousness. As a practical matter, § 922(g)(1) operates as a permanent, lifetime firearms prohibition for any person who has sustained such a conviction, regardless of the offense's character, the sentence actually imposed, or the passage of time.

Edward Cockerham was convicted under Mississippi state law of failing to pay child support — a non-payment he subsequently cured by satisfying the obligation in full. The Mississippi statute under which he was convicted carried a maximum potential sentence of five years imprisonment, bringing it within § 922(g)(1)'s definitional threshold. Cockerham served zero days in prison. He was later found in possession of two firearms and was prosecuted under § 922(g)(1). He preserved an as-applied Second Amendment challenge, arguing that the statute's application to him — a person convicted of a wholly nonviolent financial offense who had remedied the underlying conduct — was unconstitutional under the *Bruen* framework.

## The As-Applied Challenge Framework

The as-applied constitutional challenge is the appropriate vehicle for addressing the overbreadth of § 922(g)(1)'s categorical sweep. The statute can be facially constitutional — meaning there exist applications of it that are consistent with the Second Amendment, such as disarming persons convicted of violent crimes — while simultaneously being unconstitutional in particular applications, such as the permanent disarmament of a person whose entire criminal history consists of a nonviolent financial offense.

The Third Circuit had established this framework in **Range v. Attorney General** (2023), where Bryan Range challenged his § 922(g)(1) disarmament based on a decades-old Pennsylvania misdemeanor for food stamp fraud involving a few hundred dollars. The en banc Third Circuit held that § 922(g)(1) was unconstitutional as applied to Range. The historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States — the *Bruen* standard — did not support the permanent disarmament of a person whose sole predicate offense was a nonviolent financial crime with no connection to physical dangerousness.

*Cockerham* follows this analytical path in the Fifth Circuit.

## Judge Ho's Analysis

The Fifth Circuit's majority opinion, authored by Judge Ho, applies the *Bruen* text-and-history methodology directly. The operative constitutional question is not whether Congress had a policy reason to enact § 922(g)(1) in its broad categorical form, or whether the legislature believed such a prohibition would serve public safety interests. The question is whether the modern prohibition is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation.

Judge Ho's opinion identifies two critical historical propositions that the *Bruen* framework supports. First, the historical tradition unquestionably permits the government to disarm violent criminals. The founding-era and Reconstruction-era practice of disarming individuals judged to be physically dangerous to others is amply documented, and those precedents justify the application of § 922(g)(1) to persons convicted of genuinely dangerous offenses. Second, that same historical tradition provides no support for the proposition that persons convicted of nonviolent offenses forfeit their Second Amendment rights permanently and categorically. As Judge Ho's opinion states, history does not support the conclusion that felons lose their Second Amendment rights "solely because of their status as felons."

The critical insight is that the founding generation understood disarmament as a response to demonstrated physical dangerousness, not as a categorical consequence of any conviction carrying more than one year of potential imprisonment. § 922(g)(1)'s mechanical application of the latter rule — imposed for life, regardless of the nature of the offense, regardless of whether any incarceration occurred, and regardless of subsequent rehabilitation — exceeds what the historical tradition supports.

Judge Ho's statement that Second Amendment analysis "must be guided by history, not hoplophobia" is directed at judicial reasoning that substitutes an emotional or policy-based aversion to firearms for the demanding historical inquiry that *Bruen* requires. Courts that uphold sweeping gun regulations by invoking generalized concerns about public safety, rather than by identifying historical analogues, are engaged in precisely the interest-balancing approach that *Bruen* forbade.

## The Overcriminalization Problem

Judge Ho's opinion also engages the broader problem of overcriminalization — the phenomenon by which the criminal code has expanded so dramatically that it now criminalizes vast categories of conduct bearing no historical connection to the kind of danger or moral culpability that traditionally justified disarmament.

Citing Justice Neil Gorsuch's observation that criminal laws "have grown so exuberantly and come to cover so much more previously innocent conduct that almost anyone can be arrested for something," the opinion situates the § 922(g)(1) problem within the larger pathology of an overextended criminal law. When the category of "felonies" sweeps in regulatory violations, financial misdemeanors elevated by prosecutorial charging choices, and minor offenses punishable by more than one year, the mechanical application of § 922(g)(1) to that entire universe of convictions produces constitutional violations at scale.

## The Dissent and Its Significance

Judge Higginson dissented, but notably did not challenge the majority's conclusion that the government had failed to satisfy its burden of demonstrating historical support for the disarmament of Cockerham specifically. Rather, the dissent argued that the case should be remanded to the district court for additional factual development regarding the historical record. The majority correctly rejected this approach, noting that historical analysis in the Second Amendment context does not require evidentiary proceedings or expert witnesses — the Supreme Court itself has conducted historical analysis in *Heller*, *Bruen*, and *Rahimi* without such proceedings.

The dissent's position thus acknowledges the core constitutional problem while seeking procedural delay rather than principled resolution. That concession is itself significant: even the dissenting judge could not identify a historical tradition sufficient to justify § 922(g)(1)'s application to Cockerham as the record then stood.

## Implications

The Fifth Circuit's decision in *United States v. Cockerham* adds to a growing body of post-*Bruen* decisions establishing that § 922(g)(1) cannot constitutionally operate as a permanent, categorical firearms prohibition applicable to every person convicted of any offense punishable by more than one year, regardless of the offense's character. The Third Circuit's *Range* decision and now the Fifth Circuit's *Cockerham* decision establish that circuit-level authority is developing in favor of as-applied challenges by nonviolent offenders.

For Second Amendment practitioners, the doctrinal implication is clear: defendants facing § 922(g)(1) charges, or persons seeking to restore firearms rights following nonviolent convictions, have a well-developed constitutional argument under *Bruen* that the statute cannot constitutionally be applied to them. The strength of the argument is a function of the predicate offense — the further that offense is from the historical tradition of disarming the physically dangerous, the stronger the as-applied challenge.

The broader principle that Judge Ho's opinion captures — that the Second Amendment's scope is determined by history, not by judicial discomfort with firearms — is the correct constitutional rule. Courts that take that principle seriously will continue to find § 922(g)(1) unconstitutionally overextended as applied to the nonviolent, the reformed, and the marginally convicted.
