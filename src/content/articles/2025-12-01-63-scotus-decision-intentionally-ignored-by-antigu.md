## Summary

I have recently published a scholarly article in the Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy Per Curiam entitled "Licenses Delayed, Rights Denied: How Contemporary Firearm Carry Licensing Regimes Continue to Violate the Second Amendment." This article presents what I believe is the first comprehensive post-**Bruen** analysis of how anti-gun licensing jurisdictions — California, New York, New Jersey, Maryland, Hawaii, and their allies — have systematically shifted their strategies for denying Second Amendment rights in the wake of the Supreme Court's 2022 ruling that struck down discretionary "good reason" licensing requirements. As I document in detail, these jurisdictions have not abandoned their goal of nullifying the right to carry; they have merely changed their tactics, employing what I term "administrative nullification" — the use of facially neutral bureaucratic processes laden with hidden discretion, unreasonable delays, prohibitive costs, and character-assessment requirements — to achieve the same suppression of Second Amendment rights that was prohibited by **Bruen**.

## Background: The Pre-Bruen Licensing Regime

Prior to the Supreme Court's landmark 2022 decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, a cluster of approximately six jurisdictions — New York, New Jersey, California, Maryland, Hawaii, and a handful of others — operated under "may-issue" concealed carry licensing regimes. Under these regimes, a licensing official possessed essentially unlimited discretion to deny a permit application from an otherwise qualified applicant who could not demonstrate a "good reason," "justifiable need," or "proper cause" for wanting to carry a firearm in public. Self-defense alone — the quintessential purpose for which the right to bear arms exists — was explicitly declared to be insufficient. Only applicants who could demonstrate a particular, individualized, and exigent threat to their personal safety received permits; ordinary law-abiding citizens who simply wished to exercise their constitutional right to carry a firearm for self-defense were routinely denied.

The **Bruen** decision obliterated this regime. The Court held, in a landmark opinion authored by Justice Clarence Thomas and joined by five colleagues, that the Second and Fourteenth Amendments protect an individual's right to carry a handgun in public for self-defense, and that New York's "proper cause" requirement — and by implication the similar requirements of the other may-issue states — violated that right. States could require applicants for carry permits to demonstrate that they met objective criteria such as age and absence of a disqualifying criminal history, but they could not vest licensing officials with discretion to deny permits to ordinary law-abiding citizens.

## The Shift to "Administrative Nullification"

In the aftermath of **Bruen**, the six outlier jurisdictions did not comply in good faith with the Supreme Court's mandate. Instead, as I document extensively in my Harvard article, they pivoted to a new strategy that I term "administrative nullification." Rather than simply accepting that the right to carry must be recognized for all law-abiding citizens, these jurisdictions engineered their licensing processes to preserve as much of the practical effect of the old denial regime as possible while maintaining the surface appearance of compliance with **Bruen**.

The strategy operates through several interconnected mechanisms:

### The Shift from "Good Reason" to "Good Character"

The most elegant piece of legal sleight of hand employed by the administrative nullification states involves the recharacterization of impermissible discretion. Before **Bruen**, licensing officials asked: Do you have a good enough *reason* to carry a gun? After **Bruen**, the question became: Are you a good enough *person* to carry a gun?

This substitution is constitutionally indistinguishable from the regime **Bruen** condemned, but it was marketed as compliance. Under the revised schemes, licensing officials claim authority to assess the "good moral character" of applicants — a standard that, as applied in practice, vests the same unbounded discretion in the same anti-gun officials that previously exercised discretion over "proper cause" determinations. The official's political and ideological opposition to gun ownership is laundered through the nominally neutral category of "character," but the practical result is identical: law-abiding citizens who want to carry are denied permits, and the denial is insulated from review by the vague and subjective nature of the "character" determination.

The Supreme Court addressed this directly in **United States v. Rahimi** (2024), where Chief Justice Roberts' opinion made clear that the relevant constitutional touchstone for disarmament is whether an individual is "violently or physically a dangerous threat to himself or to others" — not the broader and more subjective question of whether the licensing official considers the applicant to be a person of sufficiently good "moral character." The administrative nullification states' character requirements cannot be reconciled with this standard, and they must be challenged and struck down.

### Unreasonable Delays as a Tool of Deprivation

A second mechanism of administrative nullification involves deliberate processing delays that can extend for a year or more in the most anti-gun jurisdictions. The NYPD's licensing division, the Los Angeles Sheriff's Department, and analogous agencies in the other outlier jurisdictions have converted what should be a ministerial review process — essentially a background check to confirm that the applicant has no disqualifying criminal history — into a marathon bureaucratic ordeal lasting many months.

During this delay, the applicant possesses the constitutional right to carry but cannot lawfully exercise it. The right is held in suspense, in practical terms nullified, by the government's deliberate refusal to process the application in a timely manner. The practical effect of a twelve-month processing delay on the exercise of a constitutional right is comparable to what would happen if a state imposed a twelve-month waiting period on the publication of a newspaper, the exercise of a religion, or the retention of an attorney. No court would countenance such delays in the context of First, Fourth, or Sixth Amendment rights; the same constitutional logic must apply to Second Amendment rights.

In my article, I document specific processing times and compare them with the timelines applicable to licensing in shall-issue states, demonstrating the stark disparity between jurisdictions that comply in good faith with **Bruen** and those that employ administrative nullification.

### Prohibitive Costs as a Deterrent

Related to processing delays are the fees and costs associated with the licensing process in the outlier jurisdictions. Application fees, mandatory training requirements, fingerprinting costs, and ancillary administrative charges have been structured in some jurisdictions to impose costs measured in hundreds or even thousands of dollars on applicants seeking to exercise a constitutional right. When these costs are combined with the requirement to retain an attorney to navigate the complexity of the application process — a de facto requirement in some jurisdictions — the total cost of exercising the right to carry can reach levels that are prohibitive for ordinary working Americans.

A constitutional right that can only be practically exercised by the wealthy is not a right in any meaningful sense. The historical tradition of the Second Amendment includes no analog for wealth-based restrictions on the right to keep and bear arms, and no such restriction can be sustained under **Bruen**'s text-history-tradition methodology.

## The Role of the Courts

The administrative nullification regime I have described is not self-correcting. The officials who administer these licensing schemes have strong political incentives to resist compliance with **Bruen**, and absent judicial intervention, they will continue to find new and creative ways to deny the practical exercise of Second Amendment rights. The courts must step in to enforce **Bruen**'s mandate.

The immediate need is for litigants challenging the character-assessment requirements and processing delay practices in the outlier jurisdictions to bring well-developed cases to the federal courts of appeals and ultimately to the Supreme Court. The legal arguments are strong. The evidentiary record that I have compiled in my article provides a foundation for demonstrating the systematic nature of the administrative nullification strategy and its practical effect on the exercise of Second Amendment rights.

The Supreme Court has already signaled its impatience with lower-court resistance to **Bruen** through its summary reversals in several cases. That signal must be amplified and given enforcement force through new litigation targeting the specific mechanisms of administrative nullification.

## Implications

The publication of my Harvard article represents an attempt to provide the scholarly and evidentiary foundation that litigants, judges, and policymakers need to understand and address the administrative nullification problem. Several implications warrant emphasis:

**The fight for Bruen compliance is not over.** Winning **Bruen** was essential, but it was not sufficient. The anti-gun states immediately began circumventing its mandate, and that circumvention continues to this day. Second Amendment advocates must remain on offense, filing new cases and building the evidentiary record needed to demonstrate the systematic nature of the noncompliance.

**Character requirements must be specifically targeted.** The shift from "good reason" to "good moral character" as a basis for permit denial is the most important specific mechanism of administrative nullification currently in operation, and it must be the primary target of future litigation. The Supreme Court's language in **Rahimi** provides powerful ammunition for this challenge.

**Processing delays are independently litigable.** Even in jurisdictions that have formally eliminated discretionary character assessments, unreasonable processing delays may constitute an independent Second Amendment violation. Litigants should develop this theory and bring cases specifically targeting the delay practices of the NYPD, the Los Angeles Sheriff's Department, and their counterparts.

**The scholarly record matters.** Courts and policymakers need rigorous empirical documentation of how the administrative nullification strategy operates in practice. My Harvard article is a contribution to that record, but more scholarship in this area is needed.

The right to carry a firearm for self-defense, won in **Bruen**, will not be made real without a sustained, systematic, and sophisticated litigation strategy targeting every mechanism that the anti-gun states deploy to obstruct it. That strategy begins with understanding what those mechanisms are — and this article is my contribution to that understanding.
