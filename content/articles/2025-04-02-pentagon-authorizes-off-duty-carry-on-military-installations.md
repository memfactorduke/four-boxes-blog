## Summary

Secretary of Defense Pete Hegseth has signed a memorandum directing all military installation commanders to allow uniformed service members to request permission to carry privately owned firearms while off duty on Department of Defense property within the United States. The policy creates a presumption in favor of approval and requires any denial to be issued in writing with a detailed explanation. The directive represents a significant reversal of longstanding restrictions that effectively made military bases gun-free zones.

## Background

For decades, U.S. military installations have operated under policies that severely restricted service members from carrying personal firearms on base. Unless personnel were actively training or serving as military police, privately owned weapons were generally prohibited on post. In practical terms, as Secretary Hegseth acknowledged in his announcement, American military bases functioned as gun-free zones.

This policy framework persisted despite a series of high-profile attacks on military installations that exposed the vulnerability of unarmed personnel. Secretary Hegseth specifically referenced incidents at Fort Hood, Holloman Air Force Base, and Naval Air Station Pensacola as illustrative of the threat. In each instance, service members -- trained in the use of firearms and entrusted with national defense -- were unable to defend themselves or their colleagues during active shooter situations because they lacked access to personal weapons on base.

The statutory authority for the new policy traces to Section 526 of the National Defense Authorization Act (NDAA) for Fiscal Year 2016, which provides that the Secretary of Defense "may authorize a member of the armed forces who is assigned to duty at an installation, center, or facility to carry an appropriate firearm on the installation, center, or facility if the commander determines that carrying such a firearm is necessary as a personal or force protection measure." While that provision has been on the books for nearly a decade, it had not previously been operationalized at this scale.

## The New Policy

Secretary Hegseth's memorandum, addressed to senior Pentagon leadership, Defense Agency directors, and Department of Defense field activity directors, is titled "Non-Official Personal Protection Arming on Department of Defense Property." Its core provisions include:

- **Presumption of approval.** Service members requesting to carry privately owned firearms while off duty on military installations will benefit from a presumption that carrying is necessary for personal protection. This effectively inverts the prior framework, under which permission was exceedingly difficult to obtain.

- **Written denial requirement.** If an installation commander denies a request, the denial must be issued in writing and must explain in detail the basis for the decision. This procedural safeguard creates accountability and a reviewable record.

- **Consistency with state law.** The policy requires that personal carry on installations align with the laws of the state in which the installation is located, meaning service members must still comply with applicable state firearms regulations.

- **Scope.** The memorandum applies to uniformed service members in their non-official duty capacity at Department of Defense properties within the United States.

In his video announcement, Secretary Hegseth framed the policy in constitutional terms, asserting that the Second Amendment "enshrines the right of all citizens to carry weapons to protect themselves, their families, and their fellow countrymen," and that service members "are no less entitled to exercise their God-given right to keep and bear arms than any other American."

## Analysis

The policy shift is notable for several reasons.

**Constitutional framing.** The memorandum is grounded not merely in force-protection pragmatism but in an explicitly rights-based rationale. By invoking the Second Amendment and characterizing the right to bear arms as pre-political and God-given, the Department of Defense is signaling a philosophical alignment between military policy and the broader originalist interpretation of the Second Amendment that has gained traction in federal courts.

**The gun-free zone critique.** The policy reflects a longstanding argument, advanced by researchers including Dr. John Lott, that gun-free zones create environments of heightened vulnerability. The logic is straightforward: individuals intent on mass violence select targets where resistance is least likely. Military installations, despite their association with armed force, were paradoxically among the most restrictive environments for personal firearms carry. The new policy directly addresses that contradiction.

**Broader administration strategy.** The memorandum does not exist in isolation. It is part of a wider Trump administration effort to advance Second Amendment interests across multiple federal agencies. The Department of Justice has established a Second Amendment unit within its Civil Rights Division, led by Harlan Dillon, which has filed amicus briefs and initiated litigation challenging state-level firearms restrictions, including assault weapon bans and large-capacity magazine bans. Separately, the United States Postal Service has moved to permit the shipment of certain firearms through the mail, reversing prior restrictions. The Pentagon memorandum adds the Department of Defense to this multi-agency effort.

**The Overton window.** The policy arrives amid a broader expansion of firearms carry rights at the state level. The number of states adopting permitless carry laws has grown from one (Vermont) to twenty-nine, with North Carolina potentially becoming the thirtieth. Against this backdrop, the federal government's decision to extend carry rights onto military installations reflects and reinforces a national trend toward expanded firearms access.

## Implications

The practical impact of the policy will depend substantially on implementation at the installation level. The memorandum creates a presumption in favor of carry, but individual commanders retain authority to deny requests, subject to the written-explanation requirement. How broadly or narrowly commanders exercise that residual discretion will determine whether the policy produces a meaningful change in day-to-day conditions on military bases or remains largely aspirational.

The state-law compliance requirement introduces additional complexity. Military installations span jurisdictions with vastly different firearms regulatory regimes. A service member stationed in Texas will face a different legal landscape than one at a base in California or New York. The interaction between federal authorization and state-level restrictions -- particularly in states with stringent permitting requirements -- may generate friction and, potentially, litigation.

From a constitutional perspective, the policy reinforces the position that the Second Amendment's reference to "the people" encompasses all Americans, including those in military service. While service members have historically been subject to distinct regulatory frameworks by virtue of their status, the memorandum's rights-based framing pushes back against the notion that government employment diminishes constitutional protections.

Whether this policy survives future administrations is an open question. Executive memoranda are reversible instruments, and a subsequent administration with different policy priorities could rescind or narrow the directive. For the present, however, the memorandum represents a significant and concrete expansion of firearms carry rights on federal property.
