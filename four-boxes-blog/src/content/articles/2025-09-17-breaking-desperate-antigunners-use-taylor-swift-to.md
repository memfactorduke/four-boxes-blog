## Summary

Anti-gun advocates and certain federal courts have advanced the argument that government-mandated gun-free zones may be constitutionally extended to any location where large numbers of people congregate — including concert venues, sporting events, zoos, and similar public spaces. A Bloomberg News article summarizing this emerging trend cited my analysis among others, and the article's discussion highlights how lower courts are attempting to expand the "sensitive places" concept recognized in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), beyond the narrow historical examples the Supreme Court identified. In my assessment, the extension of sensitive places doctrine to concert venues and other crowded public spaces is constitutionally insupportable under *Bruen* and *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and represents another attempt by anti-gun advocates to use an exception as a mechanism for swallowing the constitutional rule.

## Background

The *Bruen* decision held that the Second Amendment protects the right to carry firearms in public for self-defense. The Court acknowledged that some locations — denominated "sensitive places" — may constitutionally be designated as gun-free zones by the government. The Court identified three specific historical examples of sensitive places: courthouses, legislative chambers, and polling places. It further indicated that analogies to those three categories might support some additional designations.

The *Bruen* Court's sensitive places analysis was brief and deliberately limited. The Court expressed no view on where the outer boundaries of the doctrine lie, but it specified three places with a clear historical pedigree. The Court did not hold that sensitive places are defined by the number of people present, the nature of the activity occurring, or the preferences of property owners or event organizers. It held that sensitive places are defined by historical practice.

Anti-gun advocates have seized on the *Bruen* Court's brief and tentative sensitive places discussion to argue that the category should be expanded dramatically — to any location where, in their view, the presence of firearms poses an elevated risk. The argument is that concert venues, stadiums, zoos, and similar locations where crowds gather are analogous to the historical sensitive places the Court identified. This argument is, in my analysis, legally and historically wrong.

## The Historical Meaning of Sensitive Places

The three locations the *Bruen* Court identified as historical sensitive places — courthouses, legislative chambers, and polling places — share a specific structural characteristic that distinguishes them from concert venues and other crowded public spaces: they were locations where the government assumed responsibility for providing armed security to those who entered, thereby relieving the individual of the need to be personally armed for self-defense.

Courts are attended by marshals and officers of the court, who are responsible for maintaining order and protecting those within the courthouse. Legislative chambers are protected by legislative police and security personnel. Polling places, in the historical period, were supervised locations where the government controlled the environment and maintained order. In each case, the individual's right to be personally armed was displaced by the government's assumption of responsibility for providing armed protection.

This structural rationale — the government provides armed security in exchange for the individual surrendering the right to personal arms — is the constitutional foundation for sensitive places designations. It is not rooted in the density of crowds, the nature of the activity, or the preferences of private parties. And it provides the analytical limit on what locations may constitutionally be designated as sensitive places.

Concert venues, sporting events, and zoos do not share this structural characteristic. Event organizers are not government officials. The security presence at a Taylor Swift concert, however extensive, is not analogous to the government-provided security that historically justified disarming individuals at courthouses and legislative chambers. The government is not assuming responsibility for the individual's defense — the event organizer is providing crowd management, which is a different thing entirely.

## The Slippery Slope of an Unprincipled Sensitive Places Doctrine

The practical consequences of adopting the anti-gun advocates' position — that any crowded public space may be designated a gun-free zone — would be to transform the exception into the rule. Under this theory, any outdoor market, any public festival, any busy street corner at certain times of day could be designated a government-mandated gun-free zone. The right to carry firearms in public, which the *Bruen* Court held is protected by the Second Amendment, would effectively be reduced to a right to carry in the least-populated and least-traveled locations — which is to say, the right would be drained of its practical significance.

The *Bruen* Court was attentive to this danger. It rejected New York's "proper cause" requirement precisely because that requirement had the practical effect of prohibiting most law-abiding citizens from exercising the right to public carry. An expansive sensitive places doctrine that sweeps in all locations where crowds gather would accomplish the same prohibition through a different mechanism, and the constitutional result would be the same.

## The Security-for-Rights Exchange That Justifies Sensitive Places

The principle I have articulated in this area — that the government may designate a location as a sensitive place only where it provides armed security that substitutes for the individual's personal right to be armed — is, I believe, consistent with the historical record and with the *Bruen* Court's reasoning. It provides a principled limit that prevents the exception from consuming the rule, and it is grounded in a concrete historical rationale rather than in open-ended policy balancing.

Under this principle, locations that wish to prohibit the carrying of firearms by lawfully armed citizens must provide meaningful armed security that credibly substitutes for the individual's right to self-defense. Concert promoters and venue operators who prohibit concealed carry but provide minimal security are not entitled to government enforcement of a prohibition that the Constitution would otherwise not permit.

## The Duty to Protect and the Right to Self-Defense

A related principle bears mention: the government, as a general matter, has no duty to protect individual citizens from criminal harm. This proposition, established in federal and state law, means that when the government — or a private entity acting with government authority — strips an individual of the right to be personally armed, it assumes a moral and perhaps legal responsibility that is not otherwise borne. The constitutionally problematic extension of gun-free zones to concert venues is compounded by the absence of any corresponding duty to protect those who are disarmed.

This is precisely the circumstance in which the right to keep and bear arms has its greatest practical significance: when individuals are in public spaces where the government is not present to protect them and where the risk of criminal violence is real. Stripping individuals of the right to personal arms in these circumstances — based on nothing more than the presence of a crowd — is a serious and unjustified infringement of the constitutional right the *Bruen* Court recognized.

## Implications

The attempt to extend the sensitive places doctrine to concert venues and other crowded public spaces is, in my assessment, constitutionally indefensible under *Bruen* and represents the latest iteration of the anti-gun movement's strategy of using judicial doctrines as progressive ratchets to erode Second Amendment rights. The *Bruen* framework, properly applied, does not support this extension: sensitive places are defined by historical practice and by the government's assumption of responsibility for providing armed security, not by the number of people present.

The lower courts that have been receptive to expanded sensitive places designations are applying *Bruen* in a manner inconsistent with its text and reasoning. The Supreme Court will, eventually, need to provide further guidance on the outer limits of the sensitive places doctrine. When it does so, the analysis I have described — rooted in the historical rationale for sensitive places designations and in the constitutional principle that a right cannot be effectively abolished through the use of an expanding exception — should provide the foundation for a ruling that preserves the public carry right the *Bruen* Court recognized.
