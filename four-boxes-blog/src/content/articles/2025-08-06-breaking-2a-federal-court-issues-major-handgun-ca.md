## Summary

On August 6, 2025, United States District Judge William Smith — a George W. Bush appointee sitting in the United States District Court for the District of Rhode Island — issued a decision in **O'Neal v. Noona** holding that Rhode Island's permit scheme for the open carry of handguns does not violate the Second Amendment. The plaintiffs — approximately six to seven individuals who possessed concealed carry permits under Rhode Island's shall-issue regime but had been denied "unrestricted" permits that would have authorized open carry — argued that the Second Amendment's guarantee of the right to "bear" arms necessarily encompasses the right to carry a firearm openly and that Rhode Island's discretionary process for issuing unrestricted permits was constitutionally invalid. Judge Smith disagreed, concluding that because the plaintiffs already possessed concealed carry permits that authorized them to carry handguns in public, their individual Second Amendment rights had not been violated by the state's refusal to additionally authorize open carry. This decision is consistent with the current legal trajectory on the open carry question and, while it may disappoint some in the Second Amendment community, reflects the realistic state of the law as applied by today's federal courts.

## Background

Rhode Island's permit framework for carrying handguns involves two distinct instruments. The first is a shall-issue concealed carry permit: any qualifying individual who applies — meaning any person who is not a prohibited person, who meets basic statutory requirements, and who completes the necessary application — is entitled as a matter of right to receive a concealed carry permit authorizing the concealment and carry of handguns in public. This permit is effectively non-discretionary; it cannot be denied on the basis of official whim or the opinion of a licensing authority that the applicant lacks a particularized need for self-protection.

The second instrument is an "unrestricted" permit — a discretionary permit that, when issued, authorizes both open carry and concealed carry of handguns. This permit is not shall-issue; the relevant licensing authority retains discretion over whether to grant it. Under Rhode Island's scheme as it existed at the time of this litigation, a person wishing to open carry a handgun in public was required to obtain this second, discretionary permit rather than relying on the shall-issue concealed carry permit.

The plaintiffs in **O'Neal v. Noona** held shall-issue concealed carry permits but had been denied renewal of their unrestricted permits by the relevant Rhode Island authority. They argued that the Second Amendment's right to "bear" arms — which the Supreme Court confirmed in *Bruen* includes the right to carry firearms in public for self-defense — necessarily encompasses the right to carry openly, not merely concealed, and that Rhode Island's discretionary refusal to grant unrestricted permits violated that constitutional guarantee.

## Judge Smith's Ruling

Judge Smith ruled against the plaintiffs. His reasoning proceeded in two analytical steps.

First, he acknowledged, as *Bruen* requires, that the conduct of carrying a handgun in public — whether open or concealed — falls within the plain text of the Second Amendment's guarantee of the right to "bear" arms. The word "bear" means to carry, and carrying a handgun in public is undeniably covered by the text.

Second, he applied the *Bruen* burden-shifting framework: once the court determines that the challenged conduct falls within the Second Amendment's textual coverage, the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that the regulation at issue is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation. On this second question, Judge Smith concluded that the historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States supports the government's authority to regulate the manner of public carry — including the authority to require concealed carry rather than open carry — when it simultaneously ensures that law-abiding citizens retain meaningful access to the right to carry for self-defense through an alternative permitted pathway.

Because the plaintiffs already possessed shall-issue concealed carry permits — and thus already had a constitutional avenue for carrying handguns in public — the court concluded that their Second Amendment rights had not been violated by the state's refusal to additionally authorize open carry under the discretionary unrestricted permit scheme. The key factual predicate for the ruling was the availability of the shall-issue concealed carry option; the court's holding would almost certainly have been different if Rhode Island had denied the plaintiffs any opportunity to carry a handgun in public.

## Assessment of the Legal Framework

I will be direct: while I may personally prefer a legal regime in which law-abiding citizens possess an unambiguous constitutional right to carry firearms openly as well as concealed, the judge's ruling in **O'Neal v. Noona** is legally defensible under the current state of Second Amendment doctrine and is consistent with how today's federal courts are likely to apply the *Bruen* framework to the open-versus-concealed carry question.

The historical record on open versus concealed carry is genuinely complex. Some founding-era and Reconstruction-era legal authorities distinguished between open carry — which was sometimes viewed as the more traditional and constitutionally protected form of carry — and concealed carry, which was viewed with some suspicion in certain jurisdictions. But other historical authorities, and particularly the pattern of state regulation that emerged in the nineteenth century, focused primarily on concealed carry as the regulated mode, leaving open carry less restricted.

Under *Bruen*, the key question is whether there is a well-established, historically deep tradition of regulation analogous to what the challenged law imposes. If a state requires a permit for open carry while providing a shall-issue permit for concealed carry, the historical tradition of channeling public carry through permit requirements — while ensuring that those permits are available as a matter of right to law-abiding citizens — arguably satisfies the government's burden under *Bruen*, at least as that burden is presently being interpreted by the lower courts.

The practical lesson from this ruling is important: the strongest Second Amendment argument is not always the broadest one. A court asked to hold that the Second Amendment categorically prohibits any regulation of open carry, regardless of the availability of concealed carry alternatives, is being asked to go further than the current *Bruen* framework clearly requires. A more focused argument — that the Second Amendment prohibits the specific combination of open carry restriction and concealed carry restriction that together deprive law-abiding citizens of any meaningful right to carry — is the argument most likely to prevail.

## The Open Carry Question Going Forward

The question of whether the Second Amendment independently guarantees a right to open carry, separate from and in addition to the right to concealed carry, remains unresolved in the Supreme Court. **O'Neal v. Noona** is a district court decision within the First Circuit — it binds neither any other court in the First Circuit nor any courts in other circuits. Other federal courts will reach the question from different factual postures, and ultimately the Supreme Court will likely be called upon to resolve the circuit disagreements that will develop.

My prediction, based on my reading of *Heller*, *McDonald v. City of Chicago* (2010), *Bruen*, and the Court's subsequent jurisprudence, is that the eventual Supreme Court resolution of the open carry question will be something like the following: the Second Amendment guarantees the right of law-abiding citizens to carry arms for self-defense in public; states may channel that right through reasonable permit requirements; but states may not, through a combination of restrictions, effectively deny law-abiding citizens any meaningful pathway to public carry. Whether the Second Amendment additionally guarantees a categorical right to open carry in all circumstances — irrespective of the availability of concealed carry — is a question that the current Court may be unwilling to answer definitively in the affirmative.

## Implications

The decision in **O'Neal v. Noona** has limited direct effect beyond the specific plaintiffs in the case and the particular Rhode Island permit scheme at issue. Its broader significance lies in the guidance it provides about how federal courts in the First Circuit are likely to approach open carry challenges.

For law-abiding Rhode Island residents, the ruling confirms that the shall-issue concealed carry permit scheme provides a constitutionally protected pathway to public carry. For Second Amendment litigants nationally, the decision is a reminder that challenges to open carry restrictions are most likely to succeed when they attack not the isolated open carry restriction but the totality of a state's carry regime — particularly when that regime combines restrictions on both open and concealed carry in ways that effectively deny law-abiding citizens any meaningful right to armed self-defense in public.
