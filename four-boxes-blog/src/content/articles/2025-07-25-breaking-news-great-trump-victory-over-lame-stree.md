## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit has denied the Associated Press's emergency petition for en banc reconsideration of a three-judge panel's June 6, 2025 order that stayed — for all practical purposes — the district court's injunction requiring the Trump administration to restore the Associated Press's access to the White House press pool, the Oval Office, Air Force One, and other restricted spaces. The full DC Circuit's denial of en banc review is a significant procedural and substantive win for President Trump. The stay issued by the three-judge panel remains in place, meaning the Associated Press continues to be excluded from those restricted spaces to which the Trump administration has denied access. Judge Justin Walker filed a notable concurrence that illuminates the constitutional terrain governing this dispute.

## Background

The underlying dispute arises from the Trump administration's decision to deny the Associated Press access to certain restricted White House spaces after the Associated Press refused to adopt the administration's preferred geographic nomenclature. In January 2025, President Trump issued an executive order renaming the Gulf of Mexico the "Gulf of America" — a designation now reflected in official federal government usage and recognized by major mapping services. The Associated Press, adhering to its internal stylebook, declined to use the name "Gulf of America" and continued to refer to the body of water as the "Gulf of Mexico."

The Trump administration took the position that the Associated Press's refusal to use the government's official geographic designation demonstrated that it was not capable of accurately reporting basic facts from White House grounds, and the administration accordingly excluded Associated Press reporters and photographers from the Oval Office, Air Force One, and other restricted spaces that had historically been accessible to members of the White House press pool. Access to less restricted White House premises was not categorically denied.

The Associated Press responded by filing suit in federal district court, alleging that the exclusion violated its First Amendment rights to free speech and freedom of the press, and constituted unlawful retaliation against the wire service for its editorial position on the geographic naming controversy. In the Associated Press's view, excluding its reporters based on the content of their coverage — specifically, their refusal to adopt the administration's preferred terminology — constituted unconstitutional viewpoint discrimination.

## District Court and Initial Appellate Proceedings

Judge Trevor McFadden of the United States District Court for the District of Columbia initially granted the Associated Press's motion for a preliminary injunction, concluding that the wire service had demonstrated a likelihood of success on the merits of its First Amendment claim. The district court found that the government's exclusion of the Associated Press from spaces made available to other press pool members, based on the Associated Press's editorial stance regarding the Gulf naming controversy, constituted viewpoint-based discrimination that raised serious constitutional concerns.

The Trump administration sought an emergency stay of the district court's injunction from the DC Circuit pending appeal. A three-judge panel of the DC Circuit granted that stay in substantial part in its June 6, 2025 order, concluding that the administration had demonstrated a likelihood of success on appeal and that the government faced a greater risk of harm from an order compelling it to admit a news organization it had deliberately excluded than the Associated Press faced from the continuation of the exclusion. The practical effect of the stay was to permit the Trump administration to maintain its exclusion of the Associated Press from the Oval Office, Air Force One, and other restricted spaces.

The Associated Press then filed an emergency petition for rehearing en banc — that is, a petition for review by the full DC Circuit sitting as a whole rather than the three-judge panel — seeking to vacate the panel's stay order.

## The En Banc Denial

The full DC Circuit has now denied the Associated Press's emergency petition for en banc reconsideration. The court's order is terse: "Upon consideration of the emergency petition for a rehearing en banc, construed as a motion for en banc reconsideration and vacatur of the court's June 6th, 2025 order granting in part Donald Trump's motion for a stay pending appeal, and the response thereto, it is ordered that the motion by the Associated Press be denied."

That denial means the three-judge panel's June 2025 stay remains in effect. The Associated Press continues to be excluded from restricted White House spaces. The case returns to the district court for proceedings on the merits of the First Amendment claims.

## Judge Walker's Concurrence

Judge Justin Walker filed a concurrence agreeing that the emergency petition for en banc review should be denied. His concurrence makes several legally significant observations about the constitutional framework applicable to press access disputes involving the White House.

Judge Walker noted that the district court enjoined the government from excluding the Associated Press from the Oval Office, Air Force One, and other limited spaces based on the wire service's viewpoint — specifically, its use of "Gulf of Mexico" rather than "Gulf of America" — when such spaces are made open to other members of the White House press pool. The emergency three-judge panel partially stayed that injunction pending appeal, reflecting the panel's judgment that the administration had raised serious questions about the merits of the Associated Press's First Amendment claims.

Judge Walker's observations highlight a genuine and unresolved constitutional tension in this area of law. The First Amendment's protection of editorial independence runs squarely against the executive's historically recognized authority to control access to its own offices and personnel. The White House press pool is not a public forum in the traditional constitutional sense; it is a restricted space made available at the President's discretion for purposes of facilitating press coverage of the executive. Whether and to what extent the government may condition access to that restricted space on the content or viewpoint of a news organization's coverage is a question that the courts have not definitively resolved.

## The Constitutional Question on the Merits

The core First Amendment question in this litigation is whether the government's decision to exclude a particular news organization from spaces made generally available to the press pool constitutes unconstitutional viewpoint discrimination, or whether the executive retains sufficient authority over access to its own offices to exclude news organizations based on its assessment of their reporting.

The Associated Press's theory is that once the government opens a space to press access, it may not selectively exclude news organizations based on the viewpoint of their coverage. That principle has some support in the case law governing access to other government-controlled forums, where viewpoint-neutral access rules are generally required once a forum has been made available to some speakers.

The administration's theory is that the White House — including the Oval Office and Air Force One — is not a traditional public forum and that the executive has broad discretion to determine which journalists are granted access to the highest levels of the executive branch. On this view, the decision to exclude a news organization based on its editorial practices is a permissible exercise of executive discretion rather than unconstitutional viewpoint discrimination.

Both positions have legal force. The case will ultimately need to be resolved on the merits, likely with further proceedings in the district court, a full merits decision by a DC Circuit panel, and potentially Supreme Court review.

## Implications

The DC Circuit's denial of en banc review is a meaningful victory for the Trump administration in this litigation, even if it is not a final resolution on the merits. The stay remains in effect. The Associated Press remains excluded from the restricted spaces at issue. And the full DC Circuit has declined to disturb the three-judge panel's assessment that the administration's position is sufficiently meritorious to justify maintaining the status quo pending appeal.

Whether the Trump administration ultimately prevails on the merits remains to be seen. The First Amendment questions presented are novel and consequential. But the trajectory of this litigation, as of this writing, favors the administration. The Associated Press will need to prevail on the merits before a district court and then survive appellate review before it can obtain the access it seeks. Given the pace of federal litigation, that process may not be resolved for many months or years.

This case will be watched closely by First Amendment scholars, press freedom advocates, and constitutional law practitioners across the country. Its ultimate resolution will have significant implications for the scope of executive authority to manage press access to the White House and for the boundaries of the First Amendment's protection against viewpoint discrimination in non-public government forums.
