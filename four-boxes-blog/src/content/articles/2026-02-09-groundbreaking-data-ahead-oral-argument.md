## Summary

On the eve of the en banc oral argument in **Coons v. Attorney General of New Jersey** and **Siegel v. Attorney General of New Jersey** before the full Third Circuit, a major historical study — nearly one hundred pages in length — was published on the Social Science Research Network, providing comprehensive historical documentation that directly supports the constitutional framework I have argued for in my own scholarship: that the government may designate a location as a "sensitive place" where firearms are prohibited only when it provides comprehensive security, including armed guards and metal detectors, as a substitute for the individual's right of self-defense. This study, authored by Dr. Angus McLean and published on SSRN under the title "The Second Amendment, Sensitive Places, and Comprehensive Government Security," arrives at a critical moment and provides the historical analysis that New Jersey manifestly cannot rebut.

## Background

The en banc argument in **Coons** and **Siegel** — in which the entire active bench of the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit considered whether New Jersey's sweeping network of government-mandated gun-free zones is consistent with the Second Amendment — was one of the most consequential oral arguments in Second Amendment law since **New York State Rifle & Pistol Ass'n v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022).

In September 2025, a three-judge panel of the Third Circuit, in a 2-1 decision over a powerful dissent by Judge David Porter, upheld most of New Jersey's sensitive-places scheme. The Third Circuit then agreed to rehear the case en banc — with the addition of two new Trump-appointed judges, Judge Jennifer Mascott and Judge Emil Bove — reflecting the court's recognition that the panel decision raised significant questions about the proper application of Bruen's historical methodology.

The en banc argument was scheduled for a Wednesday in mid-February 2026. On the eve of that argument, Dr. McLean published his comprehensive historical study. Its timing was significant: it provided the full Third Circuit with a rigorous, documented historical record precisely as the judges were preparing to evaluate New Jersey's claims about the founding-era tradition of sensitive-places regulation.

## The McLean Study: Core Findings

The central thesis of Dr. McLean's study, which I have reviewed and which powerfully corroborates arguments I have advanced in my own scholarship, is as follows: when the founding-era government designated a location as a "sensitive place" in which the carrying of firearms was restricted, it did not simply post a sign and leave citizens disarmed and defenseless. Rather, the government provided comprehensive security — armed men, guards, or marshals — as a substitute for the individual's right to self-defense.

The historical pattern documented in the study is coherent and consistent. Government buildings and official proceedings where firearms were restricted were also places where the government itself provided armed protection to those present. The restriction on individual carry was thus not an uncompensated taking of the individual's right of self-defense; it was a substitution, in which the government assumed the defensive function by providing equivalent protection through its own armed agents.

This historical finding is enormously significant for the current litigation landscape. New Jersey's sensitive-places designations — covering beaches, public libraries, museums, zoos, casinos, sports stadiums, public transit, and dozens of other locations — are imposed without any accompanying provision of comprehensive security. The state designates these locations as gun-free zones and leaves citizens wholly disarmed and wholly unprotected. There are no metal detectors at New Jersey's beaches. There are no armed guards outside every library. There is no security screening at museums or zoos. The state simply commands that citizens disarm themselves and then provides nothing in return.

Under the historical framework the McLean study documents, this is constitutionally impermissible. The government can restrict individual carry in a location — but only when it assumes the protective function itself by providing comprehensive security. When it does neither — when it disarms citizens and provides no security substitute — it has infringed the Second Amendment right without historical justification.

## The Constitutional Framework: Comprehensive Security as the Condition for Sensitive Place Designation

I have advanced this framework in my own scholarship and analysis for some time, and it is gratifying to see a rigorous historical study confirm it through extensive primary source research. The principle is intuitive as well as historically documented: the Second Amendment protects the individual's right to armed self-defense. If the government eliminates that right in a particular location, it must provide an equivalent — armed protection of the citizens it has disarmed. Anything less is not a regulation of the right; it is an abrogation of the right.

The contemporary analogs are illustrative. Federal courthouses are one of the paradigmatic examples of constitutionally valid sensitive-place designations. No one may bring a firearm into a federal courthouse, and this prohibition has never been seriously questioned. But federal courthouses are also locations with comprehensive security: metal detectors at every entry point, armed federal marshals throughout the building, and systematic screening to ensure that no unauthorized person can bring a weapon inside. The government has provided the security substitute that the sensitive-places designation requires.

The same is true of airport sterile areas, the floors of Congress, the White House, and other major federal buildings. Citizens are required to submit to security screening; weapons are prohibited inside; but the government provides comprehensive armed protection within those zones. The trade is constitutionally defensible because the individual's right of self-defense is not simply eliminated — it is replaced by a governmental undertaking to provide security.

New Jersey's sensitive-places designations provide none of this. A citizen disarmed by New Jersey's law at a public beach has no metal detector screening the beach, no armed guard ensuring that bad actors cannot bring weapons to the beach, and no meaningful security substitute for the right of self-defense that the state has suspended. This is not the historical sensitive-places model the founding era recognized. It is something categorically different — a comprehensive disarmament regime in public spaces, with no security trade and no historical precedent.

## Significance for the En Banc Argument

The McLean study arrived at an optimal moment for the Third Circuit's consideration. The en banc court was preparing to evaluate whether the three-judge panel had correctly applied Bruen's historical methodology to New Jersey's sensitive-places scheme. The study provides a systematic, documented account of what the historical tradition of sensitive-places regulation actually was — and demonstrates that it bears no resemblance to what New Jersey has enacted.

For the Second Amendment plaintiffs — represented by organizations including those that litigated Bruen itself — the study provides powerful support for the argument that New Jersey's scheme fails the historical test Bruen imposes. For New Jersey, the study presents a historical record it cannot rebut: there are no founding-era examples of a government designating 24 broad categories of public locations as gun-free zones without providing comprehensive security in those locations.

The study also bears directly on the temporal question I have addressed in my own writing: whether founding-era or Reconstruction-era history should govern the analysis. The McLean study focuses on founding-era history, confirming that the correct time period for the Bruen analysis is 1791, not the post-Civil War period that New Jersey would prefer courts to examine. The historical pattern of comprehensive-security-as-a-condition-for-sensitive-place-designation is rooted in the founding era; it is not a Reconstruction-era development.

## Implications

The publication of the McLean study on the eve of the Third Circuit en banc argument in **Coons** and **Siegel** represents exactly the kind of scholarly contribution that Second Amendment jurisprudence needs. The Bruen framework is a historical inquiry, and rigorous historical research that documents founding-era practices is directly probative in cases where the government claims a historical tradition justifying its regulations.

The study's core finding — that the sensitive-places doctrine has always been conditioned on comprehensive government security as a substitute for individual self-defense — provides a principled, historically grounded limit on the government's power to designate gun-free zones. It preserves a reasonable space for genuinely secured government locations to restrict carry while preventing the unlimited expansion of gun-free zones that states like New Jersey have attempted.

In the broader context of Second Amendment litigation, the McLean study also illustrates the growing sophistication of the historical scholarship on the founding-era right to keep and bear arms. As courts have been compelled by Bruen to engage seriously with historical evidence, the quality and depth of that evidence has improved substantially. The historical record, examined carefully, consistently supports the conclusion that the Second Amendment's protections are robust and that the founding generation recognized no general authority in government to disarm citizens in broad swaths of public space without providing comprehensive security in exchange.
