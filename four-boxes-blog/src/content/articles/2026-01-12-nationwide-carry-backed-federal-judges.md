## Summary

Three prominent federal judges — Judge Elizabeth Branch of the United States Court of Appeals for the Eleventh Circuit, Judge Robert Wilkins of the United States Court of Appeals for the DC Circuit, and District Court Judge Trevor McFadden of the District of Columbia — have published a Wall Street Journal op-ed endorsing Senator Tom Cotton's proposed "Protect Our Prosecutors and Judges Act," which would allow federal judges and prosecutors who complete law enforcement firearms training to carry concealed firearms nationwide, regardless of the varying concealed-carry laws of the states through which they travel. The judges' joint statement is remarkable both for its candor about the dangers facing the federal judiciary and for the unintended — though analytically inescapable — argument it makes in favor of universal nationwide concealed carry reciprocity for all law-abiding Americans.

## Background

### Rising Threats Against the Federal Judiciary

Chief Justice John Roberts, in his annual year-end report, warned of an alarming rise in threats and attacks directed at federal judges. As the three judges note in their op-ed, hostile threats against the judiciary have more than tripled over the past decade. Recent years have seen a foiled assassination attempt against a sitting Supreme Court Justice — Justice Brett Kavanaugh — as well as the killing of the son of a federal district court judge, whose family was targeted in an attack apparently directed at the judge herself. The three judges state further that threats against the judiciary escalated significantly in 2025.

It is within this context of genuine personal danger that the three judges stepped forward to endorse legislation that would allow them to protect themselves — a step that active federal jurists very rarely take.

### The Proposed Legislation: The Protect Our Prosecutors and Judges Act

Senator Tom Cotton of Arkansas has introduced the Protect Our Prosecutors and Judges Act, which would permit federal judges and federal prosecutors who have completed law enforcement firearms training in their home jurisdictions to carry concealed firearms across state lines. The proposal is conceptually analogous to the Law Enforcement Officers Safety Act (LEOSA), 18 U.S.C. §§ 926B–926C, which allows active and retired law enforcement officers to carry concealed firearms nationwide, preempting the patchwork of varying state-law restrictions.

The Cotton bill would effectively create a LEOSA-style carve-out for the federal judiciary and federal prosecutors — professionals who, by reason of their official duties, regularly face elevated personal risk and who regularly travel across state lines in the performance of those duties.

## The Judges' Case for Nationwide Carry

### The Argument from Necessity

The three judges open their argument by identifying the structural vulnerability that traveling federal jurists face. Appellate judges ride circuit each month, hearing arguments at courthouses throughout their multi-state regions, far from the protection available in their home districts. District judges travel to attend judicial conferences, serve as visiting judges in distant courts that are short-staffed, and participate in bar association and law school events. In each of these contexts, judges find themselves in unfamiliar territory, away from the protective infrastructure — U.S. Marshals, courthouse security, local law enforcement relationships — that they rely upon at home.

The judges write: "Away from home, federal judges are incredibly vulnerable. Their attendance at hearings and other events is often publicized ahead of time. They frequently find themselves in unfamiliar territory where they lack the ready access to local law enforcement and federal marshals they enjoy in their home districts."

### The Patchwork Problem

The judges then make what is, analytically, the most significant observation in their piece: even when federal judges hold valid concealed-carry permits in their home jurisdictions, those permits may be entirely unrecognized in the states to which they travel. Concealed-carry reciprocity varies dramatically across the country. Some states recognize permits from nearly all other states; others recognize permits only from states that meet specific training standards; others, such as New York, New Jersey, California, Illinois, Hawaii, and Maryland, effectively refuse to recognize out-of-state permits at all and impose among the most restrictive concealed-carry licensing regimes in the nation.

The result, as the judges describe it, is that federal jurists "often reluctantly leave their weapons at home when traveling" — meaning they are disarmed precisely in the environments where their exposure to risk is greatest.

## The Broader Argument for Universal Reciprocity

### The Judges' Argument Applies to All Law-Abiding Americans

Here is where the analysis becomes constitutionally significant in a way that extends far beyond the particular vulnerability of federal judges. The argument the three judges make — that law-abiding, trained citizens cannot practically exercise their right to carry because the patchwork of state laws makes it impossible to know whether they are in compliance as they cross state lines — is an argument that applies with equal force to every law-abiding American who holds a concealed-carry permit.

The argument is not unique to federal judges. Any American who holds a concealed-carry permit in her home state faces precisely the same legal uncertainty when she travels for work, visits family in another state, or simply drives through jurisdictions with incompatible carry laws. She, too, is often forced to leave her firearm at home — or risk felony prosecution — simply because the state through which she passes does not recognize her home-state permit.

I submit that by identifying the practical difficulty of navigating fifty different concealed-carry regimes as a justification for federal preemption in their specific case, these three judges have inadvertently articulated one of the strongest practical arguments for universal nationwide concealed-carry reciprocity for all law-abiding Americans.

### The Second Amendment Right to Carry Beyond One's Home State

**New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), held that the Second Amendment protects the right to carry a firearm in public for self-defense — not merely within one's home. The Court struck down New York's "proper cause" requirement, which effectively limited public carry to an elite few who could demonstrate a particularized need.

The logic of **Bruen** extends naturally to the interstate carry question. If the Second Amendment protects the right to carry for self-defense in public within one's home state, it is difficult to see why that right evaporates the moment a law-abiding citizen crosses a state line. The right is a federal constitutional right; it does not shrink and expand based on the whims of state legislatures. The variation the judges describe — the inconsistency that makes it practically impossible for even well-trained, permit-holding citizens to know whether they are lawfully armed as they travel — is itself an argument for the unconstitutionality of the most restrictive state carry regimes, at least as applied to citizens licensed to carry in their home states.

### The Overton Window Shifts

The significance of this Wall Street Journal op-ed lies not only in its specific argument but in what it signals about the evolving consensus among informed legal professionals. These are not gun-rights advocates writing in a firearms publication. Judge Wilkins was appointed by President Obama. Judge Branch sits on the Eleventh Circuit. Judge McFadden, a Trump appointee, is widely regarded as an exceptionally careful and principled jurist. That this ideologically diverse group of sitting federal judges can agree that the current patchwork of concealed-carry laws is practically unworkable and that legislation enabling broader carry rights is appropriate — this represents a significant shift in the Overton window.

### LEOSA as the Legislative Model

The reference to LEOSA in the judges' op-ed is not incidental. LEOSA was itself a significant milestone: Congress made a categorical judgment that law enforcement officers' need to protect themselves and others, and the practical impossibility of navigating state-by-state carry laws, justified federal preemption. The same logic, applied consistently, supports broader reciprocity. If the federal interest in protecting law enforcement officers justifies nationwide carry rights for that class, the federal interest in protecting the constitutional rights of law-abiding, trained civilians — and in ensuring the practical exercisability of a fundamental constitutional right — justifies the same treatment.

## Implications

### Legislative Momentum

Senator Cotton's bill now carries the public endorsement of three sitting federal judges. That endorsement — particularly from a bipartisan group of jurists of this caliber — will provide legislative momentum and political cover for broader support. The bill addresses a genuine and growing safety problem for the federal judiciary; its passage would be a meaningful, if incremental, step toward universal reciprocity.

### The Argument for Universal Legislation

In my assessment, the judges' op-ed should be read not as a call for a narrow, profession-specific carve-out, but as a principled recognition that the Second Amendment right to carry cannot practically be exercised in a fifty-state patchwork of inconsistent, often hostile carry regimes. The argument they make for themselves is the argument that supports universal nationwide concealed-carry reciprocity for all law-abiding Americans who hold valid permits.

The trend is moving in the right direction. The Overton window continues to open. The question is not whether universal reciprocity is constitutionally and practically justified — it clearly is — but whether Congress will act to provide it, or whether the courts will ultimately be required to compel the result under the Second Amendment.
