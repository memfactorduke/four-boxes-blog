## Summary

In my analysis, the United States Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit has issued a landmark 2-to-1 ruling in **Ortega v. Governor Michelle Lujan Grisham**, holding that New Mexico's seven-day firearms waiting period is likely unconstitutional under the Second Amendment and granting a preliminary injunction against its enforcement. The court found that a waiting period burdens the textual right of the people to keep and bear arms by preventing the lawful acquisition of firearms, and that New Mexico failed to demonstrate any historical tradition of analogous regulation reaching back to the Founding era. As I will explain, this decision has significant implications for waiting period laws across the country.

## Background

New Mexico enacted a seven-day waiting period law applicable to virtually all firearms transactions conducted through a federally licensed firearms dealer. The waiting period applies even when a background check clears immediately, meaning a law-abiding citizen who passes an NICS check in minutes is nonetheless required to wait a full seven calendar days before taking possession of the purchased firearm. There is no individualized determination -- the delay is categorical and applies to everyone in the state with only narrow exceptions.

The stated legislative rationale for the waiting period is to prevent so-called impulsive acts of violence or self-harm by imposing a mandatory "cooling off" period between the decision to acquire a firearm and its actual acquisition. Plaintiffs in **Ortega v. Governor Michelle Lujan Grisham** challenged the law as a violation of the Second Amendment under the framework established by the Supreme Court in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen* (2022).

## The Tenth Circuit's Majority Opinion

The majority opinion, joined by two judges of the Tenth Circuit panel, proceeded through the two-step analytical framework mandated by *Bruen*. First, the court assessed whether New Mexico's waiting period law implicates the plain text of the Second Amendment. Second, it evaluated whether the government could demonstrate a historical tradition of analogous regulation from the Founding era.

### Step One: The Waiting Period Implicates the Text

In my analysis, the majority correctly held that the right to keep and bear arms necessarily implies the antecedent right to acquire them. The court's reasoning is compelling: one cannot keep arms one has never acquired, and one cannot bear arms one does not possess. Acquisition is a necessary predicate to both keeping and bearing. As paper is a necessary predicate to the exercise of freedom of the press, and ownership of property is a necessary predicate to the right to just compensation upon a taking, the right to acquire a firearm is necessarily encompassed within the Second Amendment's guarantee.

Because New Mexico's waiting period operates as a direct restriction on the ability of law-abiding citizens to acquire firearms -- imposing a seven-day delay on every transaction regardless of circumstances -- the majority found that the law implicates and burdens conduct protected by the Second Amendment's text. Under *Bruen*, this textual burden triggers the historical tradition inquiry and shifts the evidentiary burden to the government.

### Step Two: No Historical Tradition of Waiting Periods

Having determined that the waiting period implicates the Second Amendment's text, the majority then examined whether New Mexico could demonstrate a historical tradition of analogous regulation consistent with the nation's history and tradition of firearms regulation. The court concluded that it could not.

The government offered no historical evidence of any waiting period law in existence at the time of the Founding, during the antebellum period, or in the late nineteenth century. As the majority observed, cooling-off periods are entirely a modern invention with no roots in the historical tradition that the Supreme Court in *Bruen* held must anchor any permissible firearms regulation. The absence of historical precedent is fatal to the law's constitutionality under the controlling framework.

The majority further observed that the waiting period has no mechanism for individualized assessment. Every prospective firearms purchaser in New Mexico -- regardless of whether they have an urgent and legitimate need for self-defense, regardless of whether their background check clears instantaneously, and regardless of any other individual circumstances -- is subject to the same categorical seven-day delay. A restriction this sweeping, the court concluded, has no historical analog that could justify it.

## The Dissent

One judge dissented, arguing that the waiting period constitutes a minimal, time-limited burden on Second Amendment rights that the government has a sufficient public safety interest in imposing. In my view, the dissent fundamentally misapplies *Bruen*, which requires the government to justify firearms regulations through historical tradition rather than through a balancing of interests or means-end scrutiny. The *Bruen* framework expressly rejected interest-balancing as the governing constitutional standard. The dissent's approach would effectively reinstate means-end scrutiny through the back door, which the Supreme Court unanimously foreclosed.

## The Right to Acquire: A Necessary Predicate

In my analysis, one of the most significant aspects of the majority's opinion is its rigorous treatment of the right to acquire firearms as an implied but constitutionally protected component of the right to keep and bear arms. The Tenth Circuit's majority opinion draws upon a well-established principle of constitutional interpretation: when the text of a constitutional provision expressly protects a right, the necessary conditions for exercising that right receive co-extensive protection.

The court quoted its own analysis: "One cannot keep or bear arms if one cannot acquire them." The right to purchase, procure, and take possession of a firearm is therefore not a separate, unprotected privilege that the government may regulate without constitutional constraint. It is part of the fabric of the Second Amendment right itself.

This holding has significant implications for other firearms regulations that operate at the point of acquisition, including excessive fees, bureaucratic delays, and onerous procedural requirements that function as practical barriers to lawful firearms ownership.

## Implications for Other Waiting Period Laws

In my assessment, the Tenth Circuit's decision in **Ortega** represents the most significant appellate ruling on waiting period laws since the Supreme Court's modern Second Amendment jurisprudence began with *Heller*. If the majority's analysis is ultimately affirmed on the merits, waiting period laws in other jurisdictions will face serious constitutional challenges under the same framework.

Several states impose waiting periods ranging from a few days to as long as fourteen days. Under the *Bruen* framework as applied by the Tenth Circuit majority, all of those laws are potentially vulnerable, as the historical record does not appear to support any tradition of cooling-off periods at the Founding. The government will bear the burden of identifying a sufficiently analogous historical regulation in any future challenge, and the Tenth Circuit's analysis suggests that burden is a difficult one to carry.

The Tenth Circuit's ruling is, at the preliminary injunction stage, a finding that the plaintiffs are likely to succeed on the merits. The case will now proceed in the district court, where the government will have the opportunity to present fuller historical evidence. The ultimate merits ruling, when it comes, may be among the most consequential Second Amendment decisions in the post-*Bruen* era.
