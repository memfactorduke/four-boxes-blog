## Summary

On September 3, 2025, the United States Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit, in **United States v. Morgan**, upheld the federal prohibition on machine guns as applied to defendant Tamarie Morgan, concluding that machine guns are not protected arms under the Second Amendment. While the outcome of this decision — sustaining the machine gun ban — is the result that responsible Second Amendment advocates should welcome, the Tenth Circuit's reasoning is deeply flawed in ways that merit careful examination. The court elevated the "in common use" test to the wrong analytical stage in the **Heller-Bruen** framework, misapplying the structure of Second Amendment analysis in a manner that could generate troublesome precedent if left unexamined. Equally important, the underlying facts of **Morgan** illustrate precisely why careful attention to litigation strategy — specifically, the selection of appropriate vehicles for advancing Second Amendment claims — is essential to the long-term health of Second Amendment jurisprudence.

## Background

In October 2022, law enforcement officers stopped and searched a vehicle in which Tamarie Morgan was a passenger. Officers found and seized an Anderson Manufacturing model AM15 that had been configured to fire automatically, a .357 caliber Glock handgun, and a Glock switch — a device that, when attached to a Glock pistol, converts it from a semi-automatic to a fully automatic firearm. A Snapchat video showed Morgan firing a Glock handgun with a Glock switch attached, with the weapon appearing to discharge more than one round per trigger pull, demonstrating fully automatic function.

Morgan was criminally indicted under the National Firearms Act's prohibition on the possession of machine guns without the requisite registration and tax payment. He raised a Second Amendment challenge, arguing that the federal machine gun ban, as applied to him, violated his constitutional right to keep and bear arms.

## Why This Fact Pattern Was a Poor Vehicle for Second Amendment Litigation

I must address directly the threshold strategic point before analyzing the Tenth Circuit's legal reasoning: **United States v. Morgan** presented one of the least favorable fact patterns imaginable for a Second Amendment challenge to the machine gun ban, and it is precisely the kind of case that Second Amendment advocates should hope does not reach the Supreme Court.

The plaintiffs and defendants we want before the Supreme Court on Second Amendment questions are law-abiding Americans who wish to exercise their constitutional rights and are being prevented from doing so by overreaching gun control legislation. The paradigmatic Second Amendment plaintiff is Dick Heller — a Capitol Police officer who carried a firearm in the performance of his duties throughout the day and was denied the right to possess a handgun in his own home for self-defense. The paradigmatic Second Amendment plaintiff is Otis McDonald — an elderly African American man living in a dangerous neighborhood in Chicago who wished only to keep a firearm in his home for protection against the criminal element that threatened his safety.

Tamarie Morgan is not that plaintiff. Morgan was found in possession of an illegally converted machine gun and a Glock switch based on social media evidence showing him firing the weapon fully automatically. These are not the facts of a law-abiding citizen being denied a fundamental right; these are the facts of a criminal prosecution. Bringing a Second Amendment challenge in this context creates the risk of producing precedent in a posture maximally unfavorable to the Second Amendment community, with a fact pattern that invites the Supreme Court to adopt broad language cutting against firearms rights.

As I have stated unequivocally on prior occasions, if a machine gun case with these facts reaches the Supreme Court, we will lose, and the resulting precedent will create collateral damage to Second Amendment claims involving semi-automatic rifles, magazines, and other firearms that millions of law-abiding Americans own and use.

## The Tenth Circuit's Flawed Analytical Framework

The Tenth Circuit upheld the machine gun ban, but its reasoning reveals a persistent misapplication of the two-step **Heller-Bruen** framework that I identified and analyzed in my scholarship in the Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy.

Under the correct analytical framework established by **Heller** and reaffirmed in **Bruen**, the inquiry proceeds as follows. First, the court asks whether the conduct at issue — possessing, carrying, or using a particular type of arm — falls within the plain text of the Second Amendment. Second, if the answer is yes (that is, if the arm qualifies as an "arm" within the meaning of the text), the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate a longstanding historical tradition of analogous regulation. The inquiry regarding whether an arm is "dangerous and unusual" — and therefore unprotected — is a historical inquiry that occurs at the second step, where the government bears the burden of proof.

The Tenth Circuit's error was to treat the "in common use" inquiry as if it belonged at the plain-text stage rather than the historical stage. The court in **Morgan** effectively asked whether machine guns are in common use as a threshold question to determine whether they qualify as "arms" at all under the text of the Second Amendment. This inverts the **Heller** framework. The "in common use" test — and its inverse, the "dangerous and unusual" exception — is a historical test that the government must satisfy to overcome a constitutional protection that attaches at the textual level. By relocating this inquiry to the threshold stage and placing it in the analytical framework incorrectly, the Tenth Circuit created a methodological error that, if replicated in cases involving semi-automatic rifles or other commonly owned firearms, could be weaponized against Second Amendment rights in future litigation.

## The Correct Outcome for the Wrong Reasons

Despite the flawed reasoning, the outcome — sustaining the machine gun ban — is, for now, the result that Second Amendment advocates should not contest. The federal machine gun ban, as it applies to automatic weapons manufactured after May 19, 1986, enjoys a practical historical basis: machine guns in their modern, post-1986 iteration have never been in common civilian use for lawful purposes in the same manner as semi-automatic firearms. The ban has been in effect for nearly four decades, during which time the common use of firearms in America has been defined overwhelmingly by semi-automatic platforms.

The strategic concern is not the outcome in **Morgan** — it is the precedential methodology that the Tenth Circuit deployed to reach that outcome. A well-reasoned machine gun ban decision would uphold the prohibition through a careful application of the **Heller** "dangerous and unusual" test at the historical stage, with the burden properly placed on the government and with express language distinguishing machine guns from semi-automatic rifles. The Tenth Circuit's reasoning does not clearly achieve that differentiation, and this creates potential for misuse of the **Morgan** decision in the semi-automatic rifle context.

## Implications for Certiorari Strategy

I assess there is essentially no prospect that the Supreme Court will grant certiorari in **Morgan** to review the machine gun ban question in this posture. The Trump Department of Justice, which has demonstrated a sophisticated understanding of Second Amendment litigation strategy, is unlikely to seek cert in a machine gun case with facts like those presented in **Morgan**. And the defendant, having lost on a facial constitutional challenge in a criminal proceeding, presents the least favorable vehicle for Supreme Court review of any firearms question.

This outcome — no Supreme Court review — is exactly what the Second Amendment movement should want in the machine gun context. The energy and attention of the Supreme Court's Second Amendment docket should be directed toward the cases that will advance the rights of law-abiding Americans: cases involving semi-automatic rifle bans, magazine restrictions, carry permit denials, and similar restrictions that directly burden millions of lawful gun owners. Machine gun cases, in this political and legal environment, are a distraction at best and a liability at worst.

The Tenth Circuit's decision in **Morgan**, whatever its analytical deficiencies, will stay in place. The important work continues in cases like **Barnett v. Raoul** and other challenges to the restrictions that actually burden America's law-abiding gun owners.
