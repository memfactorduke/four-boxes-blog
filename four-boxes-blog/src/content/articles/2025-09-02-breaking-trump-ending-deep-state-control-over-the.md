## Summary

The litigation arising from President Donald J. Trump's termination of Federal Reserve Board Governor Lisa Cook has generated one of the most constitutionally significant legal battles of the current era: a direct confrontation between the President of the United States, exercising his Article II removal authority, and the Federal Reserve System, an institution that has functioned in practice as a largely unaccountable fourth branch of government. The legal proceedings, unfolding before the courts of the District of Columbia, present questions of presidential removal power that the Supreme Court has addressed in recent years through **Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau** (2020) and **Collins v. Yellen** (2021) — decisions that have steadily expanded the constitutional recognition of presidential removal authority and steadily contracted the domain of Congress's ability to insulate executive officials from presidential supervision.

## Background

The Federal Reserve System was created in 1913 by the Federal Reserve Act, signed into law by President Woodrow Wilson. The Act establishes a Board of Governors consisting of seven members appointed by the President and confirmed by the Senate, each serving staggered fourteen-year terms. The statute provides that governors may be removed by the President "for cause" — a phrase that has been the subject of significant constitutional litigation and scholarly debate.

Lisa Cook was nominated by President Biden and confirmed by the Democratic-controlled Senate. She is one of the seven voting members of the Federal Reserve Board of Governors and participates as a voting member of the Federal Open Market Committee, the body responsible for setting short-term interest rates and conducting open market operations. President Trump terminated her appointment, citing alleged mortgage fraud and the impossibility of permitting an official under criminal investigation to continue exercising the enormous monetary policy authority vested in Federal Reserve governors.

Governor Cook responded by filing suit, seeking a declaration that her termination was unlawful and a court order reinstating her to her position. The DC district court is now adjudicating the legal questions raised by her challenge.

## The Constitutional Architecture of Presidential Removal Power

The Constitution's Article II vesting clause — "The executive Power shall be vested in a President of the United States of America" — has been consistently interpreted by the Supreme Court as conferring on the President plenary authority to supervise and direct the officers who carry out executive functions. This interpretive principle finds its most forceful modern expression in the Supreme Court's 2020 decision in **Seila Law LLC v. Consumer Financial Protection Bureau**, in which the Court struck down a statutory provision limiting the President's ability to remove the CFPB Director to "for cause" removal only.

The **Seila Law** Court recognized two narrow exceptions to the general rule of at-will presidential removal: multi-member bodies exercising quasi-legislative or quasi-judicial functions (derived from the 1935 decision in **Humphrey's Executor v. United States**), and inferior officers who occupy principal officer-like positions within larger executive agencies with for-cause protections. Federal Reserve Board governors have been argued to fall within the **Humphrey's Executor** exception on the theory that the Board exercises quasi-legislative functions through monetary policy. But that argument has become increasingly difficult to sustain in light of **Seila Law**'s limitation of **Humphrey's Executor** to its specific facts and its clear signal that the independent agency model is constitutionally suspect to the extent it impairs the President's ability to supervise the execution of federal law.

## The "For Cause" Standard as Applied

Even accepting, arguendo, that the Federal Reserve governors enjoy "for cause" removal protection under the governing statute and applicable precedent, the question remains whether the evidence supporting President Trump's termination of Governor Cook satisfies that standard.

The "for cause" standard in the context of executive officer removal has never been authoritatively defined by the Supreme Court in a comprehensive manner. Lower courts and administrative law scholars have generally recognized that "cause" encompasses, at minimum: malfeasance in office, misconduct, neglect of duty, and incapacity to perform the duties of the office. The argument that alleged mortgage fraud — the submission of materially false information to federally backed lending institutions — constitutes cause for removal of an official who exercises direct authority over the nation's mortgage and interest rate environment is, in my assessment, overwhelmingly compelling. The very nature of the alleged misconduct implicates the integrity and credibility of the office and creates an untenable conflict of interest.

Moreover, as I have noted on prior occasions, two criminal referrals from the Director of the Federal Housing Finance Agency — the federal entity that oversees Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac — and the subsequent issuance of grand jury subpoenas by the Department of Justice provide an additional factual basis for a "for cause" determination that is, if anything, even stronger than the mortgage fraud allegation alone. A sitting Federal Reserve governor who is the subject of an active federal criminal investigation cannot credibly exercise monetary policy authority without raising profound questions about the integrity of the institution she serves.

## The Broader Constitutional Stakes

The litigation in **Cook v. Federal Reserve** is, at its deepest level, a contest over a question that has practical consequences for every American: should the Federal Reserve System — an institution that controls the money supply, sets interest rates, and directly shapes the economic fortunes of three hundred thirty million people — be subject to meaningful democratic accountability through the elected President, or should it operate as an effectively autonomous fourth branch of government?

The Constitution does not contemplate a fourth branch. Article I creates the legislative branch. Article II creates the executive branch. Article III creates the judicial branch. That is the complete architecture of the federal government as the Framers designed it. The Federal Reserve System, as it has operated in practice for more than a century, represents a significant departure from that constitutional design — one that has never been definitively upheld as constitutional by the Supreme Court in the current legal landscape.

The decisions in **Seila Law** and **Collins v. Yellen** represent significant steps toward bringing independent agencies into constitutional conformity with Article II's vesting clause. The Cook litigation, if it reaches the Supreme Court, may represent the next step in that doctrinal development — one that would bring monetary policy within the scope of democratic accountability and presidential supervision in a manner consistent with the Founders' design.

## Implications

The outcome of the Cook litigation will carry consequences across at least three distinct dimensions.

First, a judicial ruling on the scope of presidential removal authority over Federal Reserve governors will either reinforce or fundamentally reshape the conventional understanding of Federal Reserve independence. A ruling that the President may remove governors at will — or for any reasonable policy-related cause — would transform the Federal Reserve from a nominally independent institution into one directly accountable to elected presidential authority.

Second, the resolution of the "for cause" question in the context of Governor Cook's alleged mortgage fraud will establish precedent governing the factual standard required to justify removal of a Federal Reserve governor, with implications for future administrations and future disputes about the composition of the Board.

Third, the practical implications for monetary policy are immediate and significant. If President Trump ultimately prevails in this litigation and succeeds in replacing a majority of the Board's voting governors with officials aligned with his economic philosophy, the Federal Reserve will, for the first time in decades, be operating monetary policy under the effective influence of a directly elected and politically accountable administration. Whether one regards this as a restoration of constitutional order or a threat to institutional independence, it would represent a fundamental change in the operation of American monetary policy.
