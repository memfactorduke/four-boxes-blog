## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Tenth Circuit has declined to rehear en banc the case of **Ortega v. Grisham**, leaving intact a three-judge panel decision that struck down New Mexico's seven-day firearm waiting period law as unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The full court's refusal to reconsider the panel ruling means that New Mexico's Governor Michelle Lujan Grisham has suffered a decisive and now-confirmed legal defeat. The waiting period law — which mandated that even purchasers who passed a near-instantaneous federal background check could not take possession of a lawfully purchased firearm for a minimum of seven days — has been held to violate the right to keep and bear arms under the framework established in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022).

This decision represents a significant and durable Second Amendment victory in the Tenth Circuit, which covers Colorado, New Mexico, Utah, and other western states. The ruling's continued vitality, now confirmed by the full court's refusal to disturb it, will have broad implications for the constitutionality of waiting period statutes across the country.

## Background

New Mexico enacted a waiting period law requiring any person seeking to purchase a firearm to wait at least seven days before taking possession of the weapon — even if that purchaser cleared an instantaneous NICS (National Instant Criminal Background Check System) background check within minutes of initiating the transaction. Under this statutory scheme, a law-abiding New Mexico resident could walk into a gun store, be cleared by the federal background check system in thirty minutes, and still be legally prohibited from taking possession of their lawfully purchased firearm for an entire week.

The law provided no exception for individuals who already owned firearms, for individuals facing imminent threats to their safety, or for individuals who had been previously approved through other licensing mechanisms. The waiting period operated as a bare delay imposed on the exercise of the constitutional right to acquire a firearm — a delay untethered to any individualized showing of dangerousness and serving no function beyond deferring the moment of possession.

## The Panel Decision and Its Legal Foundation

The three-judge panel of the Tenth Circuit found that New Mexico's waiting period law violated the Second Amendment. The analysis proceeded under the two-step *Bruen* methodology: first, whether the regulated conduct falls within the plain text of the Second Amendment; and second, whether the regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States.

On the textual question, the panel correctly recognized that the right to "keep and bear Arms" necessarily encompasses the right to *acquire* arms. The right to possess a firearm is hollow if the state may indefinitely — or even temporarily — bar the act of acquisition. This principle is well grounded in broader constitutional jurisprudence.

The Supreme Court addressed an analogous circumstance in *Minneapolis Star & Tribune Co. v. Minnesota Commissioner of Revenue* (1983), where the Court held that a targeted tax on ink — a prerequisite to the exercise of the First Amendment right to publish a newspaper — constituted an unconstitutional burden on that right. The constitutional principle is straightforward: when a specific prerequisite is necessary to exercise a constitutional right, restrictions on that prerequisite implicate and may violate that right. To possess a firearm, one must first acquire it. The acquisition right is therefore subsumed within the textual protection of the Second Amendment.

## No Historical Tradition Supports Waiting Period Laws

Having established that the right to acquire firearms is textually protected, the Tenth Circuit panel then examined whether any historical tradition of analogous waiting period regulation exists. The court found none.

There is no established pattern in American history of governments requiring law-abiding citizens to wait a fixed period of time after a completed purchase before taking possession of a lawfully acquired arm. The absence of any such historical tradition is legally dispositive under *Bruen*. Without a historical analogue demonstrating that the founders' generation or the Reconstruction-era Congress endorsed delay-for-delay's-sake restrictions on firearm acquisition, the government cannot justify the law.

The dissent within the Tenth Circuit argued that public policy and public safety considerations should weigh in favor of upholding the law. That argument, however, misunderstands the *Bruen* framework. The Supreme Court was explicit: the Second Amendment's scope is not determined by contemporary policy preferences, utilitarian balancing, or means-ends scrutiny. Courts are not permitted to uphold a firearms regulation simply because they — or the government — believe it to be a good idea. The relevant inquiry is historical, not consequentialist.

## The Heckler's Veto Analogy

The legal infirmity of waiting period laws becomes even clearer when the constitutional parallel to the First Amendment is pressed further. In the First Amendment context, the Supreme Court has consistently refused to recognize what is known as a "heckler's veto" — the principle that the government may silence a speaker because someone, somewhere, might react badly to that speech. The potential for harm from third parties does not license prior restraint on the speech of innocent individuals.

A firearm waiting period operates on functionally identical logic. The government says, in effect: "We know you passed the background check. We know you are a law-abiding person entitled to purchase this firearm. But because some other person, in some other circumstance, might misuse a firearm, you must wait seven days to exercise your constitutional right." The law burdens the constitutional rights of the innocent majority because of the speculative misconduct of a hypothetical minority. Courts have rejected this reasoning in the First Amendment context, and the *Bruen* framework demands its rejection in the Second Amendment context as well.

## Significance of the En Banc Denial

The full Tenth Circuit's refusal to grant en banc rehearing is significant beyond the immediate parties. Under the federal court system, a panel decision becomes final circuit precedent when the full court declines to rehear it. The *Ortega* ruling now stands as binding Tenth Circuit authority that waiting period laws — at least those of this character — violate the Second Amendment.

For practitioners litigating similar challenges in the Tenth Circuit, *Ortega* is now controlling law. For practitioners in other circuits, the opinion will serve as highly persuasive authority in circuits that have not yet addressed the question. The reasoning — that the right to acquire is textually protected, and that no historical tradition supports waiting period restrictions on law-abiding purchasers — applies with equal force in every federal circuit.

## Implications

New Mexico's waiting period law has been struck down, and the Tenth Circuit has confirmed that result. This ruling should send a clear signal to state legislatures that have enacted or are contemplating similar statutes: a bare waiting period imposed on a law-abiding citizen who has already cleared the federal background check cannot survive Second Amendment scrutiny under *Bruen*.

More broadly, *Ortega* is part of a growing body of post-*Bruen* lower court decisions that take the Supreme Court's text-and-history methodology seriously and refuse to permit modern gun control preferences to substitute for the demanding historical analysis the Court has required. As these decisions accumulate, the constitutional foundation for the modern administrative state of firearms restrictions continues to erode — one legally indefensible restriction at a time.

The right to keep and bear arms means the right to acquire them. Courts that take their oath to the Constitution seriously have no other honest conclusion available to them.
