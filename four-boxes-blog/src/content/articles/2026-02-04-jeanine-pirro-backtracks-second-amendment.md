## Summary

United States Attorney for the District of Columbia Jeanine Pirro generated significant controversy in the Second Amendment community when, during a Fox News interview celebrating the Trump administration's success in reducing violent crime in Washington, D.C., she stated that anyone who brings a gun into the District of Columbia—regardless of their home state licensing status—would go to jail. The statement, which was interpreted by many as an indifference to the constitutional dimensions of D.C.'s firearms regulations, warrants careful legal analysis. In my analysis, Pirro's remarks were almost certainly intended to address unlawful carry specifically, not to endorse D.C.'s historically unconstitutional gun restrictions, but the phrasing was sufficiently loose to raise legitimate concerns—and those concerns reflect a deeper tension between prosecutorial law-and-order instincts and Second Amendment constitutional rights that conservatives must navigate carefully.

## Background

Jeanine Pirro was appointed by the Trump administration as the United States Attorney for the District of Columbia—the chief federal prosecutor in the nation's capital. Her background is as a tough-on-crime prosecutor and judge from Westchester County, New York, and she has earned a well-deserved reputation for being serious about criminal accountability. The Trump administration's approach to crime in Washington, D.C. has produced measurable results: murder rates and other violent crime metrics have fallen substantially since January 2025, a development attributable to stricter bail practices, more aggressive prosecution, and a general shift away from the progressive prosecutorial leniency that characterized prior administrations.

During an interview on Fox News, Pirro was discussing these law enforcement successes when she made the following statement: "You bring a gun into the district, mark my words, you're going to jail. I don't care if you have a license in another district and I don't care if you're a law-abiding gun owner somewhere else. You bring a gun into this district, count on going to jail, and hope you get the gun back."

This statement produced immediate and substantial reaction from the Second Amendment community, and not without reason.

## The Current State of D.C. Firearms Law

To understand the tension Pirro's remarks created, it is necessary to understand the current legal framework governing firearms in Washington, D.C.—a framework that has been substantially reshaped by litigation following **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008).

**Heller** struck down D.C.'s categorical ban on handgun possession in the home, establishing that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep arms for self-defense. Following **Heller**, D.C. established a shall-issue concealed carry permit regime, initially implemented as may-issue but subsequently converted to shall-issue following the D.C. Circuit's 2017 decision in **Wrenn v. District of Columbia**, 864 F.3d 650 (D.C. Cir. 2017), which struck down D.C.'s "good reason" requirement for carry permits as inconsistent with the Second Amendment.

Following **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), which struck down may-issue carry permit schemes nationwide, D.C.'s shall-issue regime became further constitutionalized. Under the current D.C. legal framework, both D.C. residents and non-residents may apply for a carry permit in the District, and that permit must be issued to qualified applicants who meet objective statutory criteria.

The critical legal wrinkle that Pirro's remarks touched is the following: D.C. is not a party to any reciprocity agreement with other states. A concealed carry permit from Virginia, Maryland, Texas, or any other state does not authorize the holder to carry a firearm in the District of Columbia. If a gun owner carries a firearm in D.C. without a D.C.-issued permit, they are in violation of D.C. law—even if they hold a valid permit from every state in the union.

## Analyzing Pirro's Statement

Reading Pirro's statement in context, it appears that she was specifically addressing individuals who bring firearms into D.C. in violation of D.C. law—i.e., without a valid D.C. permit. Her reference to "a law-abiding gun owner somewhere else" was addressing the situation of someone who is lawful in their home state but unlawful in D.C. by reason of the non-reciprocity rule.

If this is the correct reading, her statement is an accurate description of D.C. law as it currently stands. Bringing an unregistered or unlicensed firearm into the District is a criminal offense, and the U.S. Attorney's office is entitled to prosecute it.

The problem with the statement is not its technical accuracy but its rhetorical framing. Saying "I don't care if you have a license in another district" sounds, in isolation, dismissive of constitutional carry rights. It sounds as though the U.S. Attorney is indifferent to whether an individual has established their constitutional right to keep and bear arms through whatever process their home state requires—and that indifference, expressed by the chief prosecutor of the federal enclave that is home to the national government, is legitimately concerning.

## The Deeper Constitutional Tension

The Pirro episode illuminates a genuine and important tension within conservative law-and-order politics. There is a tradition of prosecutorial conservatism that is deeply committed to the rule of law, to the enforcement of positive law as written, and to the proposition that if a law is on the books, it must be enforced. This tradition has virtues: it prevents selective enforcement, maintains institutional credibility, and embodies a proper respect for democratic governance.

But it has a potential blind spot when applied to firearms law in jurisdictions like the District of Columbia. D.C. has a long history of enacting firearms regulations that are, on their face, constitutionally questionable or outright unconstitutional. The pre-**Heller** handgun ban is the paradigm case. But even post-**Heller** and post-**Bruen**, D.C. retains restrictions—including limitations on the types of firearms that may be possessed and requirements for storage and transportation—that are of doubtful constitutional validity.

A U.S. Attorney who enforces all D.C. firearms laws with equal vigor, without regard to their constitutional status, is not simply being a "law and order" prosecutor. They are lending the prestige and coercive power of the federal government to the enforcement of laws that may violate the constitutional rights of the very citizens being prosecuted. That is a different thing—and it requires a different analysis.

## The Shall-Issue Regime and Reciprocity

From a Second Amendment policy perspective, the non-reciprocity problem Pirro's remarks highlighted is worth addressing in its own right. The failure of the District of Columbia to recognize carry permits issued by the states creates a constitutional anomaly: a law-abiding gun owner who can lawfully carry in 35 states becomes a criminal the moment they cross into D.C. to visit their congressional representatives, attend a federal court proceeding, or simply pass through the District.

The Supreme Court, following **Bruen**, has held that shall-issue licensing schemes are constitutionally permissible. But the doctrinal development of Second Amendment carry rights has not yet fully addressed the national reciprocity question—the extent to which a state-issued carry permit should have constitutional force in other jurisdictions. That question is likely to arise in future litigation, and D.C.'s current non-reciprocity regime is a potential vehicle for it.

## Implications

Jeanine Pirro is a capable prosecutor and a generally reliable ally of the Trump administration's law enforcement priorities. I do not believe her Fox News remarks reflected hostility to the Second Amendment as such. They reflected, rather, the prosecution-forward instincts of a former district attorney applying those instincts in a jurisdiction with a complex and still-evolving firearms regulatory framework.

But the Second Amendment community was right to notice the statement and right to raise questions about it. The U.S. Attorney for the District of Columbia occupies a unique position: the District is the seat of the federal government, the locus of national Second Amendment policymaking, and a jurisdiction with a history of firearms overreach that has required repeated Supreme Court intervention to correct. The chief federal prosecutor in that jurisdiction should be acutely aware of the constitutional dimensions of firearms enforcement—and should calibrate enforcement decisions accordingly.

The broader lesson is this: law-and-order conservatism and Second Amendment conservatism are natural allies, but they are not identical. A commitment to enforcing the law as written is essential to constitutional governance. But it must be accompanied by a commitment to ensuring that the laws being enforced are themselves constitutional—and to declining to lend prosecutorial resources to the enforcement of laws that are not.
