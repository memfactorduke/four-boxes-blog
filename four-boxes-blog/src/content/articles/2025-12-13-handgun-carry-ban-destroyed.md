## Summary

In a major Second Amendment development, the state of Florida is now effectively an open carry jurisdiction following two compounding legal victories. First, the Florida First District Court of Appeal issued a ruling in **McDaniels v. State** striking down Florida's longstanding ban on the open carry of handguns as violating the Second Amendment, prompting the state's attorney general to instruct law enforcement agencies not to enforce the prohibition. Second, Gun Owners of America and several other plaintiffs, following a 2024 federal lawsuit, reached a settlement agreement with multiple Florida sheriffs and a critical component of the state attorney general's office in which the defendants concede that the right to open carry is protected under the Second Amendment, that no historical tradition exists to support banning open carry of firearms, and that they will not enforce the relevant criminal statute going forward. Florida is now both an open carry state and a permanent concealed carry state.

## Background

Florida has long been a concealed carry state, and several years ago enacted constitutional carry — allowing permitless concealed carry for law-abiding citizens. But open carry of handguns remained criminally prohibited under Florida Statute § 790.053. That prohibition has now been dismantled through the intersection of state appellate litigation and federal settlement, providing a powerful illustration of how coordinated legal strategy can achieve results that neither avenue might accomplish alone.

## The McDaniels Decision

The **McDaniels v. State** decision from Florida's First District Court of Appeal addressed the question of whether the post-**Bruen** legal landscape renders Florida's open carry prohibition unconstitutional. The case arose from a straightforward set of facts: the defendant carried a sidearm in a holster tucked in his pants, but the firearm was visible — uncovered — and thus technically constituted open carry under Florida law. The defendant was a law-abiding citizen. He did not threaten anyone. His conduct was entirely peaceful and orderly. He was arrested and charged solely for the visible presence of his lawfully owned firearm.

The First District Court of Appeal, applying the text-and-history methodology mandated by **Bruen**, held that the ban on the open carrying of handguns by law-abiding citizens violates the Second Amendment. The court found that the prior Florida Supreme Court decision upholding the open carry prohibition — issued approximately in 2017-2018 — was no longer valid because it had applied the interest-balancing or tiers-of-scrutiny approach to Second Amendment analysis, a methodology the United States Supreme Court explicitly and comprehensively rejected in **Bruen**. The prior state decision was thus analytically unsound and could not bind the lower courts.

## The Attorney General's Response

Following the **McDaniels** decision, Florida Attorney General James Uthmeier issued a September 15, 2025 memorandum to Florida's law enforcement agencies and prosecuting authorities providing formal guidance on the impact of the ruling. The memorandum stated that the First District Court of Appeal had struck down Florida's ban on open carry, finding that law-abiding adult citizens have the right to carry firearms openly in public under the Second Amendment. The attorney general declared that his office would not enforce Florida Statute § 790.053 as applied to the open carrying of handguns by law-abiding citizens.

This is a significant exercise of prosecutorial and executive discretion in favor of constitutional rights — a formal acknowledgment by the state's chief law enforcement officer that the open carry prohibition is unconstitutional and that continuing to enforce it would require prosecuting individuals for the exercise of a constitutionally protected right.

## The Gun Owners of America Settlement

The second prong of this development is the settlement agreement reached by Gun Owners of America and its co-plaintiffs in a federal lawsuit filed in 2024. The settlement, entered into with multiple Florida county sheriffs and a component of the attorney general's office, contains concessions of extraordinary legal significance.

The settling defendants have agreed and conceded: first, that the right to open carry firearms is a protected right under the plain text of the Second Amendment; second, that there is no historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States that would support a prohibition on the open carrying of firearms consistent with the Second Amendment; and third, that they will not enforce Florida Statute § 790.053 going forward as it applies to the open carrying of handguns.

These concessions — made by the state's own law enforcement defendants — constitute a powerful factual and legal record. By acknowledging that no historical tradition supports the open carry ban, the defendants have effectively foreclosed the argument that **Bruen**'s historical analysis would support such a prohibition. This record will be available to Second Amendment litigants not only in Florida but in other jurisdictions where open carry remains prohibited.

## Why the Historical Concession Matters

I want to pause on the historical concession, because it is the most legally significant element of this settlement. Under the **Bruen** framework, a firearms restriction that burdens conduct covered by the Second Amendment's plain text is unconstitutional unless the government can identify a historical tradition of analogous regulation dating to the Founding era or the Reconstruction era.

When government defendants concede in sworn legal proceedings that no such historical tradition exists — that there is no historical analog to the open carry prohibition — they are doing more than resolving a single lawsuit. They are creating a record that other courts can consult, and that advocates can cite, in future Second Amendment cases. The concession has persuasive force beyond the four corners of the Florida settlement.

## The Combined Effect: Florida as an Open and Concealed Carry State

The combined effect of **McDaniels**, the attorney general's non-enforcement guidance, and the GOA settlement is to render Florida effectively both an open carry and a permanent concealed carry jurisdiction. Law-abiding Floridians can now carry their firearms both openly and concealed without fear of criminal prosecution under the previously applicable statutory prohibition.

This comes on the heels of Florida's earlier adoption of constitutional carry, which eliminated the permit requirement for concealed carry. Florida has moved from a restrictive permitting system to one of the most gun-rights-protective legal frameworks in the country — not through legislative action alone, but through a combination of legislative reform, judicial decision, executive non-enforcement, and private plaintiff litigation.

## A Model for Other Jurisdictions

Florida's experience offers a model for Second Amendment advocates in other states. The coordinated use of state court litigation, federal court litigation, executive-branch engagement, and private plaintiff settlement negotiations can, when executed strategically, achieve results that no single avenue could accomplish alone. The fact that multiple sheriffs and the attorney general's office have formally conceded the unconstitutionality of open carry bans — on the record, in binding legal proceedings — provides a template for challenging similar prohibitions elsewhere.

## Implications

For the millions of law-abiding Floridians who exercise their Second Amendment rights, this is a historic victory. Open carry is no longer a criminal act in Florida. The state's law enforcement community has acknowledged that the Constitution's text, properly applied under **Bruen**, does not permit the criminalization of peaceful, open firearms carriage by individuals who have done nothing wrong.

For the broader Second Amendment movement, Florida demonstrates what is possible when legal strategy is properly designed and persistently executed. The open carry prohibition — which had stood for decades — has been dismantled not by a single dramatic court victory, but through the patient accumulation of favorable legal decisions and the strategic extraction of concessions that build upon each other.

Florida's Second Amendment record in 2025 represents some of the most significant progress in the practical protection of the right to keep and bear arms in the nation's most populous swing state.
