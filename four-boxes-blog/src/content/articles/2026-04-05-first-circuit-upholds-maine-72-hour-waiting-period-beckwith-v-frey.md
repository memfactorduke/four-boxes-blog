## Summary

In a unanimous three-judge decision, the U.S. Court of Appeals for the First Circuit reversed a district court ruling that had struck down Maine's 72-hour waiting period for firearm purchases. The panel held that the waiting period does not regulate conduct covered by the Second Amendment's plain text and is therefore not subject to the historical-tradition analysis required by *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022). The decision introduces a novel analytical layer to the *Bruen* framework, distinguishing between conduct that falls within the Second Amendment's plain text and conduct that merely "burdens" the right to keep and bear arms -- a distinction that critics argue is analytically incoherent and inconsistent with Supreme Court precedent.

## Background

Maine enacted its 72-hour waiting period for firearm purchases in the wake of a mass shooting. The law requires all purchasers to wait three days between completing the purchase of a firearm and taking physical possession of it. The statute includes exemptions for law enforcement officers and other government employees, who are not subject to the delay. The stated rationale for the law tracks familiar arguments advanced by gun-control advocates: that waiting periods reduce impulsive acts of violence and self-harm by imposing a "cooling off" period between the decision to acquire a firearm and the ability to use one.

Second Amendment plaintiffs challenged the law and sought a preliminary injunction in federal district court in Maine. The district court conducted what I describe as a rigorous analysis under the *Bruen* framework and concluded that the waiting period was unconstitutional. The court's reasoning proceeded in two steps. First, it found that the right to acquire firearms is protected by the Second Amendment's plain text. The logic was straightforward: the right to "keep and bear arms" necessarily presupposes the ability to obtain arms, because one cannot keep or carry a firearm one does not possess. A blanket delay on acquisition thus directly implicates the textual right. Second, having found the plain text implicated, the court shifted the burden to the state to demonstrate a historical tradition of analogous regulations dating to the founding era. The state was unable to identify any historical precedent for a blanket waiting period applicable to all firearm purchasers, and the district court accordingly enjoined enforcement of the law.

Maine appealed to the First Circuit, with the support of gun-control advocacy organizations. The case was heard by a three-judge panel consisting of one judge appointed by President Obama and two judges appointed by President Biden.

## The First Circuit's Analysis

The panel reversed unanimously. The core of its reasoning lies in a distinction it drew between conduct that the Second Amendment's plain text "covers" and conduct that merely "burdens" the right to keep and bear arms.

The court acknowledged the two-step *Bruen* framework: first, determine whether the challenged regulation implicates conduct covered by the Second Amendment's plain text; if so, the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that the regulation is consistent with this nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. However, the panel then introduced what it characterized as a middle category. Citing *Bruen*, the court stated that laws regulating conduct outside the Second Amendment's plain text but that nevertheless burden a person's right to keep and bear arms are "only presumptively constitutional." Such laws, the court explained, may still violate the Second Amendment if the challenger demonstrates that they are being "put toward an abusive end."

Applying this framework, the panel concluded that Maine's waiting period does not regulate conduct covered by the Second Amendment's plain text. The court reasoned that the amendment's text guarantees the right to "keep and bear" arms -- meaning to have and to carry firearms -- but that the act of purchasing or acquiring a firearm occurs prior to keeping or carrying it. The waiting period, in the court's view, imposes conditions on a commercial transaction that precedes the exercise of the textual right, rather than directly restricting that right. Accordingly, the panel held that the law falls outside the plain-text step of *Bruen* and is presumptively constitutional.

The court further found that the plaintiffs had not demonstrated the law was being applied toward an abusive end, and it therefore reversed the preliminary injunction.

## Analysis

The First Circuit's opinion raises several significant analytical concerns that I have reviewed carefully.

**The plain text and the right to acquire firearms.** The court's conclusion that firearm acquisition falls outside the Second Amendment's plain text stands in tension with a basic logical proposition: one cannot exercise the right to keep and bear arms without first acquiring arms. If a law prohibits or delays acquisition, it necessarily constrains the ability to keep and bear. This is not a novel observation. Other courts, including the Seventh Circuit in cases addressing the right to purchase ammunition and to train with firearms, have recognized that the right to keep and bear arms carries with it ancillary rights -- including the right to acquire firearms and ammunition -- without which the textual right would be a nullity. The First Circuit's attempt to sever the acquisition of firearms from the keeping and bearing of firearms creates a potential doctrinal gap through which substantial regulation of firearms commerce could pass without triggering *Bruen*'s historical-tradition requirement. As I have analyzed, this is a critical flaw in the reasoning.

**The "presumptively constitutional" middle category.** The panel's introduction of a category of laws that burden Second Amendment rights but are not subject to *Bruen*'s full two-step analysis is perhaps the most consequential aspect of the opinion. Under this framework, a regulation that admittedly burdens the right to keep and bear arms is nonetheless presumed valid unless the challenger can demonstrate an "abusive end." This standard is far more permissive than the historical-tradition test that *Bruen* prescribes for laws that implicate the plain text. Critics argue that this approach effectively reintroduces a form of means-end scrutiny -- the very analytical method that the Supreme Court in *Bruen* explicitly rejected. If a court can acknowledge that a law burdens Second Amendment rights and yet uphold it based on something other than historical tradition, the result resembles the interest-balancing framework that *Bruen* sought to displace. I find this framework analytically indefensible.

**Interest balancing by another name.** The opinion devotes several pages to discussing the public-policy rationale for waiting periods, including their purported effects on impulsive acts of self-harm and violence. Under the *Bruen* framework, the constitutionality of a firearms regulation does not turn on whether the government can demonstrate a compelling or substantial interest served by the law. It turns on whether the regulation is consistent with historical tradition. The First Circuit's extended treatment of the social-science justifications for waiting periods suggests a methodology more aligned with the tiers-of-scrutiny approach -- strict or intermediate scrutiny -- than with the historical-tradition test. The Supreme Court has repeatedly stated that such interest-balancing is impermissible in the Second Amendment context, and I believe this represents a fundamental misapplication of *Bruen*.

**Reliance on outdated authority.** The opinion cites a 1996 law review article addressing the infringement of fundamental rights. That article predates *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), by twelve years and *Bruen* by twenty-six years. Whatever relevance the article may have had in the pre-*Heller* legal landscape, its utility as an interpretive guide to a post-*Bruen* framework is, at best, questionable. As I have noted, this represents poor legal reasoning.

**Analogies to other rights.** The opinion's reasoning, if applied consistently to other constitutional rights, would produce results that no court would likely accept. A 72-hour waiting period before a citizen could purchase a book, register to vote, or publish a newspaper article would almost certainly be struck down as an impermissible burden on the First Amendment or the right to vote. The fact that such hypothetical restrictions would be deemed unconstitutional -- even though they regulate conduct that "precedes" the exercise of the textual right (reading the book, casting the ballot) -- underscores the tension in the First Circuit's reasoning. I have emphasized this inconsistency on The Four Boxes Diner.

## Implications

The decision sets the stage for potential Supreme Court review. I note that the ruling may create a circuit split with the Tenth Circuit on the question of whether waiting periods implicate the Second Amendment's plain text. A circuit split on a significant Second Amendment question arising under *Bruen* would be a strong candidate for certiorari.

The plaintiffs are represented by Aaron Murphy and Paul Clement, the latter of whom successfully argued *Bruen* before the Supreme Court. The involvement of experienced Supreme Court counsel increases the likelihood that the case will be competently positioned for further review.

If the Supreme Court were to take the case, it could address the First Circuit's novel "presumptively constitutional" framework directly. A reversal -- even a summary one -- would reinforce that the *Bruen* methodology requires courts to apply the historical-tradition test whenever a regulation implicates conduct protected by the Second Amendment, without recourse to intermediate categories or policy-based reasoning. I expect this issue will reach the Supreme Court soon.

In the interim, the First Circuit's decision permits Maine to enforce its 72-hour waiting period and may embolden other jurisdictions within the circuit -- which includes Maine, Massachusetts, New Hampshire, Rhode Island, and Puerto Rico -- to adopt or defend similar regulations. The opinion also provides a template for courts in other circuits inclined to limit *Bruen*'s reach by narrowly construing the scope of the Second Amendment's plain text, and I will be watching closely for how lower courts apply this flawed reasoning.
