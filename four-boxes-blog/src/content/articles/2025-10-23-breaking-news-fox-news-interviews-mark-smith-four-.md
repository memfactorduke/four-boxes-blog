## Summary

In a recent appearance on Fox News, I was asked to comment on two significant legal and political developments: President Trump's commutation of the sentence of former Congressman George Santos, and the indictment of former National Security Adviser John Bolton on charges related to mishandling classified information. Both events illuminate an important principle with direct relevance to constitutional governance and the rule of law — the principle that the law must apply equally to all Americans, regardless of their position, connections, or political affiliation. That principle is the bedrock on which all constitutional rights, including the Second Amendment, ultimately rest.

## Background

George Santos, the former Republican Congressman from New York, was convicted of federal felony charges and sentenced to seven years in federal prison. President Trump subsequently commuted — not pardoned — Santos's sentence. A commutation reduces the prison term but does not erase the underlying conviction; Santos remains a convicted felon with all the collateral legal consequences that status entails, including the loss of Second Amendment rights under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1).

At roughly the same time, the Trump administration's Department of Justice indicted former National Security Adviser John Bolton on charges related to the alleged mishandling of classified information. Bolton pleaded not guilty and characterized the prosecution as politically motivated, claiming to be "the latest target in the weaponizing of the Justice Department."

Both developments sparked significant legal commentary, and I was invited to discuss them on Fox News.

## The Santos Commutation

On the question of whether Santos was over-sentenced, I expressed the view that a serious argument can be made that the seven-year sentence was disproportionate to the offenses. Santos was a first-time, nonviolent offender. While his conduct was undoubtedly dishonest and his fall from a seat in Congress was itself a form of punishment, seven years in the federal system for a nonviolent, first-time offense raises legitimate proportionality concerns.

The constitutional authority of the President to grant pardons and commutations is explicit and broad. Article II, Section 2 of the Constitution vests in the President the "Power to grant Reprieves and Pardons for Offenses against the United States, except in Cases of Impeachment." A commutation — which reduces a sentence without eliminating the conviction — falls squarely within that authority. President Trump's decision to exercise that authority on Santos's behalf is not a legal anomaly; it is a routine exercise of executive power.

It is worth noting, because it is directly relevant to Second Amendment law, that the commutation does not restore Santos's civil rights, including his firearms rights. He remains a convicted felon. The commutation addresses only the prison sentence, not the underlying conviction or its attendant legal disabilities. This distinction — between a pardon, which restores rights, and a commutation, which merely reduces a sentence — is important and is frequently misunderstood in public commentary.

## The Bolton Indictment and the Equal Application of Law

The indictment of John Bolton presents a set of legal and constitutional questions that are both more complex and more significant than the Santos commutation. Bolton — who served as National Security Adviser under President Trump and whose memoir generated considerable controversy — is accused of improperly retaining and disclosing classified information.

Bolton characterizes the prosecution as politically motivated, noting that the Biden administration, which possessed the same factual record, chose not to pursue charges against him. The question of whether the Biden administration's decision not to prosecute was itself an act of politicized non-enforcement — motivated by the fact that Bolton was a Trump critic who had provided political ammunition to the Democratic Party through his memoir — is a legitimate one. If the same underlying facts that support the current indictment were available to the Biden DOJ, and the Biden DOJ declined to act on political grounds, that is itself evidence of a two-tiered justice system of the sort that has undermined public confidence in the rule of law.

At the same time, it is important not to lose sight of the legal substance. If John Bolton did what the indictment alleges — if he retained classified information, shared it with persons not authorized to receive it, and did so in a manner that compromised national security for purposes of personal enrichment through his memoir — those are serious offenses. The classification system exists to protect American lives and national interests. Violations of that system, if proven, warrant prosecution regardless of the offender's political alignment or connections.

The larger principle at stake is one that transcends this particular case: there must be one system of justice for all Americans. The corrosive pattern of the past several decades, in which senior government officials and politically connected individuals have routinely escaped accountability for conduct that would result in prosecution and imprisonment for ordinary citizens, has done profound damage to the constitutional order. The rule of law depends on the proposition that the law applies equally — to the powerful and the powerless alike, to those with political connections and those without them.

## The Connection to Second Amendment Rights

The principle of equal application of law has direct relevance to Second Amendment jurisprudence. For decades, prosecutors in jurisdictions hostile to gun rights have used selective enforcement of federal and state gun laws to target law-abiding citizens who engaged in entirely lawful conduct, while declining to prosecute politically connected individuals for far more serious firearms offenses. The anti-gun prosecutor who charges a gun owner for a purely technical violation while ignoring actual violent criminals is engaged in the same kind of two-tiered justice that the Bolton and Santos cases illustrate.

The Second Amendment guarantees a constitutional right to all Americans. That right must be enforced equally — not selectively deployed against political opponents or law-abiding citizens who fail to share the prosecutorial politics of a given administration or jurisdiction. When the rule of law is applied consistently and without political bias, the Second Amendment is more secure. When the rule of law is applied selectively, all constitutional rights are vulnerable.

## Implications

The Santos commutation and the Bolton indictment are, on the surface, distinct and disconnected legal events. At a deeper level, however, both illuminate the same fundamental question about the American constitutional order: whether the rule of law in the United States is a genuine operating principle or merely an aspiration that yields whenever politically powerful actors find it inconvenient.

I believe — and I expressed this view on Fox News — that the trend toward greater accountability for senior government officials represents a positive development for the constitutional order, even when the individuals being prosecuted are critics of the current administration. A government in which officials who violate the law face real consequences is a government that takes the rule of law seriously. And a government that takes the rule of law seriously is a government that will also take the Second Amendment seriously.

The alternative — a government in which the powerful enjoy immunity from the law that binds everyone else — is precisely the kind of despotism that the Founders designed the Second Amendment, among other constitutional provisions, to prevent.
