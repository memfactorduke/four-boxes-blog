## Summary

I recently had the privilege of presenting at the Second Amendment Foundation's annual Gun Rights Policy Conference — one of the most important gatherings in the Second Amendment advocacy community. The conference brought together leading attorneys, scholars, and activists to assess the state of Second Amendment law and to preview what lies ahead in the current Supreme Court term. The Foundation also honored me with the 2025 Gun Rights Defender of the Year Award at the conclusion of my remarks, an honor I accept with humility and a renewed sense of obligation to the millions of Americans whose constitutional rights remain under assault in courtrooms across this country. What I presented at that conference, and what I share here in expanded written form, is a comprehensive assessment of the five most critical categories of Second Amendment cases currently before the federal courts — cases whose outcomes will shape the constitutional landscape of the right to keep and bear arms for generations.

*Mark W. Smith is a constitutional attorney, member of the United States Supreme Court Bar, and host of The Four Boxes Diner.*

## Background

Before turning to the individual cases, it is essential to understand what makes the Supreme Court uniquely important in Second Amendment law — more important, in a structural sense, than Congress, the President, or any state legislature. Administrations change. Presidents serve four or eight years. Secretaries of State and Attorneys General come and go. But when the Supreme Court issues a constitutional decision, that decision is the supreme law of the land, binding on every court, every legislature, and every executive officer in the United States, for generations and generations.

**District of Columbia v. Heller** was decided in 2008. We live with its consequences, good and bad, to this day. **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** was decided in 2022. Its effects are still radiating through dozens of lower-court cases. The decisions the Supreme Court issues this term will bind us — and our children and grandchildren — for an equally long period. That is why the Supreme Court must be the primary focus of Second Amendment advocacy, legal analysis, and strategic planning.

## Category One: Marijuana, Drugs, and the Second Amendment

The first category of cases involves the intersection of drug use — particularly marijuana use — and the Second Amendment right to keep and bear arms. Federal law prohibits "unlawful users" of controlled substances from possessing firearms. As marijuana has been legalized for recreational or medicinal use in many states, a growing number of Americans who use marijuana lawfully under state law find themselves categorically stripped of their Second Amendment rights under federal law.

This presents a fundamental constitutional question: can the federal government impose a categorical, lifelong disarmament on the basis of controlled-substance use, without any individualized determination of dangerousness? The **Bruen** framework requires the government to identify a historical tradition of analogous regulations. There is no Founding-era tradition of categorical disarmament of drug users — a concept that was entirely foreign to eighteenth-century American law. The cases in this category have the potential to significantly narrow the scope of permissible categorical disarmament statutes.

## Category Two: The AR-15 Litigation

The second category involves the constitutional status of semi-automatic rifles — including the AR-15 and comparable firearms — and related restrictions such as magazine capacity limits. This litigation is proceeding on multiple fronts simultaneously.

**Duncan v. Bonta** is the leading California magazine-capacity case, challenging the state's prohibition on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds. The case has had a lengthy and tortuous history in the Ninth Circuit, including multiple en banc proceedings. The constitutional question is whether California's magazine limit — which would require millions of California gun owners to surrender or modify magazines they legally acquired — can survive **Bruen** scrutiny.

**Gator's Custom Guns v. Ferguson** presents a similar challenge in Washington State, which enacted magazine restrictions comparable to California's. Both cases raise the question of whether there is any historical analogue, at the time of the Founding or in the Reconstruction era, for government prohibitions on arms or accessories based on their capacity or rate of fire.

The answer, under faithful application of the **Bruen** framework, is no. There is no historical tradition of limiting the number of rounds a law-abiding citizen could load into a firearm. The cases in this category therefore present an opportunity for the Supreme Court to establish clearly that capacity restrictions fail constitutional scrutiny.

## Category Three: Young Adults and the Second Amendment

The third category — one of the most important and most actively litigated — involves whether 18-, 19-, and 20-year-old Americans possess Second Amendment rights, including the right to purchase handguns from federally licensed dealers.

I have addressed this category extensively in prior analyses. The core constitutional argument is powerful and historically grounded: at the time of the Founding, 18-year-olds were full adult members of society with the same civic rights and responsibilities as older adults. They could vote (in some jurisdictions), serve in the militia, bear arms, and exercise every prerogative of adult citizenship. There is no historical tradition of categorical exclusion of young adults from the right to keep and bear arms.

**Wolford v. Lopez** — discussed in the next category — also has young-adult dimensions, but the primary young-adult litigation is proceeding through cases like the Fifth Circuit's ruling in favor of Second Amendment rights in **Reese v. ATF** and the pending petition in **West Virginia Citizens Defense League v. ATF** in the Fourth Circuit context. The Supreme Court will need to address this question definitively in the near future, and I believe — based on my reading of the Court's precedents and its current composition — that the outcome will vindicate young adults' constitutional rights.

## Category Four: Sensitive Places and the Vampire Rule

The fourth category — and the one that received the most significant Supreme Court development as of the date of my conference presentation — involves the so-called "sensitive places" doctrine and its most pernicious modern application: the no-carry default rule, colloquially referred to as the "vampire rule."

**Wolford v. Lopez** is the leading case. Hawaii enacted a statute providing that even a licensed concealed-carry permit holder cannot carry a firearm into any private property open to the general public — any grocery store, gas station, movie theater, or restaurant — unless the property owner has affirmatively provided express permission for firearms. As I explained to my conference audience, this law functions as a near-total abolition of carry rights in practice, because property owners have no incentive and will not routinely post "firearms welcome" signs. The law inverts the traditional default: rather than permitting carry unless the property owner objects, Hawaii requires carry to be prohibited unless the property owner affirmatively consents.

The Supreme Court's grant of certiorari in **Wolford** was announced in the days surrounding my conference presentation, and I was pleased to be able to address it directly — albeit with the candid acknowledgment that this grant contradicted my earlier prediction that the Court would not take the case on an interlocutory basis. I am glad to have been wrong. The grant signals that the Court views the no-carry default rule as unconstitutional and is willing to say so.

## Category Five: Magazine Restrictions — California and Washington

The fifth category overlaps with the AR-15 litigation but deserves separate treatment because the magazine-restriction cases are at different procedural stages and raise somewhat distinct legal questions.

The California and Washington magazine restrictions have both been challenged in the federal courts. The Ninth Circuit has addressed the California restriction in **Duncan v. Bonta** across multiple rounds of litigation spanning several years. The Washington case, **Gator's Custom Guns**, is at a somewhat earlier stage. In both cases, the government bears the burden under **Bruen** of identifying a historical tradition of regulating magazine capacity — a burden the government cannot meet because no such tradition exists.

What makes the magazine cases particularly consequential is their practical scope. California and Washington have enacted outright bans on magazines holding more than ten rounds. These laws affect tens of millions of law-abiding gun owners who lawfully purchased and use standard-capacity magazines for self-defense. The Supreme Court's resolution of the magazine question will have immediate and far-reaching practical consequences for those Americans.

## The Importance of the Supreme Court's Composition

Throughout my conference presentation, I returned repeatedly to a theme that I regard as central to Second Amendment advocacy: the composition of the Supreme Court is, in the long run, the single most important factor in the constitutional fate of the right to keep and bear arms in America. The six-to-three majority on the current Court — reflecting the appointments of Justices Gorsuch, Kavanaugh, and Barrett under President Trump, building on the earlier appointment of Justice Thomas who has been the Court's most consistent and principled Second Amendment voice — has produced the **Bruen** decision and has demonstrated a willingness to take Second Amendment cases seriously.

Maintaining and, where possible, strengthening that majority must be a priority. Presidential elections, Senate confirmations, and judicial appointments may not be as immediately exciting as courtroom victories, but they are the foundation on which all those victories ultimately rest.

## Implications

The Second Amendment Foundation's Gun Rights Policy Conference was a fitting venue to take stock of where the Second Amendment stands today. The news, on balance, is genuinely good. The Supreme Court has a strong majority that takes the constitutional text seriously. The Trump administration's Department of Justice has actively supported Second Amendment positions before the Court. And the cases on the current docket — **Wolford**, the magazine cases, the young-adult cases, the drug-user cases — present opportunities for landmark rulings that will expand and entrench Second Amendment rights for the foreseeable future.

None of these victories is guaranteed. Each case requires careful lawyering, well-developed factual records, and persuasive briefing. But the legal and jurisprudential landscape has never been more favorable for the right to keep and bear arms than it is at this moment. The challenge for the Second Amendment community is to capitalize on that favorable landscape through disciplined, strategic, and constitutionally rigorous advocacy.
