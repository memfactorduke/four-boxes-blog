## Summary

The FBI's arrest of current and former NBA players in connection with an alleged illegal sports gambling and money laundering conspiracy may appear at first glance to be entirely unrelated to Second Amendment jurisprudence. It is not. The indictments, brought by the Bondi Department of Justice in the Eastern District of New York, illuminate a critical and evolving legal question that sits at the intersection of criminal law and the right to keep and bear arms: the distinction between individuals convicted of violent, dangerous crimes who may constitutionally be disarmed, and those convicted of non-violent, non-dangerous offenses whose Second Amendment rights must be preserved.

## Background

On the opening day of the NBA season, FBI Director Kash Patel and Attorney General Pam Bondi's Department of Justice announced the indictment of numerous individuals connected to the National Basketball Association in connection with an alleged illegal gambling operation. The indictment, filed in the United States District Court for the Eastern District of New York — located in Brooklyn — charged several current and former NBA players and associated individuals with illegal sports betting and money laundering conspiracy.

FBI Director Patel stated: "Today, the FBI arrested six people, including current and former NBA players, for their alleged role in illegal sports betting and money laundering conspiracy. The result of a sweeping investigation conducted by outstanding FBI professionals and partners. Using private information and positions of power to rig sports gambling outcomes is not only illegal, but destroys the integrity of the game and will never be tolerated."

## The Critical Second Amendment Question

The connection to Second Amendment law lies in a fundamental question that has been actively litigated across the federal circuits since the Supreme Court's decision in **United States v. Rahimi**: when the government seeks to disarm a person based on a criminal conviction, what category of criminal conduct is sufficient to justify permanent disarmament?

The Supreme Court in **Rahimi** established that an individual who has been found, after a due process hearing, to pose a physically violent danger to others may be temporarily disarmed consistent with the Second Amendment. This represents the constitutional floor: to disarm someone, the government must demonstrate that the individual presents a genuine physical danger.

## Mala in Se Versus Mala Prohibita

This brings us to a legal distinction that is ancient in origin but urgently relevant today: the difference between *mala in se* offenses and *mala prohibita* offenses.

*Mala in se* crimes are those that are inherently evil — conduct that would be recognized as deeply wrong even in a state of nature, entirely apart from any positive law enacted by a legislature. Murder, rape, robbery, assault, and arson are paradigmatic examples. These offenses are evil in themselves, independent of any statute declaring them illegal.

*Mala prohibita* crimes, by contrast, are offenses only because a legislature has chosen to prohibit them. There is no inherent moral dimension. Classic examples include jaywalking, loitering, carrying a handgun without a permit, or possessing a firearm with a barrel of the wrong length. These are crimes not because the underlying conduct is inherently harmful or immoral, but because a governing body has elected to regulate or prohibit them.

## The Significance of the *Brian Range* Decision

The Third Circuit's decision in **Range v. Attorney General** illustrates the stakes precisely. Brian Range had been convicted approximately twenty-five years prior of a food stamp-related welfare offense under Pennsylvania law. He was never sentenced to a single day of incarceration. Because the offense technically could have carried a multi-year prison sentence, it was classified as a felony for purposes of the federal felon-in-possession statute, 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1).

The Third Circuit, sitting en banc, concluded that Range was a non-violent felon — that he had never been found by any court to have posed a physically violent danger to himself or others. Accordingly, the court held that he could not be permanently disarmed based solely on that conviction. The conviction was a *malum prohibitum* offense with no violent or dangerous character whatsoever.

## Application to the NBA Gambling Indictments

The charges described in the NBA-related indictments — illegal sports gambling, money laundering conspiracy, exploitation of insider positions to rig betting outcomes — are precisely the category of offenses that Second Amendment scholars and advocates must carefully analyze. These are serious federal criminal charges. Conviction would constitute a felony under federal law and would, under the current statutory framework of 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1), result in permanent disarmament.

The constitutional question is whether convictions for non-violent offenses of this character — however reprehensible the underlying conduct — provide a sufficient legal basis for permanent firearms disabilities under the **Rahimi** framework. The government must demonstrate, consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation, that persons convicted of such offenses fall within the category of individuals who may be disarmed consistent with the Second Amendment.

This analysis matters not only for the specific defendants in these proceedings, but for the broader development of Second Amendment doctrine. Every federal prosecution that results in a felony conviction for non-violent conduct presents a potential future challenge to the felon-in-possession statute as applied to that specific defendant.

## The Emerging Framework

The post-**Rahimi** constitutional landscape requires courts to engage in individualized analysis. A blanket rule that any felony conviction — regardless of the nature of the underlying conduct — justifies permanent disarmament is constitutionally untenable under current Supreme Court jurisprudence. The historical tradition of firearms regulation in America does not support disarming individuals solely because they have committed non-dangerous, non-violent regulatory offenses.

Second Amendment attorneys and scholars should monitor the NBA-related prosecutions carefully. Should convictions result, the affected individuals will face firearms disabilities despite having committed no act of violence and presenting no demonstrated physical danger to others. Those future as-applied challenges to 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) will be among the most consequential Second Amendment cases of the coming decade.

## Implications

The FBI's arrest of NBA-connected individuals for illegal gambling and money laundering illustrates the continuing practical importance of the constitutional framework governing who may be disarmed under the Second Amendment. Under **United States v. Rahimi** and the Third Circuit's decision in **Range v. Attorney General**, the government bears a heavy burden to justify disarmament based on non-violent criminal convictions. The distinction between *mala in se* and *mala prohibita* offenses will be central to that analysis. Second Amendment practitioners and scholars should track the NBA prosecutions with this framework in mind, as the resulting convictions may generate significant future constitutional litigation.
