## Summary

Two recent developments — Virginia Governor Abigail Spanberger's executive order directing state law enforcement to cease cooperation with federal Immigration and Customs Enforcement operations, and the detailed public explanation by Polk County, Florida Sheriff Grady Judd of why sanctuary non-cooperation policies are both operationally dangerous and constitutionally corrosive — illustrate a pattern that I have argued consistently bears direct and material implications for the long-term security of the Second Amendment. The Second Amendment is ultimately a right that depends for its preservation on the political will of the American electorate; and that electorate is being systematically reshaped, I submit, by deliberate immigration policy choices whose long-term political consequences are as predictable as they are significant.

## Background: The Mechanics of Sanctuary Non-Cooperation

To understand why sanctuary policies matter, it is necessary to understand precisely what they do and do not involve. Sheriff Grady Judd, the elected Sheriff of Polk County, Florida, and one of the most forthright law enforcement voices in the country on immigration enforcement, explained the mechanics with admirable clarity at a recent press conference.

When local law enforcement arrests an individual who is in the country without lawful status, federal immigration authorities — Immigration and Customs Enforcement — issue what is known as a detainer request. The detainer is a formal notification from the federal government to the state or local custodial agency that the subject is subject to federal immigration enforcement, with a request that the subject be held for up to 48 hours following the completion of state proceedings so that ICE agents can take custody for deportation processing.

This is not a complex or burdensome process. The subject is already in custody in a locked state or local facility. What the detainer requests is, in essence, a phone call — "we have this individual, please come pick him up" — followed by a cell-to-cell transfer from state custody to a federal vehicle. The federal agents drive to the jail, take custody of the individual, and transport him to federal detention pending deportation proceedings. The entire process is conducted within secured facilities, out of public view, with minimal operational risk to any party.

Sheriff Judd further noted that among the illegal immigrants detained by his department, the overwhelming majority had criminal charges against them, and that in vehicle stops, a substantial proportion of passengers in a vehicle with the principal subject also had criminal records or warrants. This is not surprising: the population of individuals who have unlawfully entered and remained in the United States in violation of federal law has, by definition, already demonstrated a willingness to violate federal law. The correlation between illegal status and prior criminal history is not coincidental.

When sanctuary jurisdictions refuse to honor ICE detainers, they do not merely inconvenience federal immigration enforcement — they create genuine public safety hazards. An individual who would have been transferred quietly from local custody to federal custody for deportation is instead released back into the community, where he may and frequently does go on to commit additional offenses before being reapprehended. The second arrest triggers another cycle of potential release, and so on. Sanctuary policies thus operate as a mechanism for recycling known criminal actors back through local communities at taxpayer expense.

The alternative — non-sanctuary cooperation — requires ICE to conduct field arrests of individuals who are at large in public, rather than in-custody transfers. Field arrests in the current political environment involve deploying a large contingent of agents, navigating hostile bystanders, and operating in conditions that are inherently more dangerous for all involved than a controlled custodial transfer. Sanctuary non-cooperation thus converts a routine administrative process into a confrontational public event — and does so by deliberate political choice.

## Governor Spanberger's First Act in Office

The political dimension of sanctuary non-cooperation is illustrated with unusual clarity by the conduct of Virginia's newly inaugurated Governor Abigail Spanberger, a former CIA employee who ran on a platform calibrated to appeal to Virginia's expanding suburban and immigrant communities.

Governor Spanberger's first official act as governor was not to address infrastructure, education, public safety, or any of the ordinary concerns of state governance. Her first executive order directed Virginia law enforcement agencies to discontinue cooperation with federal immigration enforcement operations. The message was unambiguous: the protection of individuals unlawfully present in Virginia from federal law enforcement was a higher priority than compliance with federal law or cooperation with the constitutional exercise of executive branch enforcement authority.

I submit that this choice was not accidental or arbitrary. It reflects a deliberate political calculation about whose interests the Spanberger administration intends to serve. The illegal immigrant population — including those with criminal records who would otherwise be subject to deportation — represents a significant and growing component of the demographic base from which the Democratic Party draws electoral support in Virginia and other competitive states. Protecting that population from federal enforcement is not merely a humanitarian gesture; it is a structural investment in the future composition of the Virginia electorate.

## The Second Amendment's Dependency on the Electorate

The connection between immigration policy and the Second Amendment is not speculative — it is structural. I have advanced this argument consistently, and I want to state it plainly here.

The Second Amendment's practical significance depends on several institutional pillars: a federal judiciary willing to enforce it; a Congress unwilling to pass laws that gut it; state legislatures that comply with it; and presidents who appoint judges committed to its text and original public meaning. Every one of these pillars rests, ultimately, on the composition of the American electorate.

If the electorate is reshaped — through legal immigration, through the conversion of illegal residents into legal residents and eventually citizens, through demographic concentration in politically decisive states — toward populations that do not share the traditional American attachment to the right to keep and bear arms, then the institutional pillars that currently protect the Second Amendment will erode and eventually collapse. No Supreme Court decision, however favorable, is permanent in the face of a determined political majority committed to appointing justices who will read *Heller* and *Bruen* out of existence through reinterpretation.

This is not a hypothetical threat. It is the explicit program of one of the two major political coalitions in American life, and it requires no secret conspiracy to understand: it is written plainly in the policy platforms, judicial nomination criteria, and regulatory agendas of the anti-gun political coalition.

The argument that the immigration fight and the Second Amendment fight are separable — that one can defend the Second Amendment while being indifferent to or supportive of open borders and mass unlawful immigration — is, in my assessment, simply incorrect. If the immigration fight is lost — if millions of people who have no attachment to the constitutional culture of the United States become the electoral majority in the states that control the Senate and the Electoral College — then no Supreme Court decision on the Second Amendment will survive the subsequent political process.

## The Danger of Institutional Complacency

The risk I am identifying is not that any single election or any single court appointment will end the Second Amendment. The risk is more gradual: that over a period of years and decades, the political preconditions for the Second Amendment's institutional protection are slowly dismantled, making the work of the anti-gun coalition easier at each successive step.

The Supreme Court currently has a six-justice majority that has demonstrated a serious commitment to *Heller*, *McDonald*, and *Bruen*. That majority exists because of a specific and contingent series of political events: the 2016 presidential election, the Senate's refusal to confirm a replacement for Justice Scalia, and the subsequent appointments of three constitutional originalists by President Trump. None of that was inevitable. All of it depended on the political composition of the American electorate at specific historical moments.

The Second Amendment community cannot assume that those political conditions will indefinitely reproduce themselves. They are subject to change — including change driven by deliberate policy choices about who may enter the country, who may remain, and who may eventually vote. Governors like Abigail Spanberger, and the political coalition they represent, understand this perfectly well. The question is whether the Second Amendment community understands it as clearly.

## Implications

Sheriff Grady Judd's explanation of sanctuary non-cooperation mechanics is valuable because it makes concrete and visceral what might otherwise seem like an abstract policy debate. Real people — including illegal aliens with criminal records who have been arrested by local law enforcement — are being released back into American communities as a direct result of political decisions by sanctuary jurisdiction officials. The people in those communities bear the consequences of those decisions, in the form of re-offending by individuals who should have been deported and were not.

Those consequences include not only the direct harms of crime, but also the broader political and demographic consequences of a sustained policy of non-enforcement. Governor Spanberger's first executive order reveals the political logic that drives sanctuary policy: it is not primarily about humanitarian protection of the vulnerable, though it is packaged that way. It is primarily about protecting a constituency — the illegal immigrant population and those who sympathize with it — whose votes and political activation are central to the Democratic electoral coalition.

I do not offer these observations as a counsel of despair, but as a counsel of clarity. The Second Amendment will not be secured by winning court cases while ignoring the political and demographic processes that determine the long-term composition of the federal judiciary, the Congress, and the electorate. Winning requires winning on all the relevant fronts simultaneously. The Trump administration's aggressive and legally grounded immigration enforcement effort is, in my assessment, a necessary component of that broader project — and the resistance to it by sanctuary jurisdictions, illustrated vividly by Governor Spanberger's first act in office, is a measure of what is at stake.
