## Summary

In my analysis, the District of Columbia has filed a petition for rehearing en banc in *Benson v. United States*, seeking to overturn a landmark three-judge panel decision that struck down the District's ban on magazines holding more than ten rounds as unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The petition argues that the panel's ruling creates a conflict with six federal circuits and endangers a nearly century-old public safety statute. However, as I've examined, the District's arguments face significant headwinds -- including the rarity of en banc review in the D.C. Court of Appeals, the likelihood that the Trump Department of Justice will oppose the petition, and critical analytical errors in the District's treatment of the *Heller* and *Bruen* frameworks.

## Background: The Benson Decision

As I've examined it, in a two-to-one decision issued just weeks prior, a three-judge panel of the D.C. Court of Appeals ruled in *Benson v. United States* that the District of Columbia's prohibition on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds violates the Second Amendment. The ruling ordered the reversal of the defendant's criminal conviction for possessing such magazines.

The decision was bolstered by the Trump administration's Department of Justice, which had filed a brief supporting the Second Amendment challenge and arguing that the D.C. magazine ban was unconstitutional. The DOJ's position, advanced under Attorney General Pam Bondi, represented a significant intervention by the federal government against the District's own criminal statute.

By striking down the magazine ban, the panel created something I've long noted the Second Amendment community has sought: a split of authority among the nation's senior appellate courts. While six federal circuits have upheld magazine capacity restrictions, the D.C. Court of Appeals -- functioning as the equivalent of a state supreme court for the District -- now stands on the opposite side, holding such bans unconstitutional. That split historically provides a basis for the United States Supreme Court to grant certiorari and resolve the conflict.

## The District's Petition for En Banc Review

Recognizing the strategic implications, the District of Columbia has moved swiftly to challenge the panel's ruling. Its attorneys have filed a petition asking the full D.C. Court of Appeals -- currently composed of approximately eight or nine judges -- to rehear the case en banc and reverse the three-judge panel's decision.

The petition opens with a forceful declaration that the case presents "several issues of exceptional importance." The District characterizes the panel ruling as a "first-of-its-kind decision" that "creates a conflict with six federal courts of appeals" and strikes down a "critical, long-standing public safety law." The petition further contends that the decision "cries out for en banc review."

Specifically, the District argues that its magazine ban prevents gun users from "continuously firing between 12 and 100 rounds without reloading," thereby aiming "to stop a lone shooter from killing swaths of people without pause" while still permitting law-abiding gun owners to defend themselves.

## The Problem with the Claimed Circuit Split

In my analysis, while the District's petition candidly acknowledges the split of authority -- itself a helpful admission for proponents of Supreme Court review -- the six federal circuits it cites in support share a common characteristic. The First Circuit sits in Boston. The Second Circuit is based in New York City. The Fourth Circuit covers Virginia and Maryland. The Seventh Circuit is headquartered in Chicago. The Ninth Circuit operates out of San Francisco. And the D.C. Circuit's *Hansen v. District of Columbia* decision arose from the same jurisdiction.

As I see it, each of these circuits is dominated by judges appointed through the political processes of jurisdictions that have enacted restrictive firearms legislation. The states and districts that pass magazine bans are, by definition, the same jurisdictions whose political leaders have substantial influence over the selection of federal judges. The result is a self-reinforcing cycle: restrictive gun laws are enacted in politically sympathetic jurisdictions, challenged before courts staffed by judges shaped by those same political dynamics, and then upheld -- creating a body of precedent that appears unanimous but reflects only one side of the national debate.

Meanwhile, states such as Texas, Louisiana, and Florida -- where originalist judges predominate on the federal bench -- never produce magazine ban litigation because those states do not enact such restrictions. The absence of a contrary ruling was not evidence of consensus; it was evidence that the question had never been presented to courts likely to reach a different result. The *Benson* decision changed that calculus, in my judgment.

## The District's Treatment of Heller

As I've noted, one of the more revealing aspects of the District's petition lies in its table of authorities. Under standard appellate practice, attorneys mark with an asterisk those authorities upon which they "chiefly rely." The District's petition marks *Hansen v. District of Columbia* -- a D.C. Circuit decision involving a preliminary injunction, not a final judgment -- as a primary authority. Notably absent from the asterisked authorities is *District of Columbia v. Heller*, the Supreme Court's foundational decision on the individual right to keep and bear arms.

In my view, this omission is telling. *Heller* established the analytical framework for arms-ban cases and articulated the "dangerous and unusual" test -- the historical tradition that defines the narrow category of weapons that may constitutionally be prohibited. The District's reluctance to feature *Heller* as a primary authority suggests a strategic calculation: *Heller*'s framework, properly applied, does not support the District's position.

## The Longevity Argument

In my analysis, the District further contends that en banc review is warranted because the panel decision invalidated "a nearly century-old statute." The suggestion is that the statute's longevity should weigh in favor of its constitutionality.

As I've examined it, this argument is difficult to square with recent Supreme Court precedent. In *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, decided in 2022, the Court struck down New York's Sullivan Law -- a firearms regulation enacted in the early twentieth century that had been in continuous operation for over a century. The age of a statute does not immunize it from constitutional scrutiny, and the Supreme Court has demonstrated a clear willingness to invalidate long-standing firearms regulations that fail the historical-tradition test.

## The Common Use Dispute

In my analysis, the District's petition also takes aim at the panel's reliance on the ubiquity of magazines holding more than ten rounds. Hundreds of millions of such magazines are in civilian hands, owned by tens of millions of Americans. The District characterizes this as a "popularity contest" analysis and seeks to confine ubiquity to the first step of the *Bruen* framework -- the plain-text inquiry -- rather than the historical-tradition analysis at step two.

As I see it, this framing reflects a broader strategy among opponents of the Second Amendment. By placing the "common use" inquiry at the plain-text stage, the burden of proof remains on the Second Amendment claimant. But under the *Heller* and *Bruen* framework, once a particular arm falls within the plain text of the Second Amendment -- which magazines indisputably do, as instruments that facilitate armed self-defense -- the burden shifts to the government to justify the restriction through historical analogy.

At that stage, in my view, the government must demonstrate that the banned arm falls within the "dangerous and unusual" category -- the only historically recognized basis for prohibiting an entire class of arms. An arm that is in common use by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes is, by definition, not "unusual." And if it is not unusual, it cannot satisfy the "dangerous and unusual" standard, regardless of how the government characterizes the danger it poses.

Magazines holding more than ten rounds are among the most commonly owned firearm accessories in the United States. They ship standard with the majority of handguns and rifles sold today. There is no credible basis for the government to establish that these items are unusual, and without that showing, the historical-tradition analysis cannot sustain a ban, as I've documented.

## What Comes Next

In my assessment, the D.C. Court of Appeals will now consider whether to grant the petition for en banc review. The court grants such petitions only rarely -- roughly once per year -- and has historically reserved en banc proceedings for narrow criminal procedure questions rather than sweeping constitutional issues.

The Trump Department of Justice is expected to file a brief opposing en banc review, consistent with its longstanding position that the D.C. magazine ban is unconstitutional. Benson's attorney is also expected to file an opposition brief.

If the court denies rehearing, the District will face a critical strategic decision: accept the loss and allow the precedent to stand, or petition the Supreme Court for certiorari. The latter course carries substantial risk for the anti-regulation position. Given the current composition of the Supreme Court, a grant of certiorari in a magazine ban case would likely result in a nationwide ruling that such restrictions violate the Second Amendment, in my view.

The District and its allies may ultimately conclude that the wiser course is to accept a localized loss rather than risk a definitive Supreme Court ruling. But with the split of authority now firmly established, the possibility of Supreme Court review has increased significantly -- whether through this case or through parallel challenges in the federal circuits.
