## Summary

A federal district court in the state of Vermont has dismissed a lawsuit brought against Century Arms — a firearms manufacturer — arising from a mass shooting in California, ruling that the Protection of Lawful Commerce in Arms Act bars the claim. This ruling is significant for several reasons. First, it is a victory against the lawfare strategy that has been deployed against the American firearms industry for years — the coordinated effort to use civil litigation to destroy gun manufacturers not because they violated any law but because activists and their lawyers are opposed to the existence of the firearms industry. Second, and particularly noteworthy, this dismissal came from the same federal judge — Judge William Sessions — who last year had ruled against Century Arms in the same case, holding that the Protection of Lawful Commerce in Arms Act did not bar the plaintiffs' claims. The judge's reversal reflects the impact of the United States Supreme Court's decision in the **Estados Unidos Mexicanos v. Smith & Wesson Brands** case, which clarified and reinforced the proper scope of PLCAA protection. I want to analyze this ruling in detail, explain why PLCAA is constitutionally and legally sound, and address the broader significance of the Supreme Court's decision in the Mexico v. Smith & Wesson case for the American firearms industry.

## Background

The Protection of Lawful Commerce in Arms Act — PLCAA — was enacted by Congress and signed into law in 2005. The law was a direct response to a coordinated legal campaign that sought to use civil litigation to impose massive financial liability on firearms manufacturers and dealers for criminal misuse of their products by third parties — criminals, terrorists, and mass shooters — over whom the manufacturers and dealers had no control.

The legal theory underlying these suits was, and remains, deeply flawed as a matter of both common law and constitutional principle. The long-standing common-law rule is that a manufacturer or seller is not liable for the criminal acts of a third party to whom it sold a lawfully manufactured, lawfully sold product in full compliance with all applicable laws. If someone buys a car and uses it to commit vehicular homicide, the automobile manufacturer is not liable for the homicide. If someone buys a baseball bat and uses it to assault another person, the bat manufacturer is not liable for the assault. These principles have been settled common-law doctrine for centuries.

When firearms manufacturers were subjected to the same legal theory — sue the manufacturer because a criminal misused the product — the courts should have applied the same common-law principles and dismissed the cases. But because gun manufacturers were politically disfavored targets, and because many of these lawsuits were brought in anti-gun jurisdictions before anti-gun judges, some courts allowed the cases to proceed. The resulting litigation costs, even when manufacturers ultimately prevailed, threatened to bankrupt the American firearms industry through legal attrition.

Congress responded by enacting PLCAA, which codified the common-law principle that firearms manufacturers and dealers are not civilly liable for the criminal or unlawful misuse of their lawfully manufactured and lawfully sold products by third parties. The law does not create any new immunity — it simply requires courts to apply the common-law principles that should have applied all along.

## The Mexico Lawsuit Against Smith & Wesson

The most prominent recent example of anti-gun lawfare against the firearms industry was the lawsuit filed by the government of Mexico against Smith & Wesson and other American firearms manufacturers. Mexico alleged that American gun companies were legally responsible for cartel-related gun violence in Mexico, on the theory that the manufacturers knowingly facilitated the illegal trafficking of firearms across the border into the hands of Mexican drug cartels.

The theory was, as I have discussed extensively, legally absurd. Smith & Wesson manufactures firearms in Massachusetts and sells them through a multi-tiered distribution system — to licensed distributors, who sell to licensed dealers, who conduct federally mandated background checks on purchasers. At every step, the parties in that distribution chain are licensed by the federal government and are required by federal law to comply with comprehensive regulatory requirements. After a firearm has been lawfully sold at retail, any subsequent illegal diversion of that firearm — smuggled across the border, sold on the black market, used in a crime — is the result of the criminal actions of third parties over whom Smith & Wesson has no control and no legal responsibility.

The Supreme Court of the United States heard the **Estados Unidos Mexicanos v. Smith & Wesson Brands** case and ruled — on a strong vote — in favor of Smith & Wesson and against the Mexican government. The Court held that PLCAA's protection applies to the claims Mexico brought, and that Mexico could not use American courts to impose liability on American gun manufacturers for criminal violence committed in Mexico by Mexican cartels using firearms that entered Mexico through illegal trafficking networks that the manufacturers did not control. This was the correct and constitutionally sound result, and it reinforced the principle that PLCAA means what it says.

## The Vermont Case: Century Arms and the Predicate Exception

The Vermont case involving Century Arms arose from a different factual context — a mass shooting in California — but presented the same fundamental legal question: whether PLCAA bars a lawsuit against a firearms manufacturer for the criminal actions of a third-party shooter who misused the manufacturer's product.

Century Arms manufactures rifles, including AR-style semi-automatic rifles. A lawsuit was filed against Century Arms following a mass shooting in California in which one of the shooter's weapons was allegedly manufactured by Century Arms. The plaintiffs argued that PLCAA did not bar their claims because the lawsuit fit within one of PLCAA's statutory exceptions — specifically, the "predicate exception," which allows claims to proceed if the gun company knowingly violated a state or federal statute "applicable to the sale or marketing" of firearms.

The predicate exception is the primary vehicle through which anti-gun litigants have attempted to evade PLCAA's protections. The argument goes like this: even if PLCAA bars ordinary negligence or product liability claims against gun manufacturers, if the manufacturer "knowingly" violated some applicable statute — even in a technical or attenuated way — the claim can proceed.

Judge Sessions, the federal district judge in Vermont, initially accepted a version of this argument and allowed the case against Century Arms to proceed. That ruling was wrong, and the Supreme Court's decision in the Mexico case provided a clear basis for correcting it.

## Judge Sessions' Reversal

What makes this dismissal particularly noteworthy is that it represents a significant reversal by the same judge who had previously allowed the case to proceed. Judge William Sessions — the son of the former United States Attorney General, himself a longtime federal judge — had ruled in favor of the plaintiffs in an earlier phase of the litigation, finding that PLCAA did not bar the claims. He was now, after the Supreme Court's ruling in the Mexico case, revisiting that conclusion and reaching the opposite result.

This is how the system is supposed to work. When the Supreme Court issues a definitive ruling on a question of statutory and constitutional law, lower courts are bound by that ruling and are obligated to apply it to the cases before them. Judge Sessions' willingness to revisit his prior ruling in light of intervening Supreme Court authority, and to reach a correct result that dismissed the case, reflects the appropriate judicial temperament and respect for hierarchical authority within the federal court system.

The predicate exception argument that had previously allowed the case to proceed failed in light of the Supreme Court's clarification of PLCAA's scope. The plaintiffs could not demonstrate that Century Arms knowingly violated a statute in a manner that meaningfully contributed to the harm at issue. The attenuated chain of causation — manufacturer to distributor to dealer to lawful retail customer to criminal to victim — does not satisfy the statutory requirement of knowing violation by the manufacturer.

## The Broader Lawfare Problem

The Century Arms victory in Vermont is one battle won in a longer war. Anti-gun litigants and the organizations that fund them have not abandoned their strategy of using civil litigation to financially destroy the American firearms industry. They continue to file lawsuits in anti-gun jurisdictions, before anti-gun judges, hoping to find courts willing to allow PLCAA exceptions to swallow the rule.

The lesson that the progressive legal Left has drawn from these years of litigation is that originalist and textualist judges — judges who follow the law as written rather than reaching the result that their policy preferences favor — are the primary obstacle to the success of the lawfare strategy. Democratic-appointed judges in anti-gun jurisdictions have been far more willing to accept creative legal theories that circumvent PLCAA's protections, precisely because those judges share the policy goal of destroying the American firearms industry.

This dynamic underscores a point I make consistently: the composition of the federal judiciary, including and especially the Supreme Court, is the single most consequential factor in the long-term fate of Second Amendment rights and of the legal protections — like PLCAA — that the Second Amendment community has secured through legislation. Every judicial appointment matters.

## Implications

The dismissal of the lawsuit against Century Arms is a significant Second Amendment and rule-of-law victory. PLCAA means what it says: firearms manufacturers and dealers who comply with federal law are not civilly liable for the criminal actions of third parties who misuse their lawfully sold products. The Supreme Court's ruling in the Mexico case reinforced this principle at the highest level of the federal judiciary, and district courts like the Vermont court in the Century Arms case are now correctly applying that precedent to dismiss legally baseless suits.

The lawfare campaign against the American firearms industry is not over, but it is losing. Each dismissal, each appellate victory, each Supreme Court ruling in favor of PLCAA's protections makes the next lawsuit harder to bring and harder to sustain. The goal — a robust American firearms industry capable of providing law-abiding Americans with the arms necessary to exercise their Second Amendment rights — is being defended successfully, one case at a time.
