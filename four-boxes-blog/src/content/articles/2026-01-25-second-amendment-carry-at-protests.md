## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit recently issued a two-to-one decision in **Kipke v. Moore** upholding a Maryland statute that restricts the carry of firearms at public demonstrations and protests, applying what in my analysis is a historically unsupported "sensitive places" exception to the Second Amendment's protections. The decision, which arises against the backdrop of a highly publicized incident involving an individual carrying a firearm at an immigration enforcement protest in Minneapolis, presents a critical doctrinal question: whether the government may categorically prohibit licensed carry holders from bearing arms in public venues that happen to be characterized as demonstrations, in the absence of any genuine founding-era historical analogue for such a restriction. In my assessment, the Fourth Circuit's majority opinion applies *Bruen* incorrectly and the dissent has the better of the argument.

## Background

The factual and legal backdrop for this discussion involves two distinct but related events. The first is the shooting death of Alex Prey, a nurse who had been participating in protests against federal immigration enforcement operations in Minneapolis, Minnesota. Prey was shot by a Border Patrol agent who was providing perimeter security for an ICE deportation operation. The circumstances of the shooting — whether it was legally justified — are the subject of ongoing legal proceedings and commentary. I do not address the legality of the shooting itself here; that question implicates use-of-force law that falls outside the scope of this analysis.

What the shooting did, however, was catalyze a wave of statements from anti-gun advocacy organizations — including Giffords Law Center and Brady — framing the incident as a reason to prohibit the carry of firearms at public protests. Those statements coincide with the Fourth Circuit's ruling in *Kipke v. Moore*, which directly addresses the constitutionality of Maryland's protest carry restriction.

Minneapolis itself presents a specific context worth noting. The city is a sanctuary jurisdiction in which local law enforcement had declined to assist federal immigration authorities with operations in the area. Federal agents conducting ICE operations were therefore required to provide their own security perimeter — a circumstance that placed those agents in direct proximity with a hostile crowd without the backstop of cooperative local police. The dynamics of that operational environment bear on any analysis of the use of force that occurred.

## The Maryland Statute and the Fourth Circuit's Analysis

Maryland's law restricts the ability of concealed carry permit holders to bring their firearms into areas where public demonstrations are taking place, effectively conditioning the exercise of the right to carry on the absence of any protest activity in the vicinity. The practical operation of this restriction is significant: in a jurisdiction like Maryland, where political demonstrations occur regularly, the statute functions as a broad suppression of carry rights in public spaces, indexed not to any fixed geographic location but to the occurrence of an expressive activity.

Under *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the analytical framework for evaluating such a restriction is well-established. The first step asks whether the challenged law burdens conduct covered by the plain text of the Second Amendment. If it does, the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that the restriction is consistent with the historical tradition of firearm regulation in America, drawing on analogues from the founding era.

The plain text question in *Kipke* is not seriously contested. A law that restricts the ability of a licensed carry holder to bear arms in a public space — by definition — implicates the core right recognized in *Bruen* to carry a firearm for self-defense in public. Hawaii conceded as much in *Wolford v. Lopez*, a related case currently before the Supreme Court, and the Fourth Circuit majority appears to have accepted that the Maryland statute engages the Second Amendment's text.

The fight in *Kipke*, therefore, turns entirely on the historical tradition inquiry.

## The Sensitive Places Doctrine and Its Limits

*Bruen* acknowledged that "sensitive places" — such as legislative assemblies, polling places, and courthouses — have historically been subject to firearms restrictions, and that modern regulations prohibiting carry in analogous locations are presumptively valid. The question in *Kipke* is whether a public demonstration or protest qualifies as a "sensitive place" under any historically grounded definition of that concept.

In my assessment, the answer is clearly no. The founding-era authorities cited in support of "sensitive places" restrictions involve discrete, government-controlled venues in which the state has a particularized interest in excluding weapons — courthouses, legislative halls, and analogous government buildings. None of those historical examples extends to outdoor public gatherings on streets or public parks where citizens are exercising their First Amendment rights.

The Fourth Circuit majority's opinion, by a two-to-one vote, purports to find sufficient historical analogues to sustain the Maryland restriction. The dissent takes the more persuasive view that the majority's historical sources do not do the work required of them under *Bruen*'s demanding standard. As I have argued in prior analysis, a historical analogue must be meaningfully similar in both its *justification* and its *burden* on the Second Amendment right. Post-Civil War statutes, Reconstruction-era enactments, and laws that clearly targeted racial minorities — which I address below — cannot serve as the founding-era tradition that *Bruen* requires.

## The Irony of Black Codes as Historical Analogues

Perhaps the most notable dimension of the historical tradition dispute in cases like *Kipke* is that several of the laws offered by anti-gun litigants as historical analogues to modern carry restrictions are, in historical fact, Black Codes — statutes enacted in the former Confederate states in 1865 and the years immediately following the Civil War, specifically designed to disarm the newly freed African American population and render them economically and physically dependent on their former enslavers.

The Louisiana statute of 1865 that restricted carrying of firearms in public — sometimes cited in this class of cases — was enacted at the end of the Civil War precisely to deprive freed Black Americans of the ability to hunt, provide for themselves, and exercise the rights of free citizens. The purpose was to force them back onto the plantations as de facto indentured laborers by denying them the means of self-sufficiency.

That such statutes are being deployed as constitutional precedent in defense of modern gun control laws is, as I submit, deeply ironic — and profoundly revealing about the poverty of the historical record supporting these restrictions. *Bruen* instructs courts to look for a historical tradition rooted in the founding era and consistent with the Constitution's original public meaning. Laws enacted by unreconstructed Confederate governments to suppress the rights of Black Americans represent neither a legitimate nor an analogous historical tradition.

## Anti-Gun Groups' Opportunistic Use of the Minneapolis Shooting

The statements issued by Giffords, Brady, and similar organizations in the aftermath of the Minneapolis shooting illustrate a recurring pattern: anti-gun advocacy groups seize on high-profile incidents involving firearms to advance legislative and litigation agendas that bear only tangential relationship to the specific facts of those incidents.

The framing deployed in those statements — that the nurse's presence at the protest with a firearm was itself the cause of his death, or that broader carry restrictions would have prevented the incident — elides the critical factual and legal context. The question of whether law enforcement's use of force was constitutionally justified is a distinct inquiry from whether law-abiding citizens have the right to carry firearms for self-defense. These are not the same question, and conflating them serves the advocacy agenda of those organizations without advancing any coherent legal analysis.

## Implications

The Fourth Circuit's two-to-one decision in *Kipke v. Moore* exemplifies the ongoing struggle between the *Bruen* framework — which demands genuine historical justification for firearms restrictions — and the efforts of anti-gun litigants and sympathetic lower courts to find accommodating precedent in the historical record. The dissent in *Kipke*, in my analysis, correctly identifies the majority's failure to apply *Bruen*'s demanding analogical test.

The cases currently pending at the Supreme Court, including **Wolford v. Lopez**, present the Court with an opportunity to clarify the scope of the sensitive places doctrine and to set outer limits on the government's ability to invoke that doctrine as a vehicle for categorical carry restrictions in public spaces. I am confident that the Court will rule in favor of the Second Amendment, consistent with *Bruen*'s text-and-history methodology, and that ruling will provide the lower courts — including the Fourth Circuit — with clearer guidance on how to evaluate restrictions like Maryland's protest carry ban.

In the interim, the *Kipke* decision remains a setback that underscores the continuing need for vigilance in the courts, in the legislature, and in the broader public square.
