## Summary

The Trump Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi, has filed a brief in the United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit defending the federal prohibition on the sale, distribution, and possession of automatic switchblade knives — commonly known as the Federal Switchblade Act of 1958. The brief is notable not only for the outcome it advocates, which is likely incorrect as a matter of Second Amendment law, but for the analytical methodology it employs, which is demonstrably wrong under the Supreme Court's **Bruen** framework. The DOJ's brief leads with a public safety justification rooted in a 1950s Senate subcommittee study on juvenile delinquency — precisely the kind of interest-balancing approach that **Bruen** explicitly and categorically rejected. In my assessment, the brief is analytically flawed, and the question of why the Trump DOJ continues to file such briefs in arms-related cases admits of a plausible strategic explanation that, while understandable, does not excuse the methodological errors.

## Background: The Federal Switchblade Act and the Pending Challenge

The Federal Switchblade Act was enacted by Congress in 1958 and prohibits the manufacture, sale, transportation, distribution, and import into the United States of automatic switchblade knives in interstate commerce, as well as possession on federal lands. The Act arose from a Senate Judiciary Committee Subcommittee on Juvenile Delinquency investigation in the 1950s that focused on, among other things, the use of switchblade knives by juveniles in the commission of robberies and assaults.

**Knife Rights, Inc. v. Bondi** is a Fifth Circuit case challenging this prohibition on constitutional grounds. The plaintiff organization argues that automatic switchblade knives are "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment — a position that has textual and historical support — and that the federal prohibition is inconsistent with the historical tradition of arms regulation in the United States. The case is on appeal from a district court decision that upheld the federal prohibition as consistent with the Second Amendment.

The Trump DOJ filed an amicus brief defending the federal prohibition. The brief warrants analysis both for its substantive arguments and for what its methodology reveals about the DOJ's approach to Second Amendment litigation.

## The Brief's Methodological Error: Leading with Public Safety

The most fundamental error in the DOJ's brief is analytical rather than factual: the brief leads with a public safety justification. Specifically, the brief opens by citing the 1958 legislative history, emphasizing that the Federal Switchblade Act was enacted "following a full and complete study of juvenile delinquency" by the Senate Judiciary Committee, and that the subcommittee concluded — based on "interactions with police chiefs throughout the country" — that "the automatic switchblade knives were on many occasions the instrument used by juveniles in the commission of robberies and assault."

This approach is categorically foreclosed by **Bruen**. The Supreme Court in **Bruen** explicitly and unambiguously rejected the interest-balancing approach to Second Amendment challenges — the approach under which courts weigh the government's public safety interests against the burden on Second Amendment rights. Justice Thomas, writing for the majority, held that courts "should not 'make difficult empirical judgments about the costs and benefits of firearms restrictions'" and that the Second Amendment's "demands of text and history" control the analysis, not judicial assessment of whether a regulation serves a compelling governmental interest. Courts do not assess whether a particular weapon is dangerous or likely to be misused. They assess whether the regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of arms regulation in the United States.

When the DOJ leads a Second Amendment brief with the argument that Congress enacted the challenged law to protect public safety based on a study of juvenile delinquency and the testimony of police chiefs, it is deploying precisely the analytical framework that **Bruen** rejected. If the court applies **Bruen** correctly, this argument is not merely unpersuasive — it is the wrong question. The correct question is whether there exists a historical tradition, from the founding era, of prohibiting the carrying or possession of switchblade-type knives. There is no such tradition.

## Are Switchblades "Arms"?

Before reaching historical tradition, one must determine whether automatic switchblades fall within the Second Amendment's protection of "arms." The Supreme Court in **Heller** defined "arms" broadly to include any instrument that constitutes a bearable arm — any weapon that could be used for confrontation, including all instruments that constitute bearable arms. Knives have been recognized as arms throughout American legal history. Antebellum courts regularly addressed the constitutional status of bowie knives, dirks, and other edged weapons. The question is whether automatic switchblades — knives whose blades deploy automatically via a spring mechanism rather than manual unfolding — are meaningfully different from other knives for constitutional purposes.

I submit that they are not. The mechanism of blade deployment is an operational feature, not a categorical distinction. A knife is a knife; the speed at which the blade becomes accessible does not change the fundamental nature of the instrument as a bearable arm. If the Second Amendment protects the right to keep and bear knives — and the weight of historical authority suggests it does — it protects the right to keep and bear automatic switchblades as well.

## Why Does the DOJ File These Briefs? A Strategic Explanation

The Trump DOJ's decision to defend the Federal Switchblade Act, like its earlier defense of certain NFA-related regulations, raises a question that the Second Amendment community has understandably pressed: why does a putatively pro-Second Amendment administration continue to file briefs defending arms restrictions?

I believe there are three possible explanations, and I have arrived at what I consider the most plausible. The first explanation is institutional inertia: career DOJ attorneys defend existing federal statutes reflexively, regardless of administration, and political leadership has not yet overridden that reflexive defense in this category of cases. The second explanation is genuine disagreement: some within the administration believe the challenged restrictions are constitutionally defensible and should be defended. The third — and in my assessment most plausible — explanation is strategic: the DOJ may prefer to have certain arms restrictions adjudicated on facts and records of its own choosing, in cases where it controls the briefing, rather than risk adverse precedent arising from cases it did not supervise.

Under this strategic theory, the DOJ files briefs defending regulations it would not proactively enact because it wants to manage the litigation environment — to ensure that if a court is going to address the constitutional status of switchblades, the briefing reflects the government's best arguments rather than leaving a gap for a poorly-litigated case to produce a sweeping adverse ruling. This is a coherent litigation strategy, even if it produces briefs that, as here, apply the wrong analytical framework.

Whatever the explanation, the methodological errors in this brief should be corrected. The administration's defense of the Federal Switchblade Act, if it is to be maintained, should at minimum be advanced through proper **Bruen** analysis — examining whether there is a historical tradition of regulating such weapons from the founding era — rather than through interest-balancing arguments that the Supreme Court has explicitly rejected.

## Implications

**Knife Rights, Inc. v. Bondi** is a significant case. A Fifth Circuit ruling on the constitutional status of the Federal Switchblade Act will address the scope of the Second Amendment's protection for edged weapons — a question that has received less judicial attention than firearm regulations but that has significant implications for the doctrine's development. The DOJ's brief, by employing discredited **Bruen** methodology, does not serve the cause of sound constitutional adjudication. In my assessment, the Second Amendment community should advocate for the DOJ to revise its approach in this litigation, and courts applying **Bruen** faithfully should reject the public safety framework in favor of the historical tradition inquiry that **Bruen** mandates.
