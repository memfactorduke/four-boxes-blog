## Summary

In a significant ruling on executive authority, the United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit vacated a preliminary injunction that had temporarily blocked three provisions of President Trump's executive orders directing federal agencies to end diversity, equity, and inclusion programs within federal grant and contract processes. The panel held that the plaintiffs—the National Association of Diversity Officers in Higher Education, the mayor and city council of Baltimore, and the American Association of University Professors—lacked standing to challenge certain provisions, and that to the extent they had standing, they had not demonstrated a likelihood of success on the merits of their First Amendment or Fifth Amendment claims. In my analysis, this ruling is constitutionally correct and reflects a welcome reaffirmation of the President's Article II authority to direct the executive branch's expenditure of federal funds consistent with existing anti-discrimination law.

## Background

Shortly after beginning his second term, President Trump issued two executive orders directing federal executive agencies to eliminate DEI programs from federal grant and contracting processes, and to require recipients of federal funds to certify that they do not engage in race-based discrimination in violation of existing federal civil rights law. These orders reflected the President's position that DEI programs—which often prioritize applicants on the basis of race, ethnicity, or sex—constitute illegal discrimination under existing federal anti-discrimination statutes, including Title VI and Title VII.

A coalition of plaintiffs filed suit in the District of Maryland, arguing that the executive orders violated the First Amendment by penalizing speech about DEI, and the Fifth Amendment by imposing vague conditions on the receipt of federal funds. The district court entered a preliminary injunction blocking three of the orders' provisions. The Fourth Circuit stayed that injunction pending appeal, and has now vacated it on the merits.

## The Fourth Circuit's Analysis

The court's ruling, which explicitly favors President Trump in a circuit that has historically been unfavorable to his legal positions, rests on two principal grounds.

First, on standing: the court found that for several of the challenged provisions, the plaintiffs failed to establish the concrete, particularized injury required by Article III. An organization that advocates for DEI programs, or a city government that funds such programs, does not automatically have standing to challenge a federal policy that conditions grant receipt on compliance with anti-discrimination law. The injury must be actual and imminent, not speculative.

Second, on the merits: the court rejected the First Amendment challenge. The executive orders do not prohibit anyone from speaking about DEI, advocating for DEI policies, or studying DEI as an academic matter. They condition the receipt of federal money on compliance with existing law. The government's ability to attach lawful conditions to the expenditure of federal funds—conditions requiring compliance with anti-discrimination law—does not implicate the First Amendment in any cognizable way.

The court's analysis also engaged the President's Article II authority. The Take Care Clause—Article II, Section 3—imposes on the President an affirmative duty to faithfully execute the laws of the United States. Existing federal anti-discrimination law, including the Civil Rights Act, prohibits discrimination on the basis of race. If DEI programs operate by systematically preferring individuals of certain racial categories over others, they are, on their face, engaged in conduct that existing federal law already prohibits. The President's executive orders merely instruct the executive branch to enforce that existing legal prohibition consistently.

As the Fourth Circuit's opinion states: "In the first days of his second term, President Donald J. Trump issued two executive orders that directed executive agencies to end diversity, equity, and inclusion DEI programs within federal grant and contract processes." The court vacated the district court's injunction and remanded the matter.

## The Broader Significance for Constitutional Structure

I have analyzed this ruling in the context of what I call the structural Constitution—the provisions of the Constitution that define the architecture of government power, as distinct from the individual rights provisions of the Bill of Rights. Both sets of provisions are essential to the preservation of liberty.

The President's authority over how federal money is spent is grounded in Article II's vesting of executive power in the President, the Take Care Clause's requirement that he faithfully execute the laws, and the practical reality that the executive branch, not the courts or the Congress on a day-to-day basis, administers the federal government. When a district court enjoins a presidential directive about how executive agencies must structure their grant-making processes, it is exercising a form of supervisory authority over the executive branch that is constitutionally problematic on its face.

The Fourth Circuit's decision to vacate that injunction reflects a correct understanding of the separation of powers. Federal courts have authority to ensure that executive action complies with constitutional and statutory requirements. They do not have authority to second-guess every presidential policy choice about conditions on federal spending, particularly when those conditions are themselves grounded in existing statutory requirements.

## The Connection to Second Amendment Rights

This case might appear to have no direct connection to the Second Amendment, but the structural constitutional principles it implicates are foundational to all constitutional rights, including the right to keep and bear arms. A properly functioning separation of powers—with an executive branch that enforces the law as written, rather than as a sympathetic district judge might prefer—is a precondition for any constitutional right to be reliably protected.

When lower courts issue sweeping injunctions against presidential directives based on tenuous First Amendment or standing theories, they are not merely intervening in one policy dispute. They are establishing a precedent of judicial supervision of executive enforcement that can be deployed against any administration's enforcement priorities. Today the injunction targets an anti-DEI directive. Tomorrow, the same logic could be deployed to enjoin an administration's enforcement of Second Amendment-protective policies.

## Implications

The Fourth Circuit's ruling is a meaningful win for Article II executive authority and for the structural Constitution. The district court's injunction should not have been entered—the standing analysis was strained, and the First Amendment theory was, at best, novel. The appellate court's prompt vacatur of that injunction is exactly the kind of institutional correction that the appellate review process is designed to produce.

Whether the underlying litigation will proceed on remand, and whether the plaintiffs can establish standing and a meritorious constitutional claim on a fuller record, remains to be seen. But the structural principle vindicated by this ruling—that the President has broad authority to direct executive agencies' enforcement of existing federal anti-discrimination law, and that federal courts should not casually enjoin that direction—is sound and important.
