## Summary

I've analyzed the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit's denial of rehearing en banc in *Olympus Spa v. Armstrong*, No. 23-4031, a case in which the owners of a traditional Korean women-only nude spa in Washington State challenged the state's enforcement of its anti-discrimination statute against them after they declined to admit a biological male who identified as female. The denial left intact a three-judge panel opinion that rejected the spa's claims under the Free Exercise Clause, the Free Speech Clause, and the freedom of association. The en banc denial prompted a sharply worded dissent from Judge Lawrence VanDyke, whose deliberately provocative opening line -- and the heated response it drew from nearly thirty of his colleagues -- has thrust the case into the national spotlight and may strengthen the prospect of Supreme Court review, as I've observed.

## Background: The Washington Law Against Discrimination and Olympus Spa

Olympus Spa is a traditional Korean spa with locations in Lynnwood and Tacoma, Washington. Owned and operated by Korean Christians, the establishment has long served an exclusively female clientele. The spa's services -- rooted in Korean wellness traditions emphasizing restoration and well-being -- require patrons to be fully nude during treatments. The spa's owners maintain that their policy of admitting only biological females is grounded both in the nature of the business and in their sincerely held Christian beliefs regarding modesty and the separation of unclothed persons of the opposite sex.

The dispute arose when a biological male who identified as female filed a complaint with the Washington State Human Rights Commission after being denied entry to the spa. The Commission investigated and determined that the spa's policy of excluding transgender women who had not undergone gender-affirming surgery violated the Washington Law Against Discrimination (WLAD), which prohibits discrimination in public accommodations on the basis of sexual orientation -- a category that Washington state law defines to include gender identity.

Olympus Spa then filed suit against the state, raising claims under the First Amendment's Free Exercise Clause and Free Speech Clause, as well as the right to freedom of association. The spa argued that compelling it to admit biological males into an all-female nude environment violated its owners' Christian convictions and forced an expressive message inconsistent with the establishment's identity and purpose.

## The Panel Opinion

A three-judge panel of the Ninth Circuit affirmed the district court's dismissal of Olympus Spa's claims. The panel's reasoning addressed each constitutional argument in turn.

On the free exercise claim, the panel held that the WLAD is a neutral, generally applicable law that only incidentally burdens religious exercise. Under controlling precedent, such laws are subject to rational basis review rather than strict scrutiny. The panel concluded that Washington's interest in eliminating discrimination in public accommodations satisfied that standard, and it rejected the spa's argument that enforcement of the statute in this context required the spa to effectively renounce its religious beliefs.

On the free speech claim, the panel applied intermediate scrutiny and held that the WLAD imposed only an incidental restriction on speech -- one no greater than essential to the elimination of discriminatory conduct. The panel reasoned that the law targeted conduct, not expression, and that any communicative impact was secondary.

The panel likewise rejected the spa's freedom-of-association claim, finding no constitutional violation in requiring a commercial establishment to comply with a generally applicable public accommodations law.

## The En Banc Denial and Judge VanDyke's Dissent

After the panel decision, Olympus Spa petitioned for rehearing en banc. The full Ninth Circuit denied the petition, and as I've examined it, the dissent from Judge Lawrence VanDyke immediately drew national attention.

Judge VanDyke, a Trump appointee who previously served as Solicitor General of Nevada and is a graduate of Harvard Law School, opened his dissent with a line calculated to shock: "This is a case about swinging dicks." In my analysis, he argued that the panel's legal analysis, however polished, obscured the real-world consequences of its holding -- namely, that the state of Washington could compel a women-only nude spa to admit individuals with male anatomy, over the religious objections of the establishment's owners and the privacy interests of its female clientele, some as young as thirteen.

VanDyke accused the panel majority of using "slick legal arguments and deflection to studiously avoid eye contact with the actual and horrific consequences of its erroneous opinion." He argued that the case presented a straightforward collision between state anti-discrimination enforcement and the Free Exercise Clause, and that the panel had failed to apply the kind of rigorous scrutiny that the First Amendment demands, in his view.

Addressing the substance, Judge VanDyke contended that the WLAD, as applied to Olympus Spa, was neither neutral nor generally applicable in any meaningful sense, because its enforcement targeted conduct motivated by sincere religious conviction and imposed a burden that fell exclusively on religious objectors. He suggested that the case bore significant parallels to the kinds of free exercise challenges that have drawn the Supreme Court's attention in recent terms.

## The Response from Colleagues

Judge VanDyke's rhetorical choices provoked a concurrence in the denial of rehearing, signed by approximately twenty-nine Ninth Circuit judges, that sharply criticized the tone of his dissent. Judge M. Margaret McKeown, writing for the group, invoked Justice Sandra Day O'Connor's admonition that the legal system is a place "to disagree without being disagreeable." The responding judges characterized VanDyke's language as "vulgar barroom talk" and objected to his suggestion that the majority had "collectively lost their minds" or that his colleagues were "woke judges complicit in the scheme to harm ordinary Americans."

Judge VanDyke, in a separate response to his colleagues, was unapologetic. He observed that his "distressed colleagues appear to have the fastidious sensibilities of a Victorian nun when it comes to mere unpleasant words in my opinion, yet exhibit the scruples of our nearly departed colleague Judge Reinhardt when it comes to the government trampling on religious liberties and exposing women and girls to male genitalia." He described this "selective outrage" as speaking for itself.

VanDyke concluded with a quotation from Shakespeare's *The Merchant of Venice*: "In law, what plea so tainted and corrupt, but being seasoned with a gracious voice, obscures the show of evil?" His point was that dignified language can serve to camouflage an unjust result, and that "sometimes coarse and ugly words bear the truth."

## Implications and the Path to the Supreme Court

As I've analyzed it, the denial of rehearing en banc in *Olympus Spa v. Armstrong* does not end the case. The spa's owners are widely expected to file a petition for a writ of certiorari with the United States Supreme Court. In my view, several features of the case may make it an attractive candidate for review.

First, the factual posture is unusually vivid. The case involves a women-only nude spa operated by religious owners, where the state's anti-discrimination enforcement compels the admission of biological males into an environment of complete physical exposure. That fact pattern puts the competing interests -- religious liberty, women's privacy, and anti-discrimination enforcement -- into stark relief in a way that more abstract disputes do not.

Second, the case implicates an active area of the Court's docket. The Supreme Court has shown sustained interest in the intersection of anti-discrimination laws and First Amendment freedoms in recent terms, and *Olympus Spa* raises the question of whether a state may compel a religiously motivated business to engage in conduct that its owners believe violates their faith.

Third, Judge VanDyke's dissent -- whatever one thinks of its tone -- has ensured that the case will arrive at the Court's doorstep with significant public visibility. The widespread media coverage and attention from public figures may increase the likelihood that the Justices take notice of the petition.

Whether the Supreme Court ultimately grants certiorari will depend on a number of factors, including the presence or absence of a circuit split and the breadth of the questions presented. But as I see it, *Olympus Spa v. Armstrong* has plainly become one of the most closely watched religious liberty cases in the lower federal courts, and its journey may be far from over.
