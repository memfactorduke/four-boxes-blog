## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit has issued a landmark Second Amendment ruling in **Rood v. Bonta**, holding two-to-one that California's ammunition background check law — enacted as Proposition 63 in 2016 and effective in 2019, but quickly enjoined — is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. The decision affirms a federal district court's permanent injunction of the statute. Writing for the majority, the Ninth Circuit applied the textual-and-historical framework mandated by **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), and held that both the textual analysis and the historical tradition inquiry conclusively doom California's ammunition background check scheme. This is, without qualification, a major victory for the Second Amendment and for the textual methodology that **Bruen** demands.

## Background

California's Proposition 63, passed by voters in 2016, created what its proponents correctly described as the first-in-the-nation comprehensive ammunition background check law. The statute required California residents to undergo a background check through the state's Automated Firearm System before every single purchase of ammunition. In practical terms, the law established four different pathways through which a purchaser might pass a required background check before completing an ammunition transaction. Regardless of which pathway applied, the underlying principle was uniform: no California resident could purchase ammunition without first submitting to a state-administered check to verify that the purchaser was not legally prohibited from possessing firearms.

The statute's proponents argued that controlling access to ammunition was an effective mechanism for preventing individuals who are prohibited from possessing firearms from acquiring the means to use them. The statute's opponents argued that it imposed a substantial and constitutionally unjustifiable burden on law-abiding Californians who wished to exercise their Second Amendment rights.

A federal district court agreed with the challengers, permanently enjoining the statute on the ground that it was unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. California's Attorney General — Rob Bonta — appealed that ruling to the Ninth Circuit, generating the decision at issue here.

It bears noting that California itself treated the novelty of this statute as a point of pride. Attorney General Bonta and the state's legal team emphasized throughout this litigation that Proposition 63 was the first law of its kind in the nation. Under the **Bruen** framework, however, novelty is not a virtue — it is a constitutional deficiency. A law with no historical analog at the founding cannot survive Second Amendment scrutiny.

## The Bruen Framework

The Supreme Court's 2022 decision in **Bruen** established the controlling analytical framework for Second Amendment challenges to firearms regulations. The framework proceeds in two steps. First, a court asks whether the challenged regulation burdens conduct that is within the plain text of the Second Amendment. If it does, the regulation is presumptively unconstitutional, and the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that the regulation is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation at the time of the Second Amendment's ratification in 1791.

The textual analysis under **Bruen** begins with the operative clause of the Second Amendment: "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed." The threshold textual question in **Rood v. Bonta** was whether an ammunition background check law burdens conduct within the plain text of the Second Amendment. That question depends on two subsidiary inquiries: (1) whether ammunition constitutes "Arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment's text, and (2) whether the right to "keep" and "bear" arms encompasses a right to acquire them — and, by extension, their necessary components.

## The Textual Analysis

The Ninth Circuit's majority correctly resolved both textual questions in favor of the challengers.

On the first question — whether ammunition constitutes "Arms" — the court held that it does. The reasoning is straightforward and, in my view, compelled by the text. Firearms are unquestionably protected arms under the Second Amendment, as established by **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and reaffirmed in **Bruen**. A firearm without ammunition is, as the court observed, functionally equivalent to a hammer or a blunt object. It cannot discharge a round. It cannot function as a firearm. Because the utility of a firearm as an arm depends entirely on the availability of ammunition, ammunition is necessarily an arm within the meaning of the Second Amendment's text. Any other reading would render the right to "keep and bear Arms" effectively illusory — a right to possess a firearm, but not the means to make it functional.

On the second question — whether the right to "keep" and "bear" arms encompasses a right to acquire them — the court again answered correctly. The Second Amendment's protections of "keeping" (possession) and "bearing" (carrying for self-defense) are meaningless if the right does not also encompass the acquisition of the arms to be possessed and carried. One is not born possessing a firearm. The right to keep and bear arms necessarily implies a right to obtain arms in the first instance. Any regulation that substantially impedes acquisition therefore implicates the text of the Second Amendment and triggers the **Bruen** framework's burden-shifting analysis.

Applying these principles, the court held that California's ammunition background check requirement — which imposed fees, delays, administrative obstacles, and the prospect of disqualification on every single ammunition purchase — burdened conduct plainly within the text of the Second Amendment. The burden accordingly shifted to the state to justify the law through historical tradition.

## The Historical Tradition Analysis

Having found that the challenged law implicates the Second Amendment's text, the Ninth Circuit majority then turned to the historical tradition inquiry. California bore the burden of identifying a well-established historical tradition of analogous firearms regulation going back to the founding era.

California could not meet that burden. There is no founding-era analog to a requirement that law-abiding citizens submit to a government background check before each and every purchase of ammunition. The colonies and the early republic imposed no such requirement. No state in the decades following the Second Amendment's ratification in 1791 imposed anything resembling a per-transaction ammunition background check. California's inability to identify a historical analog was, in one sense, unsurprising — the statute was, as Attorney General Bonta himself acknowledged, the first of its kind in the nation. A law that has no predecessor in American legal history cannot credibly claim to reflect a longstanding well-understood tradition of firearms regulation.

The majority rejected California's attempts to analogize its ammunition background check scheme to various historical regulations that bore, at most, a superficial resemblance to the challenged statute. The **Bruen** framework requires more than a loose thematic similarity between a modern regulation and historical predecessors; it requires a genuine analog that reflects the same justification and imposes a comparable burden on the right. California's historical evidence fell far short of that standard.

## The Dissent

One judge on the three-judge panel dissented. The dissenting opinion's view — that California's law should survive Second Amendment scrutiny — is, in my assessment, inconsistent with the analytical framework the Supreme Court established in **Bruen** and is precisely the kind of result-oriented reasoning that **Bruen** was intended to foreclose.

## Implications

**Rood v. Bonta** is a critically important Second Amendment decision for several reasons. It is the first Ninth Circuit ruling to strike down a California gun law on Second Amendment grounds using the **Bruen** framework. It confirms that the right to acquire arms — and the means necessary to exercise arms — is protected by the Second Amendment's text. And it demonstrates that the **Bruen** framework, correctly applied, is capable of protecting Second Amendment rights even in the most hostile circuits.

The ruling's impact extends beyond California. At least three other states — New York, New Jersey, and Illinois — have enacted or are considering ammunition background check requirements similar to Proposition 63. The Ninth Circuit's ruling in **Rood v. Bonta** provides persuasive authority for challenges to those laws as well.

California may seek en banc review before the full Ninth Circuit, which could reverse this ruling. The Ninth Circuit's full bench has historically not been friendly to Second Amendment claims. But the **Bruen** framework's textual and historical methodology is difficult to circumvent without explicitly defying the Supreme Court's instructions, and the current Supreme Court has demonstrated a willingness to intervene when lower courts depart from **Bruen**'s dictates.

The Second Amendment community should view this decision as a significant but potentially fragile victory. The fight over California's ammunition restrictions is not over. But **Rood v. Bonta**, as it stands today, represents a landmark application of **Bruen** by the circuit that has most consistently resisted Second Amendment claims — and that, in itself, is a development worth celebrating.
