## Summary

The United States Department of Justice and the plaintiff gun rights organizations in **Ree v. Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives** have filed a joint motion to alter or amend the judgment in that case, specifically seeking to vacate the provision directing the Second Amendment Foundation, the Firearms Policy Coalition, and other organizational plaintiffs to disclose their membership lists to the federal government. The joint filing confirms in no uncertain terms that the DOJ — under Attorney General Pam Bondi — did not request membership list disclosure, never sought it, and opposes the provision on both policy and constitutional grounds. This development fully vindicates my public position that the controversy over the membership list provision was based on a fundamental mischaracterization of what actually occurred in the litigation.

## Background

**Ree v. ATF** is a Second Amendment challenge litigated in the United States District Court for the Western District of Louisiana, within the jurisdiction of the Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals. The case challenges the federal statutory prohibition on federally licensed firearms dealers selling handguns to individuals between the ages of 18 and 20. The Fifth Circuit Court of Appeals had previously ruled in favor of the Second Amendment plaintiffs, holding that the prohibition is unconstitutional as applied to this age group, as law-abiding adults between 18 and 20 are among "the people" to whom the Second Amendment guarantees the right to keep and bear arms.

Following the Fifth Circuit's ruling, the parties returned to the district court to finalize the terms of the injunctive relief. In the course of entering a final judgment, United States District Judge Robert Summerhays included a provision — not requested by either party and not the subject of briefing or argument before its entry — requiring the plaintiff organizations to disclose their membership lists to the Department of Justice. The apparent rationale was to identify which individuals would be covered by the injunction's protections.

When the membership list provision became public, it generated widespread alarm and criticism within the Second Amendment community. Multiple commentators and media outlets characterized it as evidence that the Bondi DOJ was attempting to assemble a database of gun owners — a gun registry by another name. Some called for Attorney General Bondi's resignation.

I immediately and publicly disputed that characterization. The DOJ had not requested this information. The provision was entered entirely on the court's own initiative. Furthermore, as a matter of basic First Amendment law, the government may not compel the disclosure of private organizational membership lists. The precedent of **NAACP v. Alabama** (1958) makes this plain.

## The Joint Motion

On Friday, October 10, 2025, the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives — acting on behalf of the Department of Justice — together with the Second Amendment Foundation, the Firearms Policy Coalition, and the other organizational plaintiffs, filed a joint motion to alter or amend the judgment in **Ree v. ATF**. The motion asks the district court to vacate the membership list disclosure provision.

The joint filing makes several key points. First, the DOJ never asked for the membership list and has no interest in obtaining it. The court entered the provision without consulting the parties, without affording them an opportunity to brief the issue, and without any request from either side. Second, compelling disclosure of private organizational membership is inconsistent with DOJ policy. Third, and most importantly as a constitutional matter, forcing Second Amendment organizations to reveal their membership to the government would violate the First Amendment's protection of freedom of association — the very right the Supreme Court protected in **NAACP v. Alabama** when Alabama sought to compel the NAACP to disclose its membership roster to state authorities.

The DOJ's invocation of **NAACP v. Alabama** in this context is significant. It reflects an understanding that the constitutional protections established to defend civil rights organizations from hostile government scrutiny in the 1950s apply with equal force to Second Amendment organizations today. The government's inability to force the NAACP to expose its members to potential retaliation is precisely analogous to the government's inability to force gun rights organizations to expose their members to similar risks.

## The Scope of Injunctive Relief

The joint motion also addresses the scope of the injunctive relief in **Ree v. ATF**. As I explained when the Fifth Circuit issued its ruling, the question of how broadly the injunction should extend became complicated by the Supreme Court's recent decision in **Trump v. CASA**, which imposed limitations on the use of so-called "universal" or "nationwide" injunctions that extend relief to parties who are not before the court.

The parties have agreed to an injunction that applies throughout the Fifth Circuit — covering Texas, Louisiana, and Mississippi — and that extends to the members of the plaintiff organizations regardless of their geographic location. This is the broadest relief available under the current Supreme Court framework. It means that law-abiding adults between 18 and 20 years old across the Fifth Circuit may purchase handguns from federally licensed dealers without restriction, and that members of the Second Amendment Foundation and the Firearms Policy Coalition throughout the country are covered by the injunction's protections.

## Why I Was Right and the Critics Were Wrong

I want to be direct about what this joint filing demonstrates. The critics who claimed that the Bondi DOJ was building a gun registry were wrong. The DOJ did not ask for membership lists. The DOJ opposed the provision as soon as it came to the parties' attention. The DOJ filed a joint motion with the gun rights organizations themselves to eliminate the provision. A Department of Justice that is attempting to build a gun owner registry does not file a joint motion with gun rights groups to protect those organizations' membership from government disclosure.

The critics were operating on incomplete information, were accepting a narrative not supported by the actual record in the case, and were making accusations that were, at minimum, premature and unfair. I understand the instinct: gun owners have every reason to be vigilant about government overreach, and the specter of a government-maintained registry of gun owners is legitimately alarming. But vigilance must be informed by facts. The facts here did not support the accusations.

## Implications

The joint filing in **Ree v. ATF** demonstrates the practical value of having a DOJ that is aligned with Second Amendment principles and that is willing to work cooperatively with Second Amendment organizations to protect gun owners' rights. Rather than defending the membership list provision or seeking to exploit it, the DOJ immediately joined the plaintiffs in moving to vacate it. This cooperative relationship between the Bondi DOJ and the leading Second Amendment litigation organizations is a significant asset.

For the broader Second Amendment community, the resolution of this episode should reinforce several important points: not every alarming headline reflects what actually happened; the Bondi DOJ has an overall record that is, by historical standards, exceptionally favorable to the Second Amendment; and the appropriate response to government overreach is informed, factual pushback — not reflexive outrage based on incomplete information. The Second Amendment community is well-served by accurate analysis and poorly served by panic.
