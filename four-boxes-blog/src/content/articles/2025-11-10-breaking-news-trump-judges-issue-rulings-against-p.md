## Summary

The media consistently and misleadingly characterizes certain federal judges who rule against President Trump as "Trump appointees," as though their rulings reflect some meaningful conservative jurisprudential defection. The recent ruling by U.S. District Judge Karen Immergut — who permanently enjoined President Trump's deployment of federalized National Guard troops to Portland, Oregon — provides a useful case study in why that characterization obscures a critical and deliberately buried piece of information: the Senate blue slip process. Understanding how the blue slip works is essential to understanding why many so-called "Trump appointees" are, in reality, judges who owe their confirmation to the preferences of Democratic senators, not to the President who formally nominated them.

## Background

Judge Karen Immergut of the United States District Court for the District of Oregon is, technically speaking, a Donald Trump appointee. She was nominated by President Trump and confirmed by the United States Senate. That statement is literally true. But it tells only a fraction of the story — and the fraction the media omits is the one that actually explains her jurisprudence.

Judge Immergut's record is well known in the Second Amendment community. She is the same judge who upheld Oregon Ballot Measure 114 in its entirety, sustaining a sweeping anti-gun referendum that banned standard-capacity magazines and imposed a novel permit-to-purchase scheme, purportedly consistent with the Second Amendment. Most recently, she has issued a ruling permanently enjoining President Trump's ability to deploy federalized National Guard troops to the city of Portland — a ruling the Trump administration has indicated it will appeal.

## The Blue Slip Process

To understand how Judge Immergut — and judges like her — come to sit on the federal bench as "Trump appointees," one must understand the Senate blue slip process.

Our constitutional architecture assigns the appointment of federal judges to two branches: the President nominates, and the Senate confirms. The Founding Fathers designed Article III lifetime appointments specifically to insulate federal judges from day-to-day political pressures, requiring both presidential nomination and Senate confirmation as a check on either branch acting alone.

In practice, however, a Senate tradition known as the "blue slip" process dramatically constrains the President's effective power to select district court judges — the trial-level federal judges — in states represented by senators of the opposing party. The process works as follows: when a presidential nomination for a district court judgeship is referred to the Senate Judiciary Committee, the Committee Chairman sends a blue slip of paper to each of the two home-state senators for that particular seat. The senators may return the blue slip with an indication of approval, return it with a rejection, or — critically — simply never return it at all.

Under the traditional blue slip convention, a senator's failure to return the blue slip, or affirmative objection, effectively blocks the nomination from receiving a Judiciary Committee hearing. This practice transforms what the Constitution frames as a presidential power into one subject to an informal but operationally powerful veto by home-state senators — even senators of the opposing party.

## The Practical Consequence: Negotiated Nominees

In states with two Democratic senators — Oregon, California, New York, Maryland, New Mexico — President Trump's choices for district court judgeships must, as a practical matter, be acceptable to Democratic senators. Otherwise, those senators either withhold their blue slip or signal that they will not return it favorably, blocking any hearing.

The result is that in Democratic states, Republican presidents are functionally required to negotiate with Democratic senators to identify candidates who are acceptable to both. These compromise nominees are not the judges a Republican president would choose absent the constraint. They are, in very real terms, co-selected by Democratic senators.

Judge Karen Immergut, sitting in Oregon — a state with two Democratic senators — is a product of precisely this dynamic. She is technically a Trump appointee. She is not, in any meaningful sense, a judge selected based on President Trump's own judicial philosophy or the preferences of the conservative legal movement.

## Second Amendment Implications

Judge Immergut's willingness to uphold Oregon Ballot Measure 114 — a measure that plainly conflicts with the Supreme Court's text-and-history methodology established in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** — is not a surprise when understood through the lens of the blue slip process. Judges who emerge from blue-slip negotiations in heavily Democratic states are unlikely to be committed originalists or textualists in the Bruen mold.

This dynamic has direct consequences for Second Amendment litigation. Many of the most problematic district court rulings upholding gun control measures come from precisely these negotiated, blue-slip-constrained "Trump appointees" in Democratic states. The media's eagerness to characterize these rulings as evidence of principled judicial independence from Trump is, at minimum, deeply misleading about the process that produced these judges in the first place.

## Implications

Several practical lessons follow from a clear-eyed understanding of the blue slip process.

**First**, media characterizations of "Trump appointees" ruling against Trump should be understood in context. Before accepting the implication that such rulings represent conservative apostasy, one must ask: in what state did this judge receive her seat? Did that state have two Democratic senators? If so, the blue slip process very likely shaped who that judge is in ways that have nothing to do with the President's actual judicial preferences.

**Second**, the blue slip process is a Senate-created tradition, not a constitutional mandate. The Senate Judiciary Committee under Republican leadership can, and at times has, chosen to proceed with hearings even absent a favorable blue slip. Reforming or eliminating the blue slip tradition would substantially expand a Republican president's effective power to place originalist judges on the federal bench in blue states — which is precisely where Second Amendment litigants most need them.

**Third**, understanding these structural dynamics is essential to realistic expectations about the federal judiciary. The blue slip process ensures that the federal district courts will, in Democratic-leaning states, continue to include judges who are not reliably committed to the Bruen framework — regardless of which party controls the presidency.

The Portland National Guard litigation will proceed on appeal. But the larger lesson — about who these "Trump judges" actually are and why they rule as they do — deserves far wider understanding than the media is inclined to provide.
