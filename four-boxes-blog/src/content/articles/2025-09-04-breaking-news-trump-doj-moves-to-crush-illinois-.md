## Summary

On September 4, 2025, Harmeet Dhillon, Assistant Attorney General for the Civil Rights Division of the United States Department of Justice — herself confirmed by the United States Senate as a senior officer of the United States government — filed an unopposed motion seeking permission for the United States to participate in oral argument in **Barnett v. Raoul**, pending before the United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit. The DOJ's motion requests five minutes of oral argument time allocated from the Second Amendment plaintiffs' forty-five-minute allotment, during which the government intends to argue that Illinois's so-called assault weapons ban — encompassing prohibitions on AR-15s, other semi-automatic rifles, and magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds — is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment and the controlling precedents established by the United States Supreme Court. Oral argument in **Barnett** is scheduled for September 22, 2025.

## Background

The State of Illinois enacted the Protect Illinois Communities Act, which constitutes one of the most sweeping firearms prohibition regimes in the United States. The statute bans the possession, sale, and transfer of so-called "assault weapons," defined broadly to include AR-15s, other semi-automatic rifles and pistols sharing certain cosmetic features, and standard-capacity magazines holding more than ten rounds. The Illinois legislation followed the Supreme Court's landmark ruling in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022), which established that firearms regulations must be assessed under a text-and-history methodology rather than the interest-balancing approach that lower courts had improperly employed for years following **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008).

**Barnett v. Raoul** is the lead challenge to the Illinois statute, brought by law-abiding citizens and Second Amendment advocacy organizations. The case reached the Seventh Circuit following a district court ruling, and it represents one of the most significant assault weapons ban cases now proceeding through the federal appellate system.

## The DOJ's Legal Position

The Department of Justice, having previously submitted an amicus curiae brief in the **Barnett** case, now seeks to participate directly in oral argument — an extraordinarily rare occurrence that underscores the Trump administration's commitment to affirmative Second Amendment enforcement. The government's legal argument, which I have examined carefully, is grounded in the following propositions derived from **Heller** and **Bruen**.

First, as a threshold textual matter, semi-automatic rifles — including AR-15s — are plainly "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment. The Supreme Court in **Heller** defined arms as instruments that can be used offensively or defensively. A semi-automatic rifle unambiguously satisfies this definition. Because a ban on possessing, keeping, or bearing such arms facially infringes the "right of the people to keep and bear Arms" as that text is understood at the plain-language level, the threshold constitutional inquiry is satisfied by the plaintiffs.

Second, once the plain text of the Second Amendment is implicated, the burden shifts entirely to the government — here, the State of Illinois — to demonstrate a longstanding historical tradition of analogous firearms regulation. The government bears this burden; the plaintiff does not bear the burden of proving a historical right. This is the core holding of **Bruen**, which the Seventh Circuit is obligated to follow.

Third, Illinois cannot satisfy that burden. AR-15s and semi-automatic rifles broadly are in common use by law-abiding Americans for lawful purposes — for self-defense, sport shooting, hunting, and home protection. Tens of millions of such firearms are owned by civilians throughout the United States. Under **Heller**'s "in common use" test, arms that are in common use by law-abiding Americans for lawful purposes are constitutionally protected and may not be banned under a theory that they are "dangerous and unusual." They are neither unusual nor, in any constitutionally relevant sense, more inherently dangerous than the vast array of other constitutionally protected firearms.

## The Significance of DOJ Participation

I have written and spoken at length about the importance of the federal executive branch's posture toward Second Amendment litigation. The participation of the Trump DOJ's Civil Rights Division in **Barnett** oral argument is historically significant for reasons that deserve emphasis.

First, it is extraordinarily rare for the United States Department of Justice to appear as a participant in oral argument in a case to which it is not a party. The motion filed by Assistant Attorney General Dhillon is, in the words of those who have closely followed this area of law, something that almost never occurs in American appellate practice. When the DOJ does appear, it typically does so in cases of paramount national importance. The Trump administration has unambiguously signaled that **Barnett** — and the question of whether states may categorically ban the most popular rifle platform in America — is exactly such a case.

Second, the identity of the official arguing the government's position matters. Harmeet Dhillon leads the Civil Rights Division, the same division that enforces voting rights, housing rights, and other fundamental constitutional guarantees. Her personal appearance in defense of the Second Amendment sends a powerful institutional message: the right to keep and bear arms is a civil right, entitled to the same federal protection and enforcement energy as any other right in the Bill of Rights.

Third, the DOJ's prior amicus brief in **Barnett** has already established a detailed legal record of the government's position, and oral argument participation will allow that position to be pressed directly before the three-judge panel. The panel in **Barnett** includes judges whose prior Second Amendment jurisprudence has been problematic from a fidelity-to-**Heller** perspective. That said, the presence of the United States as a participant, combined with the unambiguous clarity of **Bruen**'s text-and-history methodology, makes the legal terrain more difficult for the Seventh Circuit to navigate away from a pro-Second Amendment result — at least without producing a manifestly reversible error that the Supreme Court would be compelled to correct.

## The Heller-Bruen Analytical Framework Applied

It bears emphasis why the DOJ's legal position is correct as a matter of constitutional law and consistent with my own scholarship on this question.

**Heller** established the threshold analytical framework in 2008: "arms" are constitutionally protected instruments; the government must demonstrate that any prohibition of a class of arms falls within a historical tradition of analogous regulation; and arms in "common use" by law-abiding Americans for lawful purposes cannot be categorically banned under the "dangerous and unusual" exception. **Bruen** in 2022 reinforced and clarified these principles by prohibiting lower courts from engaging in interest-balancing analysis or applying tiers of scrutiny. Courts must look first to text, then to history, with the burden on the government at the historical stage.

The game that anti-Second Amendment lower courts have repeatedly played — and that the DOJ is now specifically contesting — is to feign ignorance of **Heller**'s arms-definition holding and to pretend that **Bruen** somehow superseded or modified **Heller**'s definition of a protected arm. This is precisely backwards. **Bruen** reaffirmed **Heller** and made the **Heller** methodology explicit and mandatory. The only meaningful difference between **Heller** and **Bruen** in arms-ban cases is that **Bruen** added the additional reminder that courts may not engage in interest balancing — a reminder necessitated by a decade of lower-court defiance of **Heller**'s plain holding.

## Implications

The outcome of **Barnett v. Raoul** in the Seventh Circuit, and the DOJ's participation therein, carries consequences that extend well beyond Illinois.

If the Seventh Circuit upholds the Illinois ban notwithstanding the DOJ's argument and the clear command of **Heller** and **Bruen**, the resulting circuit split — given that other circuits are reaching different outcomes on comparable questions — will create the conditions for Supreme Court review. A petition for certiorari in **Barnett** or a companion case would then present the Supreme Court with the opportunity to definitively resolve the question of whether categorical bans on semi-automatic rifles are constitutional — a question that has been percolating in the lower courts since **Bruen** without definitive resolution from the High Court.

In that scenario, the DOJ's participation in the Seventh Circuit proceedings, and the legal record it has helped construct, may prove invaluable at the Supreme Court stage. The Trump administration is playing a sophisticated long game in defense of the Second Amendment, and the **Barnett** intervention is an important move in that game.
