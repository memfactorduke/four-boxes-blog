## Summary

I appeared on Fox News over the weekend to discuss the emerging revelations surrounding former President Biden's use of an autopen to execute thousands of presidential pardons and grants of clemency in the final hours of his administration. An Axios report and related disclosures have raised serious questions about whether President Biden personally authorized and knowingly signed off on the nearly 4,200 pardons reportedly executed via autopen — including, reportedly, clemency grants benefiting individuals whom DOJ officials characterized as murderers and violent criminals. The central legal question is whether these autopen pardons are constitutionally valid.

## Background

In the closing hours of the Biden administration, a remarkable number of presidential pardons and grants of clemency were executed. Reporting by Axios has disclosed that an email from Biden's chief of staff, Jeff Zients, sent less than fourteen hours before Biden was to leave office, stated: "I approve the use of the autopen for the execution of all of the following pardons." The email was signed with a casual signoff — "Thanks, JZ" — a brevity that stands in stark contrast to the gravity of the constitutional act it purported to authorize.

The disclosure raises an immediate and critical factual question: did President Biden himself personally authorize the use of the autopen, or did his staff make that decision on his behalf, without the President's genuine personal knowledge and approval?

Additional reporting has added to the concern. Fox News contributor Lydia Moahan noted that DOJ officials apparently had no advance awareness of who was receiving clemency grants under this autopen process. A record number of approximately 4,500 pardons were reportedly issued, and among the recipients were individuals whom DOJ officials described as murderers and violent criminals — not the nonviolent offenders that the administration had publicly characterized as the intended beneficiaries of the clemency program.

## The Constitutional Framework for Presidential Pardons

The pardon power is among the most personal and discretionary of all presidential authorities. Article II, Section 2 of the Constitution provides that the President "shall have Power to grant Reprieves and Pardons for Offenses against the United States, except in Cases of Impeachment." The power to pardon belongs to the President alone — it cannot be delegated to staff, and it requires the President's genuine personal exercise of his constitutional authority.

The constitutional design of the pardon power reflects a deliberate choice by the Founders. Alexander Hamilton, writing in Federalist No. 74, explained that the pardon power was vested in a single individual — the President — precisely because mercy and discretion in the enforcement of criminal law require a single responsible actor who can make individualized judgments and bear personal accountability for them. A pardon is not a bureaucratic process; it is an act of executive grace that the Constitution places in the hands of one person.

The question of autopen use for pardons is therefore more complex than the question of autopen use for, say, a routine presidential proclamation or an executive order of purely administrative character. An autopen — a device that mechanically reproduces a person's signature — is constitutionally permissible when the person whose signature it replicates has genuinely authorized its use for the specific document in question. The critical word is "genuinely."

## The Factual Question: Did Biden Actually Authorize These Pardons?

The legally dispositive question is not whether a president may ever use an autopen, but whether this president — Joe Biden — actually, knowingly, and personally authorized the use of the autopen for each of the nearly 4,500 pardons reported to have been issued in this fashion.

President Biden's public position, through his spokespeople, has been that he knew exactly what he was signing. That is his official account, and it is the account one would expect him to give. The constitutional validity of the pardons depends on its truth.

What makes the Axios reporting significant, however, is that it documents internal administration communications that suggest a degree of confusion and perhaps unauthorized action surrounding the autopen process. DOJ officials who would ordinarily be involved in the clemency process apparently had no knowledge of who was receiving pardons. The pardons were issued on an extremely compressed timeline — less than fourteen hours before the end of the administration — under circumstances that make careful individual review of each recipient's background virtually impossible.

Whether or not the pardons are ultimately valid as a constitutional matter depends on facts that may only become known through the ongoing congressional oversight investigation. The House Oversight Committee is actively working to establish what Biden knew, when he knew it, and whether his personal authorization of each pardon can be substantiated by the evidence.

## The Legal Significance of Unauthorized Staff Action

If it were established through credible evidence that Biden's staff directed the autopen process — or significant portions of it — without Biden's genuine personal knowledge and authorization for each individual grant of clemency, the legal consequences would be significant. A pardon executed by a mechanical device at the direction of staff, without the President's actual authorization, is not a valid presidential pardon. It is, at most, a piece of paper with a copied signature, possessing no constitutional force.

The test is not whether the President authorized the general use of an autopen. The test is whether the President genuinely authorized the specific exercise of the pardon power on behalf of each specific individual. The two are not the same. An email from a chief of staff approving the general use of an autopen "for the following pardons" — on a list of potentially thousands of recipients, generated in the final hours of an administration, without DOJ review — does not obviously satisfy the constitutional requirement of presidential action.

## Implications

The Biden autopen pardon controversy matters for several reasons that transcend the immediate question of any individual recipient's clemency status.

First, it implicates the rule of law. If pardons can be validly issued by staff action — by a chief of staff clicking "send" on an email at 2:00 in the morning — then the pardon power has been effectively delegated to the permanent bureaucratic apparatus rather than exercised by the elected constitutional officer in whom it is vested. That is a dangerous precedent.

Second, it raises questions about the fitness of a president who may have been so incapacitated in his final days that his staff was exercising constitutional powers on his behalf without his meaningful participation. Those questions have implications for the broader debate about the circumstances of Biden's final months in office and the internal management of his administration.

Third, and perhaps most importantly for ongoing legal proceedings, if some or all of these pardons are constitutionally invalid, the individuals who received them remain subject to federal prosecution. The Department of Justice has legitimate authority to investigate and, if the facts support it, to prosecute individuals who believed they had been pardoned but who in fact received nothing more than an unauthorized piece of paper. That investigation, and the constitutional analysis underlying it, will continue to develop.
