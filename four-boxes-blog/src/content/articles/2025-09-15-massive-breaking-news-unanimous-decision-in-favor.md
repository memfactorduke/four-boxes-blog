## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit has issued a unanimous three-to-zero decision reversing a district court's preliminary injunction and holding that President Donald Trump had full legal authority under the Immigration and Nationality Act and Article II of the Constitution to terminate the Biden administration's categorical parole program that had admitted approximately 532,000 nationals of Cuba, Haiti, Nicaragua, and Venezuela to the United States. The First Circuit's ruling — remarkable both for its unanimity and for the court from which it issued — upholds a core element of the Trump administration's immigration enforcement agenda and vindicates the administration's position that categorical, mass grants of parole under the Immigration and Nationality Act are unlawful departures from the statute's individual-determination requirement.

## Background

The Immigration and Nationality Act grants the Secretary of Homeland Security authority to parole individuals into the United States "on a case-by-case basis for urgent humanitarian reasons or significant public benefit." This statutory authority was designed to address genuinely individualized circumstances — the paradigmatic case being something like Albert Einstein-level scientific talent during wartime, or a specific individual fleeing imminent persecution with documented evidence of personal danger. The statute's structure reflects a deliberate legislative judgment that parole is an exceptional remedy, not a mechanism for mass immigration policy.

The Biden administration used this authority in a manner the statute's text does not support: it granted categorical parole to nationals of Cuba, Haiti, Nicaragua, and Venezuela based solely on their countries of origin, without making the individualized determinations that the statute requires. Approximately 532,000 individuals received parole under this program, permitting them to reside and work in the United States and, in many cases, to access public benefits. The program operated as a de facto immigration category — a form of temporary protected status or provisional lawful presence — using statutory authority that Congress had confined to individualized exercises of executive discretion.

When President Trump took office, his administration terminated the CHNV parole program, finding that it did not serve the national interest, was not predicated on the humanitarian or public benefit findings the statute requires on an individual basis, and was inconsistent with the administration's foreign policy objectives. Challengers filed suit in the United States District Court for the District of Massachusetts, which issued a preliminary injunction blocking the termination.

## The First Circuit's Analysis

The United States Court of Appeals for the First Circuit, in a unanimous panel decision, reversed the district court's injunction and upheld the Trump administration's authority to terminate the CHNV parole program. The court's reasoning proceeds from the statutory text: the Immigration and Nationality Act authorizes parole "on a case-by-case basis," which means that the required determination must be made for each individual, not for a categorical group defined by nationality.

The court found that President Trump's termination notice provided multiple legally sufficient reasons for ending the program. Specifically, the court quoted from the Trump administration's termination notice, which explained that the parole programs "did not serve a significant public benefit, were not necessary to reduce levels of immigration or illegal immigration, did not sufficiently mitigate the domestic effects of illegal immigration, were not serving their intended purposes, and were inconsistent with the administration's foreign policy goals." The notice further specified that humanitarian parole considerations are "best addressed on a case-by-case basis, taking into consideration each alien's specific circumstances."

The First Circuit agreed with this analysis. The court held that the Trump administration's reasoning independently and cumulatively supported the Department of Homeland Security's decision to terminate the program, and that the district court erred in concluding that the plaintiffs were likely to succeed on the merits of their challenge.

## The Significance of a Unanimous First Circuit Ruling

The First Circuit is headquartered in Boston, Massachusetts, and has jurisdiction over the federal courts of Massachusetts, Rhode Island, New Hampshire, Maine, and Puerto Rico. It is not a court that has historically been receptive to the constitutional and statutory arguments advanced by conservative administrations on questions touching immigration, executive power, or individual rights. A unanimous First Circuit decision in favor of the Trump administration's position on a high-profile immigration policy challenge is therefore a notable development — not because courts should be expected to rule along predictable lines, but because it demonstrates that the administration's legal position on this question is not merely the product of politically favorable judicial assignments. Three judges considered the statutory and constitutional questions and found unanimously in favor of the administration.

The constitutional dimension of the case is equally significant. Article II of the United States Constitution vests the executive power in a single President, and immigration enforcement — including the administration of parole authority — falls squarely within the executive's domain. The First Circuit's recognition that President Trump, as the constitutional executive, had full authority to cancel and revoke the Biden-era parole program is consistent with the original understanding of the executive vesting clause and with the Supreme Court's recognition that immigration is a field in which executive authority is broad and subject to limited judicial override.

## The Statutory Argument: What "Case-by-Case" Means

The most important legal holding in the First Circuit's decision concerns the meaning of the statutory phrase "case-by-case basis" in the Immigration and Nationality Act's parole provision. This phrase is not ambiguous: it requires that the executive make an individual determination for each person to whom parole is granted. It does not authorize categorical grants of parole to hundreds of thousands of people based on their country of origin, without any examination of their individual circumstances.

The Biden administration's contrary argument — that the case-by-case requirement is satisfied by having created a program under which individual applications were technically reviewed, even if the review consisted of nothing more than confirming that the applicant was a national of one of the four designated countries — is a formalistic reading that drains the statutory requirement of its substantive content. The Immigration and Nationality Act's parole provision exists to address genuine individual humanitarian needs and cases of significant public benefit. It does not exist as a general immigration bypass for nationals of entire countries.

When Congress contemplated parole authority, it designed a tool for rare use in genuinely extraordinary circumstances. The Biden administration converted that tool into a mass immigration program, admitting 532,000 people through a mechanism that was available in law only for the narrowest individualized exercises of discretion. The First Circuit's ruling correctly identifies this as a departure from the statutory authority Congress granted.

## The District Court Injunction and Judicial Overreach

The district court's decision to issue a preliminary injunction blocking the Trump administration's termination of the CHNV program reflected a judicial determination that the challengers were likely to succeed on the merits — that is, that the Trump administration probably lacked legal authority to end a program that was itself of questionable statutory validity. The First Circuit's unanimous reversal suggests that this assessment was substantially wrong, and that the district court's analysis of the executive's authority over immigration parole was incorrect.

The pattern of district courts in progressive circuits issuing sweeping injunctions against Trump administration immigration enforcement policies — and those injunctions being reversed by appellate courts — is one that has recurred throughout the administration's legal battles. The appropriate institutional response to policy disagreement is political, not judicial: courts are not authorized to substitute their policy preferences for the executive's in matters within the executive's statutory and constitutional authority. The First Circuit's unanimous reversal of the district court injunction is a modest but meaningful corrective to this pattern.

## Implications

The First Circuit's unanimous decision is significant for several reasons. As a legal matter, it establishes in the First Circuit that the Immigration and Nationality Act's parole authority does not support categorical, mass grants of parole by country of origin, and that a subsequent administration has full authority to terminate such a program. The decision vindicates the statutory interpretation that the Immigration and Nationality Act's case-by-case requirement means what it says.

As a practical matter, the decision means that the approximately 532,000 individuals who received CHNV parole status under the Biden administration's program no longer have a judicial order protecting their continued presence in the United States on that basis. Their status is now subject to the Trump administration's enforcement priorities and to whatever individualized determinations may be appropriate under a proper reading of the statute.

More broadly, the decision contributes to the body of authority establishing that the executive branch has broad authority to set immigration policy within the bounds of statute, and that courts should be reluctant to second-guess executive determinations about immigration enforcement priorities. These are the correct constitutional principles, and their affirmation by a unanimous First Circuit panel — whatever the political inclinations of its judges — is a welcome development for the rule of law.
