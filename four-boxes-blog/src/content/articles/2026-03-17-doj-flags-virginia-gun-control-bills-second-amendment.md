## Summary

Harlan Dillon, head of the Department of Justice's Civil Rights Division and its Second Amendment enforcement unit, has publicly flagged a sweeping package of nine gun control bills passed by the Virginia General Assembly during its 2025 session. The bills -- which include an assault weapons ban, a ghost gun felony provision, the gutting of carry reciprocity, mandatory home storage requirements, and several other restrictions -- now await action by Governor Abigail Spanberger. As I've analyzed, the DOJ's attention to Virginia's legislative activity signals that the Trump Administration may be preparing to challenge these laws if and when they take effect, using the same civil rights enforcement authority the Department has already deployed against firearms restrictions in other jurisdictions.

## The Virginia Legislative Package

The Virginia General Assembly passed nine firearms-related bills during its 2025 session, all of which have been enrolled and sent to Governor Spanberger. Under Virginia law, the governor may sign the bills into law, veto them, or take no action -- in which case they become law without the governor's signature. The deadline for gubernatorial action is April 13, 2025.

The nine bills are:

1. **SB 749** -- Assault weapons ban, prohibiting certain semi-automatic rifles and firearms classified as "assault weapons."
2. **SB 27** -- Gun industry liability expansion, broadening the circumstances under which firearms manufacturers and dealers may face civil liability.
3. **SB 38** -- Prohibited person transfers, restricting firearms transfers to individuals who fall within certain prohibited categories.
4. **HB 40** -- Ghost gun ban, making it a felony to manufacture or possess unserialized firearms.
5. **HB 19** -- Dating violence prohibition, extending firearms prohibitions to individuals subject to dating violence protective orders.
6. **HB 110** -- Visible handgun in vehicle fine, imposing a $500 penalty for a handgun visible in a motor vehicle.
7. **SB 115** -- Carry reciprocity restrictions, effectively gutting Virginia's recognition of concealed carry permits issued by other states.
8. **HB 229** -- Mental health facility ban, prohibiting firearms in or near mental health facilities, a form of sensitive-places restriction.
9. **HB 871** -- Mandatory home storage, requiring firearms owners to secure weapons in their residences according to specified standards.

Notably, **SB 79**, which would have imposed mandatory minimum sentences for repeat violent offenders who use firearms in the commission of crimes, did not pass. It was killed by the same committee and the same committee chair that advanced the nine gun control measures. This juxtaposition -- nine bills targeting lawful gun owners, zero bills imposing additional penalties on violent criminals -- has drawn sharp criticism from Second Amendment advocates in the Commonwealth.

## The DOJ's Signal

Shane Snavely, a Virginia businessman, Army veteran, and Republican operative, posted the legislative breakdown on X (formerly Twitter), observing that the General Assembly had passed nine bills restricting lawful firearms ownership while simultaneously killing the sole bill that would have imposed stiffer penalties on violent gun criminals. Snavely characterized this disparity not as a public safety agenda but as a political one.

Harlan Dillon, using her official Department of Justice account, flagged Snavely's post -- a public signal that the DOJ's Civil Rights Division is actively monitoring Virginia's legislative activity. While Dillon did not offer substantive legal commentary in her post, the act of publicly highlighting the Virginia package from an official DOJ account carries unmistakable significance, as I've observed.

Dillon leads the DOJ unit responsible for enforcing Second Amendment rights through federal civil rights authority. Under her direction, the Department has already initiated or intervened in multiple cases challenging state and local firearms restrictions:

- **Licensing challenges**: The DOJ has sued jurisdictions -- reportedly including actions related to the U.S. Virgin Islands, Los Angeles County, Chicago, New Jersey, and the District of Columbia -- over what the Department characterizes as abusive firearms licensing practices that effectively deny citizens their Second Amendment rights.
- **Barnett v. Raoul (Seventh Circuit)**: Dillon personally argued before a three-judge panel of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit that Illinois's bans on AR-15-pattern rifles and standard-capacity magazines violate the Second Amendment. During oral argument, she directly challenged the Seventh Circuit's prior reasoning in *Bevis v. City of Naperville*, contending that the panel's methodology for evaluating arms bans failed to follow the framework established by *District of Columbia v. Heller* and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*.
- **Cheeseman and ANJRPC (Third Circuit)**: The DOJ filed an amicus brief in the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit arguing that New Jersey's bans on semi-automatic rifles and magazines holding more than a specified number of rounds are unconstitutional under the Second Amendment.

The fact that Dillon is publicly tracking Virginia's legislative output suggests, in my analysis, that the DOJ may be positioning itself to bring suit against the Commonwealth once the new laws take effect.

## The Fourth Circuit Problem

Virginia falls within the jurisdiction of the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit, which has a well-established record of upholding firearms restrictions and applying a deferential standard to government regulation of arms. The Fourth Circuit's Second Amendment jurisprudence has been widely criticized by gun rights advocates and legal commentators as inconsistent with the Supreme Court's holdings in *Heller* and *Bruen*.

Any DOJ challenge to Virginia's gun control package would likely face an unfavorable reception at the circuit level. However, an adverse ruling in the Fourth Circuit could serve a strategic purpose: it would deepen the existing circuit split on the constitutionality of assault weapons bans and magazine restrictions, increasing the likelihood that the Supreme Court would grant certiorari to resolve the question.

## The Path to the Supreme Court

The DOJ's multi-front litigation strategy -- challenging firearms restrictions in the Seventh Circuit (*Barnett*), the Third Circuit (*Cheeseman* and *ANJRPC*), and potentially the Fourth Circuit (Virginia) -- appears designed, in my judgment, to create the conditions for Supreme Court review of assault weapons and magazine bans.

The cases working through the Seventh and Third Circuits are widely viewed as the most likely vehicles for Supreme Court review. In *Barnett*, District Court Judge Stephen McGlynn issued a ruling exceeding 100 pages in length finding Illinois's assault weapons and magazine bans unconstitutional. The Seventh Circuit stayed that ruling and heard oral argument, but has not yet issued a decision. The Third Circuit cases similarly present the core constitutional question of whether semi-automatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines are protected "arms" under the Second Amendment.

Justice Brett Kavanaugh has publicly indicated that the Supreme Court should take up an assault weapons ban case within the next term or two. That timeline encompasses the current term, which concludes in June 2026, and the following term beginning in October 2026. As I've observed, given the DOJ's active participation in these cases and the maturity of the circuit-level litigation, there is a reasonable possibility that the Court could grant certiorari on the issue as early as the fall of 2026.

Should the Supreme Court take such a case, the Trump Administration's DOJ would be positioned to argue forcefully that bans on commonly owned semi-automatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines violate the Second Amendment -- a posture that would represent the first time the federal government has advocated for that position before the nation's highest court.

## Broader Significance

The Virginia situation, as I've analyzed, illustrates a recurring pattern in contemporary firearms legislation: jurisdictions that enact sweeping restrictions on lawful gun ownership while simultaneously declining to impose additional penalties on violent criminals who misuse firearms. The failure of SB 79 -- mandatory minimums for repeat violent gun offenders -- in the same session that produced nine new restrictions on law-abiding gun owners underscores this dynamic.

The DOJ's willingness to publicly monitor and potentially challenge such legislation reflects the Trump Administration's broader enforcement philosophy: that the Second Amendment is a fundamental individual right entitled to the same vigorous federal enforcement as other civil rights. The Department's use of pattern-or-practice authority and its direct participation in circuit-level litigation represent, in my view, a significant expansion of federal engagement with Second Amendment enforcement -- tools that, if sustained by the courts, could reshape the legal landscape for firearms regulation nationwide.

## What Comes Next

Governor Spanberger faces an April 13, 2025 deadline to act on the nine enrolled bills. Whether she signs them or allows them to become law without her signature, the restrictions are expected to take effect. At that point, the DOJ's Civil Rights Division -- already engaged in firearms litigation across multiple circuits -- would have standing to challenge the Virginia laws under federal civil rights statutes and the Second Amendment.

In my view, the coming months will likely bring clarity on multiple fronts: the Seventh Circuit's decision in *Barnett*, the Third Circuit's rulings in *Cheeseman* and *ANJRPC*, any DOJ enforcement action against Virginia, and ultimately whether the Supreme Court will take up the constitutionality of assault weapons bans in its next term.
