## Summary

A federal three-judge trial panel in Texas issued a 2-1 ruling striking down the state's 2025 mid-decade congressional redistricting map, blocking Texas from using its newly drawn districts for the 2026 midterm elections. The dissent filed by Fifth Circuit Judge Jerry Smith is one of the most extraordinary and scathing judicial opinions in recent memory, accusing the majority of procedural malfeasance and rendering a decision whose primary beneficiaries, in Judge Smith's own words, are "George Soros and Gavin Newsom." The case is now on a clear trajectory to the United States Supreme Court.

## Background

Every ten years, following the decennial census, states redraw their congressional district maps to reflect population changes. The census determines how many seats in the House of Representatives each state receives, and for states with multiple districts — Texas currently holds 38 House seats, with 27 Republicans, 12 Democrats, and one vacant seat — the state legislature draws the boundary lines for those districts.

The Federal Voting Rights Act imposes significant constraints on this process. In its core application, the Act prohibits states from drawing congressional maps in a manner motivated by racial discrimination — that is, states cannot deliberately dilute the electoral influence of minority voters on the basis of race.

## The Texas Redistricting Effort

In recent cycles, Republicans and others looked across the country and observed that Democratic-controlled legislatures in states like Massachusetts, Maine, Vermont, Rhode Island, Connecticut, and New York had aggressively gerrymandered their congressional maps to eliminate Republican House representation entirely — despite the fact that 30 to 40 percent of voters in those states regularly cast ballots for Republican candidates. The result: zero Republican House members from several Northeastern states, even as a substantial minority of their constituents favor Republican representation in Washington.

Responding to this imbalance, and at the urging of President Trump, Republicans in Texas proceeded to redraw their congressional maps mid-decade — a legally permissible practice — in a manner that could yield approximately five additional Republican House seats. This was understood to be a political counterbalance to decades of Democratic gerrymandering in blue states.

## The Lawsuit and the Three-Judge Panel

A group of plaintiffs, including some Democratic members of the Texas legislature, promptly filed suit arguing that the mid-decade redistricting violated the Voting Rights Act by engaging in intentional racial discrimination against Black and Hispanic voters. Texas countered that mid-decade redistricting is inherently a political exercise, not a racial one.

Because the litigation arose under the Voting Rights Act, it was assigned to a special three-judge trial panel — a procedural feature available in certain Voting Rights Act cases under federal statute. After a trial lasting approximately eight to nine days, the panel issued a 2-1 ruling against the state of Texas. The majority found that Texas had engaged in illegal racial discrimination and entered an injunction preventing the state from using its newly drawn 2025 map for the 2026 midterms, instead forcing Texas to revert to its 2021 map — a map that would, in all probability, return those five contested seats to the Democrats.

## The Majority's Procedural Misconduct

What makes this ruling remarkable — and what elevates Judge Smith's dissent into one of the most striking judicial opinions in recent memory — is not merely the merits of the ruling itself, but the manner in which the majority conducted itself.

According to Judge Smith's dissent, the two majority judges engaged in a significant departure from standard appellate procedure. In the normal course of multi-judge panel deliberations, a majority draft opinion is circulated to all panel members before issuance, giving the dissenting judge an opportunity to review, respond, and potentially cause the majority to reconsider. Here, according to Judge Smith, the majority judges failed to timely send their final opinion to him, refused to wait for his dissent to be prepared, and did not consider whether his dissenting reasoning might persuade them to alter their analysis. They issued the ruling without allowing the customary exchange of views.

This conduct prompted Judge Smith to issue his dissent several days after the majority opinion was already published — an extraordinary circumstance that itself underscores the severity of what he viewed as the majority's procedural irregularity.

## Judge Jerry Smith's Dissent

The language Judge Smith employed in his dissent is, by the standards of federal judicial writing, nothing short of extraordinary. He opened by identifying the winners and losers of the majority's ruling with stark directness: "The main winners from Judge Brown's opinion are George Soros and Gavin Newsom. The obvious losers of this case are the people of Texas and the rule of law. I therefore dissent."

He proceeded to characterize the majority opinion authored by Judge Jeffrey Brown as "replete with legal and factual errors and accompanied by naked" — the full dissent continuing in the same vein — accusing the majority of conduct that, in Judge Smith's view, amounts to misconduct rather than mere error.

Such language from a sitting federal circuit judge directed at a colleague on the same panel is virtually unprecedented. Federal judges are not known for pulling punches in their written opinions, but the level of directness and the severity of the accusation here — that the majority effectively rigged the outcome by denying Judge Smith the customary opportunity to influence deliberation — goes well beyond what is typically seen in the federal judiciary.

## Legal and Constitutional Stakes

The underlying legal question in this case turns on the meaning and scope of the Voting Rights Act as applied to mid-decade redistricting. Texas's core argument is that mid-decade redistricting is, by its nature, a political act — and that the mere fact that political decisions may correlate with racial demographics does not transform them into unlawful racial discrimination. The plaintiffs contend that the state's motivation was racial, not merely political, and that the Voting Rights Act prohibits the latter even when disguised as the former.

This tension between partisan gerrymandering — which the Supreme Court has held to be non-justiciable as a federal constitutional matter in *Rucho v. Common Cause* (2019) — and racial gerrymandering, which remains fully cognizable under the Voting Rights Act and the Equal Protection Clause, sits at the heart of this litigation. The difficulty, as courts across the country have struggled with, is that race and political affiliation often correlate closely enough to make the distinction between the two a matter of inference rather than clear proof.

## Implications

This case is almost certainly bound for the United States Supreme Court. The stakes are substantial: five House seats in Texas, a state with 38 congressional districts, could shift the majority balance in a closely divided House of Representatives. Beyond the immediate electoral consequences, the procedural conduct described in Judge Smith's dissent raises questions about the integrity of the three-judge panel process itself.

The Supreme Court will need to address not only the merits of the Voting Rights Act claim but potentially the proper standards for distinguishing permissible partisan redistricting from impermissible racial redistricting in a mid-decade context. The Court's recent jurisprudence — including *Allen v. Milligan* (2023), in which the Court reaffirmed the viability of Section 2 Voting Rights Act claims — provides the framework, but the application of that framework to mid-decade, politically motivated redistricting remains contested and unsettled.

Judge Smith's dissent, whatever one thinks of its tone, raises legitimate questions about whether the judicial process in this case was conducted fairly. Those questions will likely follow this case all the way to One First Street.
