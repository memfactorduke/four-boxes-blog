## Summary

The United States Supreme Court, in an 8-1 decision authored by Justice Neil Gorsuch, struck down a Colorado law that restricted the speech of licensed mental health counselors. In *Charles v. Salazar*, the Court held that the First Amendment prohibits a state from conditioning professional licensure on a therapist's willingness to express only state-approved viewpoints during talk therapy sessions. Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson was the sole dissenter, arguing that the state's authority to regulate licensed professions extends to controlling the content of professional speech. The decision has significant implications not only for mental health professionals but for all licensed practitioners, including attorneys and content creators who discuss legal, medical, or policy topics online.

## Background

Kaylee Charles is a Colorado-licensed mental health counselor who provides talk therapy. Her practice does not involve prescribing medication, using medical devices, or employing any physical treatment methods. She exclusively engages in conversation-based therapeutic counseling.

Among her clients were minors experiencing same-sex attraction or gender dysphoria -- that is, individuals who felt a disconnect between their biological sex and their gender identity. In 2019, Colorado enacted a statute restricting the scope of permissible speech for licensed talk therapists treating such clients. Under the law, a licensed counselor could encourage a minor to pursue gender transition but could not counsel the minor to explore whether their existing gender identity might be appropriate or whether their feelings might reflect other underlying concerns. In short, the statute permitted therapeutic speech in one direction only.

Charles challenged the law on First Amendment grounds, arguing that Colorado was engaging in viewpoint discrimination by dictating which therapeutic perspectives she could share with her clients.

## The Majority Opinion

Justice Gorsuch, writing for an eight-Justice majority, held that the Colorado statute violated the First Amendment's prohibition on viewpoint-based speech restrictions.

The opinion began by emphasizing the foundational character of the right at issue. Justice Gorsuch wrote:

> The First Amendment stands as a shield against any effort to enforce orthodoxy in thought or speech in this country. It reflects instead a judgment that every American possesses an inalienable right to think and speak freely, and a faith in the free marketplace of ideas as the best means for discovering truth. However well-intentioned any law, any law that suppresses speech based on viewpoint represents an egregious assault on both of those commitments.

The Court then turned to the nature of the speech being regulated. The opinion observed that the spoken word is "perhaps the quintessential form of protected speech," and that Charles's professional activity consisted entirely of speaking with her clients. She did not prescribe medication, perform surgery, or employ any physical methods. The only thing the Colorado statute regulated, as applied to Charles, was what she was permitted to say.

The majority further noted that the statute was not a neutral regulation of professional conduct but rather a one-sided restriction on viewpoint. A licensed therapist could counsel a minor to pursue gender transition but could not counsel the same minor to consider alternatives to transition. This asymmetry, the Court held, constituted classic viewpoint discrimination -- the most disfavored form of content-based speech regulation under established First Amendment doctrine.

Justice Gorsuch also addressed the broader implications of the state's position:

> We have recognized as well the even greater dangers associated with regulations that discriminate based on a speaker's point of view. When the government seeks not just to restrict speech based on its subject matter, but also seeks to dictate what particular opinion or perspective individuals may express on that subject, the violation of the First Amendment is all the more blatant.

## The Dissent

Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson, writing alone in dissent, advanced a theory that would have significantly expanded state authority over licensed professionals' speech. Her position was that once an individual obtains a professional license from the state, the state's regulatory authority over that profession extends to controlling the content of speech delivered in a professional capacity.

Under this framework, a state licensing board could, at least in theory, restrict what a licensed professional says not only in direct client interactions but potentially in public commentary as well, on the rationale that the professional's speech reflects on and is incident to the regulated profession. Justice Brown Jackson argued that the regulation of professional conduct has historically encompassed the regulation of professional speech and that the majority's holding improperly elevated individual expression over legitimate state regulatory interests.

## Broader Implications for Licensed Professionals

The decision carries implications well beyond the mental health context. The principle at stake -- whether the state can condition professional licensure on adherence to approved viewpoints -- applies to any licensed profession.

**Attorneys and legal commentators.** Under the dissent's reasoning, a state bar association could theoretically restrict the public advocacy of licensed attorneys on disfavored legal or policy positions. For example, a bar could determine that public advocacy in support of the right to keep and bear arms is inconsistent with the profession's values and impose disciplinary consequences on attorneys who engage in such speech. The majority's holding forecloses that possibility by establishing that professional licensure does not operate as a waiver of First Amendment rights. As someone who has engaged in public Second Amendment advocacy, I view this protection as fundamental.

**Online content creators with professional licenses.** The decision is particularly relevant to attorneys, medical professionals, and other licensed individuals who produce legal or policy commentary on platforms such as YouTube, Rumble, and X. These individuals operate at the intersection of professional licensing and public speech. Under the dissent's framework, licensing boards could argue that online commentary constitutes professional speech subject to regulatory control. The majority rejected this theory, holding that the state may not use its licensing authority as a vehicle for viewpoint suppression. This is crucial for my own work at The Four Boxes Diner.

**Other licensed professions.** The principle extends beyond traditionally speech-intensive professions. Any individual who holds a government-issued license -- whether for legal practice, medical practice, engineering, or even building and trades -- could, under the dissent's theory, be subjected to speech restrictions as a condition of maintaining that license. The majority's opinion makes clear that the First Amendment does not permit such conditions.

## Connection to the Second Amendment

While *Charles v. Salazar* is a First Amendment case, its implications for Second Amendment advocacy are direct and substantial. The ability to advocate publicly for the right to keep and bear arms depends on the security of the right to speak freely. If state licensing authorities could silence professionals who hold disfavored views on firearms policy, the resulting chilling effect would extend well beyond the individual professional to the broader public discourse on gun rights -- and I would be directly affected.

The decision reinforces the structural interdependence of constitutional rights. The First Amendment's protection of free speech is a necessary precondition for the effective exercise and defense of the Second Amendment. Without the ability to educate, advocate, and debate in the public square, the right to keep and bear arms would be far more vulnerable to erosion through legislative and regulatory action.

As the majority opinion suggests, obtaining a government license does not require the surrender of constitutional rights -- whether those rights concern speech, arms, or any other liberty protected by the Bill of Rights.

## Conclusion

*Charles v. Salazar* represents a significant reinforcement of First Amendment protections against viewpoint-based speech regulation. The 8-1 majority decisively rejected the theory that professional licensure subjects individuals to diminished free speech rights. For licensed professionals across all fields -- and particularly for those who engage in public commentary on contested legal and policy questions -- the decision provides an important safeguard against government overreach through the licensing power.
