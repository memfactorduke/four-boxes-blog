## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit has issued its third opinion in **United States v. Peterson**, the NFA suppressor case that has now been before the same three-judge panel no fewer than three times. For the third time, the panel has gotten this case wrong. The court upheld the denial of Mr. George Peterson's motion to dismiss, erroneously concluding that the NFA's shall-issue licensing and registration scheme is presumptively constitutional under **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**. The opinion is analytically defective from its opening assumptions, and I want to explain precisely where and why.

---

*The following analysis is based on my review of the Fifth Circuit's third opinion in* United States v. Peterson*, authored by Judge Jennifer Elrod, issued December 2025.*

---

## Background

Longtime readers of this channel will recall the history of this case. George Peterson was arrested during a Biden administration-era 2022 raid. In his home closet, agents discovered an unregistered, homemade suppressor. He pled guilty to violating the National Firearms Act — which requires the registration of suppressors — but preserved his right to challenge the NFA's application to his specific circumstances on Second Amendment grounds.

This is what is known as an "as-applied" constitutional challenge. Mr. Peterson is not arguing that the NFA's suppressor registration scheme is facially unconstitutional in all applications. He is arguing that, given his particular circumstances — a law-abiding citizen with a homemade suppressor for presumptively lawful purposes — applying the NFA's criminal prohibition to him violates the Second Amendment as construed by the Supreme Court in **District of Columbia v. Heller**, **McDonald v. City of Chicago**, and **Bruen**.

That is a legitimate and, in my view, a compelling constitutional argument. It is also an argument the Fifth Circuit has now managed to reject three consecutive times, each time with new errors layered atop the prior ones.

## The History of the Case

The three-judge panel, each time written by Judge Jennifer Elrod, has lurched through this litigation in a manner that reflects poorly on the judicial process. In its first opinion, the panel held that suppressors were not "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment's text — a conclusion I considered plainly wrong. When the Trump DOJ took office and filed a brief arguing that suppressors are, in fact, "arms" protected by the Second Amendment's plain text, the panel was forced to withdraw that opinion and reissue a new one.

Before the second opinion could be finalized, a request was made for en banc reconsideration — a rehearing by all judges of the Fifth Circuit. That request was denied. The panel then issued the third opinion, which is the subject of this analysis.

## The Third Opinion's Central Error

The most significant problem with the third opinion appears on page two. The court's opening assumption reads as follows: "Assuming without deciding that the Second Amendment protects suppressors, we affirm the district court's denial of Mr. Peterson's motion to dismiss because we agree with the government that the NFA's shall-issue licensing regime is presumptively constitutional under Bruen."

This framing is analytically defective on at least three levels.

**First: The presumptive constitutionality error.** The **Bruen** framework does not operate the way the Fifth Circuit characterizes it here. Under **Bruen**, the government bears the burden of demonstrating that a challenged firearm regulation is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation. The government must affirmatively justify the restriction; the restriction does not begin from a presumption of constitutionality. The court has reversed the **Bruen** burden of proof by characterizing the NFA's scheme as "presumptively constitutional."

**Second: The shall-issue licensing analogy is false.** The Fifth Circuit characterizes the NFA's machine gun and suppressor registration scheme as a "shall-issue licensing regime" — meaning one in which any qualified applicant who pays the fee and completes the process is entitled to receive a registration stamp. This characterization is plainly wrong. After the Hughes Amendment to the Firearm Owners Protection Act of 1986, it became illegal to register any new machine guns manufactured after that date for civilian ownership. The pre-1986 registration registry is frozen; no new entries are possible. That is the opposite of a shall-issue regime. It is a regime of near-absolute prohibition for all practical purposes. The court appears to have confused the pre-1986 registration requirement (under which theoretically a person could pay a $200 tax and register a suppressor) with the post-1986 reality, which forecloses new registrations entirely.

**Third: The oral argument concession problem.** The court's opinion appears to rest in part on a claim that Peterson's counsel somehow conceded at oral argument that the NFA's registration scheme is analogous to a shall-issue licensing regime. I have read this opinion carefully, and I find the panel's characterization of that alleged concession to be an unsatisfactory basis for deciding a constitutional question of this magnitude. Even if counsel made an imprecise statement during argument, courts are not obligated to hold a party to an off-the-cuff characterization that contradicts the plain operation of the statute at issue.

## What the Fifth Circuit Appears to Be Doing

Reading between the lines of this opinion, I suspect the panel understands it is on thin ice. The opinion repeatedly emphasizes the "as-applied" and case-specific nature of its holding, while simultaneously reserving — or at least leaving open — the possibility that the NFA's suppressor requirements could be found unconstitutional in a future case presented with different facts or a different record.

This is an awkward judicial posture. The court seems unwilling to rule properly for Mr. Peterson in his specific circumstances, perhaps because of the reputational difficulty of appearing to side with a criminal defendant in a gun case, while simultaneously recognizing that its reasoning cannot survive rigorous appellate scrutiny. The result is an opinion that is neither faithful to **Bruen** nor internally consistent.

## The Path Forward

This case cries out for intervention. The most direct remedy would be a petition for certiorari to the United States Supreme Court, presenting the question of whether the NFA's suppressor registration requirements survive Second Amendment scrutiny under **Bruen** as applied to a law-abiding citizen. Alternatively, the Trump DOJ — which has already signaled support for the proposition that suppressors are constitutionally protected arms — could take affirmative steps to narrow or reform the NFA's application in appropriate circumstances.

I also note that the NFA's suppressor registration requirements are, in many respects, a mala prohibitum regulatory scheme — meaning there is nothing inherently evil or morally wrong about owning a suppressor. A suppressor does not transform a firearm into a more dangerous weapon. It reduces hearing damage. The criminalization of unregistered suppressor possession is a purely legislative creation, not a reflection of moral culpability, and courts should approach it accordingly.

## Implications

The repeated failure of the Fifth Circuit to correctly analyze the suppressor question under **Bruen** is embarrassing and consequential. The Fifth Circuit covers Texas, Louisiana, and Mississippi — jurisdictions with strong gun cultures and large populations of law-abiding firearms owners. A correctly decided opinion from this court on the suppressor question could set powerful precedent. Instead, we have three iterations of a confused and analytically defective ruling.

I expect this case will eventually reach the Supreme Court, one way or another. When it does, the record built in the Fifth Circuit — including these three defective opinions — will serve as a cautionary tale about what happens when courts approach Second Amendment cases without rigorously applying the **Bruen** framework as the Supreme Court intended.
