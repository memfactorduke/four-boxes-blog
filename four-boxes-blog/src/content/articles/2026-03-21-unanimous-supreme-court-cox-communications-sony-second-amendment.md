## Summary

The United States Supreme Court issued a unanimous 9-0 decision in *Cox Communications v. Sony Music Entertainment*, holding that a company is not liable as a copyright infringer for merely providing a service to the general public with knowledge that some users will employ it to infringe copyrights. The opinion, authored by Justice Clarence Thomas, reversed a billion-dollar Fourth Circuit verdict against Cox Communications and reaffirmed the foundational principle from *Sony Corp. of America v. Universal City Studios, Inc.* (the "Betamax case") that providers of products or services capable of substantial non-infringing uses cannot be held liable for third-party misuse. As I've analyzed, while the case arose in the copyright context, its reasoning carries significant implications for the firearms industry and Second Amendment litigation, reinforcing the legal logic underlying the Protection of Lawful Commerce in Arms Act (PLCAA).

## Background

The dispute in *Cox Communications v. Sony Music Entertainment* centered on the question of whether an internet service provider could be held liable for copyright infringement committed by its subscribers. Sony Music Entertainment and other major copyright holders identified users of Cox Communications' internet service who were illegally downloading copyrighted music and video content. Rather than pursuing the individual infringers directly, Sony sued Cox Communications on the theory that the company had continued to provide internet access to subscribers it knew were engaging in copyright infringement.

A jury in the lower court returned a verdict exceeding one billion dollars against Cox Communications. The United States Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit upheld the finding, concluding that because Cox provided internet service to known infringers, the company was itself a willful infringer. Cox Communications petitioned for certiorari, and the Supreme Court agreed to hear the case.

## The Supreme Court's Decision

Justice Clarence Thomas, writing for a unanimous Court, reversed the Fourth Circuit. The core holding is direct: "A company is not liable as a copyright infringer for merely providing a service to the general public with knowledge that it will be used by some to infringe copyrights."

The Court grounded its analysis in *Sony Corp. of America v. Universal City Studios, Inc.*, 464 U.S. 417 (1984), the landmark "Betamax" decision authored by Justice John Paul Stevens. In that case, copyright owners had sued the manufacturer and retailers of the Betamax videotape recorder, arguing that the device enabled users to record copyrighted television programming. The lower court found the manufacturer liable on the ground that the recorder was not suitable for any substantial non-infringing use. The Supreme Court reversed, concluding that because the Betamax was "capable of substantial non-infringing uses" -- such as personal time-shifting of television programs -- its sale to the general public did not constitute contributory infringement.

Applying that framework to the internet service context, Justice Thomas explained that a service is "tailored to infringement" only if it is "not capable of substantial or commercially significant non-infringing uses." Because countless people use the internet for lawful activities, Cox Communications' provision of internet access plainly satisfied the substantial-non-infringing-use standard. The fact that some subscribers misused the service to infringe copyrights did not transform Cox into an infringer.

The Court emphasized that the proper remedy for Sony and other copyright holders was to pursue the actual infringers -- the individuals who downloaded copyrighted material without authorization -- rather than to hold the service provider responsible for facilitating lawful and unlawful activity alike.

## Implications for the Second Amendment and the Firearms Industry

While *Cox Communications v. Sony* is a copyright case on its facts, the legal principle it reaffirms -- that providers of lawful products and services cannot be held liable for third-party misuse -- has direct and significant implications for Second Amendment litigation and the firearms industry, as I've observed.

For years, gun control advocates have pursued a litigation strategy aimed at holding firearms manufacturers, distributors, and retailers liable for criminal misuse of their products. The theory mirrors the one rejected in *Cox Communications*: that because a company knows its product will be misused by some purchasers, the company should bear financial responsibility for the resulting harm. Under this approach, the firearms industry would face ruinous liability not for any defect in its products or any wrongful conduct of its own, but for the independent criminal acts of third parties over whom the industry exercises no control.

The analogy between the two contexts is straightforward, in my analysis. Just as Cox Communications provides internet service that the vast majority of subscribers use for lawful purposes, firearms manufacturers produce products that the overwhelming majority of purchasers use lawfully. Just as Sony sought to hold Cox liable because some users committed copyright infringement, gun control litigants seek to hold the firearms industry liable because some purchasers commit violent crimes. And just as the Supreme Court held that providing a service with knowledge that some will misuse it does not create liability, the same logic should preclude liability for producing a lawful product with the understanding that a small fraction of purchasers will misuse it.

## The Betamax Precedent and PLCAA

The doctrinal foundation for this reasoning traces to Justice Stevens' opinion in the original Betamax case. In my law review article published in 2020 -- titled "A Judicial Teaching Point: The Lesson of the Late Justice John Paul Stevens in Sony v. Universal City Studios as a Response to Civil Lawfare" -- I've argued that the logic of *Sony v. Universal City Studios* provides a powerful framework for defending the firearms industry against politically motivated lawsuits.

The irony, as I've noted, is that Justice Stevens was no friend of the Second Amendment. He dissented in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and after his retirement publicly called for repeal of the Second Amendment. Yet in the Betamax case, Justice Stevens articulated a principle of general applicability: that lawful industries are not to blame for the third-party misuse of their products. As I wrote, "judges can articulate legal principles that are truer than they know."

Congress codified a version of this logic in the Protection of Lawful Commerce in Arms Act (PLCAA), enacted in 2005 with bipartisan support and the backing of the Department of Defense and law enforcement organizations. PLCAA generally shields firearms manufacturers and dealers from civil liability for harms resulting from the criminal or unlawful misuse of their products by third parties. The statute was a direct legislative response to the coordinated lawfare campaigns of the late 1990s and early 2000s, in which municipalities and advocacy groups filed suits designed to impose liability on the firearms industry for gun violence.

The *Cox Communications* decision reinforces the constitutional and common-law foundation upon which PLCAA rests. By unanimously reaffirming that providers of lawful products and services cannot be held vicariously liable for third-party misconduct, the Court has strengthened the doctrinal architecture that supports the firearms industry's legal defenses. Attorneys defending firearms companies against lawfare-style claims now have an additional, recent, and unanimous Supreme Court precedent to cite alongside the Betamax decision and PLCAA itself.

## Broader Significance

The principle at stake in *Cox Communications* extends well beyond copyright and firearms. If providers of lawful products and services could be held liable whenever a third party misused those products, the consequences for American commerce would be severe. Manufacturers of automobiles, kitchen knives, baseball bats, computers, and telecommunications equipment could all face liability on the theory that criminals sometimes use their products to cause harm. The Supreme Court's unanimous rejection of this theory preserves a foundational principle of tort and intellectual property law: that liability follows the wrongdoer, not the provider of a lawful instrumentality.

For Second Amendment advocates, in my view, this decision is a significant development. While it does not directly address firearms regulation, it reinforces the legal framework that protects the firearms industry from elimination through litigation -- a strategy that, if successful, would render the constitutional right to keep and bear arms a "parchment tiger," as the right would mean little if the products necessary to exercise it could not be lawfully manufactured, sold, or distributed.
