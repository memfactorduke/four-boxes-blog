## Summary

Following the Trump administration's capture and delivery of Venezuelan dictator Nicolás Maduro to face drug trafficking and conspiracy charges in a United States federal court, defense counsel immediately previewed a jurisdictional defense grounded in international law, suggesting that the manner of Maduro's apprehension — characterized by defense counsel as an unlawful military abduction — deprives American courts of jurisdiction over him. In my assessment, that argument will not succeed. The applicable legal framework is domestic criminal law, not international law; the method of apprehension does not divest a United States court of jurisdiction over a criminal defendant; and Maduro's inability to claim legitimate head-of-state status removes one of the few potentially cognizable defenses available under international legal principles.

## Background

### The Maduro Prosecution

Nicolas Maduro has been indicted in a United States federal court — the Southern District of New York — on charges including drug trafficking conspiracy. The indictment alleges that Maduro, acting in concert with other senior Venezuelan government and military officials, facilitated the transit of massive quantities of narcotics through Venezuela and into the United States, in a conspiracy that involved collaboration with the FARC, designated as a foreign terrorist organization.

Following the Trump administration's extraordinary operation to secure Maduro's physical presence in the United States, he was presented for an initial appearance in federal court. At that initial appearance, defense counsel — noted New York attorney Barry Pollock, who previously negotiated the plea agreement for Julian Assange — hinted at what will likely be the first significant legal skirmish in this prosecution: a challenge to the court's jurisdiction grounded in the alleged illegality of the manner in which Maduro was brought to the United States.

### The Defense Argument: Unlawful Abduction

Defense counsel characterized Maduro's apprehension as a "military abduction," suggesting that the United States government violated principles of international law by seizing him without extradition proceedings, without the consent of the Venezuelan government, and through the use of military force. On this theory, if the method of apprehension was unlawful under international law, the federal court should decline to exercise jurisdiction over Maduro.

## Why the Defense Argument Fails

### The Ker-Frisbie Doctrine

The threshold legal obstacle to Maduro's jurisdictional defense is the long-established Ker-Frisbie doctrine — the body of constitutional law holding that a federal court's jurisdiction over a criminal defendant is not divested by the forcible or irregular manner in which the defendant was brought before it.

The doctrine originates in **Ker v. Illinois**, 119 U.S. 436 (1886), in which the Supreme Court held that a defendant forcibly abducted from Peru and returned to the United States for prosecution could not resist jurisdiction on the ground that he was not extradited through proper legal channels. The Court's reasoning was direct: the Constitution speaks to how the government must treat defendants once they are within its jurisdiction; it does not require that defendants be lawfully obtained before jurisdiction attaches.

**Frisbie v. Collins**, 342 U.S. 519 (1952), reaffirmed and extended **Ker**, holding that the Due Process Clause of the Fifth Amendment does not require that a criminal defendant be present before the court as a result of lawful process before the court may exercise jurisdiction. Forcible seizure, even if unlawful, does not strip the court of the power to try the defendant.

In **United States v. Alvarez-Machain**, 504 U.S. 655 (1992), the Supreme Court applied **Ker-Frisbie** in the international context, holding that the forcible abduction of a Mexican national from Mexico — even in alleged violation of the United States-Mexico extradition treaty — did not deprive a federal district court of jurisdiction to try the defendant. The Court held that unless the applicable extradition treaty itself specifically prohibits forcible abductions as a remedy for its violation, the manner of apprehension does not divest the court of jurisdiction.

### There Is No International Law That Binds Domestic Courts

The more fundamental point is this: there is, strictly speaking, no binding international law that can divest a United States federal court of jurisdiction. International law operates between sovereign states; it does not, in general, create judicially enforceable rights for individual criminal defendants in domestic proceedings. A defendant cannot invoke a violation of international law as a defense to criminal charges in a United States court unless Congress has specifically incorporated that international law obligation into domestic statute, and unless the relevant statutory provision specifically creates such a defense.

Defense counsel's suggestion that the manner of Maduro's apprehension violated "international law" and therefore the federal court should decline jurisdiction conflates the international political dimension of the case with its domestic legal dimension. At its core, this is a drug conspiracy case. It is governed by domestic criminal law — the Controlled Substances Act, the federal conspiracy statute, the statutes prohibiting material support to designated foreign terrorist organizations. The question whether the United States government complied with principles of customary international law or diplomatic norms in securing Maduro's presence is a diplomatic and political question; it is not a question that American criminal law places within the jurisdiction of the district court to resolve in Maduro's favor.

### Maduro Cannot Claim Head-of-State Immunity

One potentially cognizable international law defense — head-of-state immunity — is unavailable to Maduro for a straightforward reason: he is not recognized as a legitimate head of state by the United States government, the European Union, or most of the international community. Maduro's claimed presidential authority rests on disputed 2018 and 2024 elections that were widely characterized as fraudulent by international observers, including the Biden administration. He has never been recognized as Venezuela's legitimate president by the United States.

Head-of-state immunity is a doctrine that protects recognized heads of state from the jurisdiction of foreign courts. Manuel Noriega, for example, attempted to invoke head-of-state immunity when he was prosecuted by the United States following the 1989 invasion of Panama. That claim failed because Noriega had been deposed; but even Noriega had at one point held recognized authority. Maduro, having never been recognized as legitimate, cannot invoke immunity that attaches only to recognized heads of state.

I submit that Maduro's position is therefore worse than Noriega's. He is, legally speaking, a private individual facing drug conspiracy charges — not a recognized foreign sovereign entitled to diplomatic protection.

## The Road Ahead

### Defense Strategy: Motions and Negotiations

Maduro's defense will almost certainly pursue a two-track strategy. On the legal track, counsel will file motions to dismiss based on jurisdictional grounds — the Ker-Frisbie doctrine will be the central obstacle — and may also challenge the sufficiency of the indictment, the admissibility of evidence gathered through intelligence means, and various constitutional arguments regarding the manner of prosecution.

On the parallel political and diplomatic track, defense counsel will likely attempt to communicate with the Trump administration about the possibility of a plea agreement, cooperation, or other disposition short of full trial. Maduro, and the senior Venezuelan officials who might seek to cooperate with the prosecution in exchange for leniency, represent potential sources of information of significant intelligence and law-enforcement value to the United States.

### The Evidence Challenge

Perhaps the most practically significant challenge in any prosecution of this nature is evidentiary. The government's case will rest in significant part on intelligence gathered by American agencies — the CIA and other intelligence community components — as well as on testimony from cooperating witnesses, including current and former Venezuelan government and military officials who may come to the United States government in exchange for plea agreements or immunity.

The challenge of using classified intelligence materials in a public criminal trial is well understood. The Classified Information Procedures Act (CIPA) provides a framework for managing classified evidence in criminal proceedings, but its application in a case of this magnitude — involving intelligence about a foreign government's drug trafficking operations over many years — will be complex. The government will need to carefully balance the desire for full presentation of its case against the imperative to protect sources and methods that remain valuable to national security.

## Implications

### The Message to Foreign Criminal Actors

Whatever the ultimate disposition of the Maduro case, the Trump administration's willingness to secure his physical presence in the United States sends an unmistakable message: individuals who commit crimes against the United States, however powerful or however distant, are not beyond the reach of American law. The Ker-Frisbie doctrine ensures that once such individuals are before an American court, the manner of their arrival cannot provide an escape.

### The Limits of International Law as a Defense

This episode provides a concrete illustration of the distinction between international law as a framework governing relations between sovereign states and international law as a source of enforceable rights for individual defendants in domestic proceedings. The two are fundamentally different. Defense arguments that conflate them — that suggest a federal court must decline jurisdiction because the manner of a defendant's apprehension offended diplomatic norms — are legally unpersuasive and will not succeed before courts applying settled Ker-Frisbie doctrine.

### American Criminal Law Controls

At the end of the day, as I stated in my Fox News appearance on this subject: there is no international law that will prevent Maduro from standing trial if he committed crimes that affect the United States. If you commit crimes that harm America, you can be brought to the United States and tried here. That has been American law since **Ker v. Illinois**, and it remains American law today.
