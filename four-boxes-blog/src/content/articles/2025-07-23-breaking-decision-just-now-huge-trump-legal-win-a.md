## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit has issued a significant two-to-one decision in **Core Civic v. New Jersey** written by Judge Bibas, striking down a New Jersey statute that prohibited private parties within the state from entering into contracts with the federal government to operate immigration detention facilities. The Third Circuit held that New Jersey's law is unconstitutional under the Supremacy Clause of the United States Constitution because it impermissibly interferes with the federal government's ability to enforce federal immigration law — a core function of the national government that the Constitution assigns exclusively to the federal government. The United States government filed a statement of interest and an amicus brief supporting the plaintiff, Core Civic, which operates privately run immigration and customs detention facilities under contract with the federal government. This decision is a major victory for the Trump administration and for the constitutional principle that states may not use their regulatory authority to hamstring federal immigration enforcement.

## Background

Core Civic is a private company that builds, owns, and operates immigration and customs detention facilities under contract with the federal government. As a practical matter, the federal government — specifically Immigration and Customs Enforcement — is the primary customer for Core Civic's detention services. Core Civic had operated immigration detention facilities in New Jersey for many years under a series of contracts with the federal government.

In response to the Trump administration's more aggressive immigration enforcement policies, the New Jersey legislature enacted a statute prohibiting any private party within the state from entering into a contract with any government entity — including the federal government — to operate an immigration detention facility in New Jersey. The statute's stated purpose was to prevent private companies from profiting from immigration detention. Its practical effect, of course, was to prevent the federal government from contracting with any private party in New Jersey for the operation of immigration detention facilities. When Core Civic's existing contract with the federal government expired, New Jersey's statute would have prevented the parties from renewing it.

Core Civic filed suit challenging the constitutionality of New Jersey's statute under the Supremacy Clause. The United States government filed a statement of interest and amicus brief in support of Core Civic, arguing that the statute was preempted by federal immigration law and unconstitutional under the intergovernmental immunity doctrine. The district court ruled against Core Civic. The Third Circuit reversed.

## The Supremacy Clause Framework

Article VI, Clause 2 of the United States Constitution — the Supremacy Clause — provides that the Constitution, federal laws, and federal treaties are "the supreme Law of the Land," and that state laws conflicting with federal law are preempted and without effect. The Supremacy Clause gives rise to several related but distinct doctrines through which federal law displaces conflicting state law.

One such doctrine — sometimes called the intergovernmental immunity doctrine or obstacle preemption — holds that a state law is unconstitutional under the Supremacy Clause if it interferes with the federal government's ability to perform its constitutionally assigned functions. Immigration enforcement is emphatically one such function. The power to regulate immigration, to determine who may enter and remain in the United States, and to detain and remove individuals who are present unlawfully has been recognized for well over a century as an exclusively federal power inhering in the national government's sovereign authority over the nation's borders and citizenship.

## The Third Circuit's Analysis

Judge Bibas, writing for the majority, recognized the constitutional deficiency of New Jersey's statute immediately. The majority opinion's central passage is worth examining in full:

> "At bottom, this law is an effort to hamstring the federal government's immigration power, making it as hard as possible for it to hold aliens in New Jersey. That is a big step down a slippery slope. If we accept New Jersey's logic in this case, consider what else states might be able to do. As New Jersey conceded at oral argument, under its logic, all 50 states could pass laws banning federal contractors from building weapons for the federal military. Such bans would [threaten] national defense."

The majority went on to observe that New Jersey's law did not merely decline to affirmatively assist federal immigration enforcement — a choice that states are generally free to make. Rather, New Jersey's law directly regulated the federal government's contracting relationships with private parties within the state, effectively dictating the manner in which federal immigration functions would be carried out. The majority found this distinction dispositive:

> "The national government is and must be controlled by the people without collateral interference by the states. Because New Jersey knew that it could not openly bar the federal government from contracting to detain immigrants, it instead eliminated everyone with whom the federal government might contract within its borders. It asks us not to notice the federal elephant in the room. Yet, we can see the law for what it really is — claiming the authority to dictate the manner in which the federal immigration function is carried out. Letting states do that would change totally the character of our federal system by transferring the supremacy in fact to the states, but the Constitution is supreme and New Jersey's law directly regulates the federal government, so it is unconstitutional."

This reasoning is sound and compelled by the constitutional structure. The distinction between a state's refusal to affirmatively assist federal enforcement and a state's affirmative interference with federal enforcement is constitutionally meaningful. States are not required to deploy their own resources in service of federal law; that much is settled by the anti-commandeering doctrine. But states are not permitted to use their regulatory authority as a weapon against federal enforcement functions. New Jersey's statute crossed that line.

## The Slippery Slope Argument

The majority's invocation of the slippery slope concern — that if New Jersey's statute were upheld, all fifty states could pass laws prohibiting federal contractors from building weapons for the military — is not mere rhetorical flourish. It reflects the genuine structural constitutional principle at stake. If states can prohibit private parties from contracting with the federal government in areas the state finds politically objectionable, then states effectively acquire a veto over the federal government's ability to perform its constitutional functions wherever private contractors are required.

Federal immigration enforcement, federal military procurement, federal construction projects, federal communications infrastructure — all of these areas involve federal contracting with private parties. If states can ban their residents from contracting with the federal government for any of these purposes, the federal government's ability to function is rendered contingent on the approval of fifty state legislatures. That result is incompatible with the constitutional structure created by the Framers, who gave the federal government broad authority to act directly on individuals in the exercise of its enumerated powers precisely to avoid the impotence of a federal government dependent on the states' cooperation.

## Judge Bibas and Future Supreme Court Prospects

It bears noting that Judge Bibas — the author of the majority opinion — is a former University of Pennsylvania law professor and a widely respected constitutional scholar who is regularly mentioned as a potential future Supreme Court nominee. His careful and principled application of Supremacy Clause doctrine in this case reflects the kind of analytical rigor that characterizes the best federal appellate work.

## Implications

**Core Civic v. New Jersey** is a significant win for the Trump administration on two distinct fronts. First, as a practical matter, it allows the federal government to continue contracting with Core Civic and other private operators for immigration detention facilities in New Jersey — directly supporting the administration's immigration enforcement mission. Second, as a constitutional matter, it reaffirms and clarifies the limits of state authority to interfere with federal contracting relationships and federal enforcement functions.

The decision's implications extend well beyond immigration detention. Other states with sanctuary-type policies that go beyond mere non-cooperation — specifically, those that actively prohibit private parties from assisting with federal enforcement — may find their statutes subject to constitutional challenge under the principles articulated in **Core Civic v. New Jersey**.

New Jersey may seek en banc review or certiorari, but the majority's analysis is well-grounded in established Supremacy Clause doctrine and presents no obvious error that would warrant reversal. The dissenting judge's view — that New Jersey's statute is a permissible exercise of state police power — fails to grapple seriously with the majority's central point: that New Jersey's statute does not merely decline to assist but affirmatively prevents the federal government from exercising its contracting authority within the state's borders.

The federal government's ability to enforce immigration law — and to carry out all of its other constitutional functions — depends on the constitutional principle that the Supremacy Clause means what it says. **Core Civic v. New Jersey** is a welcome and well-reasoned confirmation of that principle.
