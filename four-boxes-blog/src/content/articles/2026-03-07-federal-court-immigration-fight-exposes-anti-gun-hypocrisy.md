## Summary

Recent oral arguments before the United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit in an immigration enforcement proceeding have revealed a constitutional inconsistency that merits careful legal analysis: the same judicial philosophy that insists upon individualized determinations before restricting the liberty of persons subject to immigration enforcement categorically denies equivalent individualization to American citizens asserting Second Amendment rights. In my analysis, this asymmetry is not merely a policy disagreement — it represents an internally incoherent approach to constitutional adjudication that warrants direct examination. Understanding this tension requires scrutiny of both immigration detention doctrine and the Supreme Court's Second Amendment framework established in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022).

## Background

The Ninth Circuit argument at issue concerns the federal government's authority to detain individuals subject to immigration removal proceedings without individualized bail determinations. The prevailing legal framework prior to the current administration's enforcement posture extended substantial procedural protections — including case-by-case bail assessments — to individuals apprehended in the interior of the country, even when those individuals had entered unlawfully. Courts in the Ninth Circuit and elsewhere construed immigration statutes and the Fifth Amendment's Due Process Clause as requiring these individualized determinations before civil detention could be sustained for extended periods.

The tension I wish to highlight is not with the procedural protections themselves, viewed in isolation, but with the radical disparity between this process-intensive approach to immigration detention and the categorical, non-individualized approach the same judicial philosophy applies to Second Amendment restrictions. The constitutional asymmetry is significant, and it operates consistently in one direction.

## The Immigration Detention Framework: Individualization Required

Under the pre-2025 interpretation of federal immigration statutes, the executive branch distinguished between two categories of individuals subject to removal proceedings. Persons apprehended at or near the border were subject to mandatory detention pending resolution of asylum or refugee claims. By contrast, individuals who had entered unlawfully and subsequently settled in the interior of the country — sometimes for years — were treated differently: upon apprehension by immigration authorities, they were entitled to individualized bail determinations, potentially including low-dollar release on their own recognizance while removal proceedings remained pending.

This framework rested on a reading of the immigration statutes that courts in the Ninth Circuit endorsed. Whatever one concludes about the statutory interpretation question, the doctrinal structure reflects a strong judicial commitment to case-by-case assessment before the government may restrict an individual's liberty. The principle is familiar: categorical rules that sweep broadly without regard to individual circumstances are presumptively disfavored, at least in contexts where the judiciary has determined that individualization is constitutionally required.

## The Second Amendment Contrast: Categorical Prohibition Without Individualization

The constitutional dissonance emerges when one examines how courts applying this same philosophy have approached Second Amendment claims. As I have examined in detail, many lower courts confronted with challenges to gun control statutes have resisted individualized analysis and instead upheld categorical prohibitions. Red flag laws frequently authorize the temporary seizure of firearms based on ex parte proceedings that afford the rights-holder minimal opportunity to contest the deprivation. Magazine capacity restrictions draw bright lines — ten rounds, fifteen rounds — that apply categorically across all law-abiding citizens regardless of individual circumstances, use case, or demonstrated need. Assault weapon bans define prohibited features in sweeping terms that capture the most widely owned rifle in America without regard to whether any particular owner poses any demonstrated risk to public safety.

In my analysis, the contrast in judicial treatment is pronounced. The same constitutional tradition that requires individualized assessment before detaining a person subject to immigration enforcement is not applied with equivalent force when a law-abiding citizen seeks to exercise the fundamental right to keep and bear arms. *Bruen* and *Heller* establish that the Second Amendment protects an individual right — not a collective or contingent privilege — and that this right extends to arms in common use. Yet lower courts have frequently permitted categorical bans to stand while demanding procedural protections of the highest order in entirely different doctrinal contexts.

## The Constitutional Incoherence

The Fifth Amendment's Due Process Clause — the textual basis for requiring individualized determination in immigration proceedings — and the Second Amendment share the same document and the same drafting era. Both provisions constrain government action against individual rights-holders. The originalist methodology that the Supreme Court applied in *Heller* and *Bruen* — consulting the text, the historical context, and the founding-era understanding of the right — applies with equal force to both provisions.

What I find constitutionally significant is the directional consistency of the asymmetry. In my review of the relevant doctrine, the courts that most insist upon rigorous individualization in immigration proceedings are frequently the same courts that most readily defer to legislatures when Second Amendment rights are at stake. The *Bruen* framework was designed precisely to correct this asymmetry: the Supreme Court held explicitly that the Second Amendment "is not a second-class right, subject to an entirely different body of rules than the other Bill of Rights guarantees." *Bruen*, 597 U.S. at 70 (quoting *McDonald v. City of Chicago*, 561 U.S. 742, 780 (2010)).

## Implications

The Ninth Circuit oral arguments are significant beyond the immediate immigration question they present. They illuminate a structural inconsistency in how certain courts deploy constitutional methodology — expansively when extending procedural protections to persons subject to enforcement actions, restrictively when American citizens invoke enumerated constitutional rights. As I have argued, this inconsistency cannot be reconciled on principled constitutional grounds.

The proper resolution, in my view, is not to reduce procedural protections in immigration proceedings, but to apply equally rigorous constitutional scrutiny when the government acts to restrict the Second Amendment rights of law-abiding citizens. The *Bruen* framework accomplishes precisely this: it demands that the government bear the burden of justifying firearms restrictions by demonstrating a historical tradition of analogous regulation, rather than permitting categorical prohibitions to stand on interest-balancing alone. Consistent application of that framework — by the same courts, with the same diligence, in the same circuits — would go far toward restoring coherence to the constitutional adjudication of fundamental rights.
