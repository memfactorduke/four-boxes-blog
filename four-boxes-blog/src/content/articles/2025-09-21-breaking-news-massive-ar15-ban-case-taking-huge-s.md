## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Seventh Circuit is scheduled to hear oral argument on the merits in *Barnett v. Raoul*, a consolidated series of cases challenging Illinois's prohibition on AR-15 semi-automatic rifles and magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds. The case arises on appeal from a post-trial opinion by the Honorable Steven McGlinchy of the United States District Court for the Southern District of Illinois, who found after a full trial that Illinois's ban violates the Second Amendment. In my assessment, this is the case most likely to carry the question of whether AR-15 semi-automatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines may be banned under the Second Amendment to the United States Supreme Court.

## Background

Illinois enacted a sweeping prohibition on AR-15 semi-automatic rifles and firearm magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds — among the most aggressive bans on commonly owned firearms in any state. The law was challenged in federal court, and Judge McGlinchy of the Southern District of Illinois conducted a full bench trial and issued a comprehensive post-trial findings of fact and memorandum of law concluding that the ban is unconstitutional under the Second Amendment as interpreted by the Supreme Court in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022).

Judge McGlinchy's opinion represents one of the most thorough judicial analyses of the constitutional status of semi-automatic rifle bans. Unlike cases resolved on preliminary injunction records or summary judgment, the *Barnett* trial produced a developed evidentiary record regarding the prevalence of AR-15 rifles, the characteristics of the firearms and magazines at issue, and the historical record of analogous regulation. That record will now be scrutinized by a three-judge panel of the Seventh Circuit consisting of Judge Amy St. Eve, Judge Frank Easterbrook, and Judge Michael Brennan.

The case has previously been before the Seventh Circuit on a preliminary injunction and on a stay of the district court's judgment. The oral argument now scheduled is on the full merits of the case — the final appellate determination of whether Illinois's ban is constitutional — and whichever way the Seventh Circuit rules, the case is positioned to proceed to the Supreme Court.

## The Constitutional Framework

The Second Amendment's text provides that "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed." As the Supreme Court explained in *Heller*, the operative clause guarantees to law-abiding citizens the right to possess and use firearms for traditionally lawful purposes, including self-defense in the home. The *Heller* Court further established that the Second Amendment protects arms that are "in common use at the time" for lawful purposes, and held that the government may not ban entire categories of firearms in common use.

The *Bruen* Court subsequently clarified the analytical framework applicable to Second Amendment challenges, holding that courts must assess whether the challenged regulation is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. The burden is on the government to identify historical analogues — regulations sufficiently similar in justification and burden to the challenged restriction — from the founding era or the period of the Fourteenth Amendment's ratification. The government cannot discharge this burden through creative analogy or speculation; it must identify concrete historical precedent.

## The Common Use Analysis and Its Application to AR-15 Rifles

The constitutional analysis in *Barnett* centers on the *Heller* "common use" test and the *Bruen* historical tradition inquiry. These two standards, properly understood, establish a rigorous and largely self-executing analytical framework.

On the question of whether AR-15 semi-automatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines are "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment, the answer follows directly from *Heller*'s definition. The *Heller* Court, drawing on founding-era dictionaries including Webster's and Johnson's, defined "arms" as anything that can be used offensively or defensively. All firearms, including semi-automatic rifles, plainly satisfy this definition — they are instruments designed to be used for offensive or defensive purposes. The AR-15's classification as an "arm" under the plain text of the Second Amendment is, accordingly, not genuinely contestable.

The critical question is whether the AR-15 and standard-capacity magazines are in common use by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes. The evidence on this question is overwhelming. The AR-15 is the most popular rifle in the United States, with tens of millions in private ownership. Standard-capacity magazines — those capable of holding more than ten rounds — are the factory-configuration magazines for the most widely sold centerfire pistols and rifles in America. Under *Heller*, an arm in common use for lawful purposes cannot be banned, and the burden of proof rests on the government to establish that the arm is "dangerous and unusual" — a standard that cannot, as a logical matter, be satisfied when the arm is possessed by tens of millions of law-abiding Americans.

## The Burden of Proof Is on the Government

One of the most important principles at stake in *Barnett* — and one that bears emphasis in any serious analysis of Second Amendment litigation — is the allocation of the burden of proof. Under *Heller* and *Bruen*, it is the government that bears the burden of demonstrating that a challenged firearms regulation is constitutional. The plaintiffs need not prove their innocence; the state must prove its authority to restrict a fundamental constitutional right.

This matters practically because Illinois, in defending its ban, must establish both that AR-15 rifles and standard-capacity magazines are dangerous and unusual weapons not in common use, and that a historical tradition of analogous regulation exists. Illinois cannot carry either burden. AR-15 rifles are by definition not "unusual" given their ubiquity in American households, and there is no founding-era or Reconstruction-era tradition of banning semi-automatic rifles or their standard-configuration components — such weapons did not exist in those periods, and their modern analogues face no historical precedent for categorical prohibition.

## The Seventh Circuit Panel

The three-judge panel assigned to *Barnett* presents a significant challenge for the Second Amendment. In my assessment, the panel's composition is such that the plaintiffs are likely to lose before the Seventh Circuit. Judge Easterbrook, who styled himself as a conservative jurist, has nevertheless rendered decisions in Second Amendment cases that depart from what faithful application of *Heller* and *Bruen* would require — his record on this subject has been a disappointment to those who expected principled originalist analysis from him. Judge St. Eve has similarly demonstrated in previous cases involving the *Bruen* methodology that she is not prepared to apply the framework as the Supreme Court articulated it.

Judge Michael Brennan, a Trump appointee, has a record of faithful and rigorous Second Amendment analysis and would, in my view, be the likely dissenter if the panel rules against the plaintiffs. An adverse Seventh Circuit ruling, however, is not the end of this litigation — it is the predicate for the Supreme Court petition that will be the pivotal moment in the AR-15 ban jurisprudence.

## Why *Barnett* May Be the AR-15 Ban Case That Goes to the Supreme Court

The Supreme Court has not yet directly addressed whether bans on semi-automatic rifles or standard-capacity magazines are consistent with the Second Amendment. The circuit courts have divided on the question, with some courts upholding such bans and the Southern District of Illinois, after a full trial, finding them unconstitutional. *Barnett* is positioned, after the Seventh Circuit rules, to be the vehicle through which the Supreme Court resolves this question definitively.

Several factors make *Barnett* an especially suitable vehicle for Supreme Court review. The case was tried to judgment, producing a factual record superior to most Second Amendment cases decided on preliminary injunction or summary judgment. The district court's opinion is thorough, well-reasoned, and grounded in the *Bruen* framework. And the Department of Justice, under the Trump administration, has filed a brief in related proceedings indicating the federal government's support for the Second Amendment's protection of AR-15 rifles and standard-capacity magazines — a development that materially strengthens the constitutional case and signals to the Supreme Court that the executive branch views these weapons as protected arms.

## Implications

*Barnett v. Raoul* is, in my view, one of the most consequential Second Amendment cases proceeding through the federal courts. The outcome at the Seventh Circuit level is, for the reasons discussed, likely to be adverse. But the true significance of the case lies in what comes next. A Seventh Circuit ruling upholding Illinois's ban, following the thorough contrary analysis of the district court, will ripen the case for Supreme Court review in a manner that could produce the definitive constitutional ruling on AR-15 bans and magazine capacity restrictions.

The stakes are substantial. If the anti-gun states can establish the principle that commonly owned semi-automatic rifles may be banned, the logical extension of that principle reaches further — to handguns, to any firearm deemed politically unacceptable. The *Heller* Court explicitly warned against this slippery slope when it held that the Second Amendment protects arms in common use and that the government may not disarm law-abiding citizens of the weapons they commonly keep for self-defense. *Barnett* is, ultimately, the case that will test whether that guarantee has genuine constitutional force.
