## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Fifth Circuit — the same three-judge panel that earlier in 2025 issued a deeply flawed opinion concluding that suppressors were not protected arms under the Second Amendment — has now issued a revised opinion in **United States v. Peterson** that corrects one error while committing an entirely new and equally fundamental analytical mistake. The panel now acknowledges, as it must, that a suppressor is an "arm" within the meaning of the Second Amendment. But having arrived at the correct textual conclusion, the panel then proceeds to hold that the National Firearms Act's taxation and registration scheme governing suppressors is nonetheless constitutional — reasoning from a flawed analogy that conflates the NFA's registration and taxation structure with a "shall-issue" concealed carry licensing regime. That conflation is analytically unjustifiable and, in my view, represents a serious misapplication of the Supreme Court's framework established in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**, 597 U.S. 1 (2022).

## Background

**United States v. Peterson** concerns the criminal prosecution of George Peterson for possession of a suppressor without registration and without payment of the requisite tax under the National Firearms Act, 26 U.S.C. §§ 5801 et seq. The NFA requires, as relevant here, that suppressors be registered with the federal government, that ownership be tied to the individual by serial number and fingerprint submission, and that a $200 tax be paid. Mr. Peterson was indicted under the pre-amendment version of the NFA before Congress's recent legislative changes altered the tax provisions.

Earlier in 2025, this same Fifth Circuit panel issued a ruling that attracted widespread criticism — including from gun-law scholars and commentators across the ideological spectrum — for holding that suppressors were simply not "arms" within the meaning of the Second Amendment and therefore received no Second Amendment protection at all. That reasoning was indefensible under the Supreme Court's controlling precedent. In **District of Columbia v. Heller**, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), the Court clarified that the Second Amendment's textual reference to "arms" encompasses any instrument that facilitates armed self-defense. In **Bruen**, the Court reaffirmed and extended that framework, establishing that the government bears the burden of demonstrating that any regulation of Second Amendment-protected conduct is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation.

A suppressor self-evidently facilitates armed self-defense. By suppressing the acoustic report of a firearm, a suppressor protects the hearing of the shooter and those in proximity — a critical practical benefit in a home defense scenario. Moreover, by regulating or banning suppressors, the government effectively restricts an entire category of firearms — those capable of firing suppressed rounds — because the suppressors that attach to those firearms are themselves regulated. The argument that a suppressor is not an "arm" under the Second Amendment was always without merit.

## The Panel's Revised Analysis

Facing substantial criticism and a pending motion for rehearing, the panel revoked its prior opinion. The Trump administration's Department of Justice also weighed in, acknowledging that suppressors are arms protected by the Second Amendment — a critical concession that further undermined the panel's prior position. The panel then issued the revised opinion at issue in this analysis.

The revised opinion now assumes, for purposes of its analysis, that suppressors are protected arms under the Second Amendment. That concession brings the panel into step with both the text of the Second Amendment and controlling Supreme Court precedent. Having made that concession, however, the panel proceeds to apply the second step of the **Heller**/**Bruen** analytical framework — the historical tradition inquiry — and concludes that the NFA's taxation and registration scheme is constitutional because it is purportedly analogous to "shall-issue" licensing regimes for concealed carry that the Court in **Bruen** specifically noted deserve a presumption of constitutionality.

This reasoning is wrong, and seriously so, for the following reasons.

## The Shall-Issue Licensing Analogy Is Inapt

**Bruen** stated, in the course of its analysis, that "shall-issue" licensing regimes for the carrying of firearms in public — under which the government must issue a license to any qualifying applicant — are presumptively constitutional. The Court's reasoning was straightforward: a regime that merely requires administrative verification that a carry-permit applicant is not legally prohibited from possessing firearms before issuing a permit that allows the permit-holder to exercise the full scope of their constitutional right does not infringe that right in a constitutionally meaningful way.

The NFA is emphatically not a shall-issue licensing regime. The NFA is a taxation and registration scheme. It does not issue a permit allowing the permit-holder to exercise a constitutional right. It instead imposes a tax as a condition of possessing a constitutionally protected arm, and it requires that the individual be registered in a federal government database as the owner of that specific arm, identified by serial number, fingerprint, and personal information.

These are fundamentally different regulatory structures. A shall-issue concealed carry permit scheme says: prove you are not legally prohibited, pay a nominal administrative fee, and you may carry. The NFA says: pay $200 and submit to comprehensive registration or we will criminally prosecute you for possessing a constitutionally protected arm. The former is a reasonable administrative precondition to exercising a right; the latter is, at minimum, a substantial burden on that right requiring independent historical justification.

The Fifth Circuit panel's conflation of these two distinct regulatory structures is not merely a minor analytical imprecision. It is the kind of error that, if left uncorrected, would permit legislatures to impose confiscatory taxes and comprehensive registration requirements on any arm, on the theory that such requirements are analogous to the administrative licensing systems that Bruen's majority opinion recognized as presumptively constitutional. That result would hollow out the Second Amendment's core protection.

## The Bruen "Unprecedented Societal Concerns" Language

There is a separate analytical deficiency worth noting in the Second Circuit's recent Connecticut AR-15 ruling — a deficiency that the Fifth Circuit's Peterson panel does not replicate in precisely the same way, but which illuminates a broader methodological problem across circuit courts applying **Bruen**. In **Bruen**, the Supreme Court wrote the following sentence: "While the historical analogies here and in **Heller** are relatively simple to draw, other cases implicating unprecedented societal concerns or dramatic technological changes may require a more nuanced approach."

Lower courts inclined toward upholding gun control regulations have seized on the phrase "unprecedented societal concerns" as a free-standing license to deviate from the textual and historical methodology that **Bruen** mandates. But the full sentence forecloses that reading. The opening clause specifically says that the historical analogies "here and in **Heller**" are "relatively simple to draw" — meaning the Court has already identified and applied the relevant historical principles. The caveat about "unprecedented societal concerns" refers to other cases involving situations that the Court in **Heller** and **Bruen** had not yet confronted. It is not a general escape hatch that allows lower courts to bypass the historical tradition inquiry whenever a judge concludes that the firearms at issue are dangerous or implicated in high-profile crimes.

## Implications

The Peterson litigation remains ongoing. The panel's revised opinion, while an improvement over its predecessor in acknowledging that suppressors are constitutionally protected, remains legally vulnerable at the historical tradition step. The flawed conflation of the NFA with shall-issue licensing schemes provides a clear basis for further challenge, either through a petition for rehearing en banc before the full Fifth Circuit or through a subsequent petition for certiorari to the United States Supreme Court.

As the Supreme Court continues to accept Second Amendment cases for review, it will ultimately have occasion to clarify the precise contours of what regulatory structures qualify as analogous to founding-era firearms regulations. When that day arrives, the NFA's registration and taxation framework — which has no plausible founding-era analog — will face serious constitutional scrutiny. The Peterson case, even in its flawed revised form, advances the litigation record that will inform that eventual reckoning.

The Second Amendment community should understand that this decision, frustrating as it is, represents progress from where the panel stood earlier in 2025. The panel can no longer credibly argue that suppressors fall outside the Second Amendment's protection. The remaining dispute — whether the NFA's registration and taxation scheme survives historical tradition scrutiny — is a fight we can and should win.
