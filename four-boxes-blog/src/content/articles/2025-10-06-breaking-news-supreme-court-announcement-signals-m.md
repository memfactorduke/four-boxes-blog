## Summary

The United States Supreme Court has granted certiorari in **Wolford v. Lopez**, the case challenging Hawaii's so-called "vampire rule" — a statute that prohibits licensed concealed-carry permit holders from carrying a handgun on private property open to the public unless the property owner affirmatively provides express permission. This grant of certiorari is significant for two distinct and reinforcing reasons. First, it will resolve a genuine and important circuit split on the constitutionality of the default no-carry rule, resolving what amounts to an irreconcilable conflict between federal courts of appeals on a question that directly affects millions of armed Americans. Second, and equally significant from a jurisprudential standpoint, the Supreme Court has taken this case on an interlocutory basis — meaning the case had not yet reached final judgment in the lower courts. That choice signals a developing and potentially transformative trend: the Supreme Court's willingness to intervene in Second Amendment cases at earlier stages of litigation, rather than waiting for the full appellate process to run its course.

## Background

Hawaii enacted what Second Amendment advocates have termed the "vampire rule" — formally styled as a default no-carry prohibition — in the aftermath of the Supreme Court's decision in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen**. The statute operates as follows: even a law-abiding citizen who holds a valid, state-issued concealed-carry license is legally prohibited from carrying a firearm onto private property that is open to the general public — a grocery store, a gas station, a movie theater, a restaurant — unless the property owner or manager affirmatively grants express permission to the individual permit holder to carry on that property. No such permission is given by default. Silence means prohibition.

As a practical matter, the law functions as a near-total abolition of armed self-defense in public spaces for Hawaii residents. Property owners and managers have no particular incentive to post "firearms welcome" signage, and most would never think to do so. The result is that a licensed carry permit in Hawaii confers almost no practical right to carry a firearm anywhere a member of the public might ordinarily go.

Hawaii is not the only state to have adopted this approach. Several states enacted similar no-carry default rules following **Bruen**, attempting to use the "sensitive places" language in that decision as a vehicle for categorically prohibiting carry everywhere except explicitly designated locations. New York, California, Illinois, and others adopted variations on this theme.

## The Circuit Split

The legal conflict that prompted the Supreme Court's intervention is a genuine and intractable circuit split. The United States Court of Appeals for the Ninth Circuit — the court that has jurisdiction over Hawaii — addressed the no-carry default rule in the context of both California and Hawaii statutes. Remarkably, the Ninth Circuit reached opposite conclusions about the same legal rule depending on which state's law was before it.

When the California version of the no-carry default rule was challenged, a panel of the Ninth Circuit concluded that the rule was unconstitutional under the Second Amendment. That is the correct constitutional answer: there is no historical tradition at the Founding era of requiring law-abiding armed citizens to affirmatively solicit permission from private property owners before entering premises open to the general public. Such a requirement would have been both impractical and unknown in eighteenth-century American legal culture.

Yet when the identical legal structure was presented through Hawaii's statute in **Wolford v. Lopez**, a different Ninth Circuit panel reached the opposite conclusion, holding that Hawaii's version of the no-carry default rule was constitutional. The result was an intra-circuit split — two panels of the same court of appeals reaching incompatible constitutional conclusions on the same question of law.

The United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit also addressed a version of this question in related litigation, reaching yet another variation on the analysis. This multiplying conflict created precisely the kind of disuniformity in federal constitutional law that makes Supreme Court review appropriate and necessary.

## The Interlocutory Dimension

The particularly noteworthy aspect of the Supreme Court's cert grant in **Wolford** is its procedural posture. The case was at an interlocutory stage — specifically, it arose from the preliminary injunction phase of litigation, before any final judgment had been entered in the district court. This is significant because the Supreme Court has historically been reluctant to take cases at the preliminary injunction stage. The traditional preference has been to wait until a case has proceeded to final judgment, so that the full factual record is developed, all legal arguments have been fully briefed and argued before the lower courts, and the Court can issue a ruling with the benefit of a complete evidentiary and analytical record.

I have discussed this historical preference at length in prior analyses. The standard explanation for the Court's reluctance to take interlocutory appeals is entirely sensible as a general institutional matter: unresolved cases present undeveloped factual records and legal questions, and a Supreme Court ruling at an early stage may fail to address complications that would emerge with a more complete record.

What is significant about **Wolford** is that the Supreme Court chose to depart from that default practice. The Court granted cert despite the interlocutory posture. When I combine this grant with other procedural choices the Court has made in the current Second Amendment litigation landscape, I believe I am beginning to identify a trend — a deliberate signal from the Court that it is willing to intervene in Second Amendment cases at earlier stages when the constitutional questions are sufficiently clear and the need for resolution is sufficiently urgent.

## The Question Presented

The question the Supreme Court has agreed to decide is whether the Ninth Circuit erred in holding — in direct conflict with the Second Circuit — that Hawaii may presumptively prohibit the carry of handguns by licensed concealed-carry permit holders on private property open to the public unless the property owner affirmatively gives express permission to the handgun carrier.

That question is stated with precision, and the Supreme Court's agreement to take it marks a clear signal that the Court views the no-carry default rule as constitutionally vulnerable. When the Court grants certiorari to decide whether a lower court "erred" in a particular holding, the framing of the question itself often suggests skepticism of the lower court's conclusion. Here, the Court is asking whether the Ninth Circuit "erred" — and in my judgment, the answer the Court is likely to reach is yes, it did.

## The Role of the Trump DOJ

One factor that I believe contributed to the Supreme Court's willingness to grant certiorari in **Wolford** on an interlocutory basis was the active support of the Trump administration's Department of Justice. Solicitor General John Sauer filed an amicus brief in support of the cert petition, urging the Court to take the case and resolve the constitutional question definitively in favor of the Second Amendment. The Trump DOJ's position — that the Supreme Court should grant cert in **Wolford** and rule that the no-carry default rule is unconstitutional — provided important institutional backing for the petition. The Supreme Court takes seriously the position of the Solicitor General on questions of whether to grant certiorari, and having the United States government affirmatively support the cert petition substantially strengthened the case for granting review.

## The Broader Supreme Court Term

**Wolford** is not the only significant Second Amendment case on the Supreme Court's docket this term. I have identified five categories of cases that Second Amendment advocates should monitor closely.

First, there are the marijuana-and-Second-Amendment cases, examining whether drug users may be categorically stripped of Second Amendment rights. Second, there is the AR-15 litigation, with cases like **Duncan v. Bonta** from California and **Gator's Custom Guns v. Ferguson** from Washington State presenting the magazine capacity restriction question. Third, there are the young-adult cases, of which **Wolford** is one component and **Hamani v. United States** is another. Fourth, there is the sensitive-places litigation, of which **Wolford** is the leading case. Fifth, there are the magazine-capacity cases.

The Supreme Court's decision in **Wolford** will reverberate through all of this litigation. If the Court holds that the no-carry default rule is unconstitutional — as I believe it will — that ruling will establish important principles about the scope of permissible "sensitive place" regulations and the methodology for analyzing them under the **Bruen** framework. Those principles will then apply to the magazine cases, the AR-15 cases, and the young-adult cases as well.

## Implications

The Supreme Court's grant of certiorari in **Wolford v. Lopez** is one of the most important Second Amendment developments of the current term. For the millions of Americans who live in states that have enacted no-carry default rules, the practical stakes could not be higher. If the Court rules in favor of the Second Amendment, as I anticipate, those laws will be struck down, and licensed carry permit holders in Hawaii and every other state that has adopted similar rules will finally be able to carry in the public places where they have every right to be present.

The interlocutory nature of the grant also sends a broader message: the Court is not content to allow Second Amendment cases to languish in the lower courts for years while anti-gunners obtain preliminary rulings and impose practical burdens on the exercise of constitutional rights. The trend toward interlocutory review in the Second Amendment context is, in my judgment, our friend — and it is a trend that the Trump Department of Justice has actively encouraged and supported.
