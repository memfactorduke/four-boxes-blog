## Summary

The Donald J. Trump Department of Justice, acting through its newly created Second Amendment Section within the Civil Rights Division, has filed a landmark lawsuit in the United States District Court for the District of Columbia challenging the Metropolitan Police Department's refusal to register AR-15s and other semi-automatic rifles as required by District law. Under existing DC statutes, civilians may only lawfully possess a firearm in the District if it has been registered with the DC Metropolitan Police Department. The complaint alleges that DC's categorical prohibition on registering so-called "assault rifles" — including the AR-15, the most popular rifle in America — constitutes an unconstitutional infringement of the Second Amendment rights of law-abiding DC residents.

This lawsuit, announced jointly by Attorney General Pam Bondi and Associate Attorney General Harit Dhillon, represents the opening salvo of what the Department of Justice has described as a broader campaign to vindicate Second Amendment rights through affirmative federal litigation.

## Background

The District of Columbia has long occupied a peculiar position in Second Amendment jurisprudence. It was the District's handgun ban that gave rise to the Supreme Court's landmark decision in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008), in which the Court confirmed for the first time that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms for traditionally lawful purposes, including self-defense in the home.

Despite that defeat, DC enacted a new regulatory scheme that conditioned the lawful possession of any firearm in the District upon registration with the Metropolitan Police Department. On its face, a licensing or registration requirement for public carry might be distinguished from a prohibition on home possession — then-Judge Brett Kavanaugh made precisely that distinction in his 2011 dissent in **Heller II**. However, the DC registration scheme goes further: it categorically prohibits the registration of semi-automatic rifles, including the AR-15. Because possession without registration is unlawful under DC law, the practical effect is an absolute ban on the possession of AR-15s within the District of Columbia.

## The Complaint's Core Legal Theory

The DOJ complaint makes several interconnected arguments. At the foundation is the straightforward claim that AR-15s — semi-automatic rifles chambered in 5.56mm/.223 — are in common use by law-abiding Americans for lawful purposes, including self-defense. This is not a contested empirical question. There are approximately 20 million AR-15-style rifles in civilian circulation in the United States, and multiple Supreme Court Justices have acknowledged in opinions and dissents that semi-automatic rifles are in common use.

Most notably, in the recent case of **Mexico v. Smith & Wesson**, in which the nation of Mexico sought to hold American firearms manufacturers liable for cartel violence, essentially all of the Supreme Court Justices made passing acknowledgment that semi-automatic rifles, including the AR-15, are in common use by civilians. That concession, drawn from a context entirely divorced from a Second Amendment challenge, is legally significant: the common-use standard of **Heller** cannot be overcome when the Court's own Justices, across ideological lines, acknowledge that these weapons are commonly owned.

Under the **Bruen** framework, arms that are in common use for lawful purposes are categorically protected by the Second Amendment. The government cannot ban them unless it can demonstrate a historical tradition of analogous restrictions. No such tradition exists with respect to semi-automatic rifles in general, or the AR-15 in particular. The AR-15 did not exist in the founding era, but the constitutional inquiry asks whether the modern regulation is "relevantly similar" to historical restrictions. There is no historical tradition of banning the most popular rifle in the country based on its operating mechanism.

## The Curveball: Registration as a Legal Puzzle

One of the most nuanced aspects of this lawsuit involves the relationship between DC's registration requirement and the Second Amendment. The complaint challenges DC's refusal to register AR-15s, framing the inability to register as the proximate mechanism of the ban. This is correct as a matter of DC law — without registration, possession is unlawful.

However, there is a deeper constitutional question lurking beneath the surface. In his 2011 **Heller II** dissent, then-Judge Brett Kavanaugh argued that firearms registration requirements are themselves constitutionally suspect, because registration schemes are not tools for licensing the carrying of firearms in public — a practice with some historical support — but rather instruments that facilitate government confiscation. Registration creates a list of who owns what. That list serves no safety function if the government's only legitimate purpose is ensuring that prohibited persons are not acquiring firearms — a task the NICS background check already performs — but it is extraordinarily useful to any government seeking to locate and seize lawfully owned firearms. Under *Bruen*, there is no historical tradition of government-mandated firearms registration, and thus no constitutional basis for such requirements.

The DOJ finds itself in a somewhat paradoxical position: it is suing to compel DC to allow law-abiding residents to comply with a registration requirement that may itself be constitutionally infirm. The better victory — and the one that would produce cleaner precedent — would be a ruling both that AR-15s are protected arms that cannot be banned, and that DC's registration requirement as applied to home possession cannot be maintained. Whether the litigation ultimately reaches that full resolution, or whether DOJ takes the more incremental path of simply establishing that AR-15s must be registerable, will be important to watch as the case develops.

## Significance of DOJ Enforcement

The strategic significance of the federal government as the plaintiff in this action cannot be overstated. Private Second Amendment litigation has won important victories in the courts, but those victories have sometimes been limited by the resources and standing of private plaintiffs, and by the ability of anti-gun jurisdictions to raise procedural obstacles. When the United States Department of Justice files suit under the Second Amendment, those obstacles largely disappear. The federal government has plenary standing, near-unlimited resources, and the institutional authority that commands serious attention from the courts.

Attorney General Bondi's statement that "many more important lawsuits" are planned signals a coordinated, sustained federal campaign to vindicate Second Amendment rights through affirmative litigation — a use of the government's legal machinery that mirrors the approach taken by prior administrations in other civil rights contexts.

## Implications

The DOJ's lawsuit against DC is a significant development in Second Amendment jurisprudence for multiple reasons. If successful, it will establish that the nation's capital cannot prohibit the possession of the nation's most popular rifle by its law-abiding residents. It will create circuit-level precedent in the DC Circuit — a court whose decisions carry particular national influence — confirming that AR-15s and semi-automatic rifles cannot be banned consistent with the Second Amendment. And it will mark the first time the federal government has used its own litigation authority to vindicate Second Amendment rights in an offensive posture.

The constitutional case is strong. In my view, there is effectively no chance that a faithfully applied *Bruen* analysis would permit a ban on AR-15s. The only question is whether and when the Supreme Court will grant certiorari in an AR-15 case and deliver a definitive national ruling. The DOJ's action in this case may well be designed, at least in part, to generate the appellate record necessary to bring that question to the Supreme Court on favorable terms.

The Second Amendment litigation landscape is changing rapidly, and the executive branch of the federal government is now an active participant on the side of constitutional rights.
