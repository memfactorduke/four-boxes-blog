## Summary

I recently appeared on CBS News with anchor Major Garrett to discuss the legal dimensions of the Minneapolis shooting incident — specifically, whether individuals have a Second Amendment right to carry firearms at public protests, and where the constitutional line falls between lawful armed presence and the criminal misuse of a firearm. The conversation also addressed public statements by Trump administration officials regarding the incident and whether those statements gave legitimate cause for concern among Second Amendment advocates. In my assessment, the legal framework is clear: the right to carry arms at a public protest is constitutionally protected, but that protection does not extend to the misuse of those arms to interfere with lawful law enforcement activity.

## Background: The Minneapolis Incident and the Constitutional Framework

The incident in Minneapolis — involving an individual named Alex Prey, who was present at a protest while in possession of a firearm — prompted a wave of commentary from administration officials and raised questions about the intersection of First and Second Amendment rights. Press Secretary Caroline Levitt correctly identified the core constitutional principle: Americans possess a Second Amendment right to bring a firearm to a public protest, demonstration, or march. That right does not depend on the political character of the event or the views of those participating in it.

The constitutional foundation for this position is deep. When I appeared on CBS News, I noted that John Adams — one of our most important founding fathers — defended the British soldiers charged in the Boston Massacre trial in December 1770 and explicitly argued in court that Boston citizens had every right to be armed in public and to carry arms for defensive purposes. The right to bear arms in public spaces, for self-defense, was recognized as fundamental even before the Constitution was ratified. The Second Amendment codified what was already understood to be a natural right.

## Where the Line Falls: Possession Versus Misuse

The Second Amendment protects the lawful use of firearms. It does not protect their misuse. This distinction is not novel; it reflects a long-standing principle in American legal history that arms may be carried for self-defense but may not be used to engage in riots, affrays, or the commission of crimes.

The relevant question in the Minneapolis context was not whether Alex Prey had a right to possess a firearm at a protest — he did, assuming he was a law-abiding citizen entitled to do so. The question was whether his conduct crossed the line from lawful possession into conduct that constituted interference with law enforcement. If law enforcement officers perceived his conduct as presenting a deadly threat — as engaging in something that placed them at risk of serious bodily harm — then the rules of self-defense that apply to all persons in all states would govern how they could respond. That is not a Second Amendment question; it is a fact question that investigations are designed to answer.

I made clear in the CBS interview that these are distinct inquiries. Merely possessing a firearm at a protest — even a loaded firearm with extra magazines — is constitutionally protected conduct in the same way that carrying a sign or chanting a slogan is protected under the First Amendment. These rights are co-equal. The Second Amendment is not a second-class right. Carrying a firearm at a women's rights march, a political rally, or any other public demonstration is entirely consistent with the history and tradition of the Second Amendment, provided the firearm is carried lawfully and defensively, not deployed offensively.

## The Administration's Rhetoric: Concern or Overreaction?

Major Garrett asked me directly whether I shared the concern expressed by some in the gun rights community about statements from administration officials — including the DHS Secretary's description of Prey as a "domestic terrorist" and advisor Stephen Miller's characterization of him as a "would-be assassin" — given that, by all available video evidence, Prey's firearm never left his waistband.

My answer was no, and I explained why. I distinguish between words and actions. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent's comments about the incident reflected unfamiliarity with Second Amendment doctrine — he is a finance professional, not a constitutional attorney, and it would be similarly unfair to expect a Second Amendment lawyer to opine authoritatively on repo market mechanics. Statements made by officials outside their areas of expertise, in the heat of a breaking news cycle, carry limited weight in my analysis.

What matters are actions. The Trump administration's record of action on the Second Amendment is strongly positive: challenging gun restrictions in the District of Columbia, filing favorable amicus briefs in circuit courts against state gun bans, deploying a dedicated Second Amendment unit within the Department of Justice, and arguing in favor of Second Amendment rights before the Supreme Court itself in **Wolford v. Lopez**. A stray comment by an administration official does not erase that institutional record.

## The Role of Context and Common Sense

I did note on CBS News — and I maintain this view — that individuals who carry firearms in close proximity to law enforcement activity, particularly in volatile situations involving physical contact with officers, bear a heightened responsibility for how their conduct may be perceived. This is not a legal observation about the scope of Second Amendment rights; it is a practical observation about the real-world dynamics of armed presence in tense situations. The Second Amendment does not make one immune from the factual consequences of placing oneself in circumstances where a misperception by any party can have irreversible consequences. Prudence and constitutional rights are not mutually exclusive.

The proper framework — which I articulated in the CBS interview by analogy — is the bank example: a person may lawfully carry a firearm into a bank for self-defense. The moment that person produces the weapon, points it at tellers, and demands money, they have crossed from lawful use to criminal misuse. The Second Amendment protects the former; it provides no shelter for the latter. The same logic governs armed presence at a public protest. Presence and possession are protected. Deployment of the firearm in a manner that crosses into interference with lawful governmental activity is not.

## Implications

The Minneapolis incident and the commentary surrounding it highlight an enduring challenge for Second Amendment advocates in the media landscape: the constitutional framework for armed public protest is well-established in doctrine but poorly understood in popular discourse. Every high-profile incident involving a firearm at a protest risks generating policy pressure untethered from legal reality. Accurate, accessible public explanation of the governing principles — from both legal analysts and administration spokespersons — is essential to preventing legitimate Second Amendment rights from being eroded through rhetorical slippage.

The administration's overall posture on the Second Amendment remains strongly favorable. The appropriate response to imprecise statements by individual officials is not alarm but engagement — providing the correct legal framing and trusting that an administration with an established pro-Second Amendment institutional record will, in practice, act consistently with that record.
