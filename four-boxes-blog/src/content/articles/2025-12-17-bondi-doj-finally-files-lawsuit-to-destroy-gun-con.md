## Summary

The United States Department of Justice, under Attorney General Pam Bondi and the Trump administration, has filed suit against the Virgin Islands Police Department, challenging that territory's unconstitutional practices in the effective denial of firearms permits. This lawsuit — the second of its kind filed by the current DOJ, following an earlier action against the Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department — reveals a coherent and strategically significant litigation campaign by the federal government to vindicate Second Amendment rights by targeting the most egregious outlier licensing regimes across the country.

*Analysis by Mark W. Smith, constitutional attorney and member of the United States Supreme Court Bar.*

---

## Background

The right to keep and bear arms guaranteed by the Second Amendment is not self-executing. In practical terms, it depends on whether law enforcement agencies — sheriffs, police departments, territorial law enforcement bureaus — faithfully implement the licensing frameworks through which the right is exercised. When those agencies engage in systematic delay, stonewalling, or de facto denial of permits, the right becomes illusory regardless of what the Constitution says.

My recently published article in the Harvard Journal of Law and Public Policy, titled "Licenses Delayed, Rights Denied: How Contemporary Firearms Licensing Regimes Continue to Violate the Second Amendment," documents precisely this phenomenon. Anti-gun jurisdictions have developed an array of administrative tactics — excessive fees, interminable delays, unreasonable documentation requirements, and outright non-processing — designed to deprive law-abiding citizens of the permits to which they are entitled. The Virgin Islands is a textbook example of this pattern.

## The Virgin Islands Lawsuit

The Virgin Islands falls within the jurisdiction of the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit, which covers New Jersey, Pennsylvania, and Delaware. The DOJ's decision to bring this particular lawsuit in the Virgin Islands is therefore strategically important: a favorable precedent from the Virgin Islands District Court, affirmed by the Third Circuit, would constitute binding authority for the entire Third Circuit — including New Jersey, which has historically been one of the most aggressively anti-gun jurisdictions in the country.

The complaint alleges that the Virgin Islands Police Department has engaged in practices that effectively deny residents their constitutional right to obtain firearms permits, including the right to carry firearms in public. The specific unconstitutional practices at issue involve the VIPD's licensing regime for firearms possession and carry, which operates in a manner designed to suppress the exercise of Second Amendment rights rather than facilitate lawful permitting.

## The Pattern: A DOJ Second Amendment Campaign

This Virgin Islands lawsuit does not stand in isolation. It is the second in what appears to be a deliberate and systematic campaign by the Trump DOJ to target jurisdictions that have made a practice of nullifying Second Amendment rights through administrative obstruction.

The first such lawsuit targeted the Los Angeles County Sheriff's Department, which has a long-established practice of effectively denying carry permits to ordinary law-abiding citizens who cannot demonstrate particularized need — a requirement that the Supreme Court unanimously rejected in **Bruen**. The DOJ's decision to follow that action immediately with a second lawsuit in the Virgin Islands signals a clear enforcement philosophy: go after the most egregious outlier jurisdictions, establish precedent in favorable circuits, and use those precedents to progressively constrain anti-gun licensing practices nationwide.

Furthermore, the DOJ has not limited its Second Amendment activity to civil enforcement suits. Deputy Attorney General Harmeet Dhillon personally argued before a three-judge panel of the Seventh Circuit in the **Barnett** case, urging that Illinois's assault weapons ban and magazine capacity restrictions violate the Second Amendment. The DOJ also filed an amicus brief in consolidated New Jersey cases — **Cheeseman** and the **Association of New Jersey Rifle and Pistol Clubs** case — arguing that New Jersey's AR-15 ban is unconstitutional.

## Strategic Analysis

What we are witnessing is an executive branch approach to Second Amendment enforcement that is deliberately calibrated to the composition of the Supreme Court. Given that Chief Justice Roberts and, to a lesser extent, Justice Kavanaugh represent the swing votes on the Court's current configuration, and given the Court's demonstrated preference for carefully bounded, incremental decisions over sweeping categorical rules, the DOJ appears to have concluded that the most effective strategy is to build a record of favorable lower court decisions in egregious cases — cases involving the most blatant and unjustifiable infringements — that the Court can affirm without needing to issue a broad ruling.

The Virgin Islands presents exactly this kind of case. A jurisdiction operating in a manner that makes firearms permits functionally unavailable to ordinary residents is about as clear a Second Amendment violation as one can identify post-**Bruen**. A decision striking down the VIPD's practices would not require the Court to reach difficult questions about the scope of permissible regulations; it would simply apply **Bruen**'s text-and-history test to an obvious case and reach the obvious result.

## Implications

The DOJ's emerging pattern of Second Amendment civil enforcement litigation represents a qualitatively different and important development in the ongoing effort to vindicate the right to keep and bear arms. Rather than waiting for private plaintiffs to identify and litigate violations — a process that is expensive, slow, and dependent on the organizational resources of gun rights groups — the federal government is bringing its own enforcement power to bear directly against unconstitutional state and local practices.

This approach has several advantages. Federal civil rights litigation by the DOJ carries institutional weight that private plaintiff litigation often does not. Courts that might be skeptical of a Second Amendment argument advanced by a gun rights organization may view the same argument differently when advanced by the United States government. And a precedent established in favor of the government in a DOJ-initiated case has the same legal force as one established in a private case — it is binding on all courts within the circuit regardless of who brought it.

For residents of the Virgin Islands and, by extension, the entire Third Circuit, the DOJ's action signals that systematic denial of Second Amendment rights through licensing obstruction is no longer a cost-free enterprise. The federal government is watching, and it is prepared to litigate.
