## Summary

The United States Department of Justice has filed a motion in the United States District Court for the Northern District of Texas seeking to clarify — and effectively to narrow — the scope of an injunction entered by Judge Reed O'Connor in September 2025. That earlier ruling found that the federal statute prohibiting the possession of firearms on post office property violates the Second Amendment and enjoined its enforcement against the plaintiffs, including members of the Firearms Policy Coalition and the Second Amendment Foundation. The DOJ's motion raises important questions about the scope of injunctive relief in Second Amendment litigation and intersects with a broader pattern of the Trump administration seeking to rationalize the breadth of court-ordered injunctions across a range of legal disputes.

## Background

The federal prohibition on possessing firearms at post offices is codified at 39 C.F.R. § 232.1(l), which bans the possession of firearms on United States Postal Service property. For years, Second Amendment advocates challenged this regulation as an unconstitutional infringement of the right to keep and bear arms. That challenge was brought before Judge Reed O'Connor in the Northern District of Texas by the Firearms Policy Coalition and the Second Amendment Foundation — two of the preeminent Second Amendment litigation organizations in the country.

In September 2025, Judge O'Connor ruled decisively in favor of the plaintiffs, holding that the federal post office gun ban violated the Second Amendment as interpreted through the Supreme Court's framework in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022). Judge O'Connor entered an injunction barring the federal government from enforcing that ban against the specific plaintiffs in the case — the individual plaintiffs, the organizational plaintiffs (the Firearms Policy Coalition and the Second Amendment Foundation), and the members of those organizations.

That outcome was, in my view, entirely correct. The post office is not a historically sensitive location of the sort that the Supreme Court recognized in **District of Columbia v. Heller** (2008) as traditionally exempt from Second Amendment protection. The government has not identified a robust historical tradition, dating to the Founding, of excluding firearms from postal facilities. Under the *Bruen* test, that absence of historical analog is fatal to the regulation.

## The Scope of Organizational Standing and Injunctive Relief

Before addressing the DOJ's motion, it is important to understand a foundational principle of constitutional litigation that is directly implicated here: when an organization successfully sues on behalf of its members and obtains injunctive relief, the benefit of that relief extends not merely to the organization as an entity but to the organization's members as well. This is black-letter law.

This principle is not novel or controversial. When the NAACP successfully litigated civil rights cases in the South during the twentieth century, a victory did not benefit only the national organization — it benefited the thousands of individual members who relied on the NAACP's advocacy. The same logic applies in the Second Amendment context. The Firearms Policy Coalition and the Second Amendment Foundation exist precisely to vindicate the constitutional rights of their members. An injunction protecting those organizations necessarily protects their members. To hold otherwise would render organizational standing meaningless as a litigation tool.

## What the DOJ Is Seeking — And Why It Matters

The Department of Justice, acting through the Trump administration's legal team, has filed a motion asking Judge O'Connor to either clarify or modify the September injunction in a manner that would narrow its scope. The motion proceeds along two principal lines, neither of which I believe will succeed.

First, the DOJ appears to contend that the injunction should be limited in its geographic scope or in the class of persons it protects. Second, the motion may seek to restrict the application of the injunction to specific post office locations or to particular types of postal property rather than applying it broadly to USPS property as a class.

To understand why the DOJ filed this motion, one must appreciate the broader strategic context. The Trump administration is simultaneously fighting in dozens of federal courts across the country — in San Francisco, Los Angeles, Seattle, Chicago, Maryland, Boston, Washington, D.C., and New York — against left-wing litigants who have obtained sweeping injunctions blocking core components of the Trump policy agenda. In those cases, the administration has argued strenuously that nationwide injunctions and broad class-wide injunctions are legally and constitutionally dubious, and it has moved repeatedly to narrow or dissolve them.

The concern for Second Amendment advocates is that this legitimate legal argument — that injunctions should be no broader than necessary to remedy the demonstrated harm to the parties before the court — could, if applied mechanically in pro-Second-Amendment cases like this one, operate to hollow out victories that our community has worked hard to secure. The Second Amendment movement risks becoming collateral damage in the Trump administration's entirely reasonable effort to rein in overbroad injunctions in other contexts.

## Why the DOJ's Motion Is Unlikely to Succeed

Judge O'Connor's injunction is well-grounded in both the substantive Second Amendment analysis and in settled doctrine governing the scope of relief available to organizational plaintiffs. The law is clear that when an organization demonstrates standing on behalf of its members, the relief runs to those members.

There is no principled basis for distinguishing the Firearms Policy Coalition's members from the individual named plaintiffs for purposes of the injunction's protective scope. Both categories of plaintiffs have the same constitutional right at stake — the right to carry a firearm for self-defense without being subjected to an unconstitutional federal prosecution for visiting a post office. An injunction that protects only the named individuals while leaving hundreds of thousands of FPC and SAF members unprotected would be both legally anomalous and practically unworkable.

The DOJ's motion also faces the doctrinal headwind that injunctions in constitutional litigation have historically run with broader force precisely because constitutional violations are not susceptible to the ordinary compensatory logic of damages remedies. A person who is subjected to unconstitutional criminal prosecution for carrying at a post office cannot easily be made whole after the fact.

## Implications

The outcome of the DOJ's motion will have significant consequences for how Second Amendment organizations plan and conduct litigation going forward. If courts begin systematically narrowing the scope of injunctions to exclude organizational members — even in cases where the organization itself has standing — the incentive to bring litigation through established Second Amendment groups rather than through individual named plaintiffs will be substantially reduced.

More broadly, this episode illustrates the complexity of the current moment in Second Amendment law. We have a Trump administration that is largely supportive of gun rights and has filed strong pro-Second-Amendment briefs in important cases before the Supreme Court, yet that same administration, in its effort to combat overbroad judicial overreach in other areas of law, may inadvertently narrow the practical scope of constitutional victories our community has earned in the courts. Vigilance is required on both fronts.

The post office gun ban is unconstitutional. Judge O'Connor correctly so held. The injunction should stand in its full scope, protecting the individuals and organizations who brought this important litigation — and their members — from the burden of an unconstitutional federal prohibition.
