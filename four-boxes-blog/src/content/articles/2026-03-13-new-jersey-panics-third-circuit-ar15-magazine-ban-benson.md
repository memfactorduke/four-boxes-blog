## Summary

In my analysis, the State of New Jersey has filed an extraordinary letter brief with the United States Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit, urging the court to disregard the recent D.C. Court of Appeals decision in *Benson v. United States* -- which struck down a ban on magazines holding more than ten rounds -- as the Third Circuit prepares to rule on New Jersey's own bans on semi-automatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines. The filing, submitted in the consolidated cases of *Cheeseman* and *Association of New Jersey Rifle & Pistol Clubs*, reveals the state's deep concern that its longstanding firearms restrictions may be nearing their end. As I've examined, the letter brief contains significant logical contradictions and mischaracterizes the weight of controlling Supreme Court precedent.

## The Cases Before the Third Circuit

The Third Circuit, based in Philadelphia and exercising appellate jurisdiction over New Jersey, Delaware, and Pennsylvania, is presently considering two consolidated cases that directly challenge New Jersey's firearms restrictions. *Cheeseman v. Polillo* and *Association of New Jersey Rifle & Pistol Clubs v. Attorney General of New Jersey* collectively challenge the state's bans on AR-15-style semi-automatic rifles and on magazines capable of holding more than ten rounds. The cases were argued together several months ago, and the court's opinions are currently being drafted.

Against this backdrop, the D.C. Court of Appeals issued its decision in *Benson v. United States*, holding in a 2-1 ruling that bans on magazines holding more than ten rounds violate the Second Amendment. That decision -- a final judgment that vacated a criminal conviction -- sent New Jersey's attorneys into what can fairly be described as emergency mode.

## New Jersey's Letter Brief: The "Nonbinding Outlier" Argument

As I see it, the state's letter brief opens with a bold request: the Third Circuit should "reject the nonbinding outlier ruling" in *Benson*. The brief characterizes the D.C. Court of Appeals decision as conflicting with "every federal court to consider assault weapons and large capacity magazine laws," citing decisions from the First, Second, Fourth, Seventh, Ninth, and D.C. Circuits.

In my analysis, the argument suffers from an immediate and self-evident contradiction. New Jersey labels the *Benson* decision "nonbinding" on the Third Circuit -- which it is, as a decision from a coordinate court of appeals. But the state then invokes a series of decisions from other circuits as persuasive authority favoring its position. Those decisions are equally nonbinding on the Third Circuit. The state offers no principled reason for the Third Circuit to credit the latter group of nonbinding decisions while disregarding the former.

## The Hansen-Benson Distinction

In my analysis, one of New Jersey's central claims is that the *Benson* decision conflicts with the D.C. Circuit's decision in *Hansen v. District of Columbia*, which addressed the same District of Columbia magazine restriction and declined to enjoin it. New Jersey presents these two rulings as standing in irreconcilable tension, but a closer examination reveals a critical procedural distinction, as I see it.

*Benson* was decided on final judgment. The D.C. Court of Appeals conducted a full merits analysis and concluded that the magazine ban was unconstitutional, vacating the defendant's criminal conviction.

*Hansen*, by contrast, was decided at the preliminary injunction stage. The D.C. Circuit held only that the plaintiffs had failed to carry the heightened burden required to obtain a preliminary injunction -- an extraordinary remedy that demands, among other things, a showing of likely irreparable harm and a likelihood of success on the merits that exceeds the ordinary preponderance standard. The *Hansen* court expressly declined to resolve the constitutionality of the magazine restriction on the merits. That question remains pending in the district court.

As I've examined, because *Hansen* did not reach a final determination on the Second Amendment question, it does not stand as a merits ruling in conflict with *Benson*. New Jersey's attempt to place the two decisions on equal footing as a matter of legal authority is therefore unavailing.

## Why "Outlier" Status Is Irrelevant Under Supreme Court Precedent

In my view, New Jersey's strategy of labeling *Benson* an "outlier" reflects a broader misunderstanding of how constitutional law develops. The history of Second Amendment jurisprudence demonstrates that the Supreme Court has repeatedly sided with positions that were once considered outlier views among the lower courts, as I've documented.

Prior to the Supreme Court's 2008 decision in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, the overwhelming majority of federal circuit courts held that the Second Amendment protected only a "collective right" tied to militia service -- not an individual right to keep and bear arms. The few courts that recognized an individual right were the outliers. The Supreme Court nonetheless adopted the outlier position and held, through Justice Scalia's majority opinion, that the Second Amendment protects an individual right.

The pattern repeated itself in 2022. Before *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, most federal circuits held that there was no Second Amendment right to carry firearms in public for self-defense. The Supreme Court swept aside that majority view and held that the right to "bear" arms encompasses carrying firearms outside the home.

As I've explained, the lesson is clear: the Supreme Court decides constitutional questions based on the text, history, and tradition of the provision at issue -- not based on a headcount of lower court decisions.

## The Controlling Legal Framework: Heller's Common Use Test

In my analysis, perhaps the most significant weakness in New Jersey's letter brief is its failure to engage with the controlling Supreme Court precedent on arms bans. The state frames its argument primarily around *Bruen* and *United States v. Rahimi*, but neither of those decisions established the governing standard for determining whether a particular arm may be banned.

That standard comes from *Heller* itself. In *Heller*, the Supreme Court surveyed the entire history of arms regulations in America and identified only one historical tradition that could justify a categorical ban on a type of arm: the longstanding prohibition on weapons that are both "dangerous and unusual." The conjunctive phrasing is essential. An arm must be both dangerous *and* unusual to fall within the scope of permissible regulation.

As I've argued, the Supreme Court then applied this framework to handguns. Regardless of why individual Americans chose to purchase handguns, the Court observed, the reality was that handguns were the most popular weapon chosen by Americans for lawful purposes, including self-defense. Because handguns were in common use, they could not be considered "unusual," and therefore could not satisfy the "dangerous and unusual" standard -- even if one assumed they were "dangerous." The ban was unconstitutional.

The same logic applies with equal force to AR-15-style rifles and magazines holding more than ten rounds. In my view, there are tens of millions of AR-15-pattern rifles in civilian hands and hundreds of millions of standard-capacity magazines in circulation. These figures dwarf, by orders of magnitude, many categories of common consumer goods that no one would dispute are in widespread use in American life.

## New Jersey's Alternative Framework Fails

As I see it, in place of the "dangerous and unusual" standard, New Jersey proposes that states may regulate "unusually dangerous weapons unsuitable for and disproportionate to the objective of individual self-defense." This formulation conspicuously omits the word "unusual" as a standalone requirement. It attempts to replace the Supreme Court's conjunctive test -- dangerous *and* unusual -- with a unilateral dangerousness inquiry that has no basis in *Heller*.

In my analysis, New Jersey also invokes historical restrictions on Bowie knives and slung shots as evidence that governments historically regulated weapons after their "proliferation caused grave harm." But the Bowie knife restrictions were limited to a handful of southern jurisdictions that prohibited only the *concealed carry* of such weapons. A few regional outlier statutes do not establish a national historical tradition of firearms regulation sufficient to justify a categorical ban under *Bruen*'s framework.

The state further characterizes the common use test as "circular," arguing that popularity alone should not determine constitutional protection. But as I've argued, the test is not circular. Even if an arm is not in common use, it still cannot be banned unless it is "unusually dangerous." Common use is relevant because it demonstrates that a weapon is not unusual. The American people -- not the government -- decide what arms they wish to keep and bear. The Constitution enshrines that choice.

## The Strategic Dilemma Facing New Jersey

Should the Third Circuit rule against New Jersey, Attorney General Jennifer Davenport will face a consequential strategic decision. She may petition the Supreme Court for certiorari, but doing so carries an enormous risk: a Supreme Court ruling affirming the Third Circuit's decision would establish a nationwide precedent invalidating assault weapons bans and magazine restrictions in every state. Alternatively, Davenport could decline to seek further review, confining the loss to the Third Circuit's jurisdiction and allowing AR-15s and standard-capacity magazines to become legal in New Jersey without creating broader precedent.

## Looking Ahead

In my analysis, the Third Circuit's forthcoming decision in *Cheeseman* and *Association of New Jersey Rifle & Pistol Clubs* may represent the most significant federal appellate ruling on assault weapons and magazine bans since *Heller*. New Jersey's letter brief -- with its internal contradictions, its conflation of preliminary injunction rulings with final judgments, and its departure from controlling Supreme Court precedent -- is unlikely to alter the court's analysis. The burden rests squarely on the state to demonstrate, through historical evidence, that semi-automatic rifles and standard-capacity magazines satisfy the "dangerous and unusual" standard. That is a burden New Jersey appears unable to meet, as I see it.
