## Summary

On September 3, 2025, the United States Court of Appeals for the District of Columbia Circuit issued a 2-to-1 decision in **Climate United Fund v. Citibank** vacating a district court preliminary injunction that had purported to require the Trump administration's Environmental Protection Agency to continue disbursing approximately $16 billion in climate-related grants to a collection of nonprofit organizations. The grants at issue were established under the Biden administration's Inflation Reduction Act and had been placed in escrow accounts at Citibank for eventual distribution to the plaintiff NGOs. The DC Circuit's ruling represents a significant legal victory for the Trump administration's exercise of executive authority over federal grant programs and reinforces the critical principle that parties seeking money damages from the federal government must pursue their claims in the United States Court of Federal Claims under the Tucker Act — not through injunctive relief obtained from amenable district court judges.

## Background

The Inflation Reduction Act, enacted during the Biden administration, authorized the Environmental Protection Agency to administer substantial grant programs directed at climate-related initiatives. Pursuant to this authority, the Biden EPA entered into agreements with approximately eight nonprofit organizations — non-governmental organizations, or NGOs — designating billions of dollars in grant funding to be held in escrow at Citibank and distributed to those organizations over time.

A significant portion of these grant agreements were finalized and executed in the final months of 2024, in the period immediately preceding the inauguration of President Donald Trump. Upon assuming office, the Trump administration's EPA moved to terminate these grant agreements, concluding that the disbursement of billions of taxpayer dollars to politically aligned NGO entities was not consistent with the new administration's policy priorities and, in some cases, raised serious questions about the legality and propriety of the underlying grant processes.

The plaintiff NGOs responded by filing suit in the United States District Court for the District of Columbia, seeking a preliminary injunction requiring the Trump administration to continue releasing grant funds. The district court — applying what has become a disturbingly predictable pattern in the DC district courts — entered the requested injunction, directing the EPA to resume disbursements.

## The Tucker Act Jurisdictional Question

The DC Circuit's decision to vacate the preliminary injunction turns in significant part on a foundational principle of federal sovereign immunity and jurisdiction that I have addressed on numerous prior occasions: the Tucker Act.

The Tucker Act, codified at 28 U.S.C. § 1491, establishes that when a party seeks to recover money from the United States government based on a claim that the government violated an agreement or obligation — that is, when the claim sounds in contract or quasi-contract — the exclusive forum for that claim is the United States Court of Federal Claims, not a federal district court. The Tucker Act waives the federal government's sovereign immunity for such claims, but only in the specialized tribunal Congress created for that purpose.

The plaintiffs in **Climate United Fund** were, at bottom, seeking money — specifically, the grant funds they claimed the Biden administration had obligated the federal government to pay. Their theory was unambiguously contractual: we had an agreement with the federal government, that agreement created an obligation to disburse funds, and the Trump administration breached that agreement by terminating it. This is the paradigmatic Tucker Act claim. The proper venue for such a claim is the Court of Federal Claims, which is a specialized court staffed by judges who evaluate government contract disputes on the merits, with both parties on equal footing.

Instead, these NGOs chose to file in the DC district court — a forum that, as a matter of observable reality, has shown a consistent willingness to enter sweeping injunctions against the Trump administration. The DC Circuit, by a 2-to-1 margin, correctly identified this jurisdictional defect and vacated the improperly issued injunction.

## The Preliminary Injunction Standard

The DC Circuit's analysis also applied the standard for preliminary injunctive relief, which requires the moving party to demonstrate a likelihood of success on the merits, a likelihood of irreparable harm absent injunctive relief, that the balance of equities favors injunction, and that the public interest supports the requested relief. On each of these factors, the plaintiffs' position was legally deficient.

Most fundamentally, because the plaintiffs' claims sound in contract and must be pursued in the Court of Federal Claims, there is no likelihood of success on the merits in the district court — the district court lacks jurisdiction. A court without jurisdiction cannot be a proper forum for injunctive relief. The DC Circuit's application of this principle was legally correct and should have been obvious to the district court that originally entered the injunction.

## The Broader Pattern of Injunctive Relief Against the Trump Administration

This decision reflects a broader phenomenon that has characterized the legal challenges to the Trump administration's policy agenda: a pattern of plaintiffs filing in favorable district court jurisdictions, obtaining broad preliminary injunctions from sympathetic judges, and using those injunctions to effectively nullify executive branch policy decisions pending years of litigation. The DC Circuit's ruling in **Climate United Fund** represents a meaningful corrective to this pattern, at least with respect to claims that properly sound in contract.

The message the DC Circuit sends is important: parties who claim the federal government owes them money must go to the Court of Federal Claims to make that argument. They may not use the device of a preliminary injunction in a politically favorable district court to compel the executive branch to continue disbursing funds during litigation. The Court of Federal Claims, as the DC Circuit implicitly recognizes, is a less hospitable forum for parties seeking to leverage injunctive relief as a substitute for winning on the merits.

## The Relationship to Second Amendment Jurisprudence

I have consistently explained to my audience that developments in constitutional and administrative law that do not directly involve the Second Amendment can nonetheless carry significant implications for the protection of all constitutional rights, including the right to keep and bear arms.

The Tucker Act jurisdictional principle vindicated in **Climate United Fund** is a double-edged sword of relevance to Second Amendment litigants. On the one hand, the principle that money claims against the federal government must proceed in the Court of Federal Claims limits the ability of political opponents to use creative forum-shopping to impose injunctions against executive branch firearms policy. On the other hand, the DC Circuit's willingness to police injunction-shopping — to insist that courts respect jurisdictional boundaries even when a sympathetic judge has already acted — reinforces the rule of law as a principle applicable across ideological divides.

## Implications

The DC Circuit's decision has several practical consequences.

First, the $16 billion in grant funds previously subject to the district court injunction is no longer under a judicial order requiring disbursement. The Trump administration's EPA is now free to proceed with its termination of the grant agreements, subject to whatever claims the NGOs may bring in the Court of Federal Claims — the proper forum for their contractual dispute.

Second, the decision puts other grantees and NGOs on notice that injunctive relief in the DC district court is not an available shortcut when the underlying claim is money damages for alleged breach of a federal grant agreement. Future plaintiffs who attempt to replicate the district court injunction strategy in **Climate United Fund** face a more difficult path in the DC Circuit.

Third, the 2-to-1 decision preserves an appellate record that, depending on whether the case proceeds further, may invite en banc review or additional Supreme Court attention on questions involving the Tucker Act and the appropriate scope of district court jurisdiction over claims against the executive branch.
