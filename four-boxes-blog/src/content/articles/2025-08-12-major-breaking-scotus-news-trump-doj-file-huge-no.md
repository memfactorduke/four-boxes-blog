## Summary

On August 12, 2025, the Office of the Solicitor General of the United States, acting under the direction of Attorney General Pam Bondi and President Donald J. Trump, filed a significant legal brief in the United States Supreme Court in **Vincent v. Bondi**, addressing the constitutionality of 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1) — the federal statute commonly referred to as the felon-in-possession prohibition — as applied to individuals convicted of nonviolent offenses. The filing articulates the Trump administration's position on the deeply contested legal question of whether the federal government may permanently disarm individuals who were convicted of nonviolent felony-level offenses years or decades in the past, with no history of violence and no individualized finding that they pose a danger to themselves or others. The brief is legally sophisticated and, while it does not propose the wholesale invalidation of § 922(g)(1), charts a path toward narrowing the statute's application in a manner consistent with the *Bruen* text, history, and tradition framework. Critically, the brief also addresses Attorney General Bondi's recent creation of a Restoration of Rights process under existing federal law, which provides a procedural mechanism for qualifying individuals to regain their Second Amendment rights.

## Background

Title 18, United States Code, Section 922(g)(1) prohibits any person who has been "convicted in any court of, a crime punishable by imprisonment for a term exceeding one year" from possessing a firearm. As interpreted and applied by the federal government, this statute operates as a permanent, lifetime disarmament — once a person falls within the statutory definition, no expiration date attaches and no mechanism for reinstatement exists under federal law unless separate relief is sought.

The critical ambiguity in the statute lies in the phrase "crime punishable by imprisonment for a term exceeding one year." Federal law defines this to encompass any offense under state or federal law carrying a maximum possible sentence exceeding one year, regardless of the sentence actually imposed. This definition produces results that are, on their face, difficult to reconcile with the founding-era understanding of the right to keep and bear arms. A person convicted of a minor, nonviolent misdemeanor under state law — a crime for which no prison time was served, no probation was imposed, and no finding of dangerousness was made — can be permanently disarmed for life if the relevant state statute carries a theoretical maximum sentence of more than one year.

**Range v. Garland** — in which the Third Circuit en banc held that Brian Range, who had been convicted of a minor welfare-related fraud offense thirty years prior, could not be constitutionally disarmed — illustrated this dynamic with particular clarity. Range never spent a day in prison, posed no demonstrated danger to anyone, and had lived as a productive member of society for decades. Yet the federal government sought to deny him the right to keep a firearm based on a decades-old, nonviolent conviction.

**Vincent v. Bondi** involves a similar factual profile. Melinda Vincent was convicted of essentially a pharmaceutical bank fraud offense — writing a bad check for a small amount of money. She pleaded guilty, acknowledged the government's representations about additional fraudulent checks, but was never found to have committed any act of violence. She has sought to vindicate her Second Amendment rights on the grounds that the Constitution does not permit the permanent disarmament of a nonviolent individual who poses no individualized danger.

## The Solicitor General's Brief

The Trump Solicitor General's brief is a carefully crafted legal document that, in my assessment, represents a genuinely thoughtful attempt to navigate the difficult constitutional terrain that **Rahimi** (2024) and *Bruen* (2022) have created. The brief does not argue that § 922(g)(1) is facially unconstitutional — such a position would be extraordinarily difficult to sustain given the scope of the statute and its application to violent felons. Instead, the brief takes the position that as-applied challenges by nonviolent felons must be evaluated under the *Bruen* historical tradition framework on a case-by-case basis.

The key analytical move in the brief is this: under the *Bruen* standard, the government must demonstrate that any firearm regulation is consistent with the Nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation. For individuals who were convicted of violent offenses — those who have demonstrated a propensity for dangerous conduct — the historical tradition of disarming persons deemed dangerous or untrustworthy provides constitutionally sufficient grounding. But for individuals convicted of purely nonviolent offenses, with no history of violence and no individualized finding of dangerousness, the historical case for lifetime disarmament is considerably weaker.

The brief also addresses Attorney General Bondi's Restoration of Rights initiative. Under existing federal law, the ATF has dormant statutory authority to adjudicate applications from individuals seeking restoration of their Second Amendment rights following a felony conviction. The Bondi DOJ has moved to activate this process, providing a procedural path for qualifying nonviolent offenders to petition for reinstatement of their firearm rights through an administrative process subject to judicial review.

## Assessment of the DOJ's Approach

I regard the Solicitor General's brief as, overall, the correct approach given the present composition of the Supreme Court and the realistic range of outcomes available. The game here is not a maximalist push to eliminate § 922(g)(1) across the board — such a position would almost certainly fail with five or more justices, and a sweeping adverse ruling from the Supreme Court would set back the Second Amendment cause for a generation. The strategic objective is to establish the principle that nonviolent individuals who pose no individualized danger cannot be constitutionally disarmed for life under the Second Amendment, while preserving the government's authority to disarm genuinely dangerous individuals.

The Restoration of Rights mechanism is particularly valuable as a practical matter. If nonviolent individuals who served their time and have lived law-abiding lives can petition for and obtain restoration of their firearm rights, the constitutional harm of the overbroad application of § 922(g)(1) is substantially mitigated, and the legal pathway to a broader constitutional ruling becomes less urgent.

There are aspects of the brief that I might have written differently — in particular, I believe the historical tradition case against permanent disarmament of nonviolent individuals is stronger than the brief fully credits. But as a strategic legal document intended to advance Second Amendment rights through the Supreme Court as currently constituted, this brief is thoughtfully executed.

## Implications

The Supreme Court's eventual resolution of **Vincent v. Bondi** will have far-reaching consequences for Second Amendment law and for the hundreds of thousands of Americans who find themselves permanently disarmed under § 922(g)(1) based on nonviolent convictions.

First, if the Court adopts the Trump DOJ's framework — requiring case-by-case historical analysis for nonviolent felon disarmament claims — it will effectively invalidate the permanent disarmament of a substantial category of individuals whose convictions bear no resemblance to the dangerous conduct that historically justified forfeiture of the right to keep arms.

Second, the Restoration of Rights process, if it functions as intended, will provide a meaningful administrative remedy that reduces the population of individuals who must resort to expensive and uncertain litigation to vindicate their Second Amendment rights.

Third, and most fundamentally, the resolution of **Vincent v. Bondi** will clarify whether the federal government's approach to § 922(g)(1) must be recalibrated to focus on actual danger rather than on the categorical fact of conviction. The founding generation understood that forfeiture of rights required a genuine nexus between the individual's conduct and the harm that the restriction was designed to prevent. A permanent disarmament regime that turns on the theoretical maximum sentence for a decades-old nonviolent offense is difficult to square with that understanding.
