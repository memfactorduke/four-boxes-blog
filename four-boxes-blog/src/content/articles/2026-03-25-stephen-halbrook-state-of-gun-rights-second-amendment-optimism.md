## Summary

I'm pleased to have Second Amendment scholar and litigator Stephen Halbrook join me to assess the current state of gun rights in America. Drawing on more than five decades of involvement in firearms law and policy, Halbrook argues that despite legitimate frustrations with the pace of reform and persistent hostility in certain jurisdictions, the trajectory of Second Amendment jurisprudence and policy has moved decisively in favor of individual rights. From the days when no federal court recognized an individual right to keep and bear arms, through the landmark decisions in *District of Columbia v. Heller* and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, to the current administration's pro-Second Amendment posture at the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives, Halbrook contends that the trend favors gun rights -- even as significant battles remain.

## Background: Decades of Second Amendment Advocacy

Stephen Halbrook is among the most prolific and influential Second Amendment scholars in the country. His body of work includes major historical treatments of firearms law, ranging from the disarmament of Jews in Nazi Germany to the role of an armed citizenry in Swiss history. His scholarship on the original public meaning of the Second Amendment was cited by Justice Antonin Scalia in *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008). Halbrook is also the author of the *Firearms Law Deskbook*, a standard reference for practicing firearms attorneys, and more recent works including *The Right to Bear Arms: A Constitutional Right of the People or a Privilege of the Ruling Class* and *America's Rifle: The Case for the AR-15*.

I opened our conversation by noting that media coverage of Second Amendment issues tends to follow the principle that "if it bleeds, it leads." Negative developments -- particularly in states where Democrats hold unified government control -- dominate the headlines. But I argued that this framing obscures a more encouraging structural reality: the concentration of anti-gun legislative activity in a handful of states reflects the inability of gun-control advocates to advance their agenda at the federal level or through the federal courts.

## The Long Arc: From Collective Rights to Individual Recognition

Halbrook traced the evolution of Second Amendment law from the 1960s to the present, offering a perspective that only someone with five decades of engagement can provide.

In the 1960s and 1970s, the dominant push was toward comprehensive federal gun control. While Congress ultimately declined to adopt the most extreme proposals -- such as universal firearms registration -- it did enact the Gun Control Act of 1968, which established the modern federal regulatory framework for firearms commerce. At the state level, restrictive legislation proliferated. The Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives, which came into its own as a regulatory agency during this period, pursued aggressive enforcement policies, particularly during the Carter administration.

In the courts, the Second Amendment was largely a dead letter. The prevailing judicial interpretation treated the amendment as protecting only a "collective" militia right rather than any individual right to keep and bear arms. As Halbrook recalled, when he attended law school in the 1970s, the Second Amendment received no mention in the curriculum -- though the student newspaper carried anti-gun editorials.

The tide began to turn with the passage of the Firearms Owners' Protection Act of 1986 during the Reagan administration, which Halbrook described as a significant wave of positive reform. But judicial recognition of individual rights remained elusive for years afterward. A key turning point came with *United States v. Emerson*, 270 F.3d 203 (5th Cir. 2001), in which the Fifth Circuit became one of the first federal appellate courts to hold that the Second Amendment protects an individual right. That decision laid important groundwork for the Supreme Court's ruling in *Heller* seven years later.

## The Heller-Bruen Framework

Our conversation underscored how transformative the Supreme Court's modern Second Amendment decisions have been. In *District of Columbia v. Heller*, the Court held for the first time that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to possess firearms unconnected with militia service. In *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the Court extended that recognition to the right to carry a firearm in public for self-defense and established a historical-tradition test as the sole framework for evaluating firearms regulations under the Second Amendment.

I emphasized that neither of these decisions existed during the period Halbrook first began his work. For decades, Second Amendment advocates lacked even a foundational judicial acknowledgment that the right they were defending existed as a matter of constitutional law. The shift from that baseline to the current legal landscape -- in which courts are required to evaluate firearms regulations against the Nation's historical tradition of firearm regulation -- represents a generational transformation.

## Persistent Challenges: Resistant Circuits and Hostile Jurisdictions

Both Halbrook and I acknowledged that the post-*Bruen* litigation landscape is far from uniformly favorable. I identified several federal circuits where panels have been reluctant to apply *Bruen* faithfully, instead upholding restrictive firearms laws through what Second Amendment advocates view as results-oriented reasoning. I specifically noted concerns about the Fourth Circuit, the First Circuit, and the Ninth Circuit as jurisdictions where gun-rights litigants face an uphill battle.

At the state level, the concentration of anti-gun legislative activity in states with unified Democratic control -- including Washington, California, Illinois, Virginia, New Mexico, Colorado, New Jersey, and New York -- continues to generate restrictive laws. However, I argued that this geographic concentration is itself a sign of the gun-control movement's limited reach: unable to achieve results in Congress or in the majority of states, advocates have funneled resources into a shrinking number of receptive jurisdictions.

## The Current Administration and ATF Reform

Halbrook expressed cautious optimism about the current administration's approach to Second Amendment issues. He contrasted the Biden administration's ATF policies -- including what he described as a "no mercy" enforcement posture toward federal firearms licensees and efforts to expand the regulatory definition of "firearm" -- with the current trajectory under the Trump administration.

Specific developments Halbrook highlighted include the filing of lawsuits challenging the District of Columbia's assault weapons ban, directives to ATF to revise its enforcement policies, and the appointment of Robert Lighter, described as a Second Amendment scholar, as chief counsel at ATF. Halbrook noted that while ATF retains a statutory obligation to enforce the Gun Control Act, the manner of enforcement matters significantly, and the current leadership has moved away from the aggressive posture of the prior administration.

At the same time, Halbrook acknowledged that certain provisions of the Gun Control Act itself remain problematic and merit legislative reexamination -- a point that reflects longstanding concerns within the firearms-law community about the underlying statutory framework, independent of how any particular administration chooses to enforce it.

## The Broader Institutional Shift

Beyond the courts and the executive branch, we both pointed to broader institutional changes that favor Second Amendment interests. The growth of the Federalist Society has helped cultivate a bench of judges receptive to originalist constitutional interpretation. Second Amendment law is now taught as a distinct subject at a number of law schools -- a development that would have been unthinkable during Halbrook's time in legal education. And the increased transparency of judicial proceedings, including public access to Supreme Court oral arguments, has created a more informed and engaged community of Second Amendment advocates.

I summarized the overall posture as one in which "the trend is our friend" -- a recognition that while significant challenges remain, the structural and doctrinal foundations for Second Amendment rights are stronger than at any point in modern history. The task ahead, as we both agreed, is to sustain and build on that momentum through continued litigation, scholarship, and advocacy on The Four Boxes Diner and beyond.
