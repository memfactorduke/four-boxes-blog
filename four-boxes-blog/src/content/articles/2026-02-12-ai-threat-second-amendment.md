## Summary

Alex Karp, the chief executive of Palantir Technologies and one of the most consequential figures in the American technology and defense sector, recently delivered a stark warning at the Hudson Institute: if the United States loses the contest for artificial intelligence supremacy to the Chinese Communist Party, Americans will lose not merely technological advantage but the foundational freedoms that define the American constitutional order — including, explicitly, the right to keep and bear arms. In my analysis, Karp's warning resonates deeply with a point I have made repeatedly: Second Amendment litigation, as vital as it is, represents only one front in a much broader contest for American liberty. The preservation of constitutional rights ultimately depends on the preservation of the constitutional republic that houses them.

## Background

Alex Karp co-founded Palantir Technologies alongside Peter Thiel and others. Palantir is one of the most significant defense technology companies in the United States, providing data analytics and AI-driven capabilities to the U.S. intelligence community, military, and allied governments. Karp is a multi-billionaire with a doctorate in neoclassical social theory from Goethe University Frankfurt, and he has emerged as one of the most intellectually serious voices in the American technology sector on questions of national security, geopolitical competition, and the role of technology in preserving democratic governance.

Speaking at the Hudson Institute, Karp addressed what he described as an existential contest between the United States and China for dominance in artificial intelligence. His argument was direct: the country that wins the AI race will possess an asymmetric technological advantage so substantial that it will be able to impose its political values — including its relationship to individual rights — on the rest of the world. If China wins that race, the Chinese Communist Party's model of governance, in which individual rights are subordinate to the party's collective authority and citizens possess no meaningful right to self-defense, privacy, or political dissent, becomes the global template.

## The Connection to the Second Amendment

Karp's warning connects to the Second Amendment in a way that I have emphasized from a different angle for some time. The right to keep and bear arms is not merely an individual right in isolation. It is one component of a constitutional system — what I describe as the "four boxes" of American liberty: the ballot box, the jury box, the soapbox, and the cartridge box. Each box is a mechanism through which citizens can check governmental overreach. The four together constitute the architecture of a free society. Remove any one of them and the system is weakened; remove them all and the system collapses.

The Chinese Communist Party offers a case study in what governance looks like without any of those boxes. There is no meaningful ballot box — elections are theater. There is no independent jury box — courts are instruments of the party. There is no free soapbox — speech and press are controlled. And there is emphatically no cartridge box — individual gun ownership is prohibited, and the party's monopoly on armed force is absolute. The result is a population that is, as Karp put it, composed of peasants and peons at best, and slaves at worst.

The AI race matters to the Second Amendment community because the outcome of that race will shape the global environment in which American constitutional governance either thrives or erodes. A United States that falls behind China in AI capability will face a strategic environment in which its ability to defend its own constitutional order is diminished. The pressure on American institutions, including courts that protect constitutional rights, will increase as adversarial state actors acquire superior information, surveillance, and influence tools.

## Technology as a Civilizational Determinant

I have observed before that firearms represent one of history's most significant civilizing technologies. A firearm allows a person of lesser physical strength but greater intelligence and skill to defend herself against a person of greater physical strength. This is why the right to keep and bear arms has historically been understood as a right of the people as a whole, not merely of the strong or the wealthy. The technological equalizer of the firearm is what makes the Second Amendment's guarantee of individual self-defense meaningful for ordinary citizens.

The AI race has analogous civilizational stakes. Nations and entities that master AI will possess capabilities — in surveillance, in decision-making speed, in economic productivity, in military force projection — that are as asymmetric in their advantages as firearms were relative to earlier weapons. If a hostile power masters AI and the United States does not, the constitutional framework that protects American rights becomes more difficult to defend not just abstractly but practically.

Karp's analogy to the race for nuclear and rocket technology near the end of World War II is apt. The question of whether Nazi Germany or the United States would first develop the atomic bomb and operational long-range rockets was not merely a military question. It was a question about which civilization's values would shape the post-war world. The United States won that race, and the consequences — the post-war order, NATO, the spread of democratic governance, and the constitutional framework that has protected American rights for over two centuries — flowed from that victory.

## Implications for Second Amendment Advocates

For those of us who have devoted substantial professional effort to litigating, writing about, and advocating for the Second Amendment, Karp's warning should prompt a broader strategic perspective. Winning in the courts — and the string of Second Amendment victories from Heller through Bruen demonstrates that winning in the courts is possible — is necessary but not sufficient. The constitutional right to keep and bear arms exists within a constitutional system that requires vigilance on multiple fronts simultaneously.

Immigration policy, judicial appointments, the health of democratic institutions, and now the outcome of the AI race all bear directly on whether the Second Amendment's guarantees remain meaningful in practice. A nation that loses its technological edge to an adversarial authoritarian power, or that allows its democratic institutions to be hollowed out by internal or external pressures, will eventually find that favorable court rulings are worth less because the institutions that enforce those rulings are less reliable.

I raise these points not to suggest that Second Amendment litigation is less important than it is, but to situate that litigation in the larger context that Karp's warning illuminates. The defense of constitutional rights is a multi-front endeavor. The cartridge box matters enormously. So does the ballot box, the jury box, the soapbox, and, increasingly, the AI race that will determine which civilization's values define the next century.
