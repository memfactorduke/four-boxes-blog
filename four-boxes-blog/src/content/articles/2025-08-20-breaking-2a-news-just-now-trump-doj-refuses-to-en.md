## Summary

In my analysis, the Trump administration has taken a major and consequential step in favor of Second Amendment rights in the District of Columbia. United States Attorney for the District of Columbia Jeanine Pirro, acting in coordination with Solicitor General John Sauer and the Department of Justice, has announced that federal prosecutors will no longer bring criminal charges against individuals for the mere carrying of rifles or shotguns in the nation's capital. As I will explain, this policy change reflects a correct understanding of the Supreme Court's holdings in *District of Columbia v. Heller* and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, and represents exactly the kind of Second Amendment enforcement action I had predicted would follow once the right personnel were confirmed and in place.

## Background

The District of Columbia has, for decades, maintained extraordinarily restrictive gun laws. Among the most legally problematic of those restrictions is the District's blanket prohibition on the carrying of rifles and shotguns -- a restriction that applies to law-abiding citizens and violent criminals alike, without distinction. Under DC law, the mere carrying of a rifle or shotgun in the District, even by a person who would otherwise be entirely eligible to keep and bear arms under the Second Amendment, has been treated as a criminal offense.

The Supreme Court's 2008 decision in *District of Columbia v. Heller* struck down DC's handgun ban and recognized that the Second Amendment protects an individual right to keep and bear arms. Four years later, in **McDonald v. City of Chicago** (2010), the Court incorporated that right against state and local governments. And in 2022, the Supreme Court's decision in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen* established that the right to carry firearms outside the home for self-defense is also protected by the Second Amendment.

Against this backdrop of controlling Supreme Court precedent, DC's blanket prohibition on carrying rifles and shotguns has been constitutionally untenable for some time. The question was when, and by whom, the enforcement of that prohibition would be challenged.

## The Policy Change

As reported by the Washington Post and confirmed by The Reload, United States Attorney Jeanine Pirro has directed federal prosecutors in the District of Columbia not to seek felony charges against individuals for the mere carrying of rifles or shotguns in the District. In a statement issued to the Washington Post, US Attorney Pirro stated that DC's blanket prohibition on carrying shotguns or rifles "is clearly a violation of the Supreme Court's holdings in two landmark cases expanding the right to keep and bear arms" -- specifically citing *District of Columbia v. Heller* and *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*.

Importantly, as US Attorney Pirro clarified, this policy change does not affect the prosecution of individuals who are otherwise prohibited from possessing firearms under federal law. A convicted felon who carries a rifle or shotgun in DC remains subject to federal prosecution under 18 U.S.C. § 922(g)(1). The policy change is limited to individuals who are not otherwise prohibited persons and who are carrying rifles or shotguns without any attendant criminal conduct. The law-abiding citizen who carries a lawfully owned rifle or shotgun in DC will no longer face federal felony prosecution solely on that basis.

## The Role of Solicitor General John Sauer

In my assessment, this policy announcement is directly traceable to the confirmation of Solicitor General John Sauer. As I have noted in prior analyses, the Solicitor General of the United States serves as the federal government's chief advocate before the Supreme Court and plays a central coordinating role in establishing the federal government's legal positions on constitutional questions, including Second Amendment issues.

I had predicted early in 2025 that significant Second Amendment progress within the executive branch would require, at minimum, the Senate confirmation of two key officials: the head of the Civil Rights Division of the Department of Justice and the Solicitor General. With John Sauer confirmed as Solicitor General, the Department of Justice now has the leadership in place to identify and correct unconstitutional federal enforcement practices.

The coordination between US Attorney Pirro's office and the Office of the Solicitor General reflects exactly the kind of institutional alignment I had anticipated. This is not a unilateral prosecutorial decision by a single US Attorney's office -- it is a considered policy position endorsed at the highest levels of the Department of Justice, reflecting the administration's understanding that DC's blanket rifle and shotgun carry prohibition is constitutionally indefensible after *Heller* and *Bruen*.

## Why DC's Blanket Carry Prohibition Is Unconstitutional

In my analysis, the legal case for striking down DC's blanket prohibition on carrying rifles and shotguns is, frankly, straightforward under existing Supreme Court precedent. *Heller* recognized an individual right to keep and bear arms that is rooted in the text of the Second Amendment. *Bruen* confirmed that the right to "bear" arms -- that is, to carry them outside the home for self-defense -- is part of the core protected right. The *Bruen* framework requires the government to justify any restriction on the right to carry through a demonstration of a historical tradition of analogous regulation reaching to the Founding era.

No such historical tradition exists for a blanket prohibition on the carrying of rifles and shotguns. To the contrary, the historical record demonstrates widespread acceptance of citizens carrying long guns in public during the Founding era. DC's categorical prohibition is therefore constitutionally indefensible under the controlling framework, and the Trump DOJ's refusal to enforce it is, in my view, the correct and constitutionally required response.

## Implications

This policy change is, in my assessment, significant for several reasons. First, it provides immediate practical relief to law-abiding citizens in the District of Columbia who wish to exercise their constitutionally protected right to carry long arms. Second, it signals that the Trump administration views the Second Amendment not merely as a campaign talking point but as a constitutional right that federal law enforcement must respect and protect. Third, it may prompt legal challenges to the DC prohibition itself, potentially resulting in a court ruling that definitively strikes it down.

The announcement also illustrates a broader truth about Second Amendment rights: the personnel who occupy key executive branch positions matter enormously. The confirmation of Solicitor General John Sauer and the appointment of US Attorney Jeanine Pirro have produced concrete, tangible results for Second Amendment rights in the nation's capital. That is precisely the kind of institutional progress the Second Amendment community should be tracking and supporting.
