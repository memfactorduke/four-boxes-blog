## Summary

The text-and-history methodology prescribed by the Supreme Court in **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** (2022) has reshaped Second Amendment jurisprudence, but its proper application remains contested in the lower courts and among scholars. Professor Joel Alicea of the Catholic University of America Columbus School of Law — a Harvard Law School graduate, Princeton undergraduate, and former law clerk to Supreme Court Justice Samuel Alito — recently delivered a rigorous and illuminating exposition of the *Bruen* methodology at the Federalist Society's National Lawyers Convention. Professor Alicea's analysis provides essential clarity on both the structure of the *Bruen* test and the difficult "level of generality" problem that courts must confront in applying it.

What follows is a structured analysis of the *Bruen* framework as explained by Professor Alicea, with attention to the doctrinal questions that continue to generate confusion in the lower federal courts and among legal commentators.

## Background

Before *Bruen*, courts applying the Second Amendment generally employed a two-step framework in which the threshold inquiry asked whether the regulated conduct was within the historical scope of the Second Amendment, and then applied means-ends scrutiny — essentially interest-balancing — to determine whether the regulation survived. This approach, in practice, routinely deferred to government interests and produced outcomes indistinguishable from the rational-basis review that *Heller* had explicitly rejected.

*Bruen* abolished this approach. Justice Thomas's majority opinion held that the Second Amendment's text and history supply the only appropriate analytical tools for adjudicating Second Amendment claims. Courts may not import the interest-balancing or heightened scrutiny analysis borrowed from other areas of constitutional law. The question is not whether the government's interest is sufficiently important to justify the regulation. The question is whether the regulation is consistent with the historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States.

## Step One: The Textual Inquiry

The first step of the *Bruen* methodology requires a court to determine whether the rights claimant's conduct falls within the plain text of the Second Amendment. Professor Alicea explains that this textual inquiry focuses on the operative clause — "the right of the people to keep and bear Arms, shall not be infringed" — and asks three questions:

1. Is the rights claimant part of "the people" within the meaning of the Second Amendment?
2. Is the object at issue an "Arm" within the meaning of the text?
3. Does the claimant's desired conduct involve "keeping" or "bearing" an arm?

If all three conditions are satisfied, the claimant's conduct falls within the plain text of the Second Amendment, and that conduct is presumptively protected. The burden of proof then shifts to the government.

The reason for this burden shift, as Professor Alicea explains, is that the textual description of the right is strong prima facie evidence of the contours of the underlying natural right as understood in 1791. A violation of the text is therefore, at minimum, presumptive evidence of a violation of the underlying right. That presumption can be rebutted — but the government must do the rebutting.

## Step Two: The Historical Inquiry

At step two, the court asks whether the government's modern regulation is consistent with the "historical tradition of firearms regulation in the United States." *Bruen* describes this as an inquiry focused primarily on the era of ratification, though the precise ratification year — 1791 for the Second Amendment, 1868 for the Fourteenth Amendment's incorporation — remains contested.

Professor Alicea makes a critical and underappreciated point about this inquiry: traditions are not reducible to specific historical statutes or practices. Historical laws and regulations are *reflections* of underlying principles, but they are not themselves the principles. The constitutional inquiry therefore demands that courts identify the underlying principles that explain and unify the historical practices, not merely count the number of historical analogues.

This is what *Bruen* means when it instructs courts to examine the "how" and "why" of historical firearms regulations. Courts should ask: Why did founders-era or Reconstruction-era legislatures permit or require certain regulations of arms? What principle was being served? Does the modern regulation serve the same kind of principle in the same kind of way?

If the modern regulation cannot be justified by any principle derivable from the historical tradition, the government fails at step two and the regulation is unconstitutional.

## The Level of Generality Problem

The most vexing doctrinal challenge in applying *Bruen*, as Professor Alicea explains, is what constitutional scholars call the "level of generality problem." When asking whether a modern regulation is consistent with historical tradition, the question inevitably arises: at what level of generality should the historical inquiry proceed?

Consider a straightforward example. Suppose the government seeks to justify a modern restriction by pointing to historical laws that regulated the carrying of certain weapons in sensitive places — courthouses, for instance. At a high level of generality, one might extract the principle that "the government may restrict arms in sensitive places." At a lower level of generality, one might characterize the historical rule as "the government may restrict arms specifically in courtrooms during judicial proceedings." The modern law might survive review under the broader principle while failing under the narrower one.

The level of generality chosen determines the outcome. A court that frames historical principles too broadly will validate virtually any modern restriction by finding some abstract historical analogue. A court that frames historical principles too narrowly will invalidate any modern regulation that does not precisely replicate a historical one.

Professor Alicea argues that the correct approach is to identify the level of generality that is responsive to the "how" and "why" of the historical practice — looking at the actual mechanics and purposes of historical regulations to determine what principle they instantiate. This principle-based approach prevents both over-generalization (which would render *Bruen*'s historical methodology meaningless) and under-generalization (which would prevent the Second Amendment from being applied to modern circumstances at all).

## Application: Why This Matters for Modern Litigation

The *Bruen* methodology as correctly understood by Professor Alicea has direct implications for the constitutionality of virtually every modern gun control law.

Magazine-capacity restrictions have no historical analogue. No founding-era or Reconstruction-era regulation restricted the number of rounds a civilian firearm could hold. There is no historical principle from which such a restriction can be derived.

Red flag laws — which permit temporary disarmament without criminal conviction — can be evaluated against the historical tradition of surety and forfeiture laws, but those historical laws required specific, articulable threats of harm, not mere speculative risk assessments. The question is whether modern red flag laws are close enough in "how" and "why" to historical surety laws to survive *Bruen* scrutiny.

Categorical felon-in-possession laws must be evaluated not just facially, but as applied to particular defendants. The historical tradition undeniably permitted disarmament of dangerous persons, but the tradition provides no support for permanent, categorical disarmament of persons whose underlying convictions had no connection to violence or physical dangerousness.

## Implications

Professor Alicea's analysis provides a rigorous and faithful account of what *Bruen* requires. Courts that apply the methodology correctly — focusing on the how and why of historical regulations, identifying principles rather than merely counting analogues, and maintaining honest engagement with the historical record — will produce outcomes that vindicate the constitutional right the Supreme Court has recognized.

The challenge for Second Amendment practitioners is to bring these arguments before courts with clarity and precision. *Bruen* has given us the right framework. The task now is ensuring that lower courts apply it faithfully rather than retreating to the interest-balancing approaches that *Bruen* explicitly rejected. That project — building the case law that gives full life to the Court's methodology — is the defining work of Second Amendment litigation for the years ahead.
