## Summary

I've analyzed a new federal lawsuit filed in the Northern District of Texas that challenges the constitutionality of the Hughes Amendment to the Firearms Owners Protection Act of 1986, codified at 18 U.S.C. Section 922(o), which prohibits ordinary citizens from possessing machine guns manufactured after May 19, 1986. The plaintiffs -- the Temple Gun Club and several of its members -- advance a structural constitutional argument: that Congress lacks any Article I enumerated power to enact the prohibition, because Section 922(o) contains no jurisdictional nexus to either the Commerce Clause or the Taxing Clause. As I've examined it, the lawsuit draws on a notable dissent by then-Judge Samuel Alito in *United States v. Rybar* and raises important questions about both congressional authority and Article III standing.

## Background: The Hughes Amendment and Section 922(o)

The Hughes Amendment, named for its sponsor, New Jersey Congressman William Hughes, was added to the Firearms Owners Protection Act of 1986 during a last-minute voice vote on the House floor. The provision, codified at 18 U.S.C. Section 922(o), makes it unlawful for any person to transfer or possess a machine gun that was not lawfully possessed before May 19, 1986. The amendment was adopted with virtually no substantive debate and no congressional findings of fact to support it.

The practical effect has been significant. While machine guns manufactured before the 1986 cutoff date remain transferable under the regulatory framework of the National Firearms Act -- subject to registration, a $200 tax, and ATF approval -- post-1986 machine guns are categorically unavailable to ordinary civilians regardless of compliance with any federal licensing or registration scheme. The lawsuit filed by the Temple Gun Club seeks to dismantle that prohibition on structural constitutional grounds.

## The Article I Argument: No Enumerated Power

In my analysis, the complaint's core theory is not rooted in the Second Amendment, though that right provides important context. Instead, the plaintiffs focus squarely on Article I, Section 8 of the Constitution, which enumerates Congress's legislative powers. The complaint argues that the power to prohibit firearm possession is neither explicitly nor implicitly among those enumerated powers.

The lawsuit emphasizes that the Constitution expressly delegates to Congress authority over only four specific categories of crimes: counterfeiting securities and coin of the United States, piracies and felonies committed on the high seas, offenses against the law of nations, and treason. Section 922(o) does not fall within any of these categories, as I've examined.

The complaint further argues that any exercise of Congress's criminal law authority must be grounded in an enumerated power -- most commonly, the Commerce Clause or the Taxing Clause. This is where the Hughes Amendment is most vulnerable, in my view. Unlike many other federal firearms statutes, Section 922(o) contains no jurisdictional element requiring the government to prove a connection to interstate commerce. A federal prosecutor seeking a conviction under the provision need not demonstrate that the firearm, the defendant, or any aspect of the alleged offense touched interstate commerce. The statute simply criminalizes possession outright.

As the complaint states: "The greatest enduring stronghold of the American experiment is that the federal government is one of limited enumerated powers. Any power that is not expressly delegated to the federal government is reserved to the states and the people. Congress has no power to enact a comprehensive criminal code. Thus, any power of Congress to criminalize a person's conduct must be grounded in another enumerated power."

## Justice Alito's Rybar Dissent

I've examined how the complaint draws support from one of the most significant judicial critiques of Section 922(o): the dissent by then-Judge Samuel Alito in *United States v. Rybar*, 103 F.3d 273 (3d Cir. 1996). Writing while serving on the U.S. Court of Appeals for the Third Circuit, Judge Alito argued that Section 922(o) lacks any jurisdictional hook to either interstate commerce or the taxing power.

In his dissent, Alito examined the statute's text and legislative history, finding no evidence that Congress had made any findings linking the possession of post-1986 machine guns to interstate commerce. He further noted that the statute itself contains no element requiring proof of an interstate commerce nexus -- a feature that distinguishes it from many other federal criminal provisions, as I've analyzed.

What I find particularly significant is that Alito went on to author the Supreme Court's majority opinion in *Dobbs v. Jackson Women's Health Organization*, which underscores his significance on the current Court. His earlier analysis of Section 922(o) may prove relevant should this challenge eventually reach the justices.

## The Fifth Circuit Precedent: United States v. Nelson

In my analysis, the complaint addresses what is likely the most formidable obstacle at the circuit level: the Fifth Circuit's 1997 decision in *United States v. Nelson*, which upheld Section 922(o) as a valid exercise of Congress's Commerce Clause authority. The Fifth Circuit acknowledged in *Nelson* that every other circuit to consider the issue had reached the same conclusion, and it joined those circuits in affirming the statute's constitutionality.

However, as I've argued extensively, the fact that lower courts have uniformly upheld a statute does not foreclose Supreme Court review or reversal. The history of Second Amendment litigation illustrates this dynamic clearly. Before *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008), virtually every federal court of appeals had held that the Second Amendment protected only a collective right, not an individual one. The Supreme Court rejected that consensus decisively. Similarly, before *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), the overwhelming majority of circuits held that the Second Amendment did not protect a right to carry firearms in public. Again, the Supreme Court disagreed.

As I see it, the *Nelson* decision is nearly three decades old and predates the Supreme Court's transformative Second Amendment jurisprudence. The current Court's composition and doctrinal commitments suggest it may be receptive to revisiting the constitutionality of Section 922(o), particularly on enumerated-powers grounds.

## The Standing Question: Lessons from Parker and Heller

As I've analyzed it, perhaps the most significant practical concern with the lawsuit involves Article III standing. The complaint acknowledges that none of the plaintiffs had applied for -- and been denied -- an ATF Form 1 (to make a machine gun) or Form 4 (to transfer one) at the time the suit was filed. Instead, the plaintiffs assert that any such application would be futile, because the statute and ATF regulations require the agency to deny it.

In my view, this futility argument is not unreasonable, but the history of *Parker v. District of Columbia*, 478 F.3d 370 (D.C. Cir. 2007) -- the case that became *Heller* -- offers a cautionary lesson. In *Parker*, six plaintiffs challenged the District of Columbia's handgun ban. Five of them had never applied to register a handgun in the District. Only one -- Dick Heller -- had applied and been denied. The D.C. Circuit held that Heller was the sole plaintiff with Article III standing, because his denial constituted a concrete, particularized injury independent of any speculative threat of prosecution.

As Judge Laurence Silberman wrote for the panel: "The denial of the gun license is significant. It constitutes an injury independent of the district's prospective enforcement of its gun laws and an injury to which the stringent requirements for pre-enforcement standing . . . would not apply." The court further noted that it had "consistently treated a license or permit denial pursuant to a state or federal administrative scheme as an Article III . . . injury."

The lesson is straightforward, as I see it: courts historically require more than an assertion that applying would be pointless. The five dismissed *Parker* plaintiffs likely had strong substantive claims, but their failure to apply for registration deprived them of the concrete injury needed to satisfy Article III's case-or-controversy requirement. If the government raises a standing challenge here, the Temple Gun Club plaintiffs could face a similar obstacle.

Standing doctrines can be quirky and may evolve, particularly in the wake of *Heller* and *Bruen*. The Fifth Circuit may prove more sympathetic to pre-enforcement challenges than the D.C. Circuit was in 2007. But in my judgment, a prudent litigation strategy would seek to eliminate standing as a potential off-ramp -- particularly one that could allow the courts to avoid reaching the merits of the Article I question entirely.

## Structural Constitutionalism and Second Amendment Strategy

As I've explained on The Four Boxes Diner, the Temple Gun Club lawsuit reflects a broader and increasingly sophisticated approach to firearms litigation: challenging federal gun laws not solely on Second Amendment grounds, but on the structural constitutional principles that undergird the entire system of limited government. As Justice Scalia observed, the true protection for American liberties lies not in the Bill of Rights alone -- many authoritarian regimes have had impressive bills of rights on paper -- but in the constitutional structure of separated powers and enumerated authorities that gives those rights enforceable meaning.

In my analysis, understanding how Articles I, II, and III of the Constitution interact is essential to advancing Second Amendment interests. Article I defines and limits what Congress may enact. Article III defines and limits what cases federal courts may hear. The Temple Gun Club case sits at the intersection of both provisions, presenting questions about whether Congress exceeded its enumerated powers and whether the plaintiffs have demonstrated the kind of concrete injury that Article III demands.

## Outlook

In my assessment, the case is in its earliest stages, and its trajectory will depend on several factors: whether the government challenges standing, how the Northern District of Texas evaluates the Article I arguments, and whether the Fifth Circuit is willing to revisit or distinguish its *Nelson* precedent in light of the Supreme Court's more recent jurisprudence. I've found that the plaintiffs have crafted a well-structured complaint that identifies genuine constitutional vulnerabilities in Section 922(o). Whether the federal courts are prepared to act on those vulnerabilities remains to be seen.
