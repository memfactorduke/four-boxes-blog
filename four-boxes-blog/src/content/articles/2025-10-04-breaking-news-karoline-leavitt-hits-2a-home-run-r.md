## Summary

It is not a common occurrence for Second Amendment issues to receive substantive attention in the White House press briefing room. Policy questions about guns, carry rights, and federal firearms regulations are rarely the subject of direct exchanges between the press corps and the President's spokesperson. For that reason, a recent exchange in which White House Press Secretary Karoline Leavitt was asked directly about President Donald Trump's position on gun policy — including red flag laws and broader questions of gun violence prevention — is worth examining in careful detail. Leavitt's answer, while not the comprehensive legal analysis that a constitutional attorney would provide, conveyed several important principles that reflect the Trump administration's general orientation toward the Second Amendment and the rule of law. I want to analyze that answer, identify what it got right, and note where the administration's public messaging on these issues might be strengthened.

## Background

The question put to Press Secretary Leavitt was direct: in light of recent incidents of gun violence and political violence — including the assassination attempts against President Trump himself — has the administration reconsidered its position on gun policy? Specifically, the questioner asked whether the administration might be open to red flag laws, which were a subject of discussion during the first Trump administration, or to other gun violence prevention measures.

This is a question with significant constitutional and political dimensions. Red flag laws — formally known as Extreme Risk Protection Order statutes — allow courts to issue orders confiscating firearms from individuals who are deemed to pose a danger to themselves or others, based on petitions by family members, law enforcement, or in some jurisdictions by nearly anyone. The constitutional objections to red flag laws are substantial: they typically involve the seizure of constitutionally protected property without the procedural safeguards — notice, hearing, opportunity to be heard — that due process ordinarily requires. An ex parte order issued on the basis of an allegation, without the subject having any opportunity to contest the claim before their property is seized, is a serious due process concern. And when the property being seized is a constitutionally protected arm, the Second Amendment is also implicated.

## What Leavitt Said

Press Secretary Leavitt's response addressed several distinct themes. First, she acknowledged that gun violence and political violence are genuinely serious concerns for the administration — a factually accurate and appropriate acknowledgment. No serious person disputes that violence is harmful and that the government has legitimate interests in its prevention.

Second, she emphasized the importance of law enforcement having the resources and authority needed to identify and respond to threats before they escalate into acts of violence. This is a meaningful and constitutionally grounded point. The government's legitimate tool for addressing dangerous individuals is the enforcement of existing criminal laws — prosecution, incarceration, and the civil commitment processes that already exist for individuals who pose genuine dangers — not the prior restraint and administrative confiscation of constitutionally protected property.

Third, and most importantly from a Second Amendment perspective, Leavitt stated clearly that President Trump has consistently and repeatedly committed to protecting the constitutional right — the Second Amendment constitutional right — of Americans to own firearms. She framed this commitment in terms of the administration's respect for the constitutional order and the rule of law.

Fourth, Leavitt invoked the concept of ordered liberty — the idea that American constitutional government maximizes individual freedom within a framework of law and rules. This is a philosophically significant framing. The United States is not a libertarian utopia in which there are no rules; it is a constitutional republic in which maximum freedom is secured through a framework of laws derived from and constrained by the Constitution. The Second Amendment is part of that framework, and no administration can disregard it simply because violence occurs.

## Analysis of the Response

Karoline Leavitt is not a constitutional attorney, and the White House press briefing room is not a law school seminar. Given those constraints, her answer was genuinely quite good — better, frankly, than many politicians and public officials manage when asked about Second Amendment issues under pressure.

The core message — that the Trump administration will protect the Second Amendment, that law enforcement is the appropriate tool for addressing dangerous individuals, and that the constitutional order must be respected — is exactly right. A press secretary is not expected to provide the kind of granular legal analysis that I might offer on the constitutionality of red flag laws under the Fifth and Fourteenth Amendments' due process clauses and the Second Amendment's categorical protections. What a press secretary is expected to do is accurately convey the administration's position and reflect the President's values. On that score, Leavitt succeeded.

## The Red Flag Law Question

The specific mention of red flag laws in the reporter's question deserves direct treatment. During the first Trump administration, there were discussions — and at times more than discussions — about the possibility of federal red flag legislation. The prospect troubled many Second Amendment advocates deeply, and with good reason.

Red flag laws, as typically enacted, provide for the following: a petitioner — who may be a family member, a law enforcement officer, or in some state versions a wide variety of other persons — may apply to a court for an order requiring an individual to surrender their firearms. The court may issue that order on an ex parte basis, meaning without notice to the subject of the order and without any opportunity for the subject to be heard before the firearms are seized. Only after the seizure has occurred does the subject have the right to a hearing to contest the order.

This procedure inverts the ordinary constitutional requirement that the government may not deprive a person of property without due process of law. The Supreme Court has been clear that procedural due process requires, at a minimum, notice and an opportunity to be heard before a significant deprivation of property occurs. Red flag laws typically dispense with that requirement on the theory that the subject might use their firearms dangerously during the period between notice and hearing — an argument that, while understandable, does not satisfy constitutional due process requirements.

Moreover, when the property being seized is a firearm — a constitutionally protected arm under the Second Amendment — the constitutional analysis is even more demanding. **New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen** establishes that Second Amendment restrictions must be rooted in a historical tradition of analogous regulation. There is no historical tradition of administrative pre-seizure orders for firearms based on predicted future dangerousness. The Founding-era approach to dangerous individuals involved criminal prosecution, not administrative pre-crime confiscation.

## The Ordered Liberty Framework

Leavitt's invocation of "ordered liberty" as the framework within which the administration approaches gun policy is philosophically significant and worth examining. The concept of ordered liberty — associated with the Supreme Court's jurisprudence going back decades, and with the classical liberal tradition going back centuries further — holds that genuine freedom is not merely the absence of all constraint, but the enjoyment of maximum freedom within a framework of law that protects everyone's rights.

Within that framework, the Second Amendment establishes a right that the government may not infringe except within the narrowly defined historical exceptions that the **Bruen** framework allows. A government that respects ordered liberty respects the Constitution, including the Second Amendment. It addresses the genuine problem of violent individuals through constitutionally valid means — criminal law, civil commitment, prosecution — rather than through administrative pre-crime seizures that bypass the constitutional safeguards that make ordered liberty possible in the first place.

## Implications

The Trump administration's stated commitment to protecting Second Amendment rights is a genuine and important policy position that has been reflected not only in Karoline Leavitt's press briefing remarks but in concrete actions — including the Department of Justice's support for Second Amendment positions in the Supreme Court and in lower-court litigation. The administration's general orientation is right.

On the specific question of red flag laws, I would encourage the administration to be explicit and unequivocal: the federal government should not enact or support red flag law frameworks that seize constitutionally protected firearms from Americans without the due process protections that the Constitution demands. Law enforcement, criminal prosecution, and civil mental health commitment are the appropriate tools for addressing genuinely dangerous individuals. The right to keep and bear arms — like all constitutional rights — must be protected even when its protection is politically difficult or runs counter to the emotional response that follows acts of terrible violence.
