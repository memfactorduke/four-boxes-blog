## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Sixth Circuit has issued a decision in a case presenting one of the more unusual intersections of constitutional law and modern technology I have encountered: a First Amendment challenge to Michigan's anti-drone hunting statute brought by a company called Drone Deer Recovery LLC and its owner, Mike Yoder. In my analysis, the Sixth Circuit's per curiam decision — issued without attribution to any individual judge — correctly resolved the threshold question of standing in favor of the plaintiffs but ultimately ruled against them on the merits, holding that Michigan's prohibition on using aerial drones in conjunction with hunting does not violate the First Amendment. The case offers important lessons about the scope of First Amendment protections for information-gathering and commercial services, and it serves as a reminder that understanding all dimensions of constitutional law — not merely the Second Amendment — is essential for those who care about preserving all of our enumerated rights.

## Background: Drone Deer Recovery and Michigan Law

Drone Deer Recovery LLC is a company that offers, in states where it is lawful to do so, an aerial drone service to assist hunters. When hunters shoot deer — whether the animal is killed outright or wounded and runs — they sometimes cannot locate the animal in the field. Drone Deer Recovery deploys aerial drones equipped with heat-sensing and imaging technology to locate dead or dying deer on behalf of the hunters who retained the animal through the act of harvesting it. The company then communicates that locational information back to the hunter, who can proceed to recover the game.

Michigan, however, enacted a criminal statute that prohibits the use of drones in conjunction with hunting — a broad prohibition that encompasses precisely the kind of assistance Drone Deer Recovery offers. Because the statute carries criminal penalties, Drone Deer Recovery and Yoder declined to operate in Michigan for fear of prosecution, resulting in direct financial harm to the business.

Rather than challenge the statute on Second Amendment grounds — which would have been inapplicable on these facts — the plaintiffs challenged it on First Amendment free speech grounds. Their theory was that the process of deploying a drone, gathering thermal imaging data about the location of downed game, and communicating that factual information back to the paying customer constitutes expressive activity protected by the First Amendment. Michigan's drone law, the plaintiffs argued, effectively prohibited that expressive communication and therefore violated their free speech rights.

## The Standing Question

The federal district court dismissed the lawsuit at the threshold, holding that the plaintiffs lacked Article III standing. Standing is a constitutional prerequisite to federal court jurisdiction under Article III, which limits the judicial power to actual "cases" and "controversies." To establish standing, a plaintiff must demonstrate: (1) an injury in fact that is concrete and particularized; (2) a causal connection between that injury and the challenged conduct; and (3) redressability — that a favorable judicial decision would eliminate or substantially reduce the injury.

The district court concluded that the plaintiffs had not demonstrated a sufficient injury. The Sixth Circuit reversed that determination. As the court of appeals correctly recognized, Drone Deer Recovery suffered a concrete, particularized economic injury because it wished to operate a lawful and potentially profitable business in Michigan but could not do so without risking criminal prosecution under the drone statute. The threat of prosecution chilled the business activity the plaintiffs sought to engage in, and an injunction striking down the statute would eliminate that chill. The Sixth Circuit held that these facts satisfied all three prongs of the standing inquiry. Standing was therefore established, and the court proceeded to the merits.

## The Per Curiam Decision

A noteworthy procedural aspect of this decision is that it was issued per curiam — meaning "by the court" — without any individual judge claiming authorship. Per curiam decisions are relatively uncommon at the court of appeals level in fully briefed cases, and they typically signal one of two things: either unanimous agreement on a resolution that the panel regards as sufficiently straightforward to require no extended elaboration, or a desire on the part of the judges not to have their individual names associated with the opinion. The Sixth Circuit did not explain its decision to proceed per curiam.

## The First Amendment Analysis

On the merits, the Sixth Circuit upheld Michigan's drone hunting prohibition, rejecting the plaintiffs' First Amendment theory. The court's analysis rested on several grounds, though I would note the absence of a fully elaborated majority opinion made the doctrinal reasoning somewhat difficult to parse.

The First Amendment protects freedom of speech and, by extension, freedom of expression. The Supreme Court has held that the First Amendment also extends certain protections to the act of gathering information that will subsequently be communicated — recognizing that the act of newsgathering, for example, is constitutionally significant because it enables the protected act of publication.

The plaintiffs argued that the entire process — deploying the drone, collecting thermal imaging data, and transmitting that data to the hunter — constituted a protected communicative act. The Sixth Circuit was not persuaded. In my analysis, the court correctly recognized that the First Amendment does not extend to every act of data collection, particularly when the collection occurs in the context of regulated conduct — here, hunting — and the restriction is directed at the conduct rather than at the viewpoint or content of any speech.

Michigan's drone law does not prohibit Drone Deer Recovery from publishing information about hunting, advocating for hunters' rights, operating in states where drone-assisted deer recovery is lawful, or engaging in any form of public discourse about its services. It restricts one specific operational practice — flying drones in conjunction with the act of hunting in Michigan — which the state regulates under its wildlife management authority. The First Amendment challenge therefore failed because the statute was not directed at speech or expression but at regulated conduct that has an incidental communicative component.

## Broader Significance

As I have argued to my audience on many occasions, understanding constitutional law in its entirety — the First Amendment, the Second Amendment, the structural provisions governing separation of powers, and the post-Civil War amendments — is essential for anyone who wants to defend enumerated rights effectively. You never know when a case involving hunting regulations and aerial drone technology will implicate First Amendment doctrine that could, in a different factual posture, bear directly on Second Amendment rights.

Consider: courts have grappled with whether the act of filming police officers in public constitutes protected First Amendment expression. Similar reasoning could potentially be applied to questions about whether disseminating information about lawful firearms acquisition or use is constitutionally protected commercial or professional speech. The doctrinal lines the courts draw in cases like **Yoder** and **Drone Deer Recovery** shape the constitutional landscape within which all future First Amendment challenges must be litigated.

## Implications

The Sixth Circuit's decision in the Drone Deer Recovery case ultimately leaves Michigan's anti-drone hunting statute intact. For hunters in Michigan who hoped to utilize aerial drone recovery services, the law remains a barrier. For Drone Deer Recovery, the business remains restricted from operating in the state.

More broadly, however, the case illustrates the limits of the First Amendment as a tool for challenging conduct-based regulations that have only incidental communicative dimensions. The plaintiffs made a creative and not entirely implausible argument. But the First Amendment's protection of speech and expression has never been understood to transform every instance of information gathering into protected activity simply because the information will ultimately be communicated. The Sixth Circuit correctly identified that limitation and applied it to the facts before it.

The case also serves as an instructive example of the importance of selecting the correct constitutional theory at the outset of litigation. Whether a Second Amendment argument — premised on the argument that hunting and related activities are part of the constitutional tradition of arms bearing — might have presented a stronger avenue for challenge is a question worth considering, though such a theory would have faced its own substantial hurdles.
