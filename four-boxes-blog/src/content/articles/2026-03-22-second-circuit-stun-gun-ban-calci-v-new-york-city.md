## Summary

The United States Court of Appeals for the Second Circuit heard oral argument in *Calci v. City of New York*, a Second Amendment challenge to New York City's categorical ban on stun guns. As I've analyzed, the case raises fundamental questions about whether non-lethal electronic defense weapons constitute protected "arms" under the Second Amendment and, critically, at which step of the analytical framework established in *New York State Rifle & Pistol Association v. Bruen*, 597 U.S. 1 (2022), courts should evaluate the "common use" inquiry. The panel's questioning suggested it would follow the circuit's earlier approach in *United States v. Gomez*, placing the common-use analysis at the plain-text stage rather than as part of the government's historical-tradition burden -- a framework that effectively shifts the burden of proof away from the government and onto Second Amendment claimants.

## Background: Caetano and the Constitutional Status of Stun Guns

The constitutional status of stun guns was addressed by the Supreme Court nearly a decade ago. In *Caetano v. Massachusetts*, 577 U.S. 411 (2016), the Court issued a unanimous per curiam opinion summarily reversing the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court's holding that stun guns fell outside the Second Amendment's protection. The case involved Jaime Caetano, who had been prosecuted for possessing a stun gun she carried for protection against a threatening ex-boyfriend.

The Massachusetts high court had reasoned that stun guns were not "arms" because they did not exist at the time of the Second Amendment's ratification. The Supreme Court rejected that reasoning in a brief, two-page opinion, finding it inconsistent with *District of Columbia v. Heller*, 554 U.S. 570 (2008). On remand, Massachusetts dismissed the criminal charges against Caetano.

Given this precedent, a challenge to a municipal ban on the same category of weapon might appear to be straightforward. As the analysis below explains, however, the Second Circuit's developing approach to the *Bruen* framework has complicated what might otherwise be a simple application of existing Supreme Court authority.

## The District Court Decision

The case originated after the plaintiffs challenged New York City's complete ban on the possession of stun guns. The district court dismissed the challenge, holding that the plaintiffs had failed to demonstrate that stun guns are "in common use" by Americans for lawful purposes. By treating common use as a threshold requirement at the plain-text stage of the *Bruen* analysis -- rather than as a component of the historical-tradition inquiry at step two -- the district court effectively placed the burden on the Second Amendment claimants to prove the weapons' prevalence before triggering any government obligation to justify the regulation.

This approach allowed the court to dispose of the case without ever reaching the question of whether the city could identify a historical tradition supporting its ban.

## The Second Circuit Panel and the Gomez Precedent

The appeal was heard by a three-judge panel that included Judge Raymond Lohier. The panel's questioning during oral argument indicated it was inclined to apply the framework articulated in the circuit's earlier decision in *United States v. Gomez*, in which a panel that also included Judge Lohier and Judge Dennis Jacobs held that the arm at issue was not protected under the plain text of the Second Amendment.

The *Gomez* approach resolves the common-use question at step one of the *Bruen* framework. Under this methodology, a Second Amendment claimant must affirmatively establish that a given arm is in common use by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes before the government is ever required to present a historical justification for its regulation. If the claimant fails to make that threshold showing, the case ends without further analysis.

## The Burden-Shifting Dispute at the Heart of the Case

The central doctrinal dispute in *Calci*, in my view, concerns where the common-use inquiry belongs within the *Bruen* two-step framework -- and, correspondingly, who bears the burden of proof.

Under the *Bruen* methodology, which builds on *Heller*, the analysis proceeds in two steps. First, the Second Amendment claimant must show that the "plain text" of the Amendment covers the individual's proposed conduct -- that is, that the person seeks to "keep" or "bear" an "arm." If the plain text is implicated, the burden shifts to the government to demonstrate that its regulation is consistent with the nation's historical tradition of firearms regulation.

The question of whether a particular arm is "in common use" derives from the historical tradition concerning "dangerous and unusual weapons." As *Heller* explained, there is a longstanding tradition permitting restrictions on the carrying of "dangerous and unusual weapons." An arm that is in common use by law-abiding citizens for lawful purposes is, by definition, not "unusual" and therefore cannot be classified as "dangerous and unusual."

Because the common-use inquiry is rooted in a historical regulatory tradition, the argument follows that it properly belongs at step two of the *Bruen* framework, where the burden rests on the government. Under that reading, the government -- not the plaintiff -- would bear the burden of showing that stun guns are not in common use and are therefore "dangerous and unusual" weapons subject to historical prohibition.

As I've observed, several circuits, including the Second, Fourth, Seventh, and Ninth, have instead placed the common-use analysis at step one, treating it as part of the threshold plain-text inquiry. This approach favors the government by requiring the claimant to affirmatively prove common use before shifting any burden. The Supreme Court has not yet directly resolved this circuit-level disagreement.

## The "Dangerous" Prong and Non-Lethal Weapons

There is an additional dimension to the analysis that warrants attention. Even assuming for argument's sake that stun guns could be deemed "unusual," they present a poor candidate for classification as "dangerous and unusual" weapons. The historical tradition addresses arms that are both dangerous and unusual -- the two conditions are conjunctive.

In *Heller*, the Supreme Court held that handguns -- weapons plainly capable of lethal force -- could not be banned because they were not "dangerous and unusual." If handguns do not satisfy the "dangerous" prong, it is difficult to see how a non-lethal electronic defense weapon could. Stun guns are specifically designed to incapacitate without killing. Under any coherent reading of the "dangerous and unusual" standard, a weapon that is less lethal than a constitutionally protected handgun would appear to fall well within the scope of the Second Amendment.

## The Shadow of Summary Reversal

The Second Circuit faces a significant institutional risk in this case. In *Caetano*, the Supreme Court did not grant full briefing and oral argument. Instead, it granted certiorari and summarily reversed the Massachusetts Supreme Judicial Court in a brief per curiam opinion -- a disposition that signals the lower court's error was so clear that full merits briefing was unnecessary.

Should the Second Circuit rule against the plaintiffs and hold, whether explicitly or implicitly, that stun guns are not protected arms under the Second Amendment, it would be doing so in the face of a unanimous Supreme Court decision that reached the opposite conclusion regarding the same category of weapon. The current Supreme Court, which decided *Bruen* by a 6-3 margin, is arguably more sympathetic to Second Amendment claims than the Court that decided *Caetano* unanimously in 2016.

This dynamic may influence how the panel drafts its opinion. A narrowly written decision that avoids sweeping pronouncements about stun guns and the Second Amendment could reduce the likelihood of attracting the Supreme Court's attention. A broader holding that stun guns fall outside the plain text of the Amendment, however, would risk inviting the same type of summary reversal the Court issued in *Caetano*.

## Looking Ahead

Based on the tenor of oral argument, the Second Circuit panel appears likely to affirm the district court's dismissal of the Second Amendment challenge. The opinion, when it issues, will likely attempt to thread a narrow doctrinal needle -- ruling against the plaintiffs while avoiding language that might provoke Supreme Court review.

As I've analyzed, the case nonetheless highlights a persistent tension in the lower courts over the proper application of the *Bruen* framework, particularly regarding where the common-use inquiry belongs and who bears the associated burden of proof. Until the Supreme Court directly addresses this question, circuits will likely continue to diverge, with consequences not only for stun gun regulations but for arms-ban challenges more broadly.
